Synopsis
Oasis Petroleum Kemper 6193 14-14B [NW NE Section 14, T161N-R93W] is located ~9.5 miles
southwest of the town of Lignite, North Dakota. Kemper 6193 14-14B is situated within the Leaf
Mountain Field in Burke County. A single lateral leg trending 180.00° was proposed to be drilled
from the NW NE corner of Section 14 to the SW SE corner of Section 23 targeting the porous
silty sandstone of the Middle Bakken.
Control Wells
One completed well was used as a control offset to the Kemper 6193 14-14B.
The primary offset considered was the Rutherford 24-25-161-93H [NE NW Sec. 22, T161N-
R93W] which is a horizontal well drilled by Oasis Petroleum. Rutherford 24-25-161-93H is
located approximately 1.2 miles southeast of Kemper 6193 14-14B.
During the curve, the gamma ray that was produced was constantly compared to the Rutherford
24-25-161-93H well. Gamma ray was used to help determine a proper landing depth.
To assist in the choosing of the landing target, an isopach table (Table 1) was constructed to
measure the distance of formation tops to the target depth determined from the Rutherford 24-25-
161-93H offset well. Comparison of gamma ray markers showed the most consistent isopach for
up-hole formations to be the False Bakken.

Geologic Assessment
Methods
Geologic supervision of Kemper 6193 14-14B was provided by an experienced RPM well-site
geologists and an experienced mud logger. Gas and chromatograph levels were measured using
Wellsite Gas WatchTM real time gas detector and chromatograph system. The NOV (National
Oilwell Varco) gas detection system uses non-dispersive infrared and chemical sensor gas
detection to evaluate gases liberated from the formation by the drill bit and carried to the surface
by the drilling fluid.
The Wellsite Gas WatchTM was integrated with the Pason electronic data recorder system and
was available for viewing on location and remotely. From the Pason system we were able to
monitor and record drill rate, on-off bottom and pump strokes.
Under the direction of RPM well-site geologist, rig crews were instructed to catch lagged drill
cutting samples at 30' intervals in the vertical hole and curve build from 6,540' to 8,737' MD
and 50' samples from 8,737' to TD (18,094').
Sampled drill cuttings were examined wet and dry under a binocular microscope using both plain
(broad spectrum) and transmitted light. Cuttings were evaluated for hydrocarbon "cut" by
immersion in Acetone and inspection under a UV fluoroscope. 10% hydrochloric acid and
alizarin red were used to determine the calcareous and dolomitic content of rocks and cementing.
RPM Geologic, Inc. (RPM) well site geologists also closely examined MWD gamma-ray
information and penetration rates to aid in steering decisions and dip degree estimations.
Vertical Operations
Overview
The Kemper 6193 14-14B was spud for surface drilling on December 10th, 2014 utilizing Xtreme
25 as the drilling contractor. Prior to commencement of RPM mud logging services, a 13 1/2"
hole was drilled with fresh water to depth of 2,229' and isolated with 9 5/8" 36# J-55 casing
cemented from 2,229' to surface.
RPM well site geologists began logging the vertical interval at 6,540'.
One PDC bit was used to drill out of surface casing to vertical TD. Bit #1 (8 ¾" Varel
R616PDG2UX) was used to drill from 2,229' to 8,057' and averaged an ROP of 157.5 ft/hr in
37.0 hours of use.
Diesel invert drilling fluid with a mud weight ranging from 9.70 - 10.30 ppg was used for the
remainder of the vertical hole and in the curve builds sections. The vertical interval was drilled
to a kick off point (KOP) of 8,057', at which point vertical operations were ceased.

RPM Consulting provided equipment and personnel for directional services. Ryan Energy

Curve Build
Overview
One PDC bit was used to build the curve section. Bit #2 (8 ¾" Varel V516DUX) was used in
front of a 2.36° fixed mud motor to drill the curve from a measured depth of 8,057' to 8,737' for
20.0 hours, averaging 34.0 ft/hr.
A Chart was constructed for the curve (Chart 1) that compared the isopach of certain easily
recognizable gamma markers from the Kibbey Formation to the Middle Bakken on the control
well Rutherford 24-25-161-93H to the Kemper 6193 14-14B as marker picks were made. This
chart shows the Upper Bakken Shale top being the closest in thickness to target based on the
offset well. The Upper Bakken Shale marker was the main point used to make changes to the
target landing depth during the curve and should serve as a good reference point for future curve
landings in the immediate area. The curve was completed at a measured depth of 8,737'and TVD
of 8,532', ~27' below the base of the Upper Bakken Shale.
Chart 1: Comparing distances from gamma markers on the offset wells to markers in gamma in the curve.
Lithology
The Upper Shale of the Bakken formation [Missippian ­ Devonian] was drilled at 8,582' (8,488'
TVD) (-6,035') with sample returns of black, carbonaceous, and petroliferous shale (Figure 5)
characterized by gamma ray values in excess of 255 API counts. The Upper Shale was 51' high
to the Rutherford 24-25-161-93H. Samples were described as:
SHALE: black to dark gray, firm, friable, slightly calcareous, sub-blocky to blocky,
earthy to waxy texture, common disseminated pyrite, no visible porosity
Figure 5: Photograph sample from the Upper Bakken Shale.
The Middle Member of the Bakken formation was penetrated at 8,622' MD, (8,504' TVD) (-
6,051'), 50' high to the Rutherford 24-25-161-93H. The Middle Member contained layers of
sandstones and silty sandstones with varying amounts of porosity and oil stain (Figure 6).
Samples from the Middle Member were described as:
SILTY SANDSTONE: light to medium gray, light brown, off white, very fine to fine
grain, friable to soft, sub angular to sub rounded, moderately sorted, trace disseminated
pyrite, calcareous, weak white diffuse cut
Figure 6: Photograph of sandstone and silty sandstone from the middle member of the Bakken formation.

Lateral
Overview
Drilling fluid consisting of diesel invert (9.8-10.3 ppg) was used while drilling the lateral section
up to the TD of 18,094'. Three 6" PDC bits were used to drill the lateral.
Bit #3 (6" Varel UM613P2) was a PDC bit used to drill the lateral from 8,737' to 14,908'. It was
used for 96.5 hours at an average drilling rate of 63.9 ft/hr. Bit #4 (6" Varel UM613P2) was a
PDC bit used to drill from 14,908' to 16,106'. It was used for 29.5 hours at an average drilling
rate of 40.6 ft/hr. Bit #5 (6" Varel V513PDU) was a PDC bit used to drill from 16,106' to
18,094' (TD). It was used for 56.4 hours at an average drilling rate of 35.2 ft/hr.
Drilling operations were concluded at 1:00 PM on January 1st, 2015 at a bottom hole location of
9,750.60' S & 24.91' W of surface location or approximately 530.39' FSL & 1,344.91' FEL of
SW SE Sec. 23, T161N-R93W.
Lithology
To aid in communications while drilling the lateral, a type log was created using the gamma
signature from the Rutherford 24-25-161-93H Well in Sec. 22, T161N-R93W (Diagram 1) as it
closely resembled the gamma signature of the Kemper 6193 14-14B Well. The goal was to land
in the cool GR signature at the B marker, drop slightly in section after drilling out, then drill near
formation dip for the duration of the well using the A marker and the B marker as steering
guides.
Diagram 1: Type log created from gamma log of the Rutherford 24-25-161-93H.
The Target Zone of the Middle Bakken consisted of Silty Sandstone and apparent intergranular
porosity, moderate gas, and trace to moderate oil show (Figure 7). Samples from the target Zone
were generally described as:
SILTY SANDSTONE: clear to white, occasional light gray, light brown oil stain, friable,
fine grain, well sorted, calcite cemented, occasional disseminated pyrite, visible
intergranular porosity, possible fracture porosity, white streaming cut
Figure 7: Photograph of sandstone from "Target Zone" on Diagram 1
Gas and hydrocarbon Shows
It was found that the gas and hydrocarbon shows were a moderate-poor indicator of placement in
Zone throughout the lateral. Gas fluctuated between 100-300 units while in the Target Zone.
Background gas values ran on average around 150 units in the center of the target window
marked by rather consistent gamma around 90-110 API. The background gas remained
consistant throughout the lateral regardless to position within the target window.
The Wellsite Gas WatchTM gas chromatograph showed components of C1 and C3 present
through the entire lateral. We were not able to conduct sample cuts due to using a diesel invert
drilling fluid throughout the lateral. Trace oil was observed in the sample wash water.
Summary
1) The Kemper 6193 14-14B was initially spud for drilling on December 10th, 2014 in the
Leaf Mountain Field of Burke County, North Dakota. The well was spud by Xtreme 25
and the vertical hole was drilled to a total depth (KOP) of 8,057'.
2) A mud program consisted of diesel invert (9.8 ­ 10.3 ppg) after surface casing through
the vertical, curve build, and lateral sections to TD. The drilling mud was successful in
maintaining stable hole conditions and minimizing washout through the salt intervals.
3) One 8 ¾" Varel PDC bit was used to drill out of surface casing to a depth of 8,057'. One
8 ¾" Varel PDC bit was used to drill the curve to 8,737' MD. Three 6" Varel PDC bits
were used to drill the lateral to 18,094' TD.
4) The lateral was targeting the Middle Bakken member ranging from about 16' below the
Upper Bakken Shale down to approximately 30' below the Upper Bakken Shale.
5) Gas levels between 100-300 units were maintained through the lateral drilling operations
while in the Middle Bakken.
6) The Middle Bakken target consisted of one observed gross lithology. The Middle
Bakken was observed to be Silty Sandstone with apparent intergranular porosity.
7) Trace to moderate oil stain was observed in zone. Trace oil washed out of the samples
into the wash water when they were cleaned.
8) Drilling operations were concluded at 1:00 PM on January 1st, 2015 at a bottom hole
location of 9,750.60' S & 24.91' W of surface location or approximately 530.39' FSL &
1,344.91' FEL of the SW SE Sec. 23, T161N-R93W.
9) Exposure to target zone was ~59% of the lateral. Oasis Petroleum's Kemper 6193 14-14B
awaits completion operations to determine its ultimate production potential.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
