Introduction
Continental Resources, Inc. successfully drilled the Anderson 3-4H horizontal well at an
average Azimuth of 181.60º and an average Inclination of 89.6°, targeting the Middle
Bakken Formation from a surface hole location Section 4, T152N, R100W, (235' FNL &
967' FWL) in Williams County, North Dakota, drilling south to the south side of Section
9, T152N, R100W, with a spacing unit consisting of 1,280 acres. The well is located in
the Willow Creek Field in the West Central region of the Williston Basin, North Dakota.
The main well used for correlation was the Anderson 1-17H in Section 4, T152N,
R100W, in Williams County, North Dakota. The other offset well used was the Stokke
22-22 1 in Section 22, T152N, R100W, in Williams County, North Dakota.
Operations Summary
Vertical Section
The Anderson 3-4 was spud on June 21, 2014. Brara Geologic Consulting
arrived on site July 15, 2014 and began analysis on the Anderson 3-4H on July
16. A Mudlogging Systems, Inc. Redbox gas detection system was used for
analyzing and recording total gas and chromatograph data. The Anderson 3-4H
had preset 9 5/8" surface casing at 1960' MD. An invert mud system was used to
drill out from surface through curve landing The first BHA used included (Bit #1)
an 8 ¾'' Security MM65D, and a Hunting 7/8, 3.5 Stage, 1.5° Fixed Bent Housing
mud motor, which was used to drill out from surface casing at 1960' MD and
drilled 8264' MD to 10224' MD in 69.83 hours. The kick-off point (KOP) was
reached at 00:35 hours on July 18, 2014 at 10224' MD, 10223' TVD, (-8320' SS).
Curve Section
Drilling operations resumed on July 19, 2014 at 06:20 with the curve
assembly. This BHA included (Bit #2) an 8 ¾'' Security MMD55M, a Leam 4/5
Lobe, 7.0 stage mud motor with 2.38º Fixed Bent Housing, 4.5' bit to bend and a
Scientific MWD tool. This assembly drilled the curve of 872' in 15.96 hours to
11096' MD, 10696' TVD and (-8793 SS), curve landing. The curve was landed at
07:50 hours on July 20, 2014 at an Inclination of 91.28° and an Azimuth of
195.84° in Section 4, T152N, R100W (823' FNL & 754' FWL). Seven inch (7")
Intermediate casing was set at 11056' MD on July 21-22, 2014 and cemented.
Lateral Section
Curve cement was drilled out at 16:50 on August 14, 2014 at 10886' MD.
The new BHA included (Bit #3) an 6'' Smith MDI613, a Discovery 6/7 Lobe, 8
stage mud motor with 1.5º Fixed Bent Housing, 4.58' bit to bend and a Scientific
MWD tool. This assembly drilled 10397' to 21493' MD in 87.73 bit hours. Total
depth (TD) of 21493' MD, 10760.03' TVD, (-8857' SS) was reached at 13:35
hours on August 19, 2014 with an Inclination of 90.92° and an Azimuth of
178.96° and a bottom hole location in Section 9, T152N, R100W (236' FSL &
678' FWL). After TD and running liner Sonic Wire Line Logs and cement bond
logs (CBL), were run for the Anderson 3-4H, by Schlumberger.

Base of Last Charles Salt Formation
The Base of Last Charles Salt was picked from samples, ROP, WOB and
differential pressure at 9114' MD, 9113' TVD, (-7210' SS), 19 feet high to the estimated
prognosis. Formation gas averaged 58 units (u) and ranged between 32u and 121u. No
DST was recommended or conducted.
Base of Last Charles Salt Formation Lithology Descriptions
Samples were caught from the top of the Charles Salt Formation at
9114' MD to 9347' MD (Mission Canyon top) in 30' intervals.
Samples high in the section contained abundant amounts of halite that
was off-white to cream, semi-translucent to translucent and frosty, with coarse
grain.
Dolomite in this section was light gray to gray, tan, light brown, sub-blocky
to blocky, firm, and brittle. Texturally, most of the dolomite was microcrystalline
and mudstone. There was generally very poor inter-crystalline porosity
throughout the formation. Sample fluorescence was dull yellow/orange when
observed under a fluoroscope, though fluorescence is most likely due to residual
invert mud in the cuttings. Faint milky cuts that were observed were also
attributed to the presence of invert residue in the samples.
The limestone in the samples was gray to dark gray and tan. Samples
were mostly microcrystalline and mudstone. Cuttings were predominately sub-
blocky, firm, with no to poor visible inter-granular and inter-crystalline porosity.
Traces of anhydrite were observed in some samples. Limestone samples
fluoresced dull yellow/orange when observed under a fluoroscope, though
fluorescence is most likely due to residual invert mud in the cuttings. Faint milky
cuts observed were also attributed to the presence of invert residue in the
samples.
Mission Canyon Formation
The Mission Canyon formation top was picked from samples at 9347' MD, 9346'
TVD, (-7443' SS). Background formation gas readings through the Mission Canyon
averaged 82u and varied from 40u to 190u. Faint milky cuts were observed, but were
attributed to the presence of invert residue in the samples. No DST was recommended
or conducted.

Samples were caught in 30' intervals through the Mission Canyon
Formation from 9347' MD to 9919' MD (Lodgepole Formation top).

Lodgepole Formation
The Lodgepole top was picked from ROP and samples at 9919' MD, 9918' TVD,
(-8015' SS). Background gas averaged 518u and with connection gasses ranging from
38u to 7364u. A planned trip for the curve assembly at 10224' MD (KOP) yielded a trip
gas of 1425u. No DST was recommended or conducted.
Lodgepole Formation Lithology Descriptions
Samples were caught through the Lodgepole formation in 30' intervals
from 9919' MD to 10787' MD (False Bakken Shale).
The Lodgepole formation samples observed were predominately
comprised of limestone that was light gray to gray with trace amounts of light
brown. Cuttings were blocky to sub-blocky, firm, microcrystalline, and
occasionally argillaceous. Traces of white to off white calcite-filled fractures were
observed in some of the samples. Intercrystalline porosity was predominately
absent or extremely poor. No oil staining was observed. Slow milky blue cuts
were observed but most likely due to residual invert drilling fluid.
False Bakken Shale

10787' MD, 10654' TVD, (-8751' SS). Gas increased to a maximum of 2894u upon
penetration of the formation with the wellbore.

from 10787' MD to 10811' MD, the top of the Upper Bakken Shale.
Shale in the False Bakken was black to very dark brown, blocky to sub-
blocky, firm, moderately to very calcareous, and carbonaceous. No oil staining
was observed. Poor visible porosity was observed. Dull, even yellow/orange

Upper Bakken Shale
The Upper Bakken top was picked from samples, gamma ray, ROP, WOB and
differential pressure at 10811' MD, 10662' TVD, (-8759' SS). Background gas readings

Upper Bakken Shale Lithology Descriptions
Samples were caught through the Upper Bakken Shale Formation in 30'
intervals from 10812' MD to 10873' MD (Middle Bakken top).
Shale in the Upper Bakken was black, blocky to sub-blocky, firm,
carbonaceous and petroliferous. No visible porosity was present. Even
yellow/orange fluorescence and slow, faint milky blue/white cuts were observed
when examined under a fluoroscope, most likely the result of invert drilling fluid in
the cuttings.
Middle Bakken Dolomite
The Middle Bakken top was picked from gamma gay, ROP and samples at
10873' MD, 10682' TVD, (-8779' SS), seventeen feet deep to the estimated prognosis

Middle Bakken Lithology Descriptions
Samples were caught in 30' intervals through the Middle Bakken
Formation from 10873' MD to 11096' MD (Casing Point).
Middle Bakken dolomite samples were light gray to gray, occasionally tan,
light brown, and mottled gray. Cuttings were blocky to sub-platy, soft to firm, and
microcrystalline to fine crystalline with a chalky matrix. Samples were frequently
argillaceous to silty, occasionally grading to sandy. Cuttings were tight,
exhibiting poor visible porosity. Even, dull, yellow/orange fluorescence was
observed in nearly all samples when examined under a fluoroscope. Moderately
slow to fast milky cuts were noted throughout the Middle Bakken. Some cuts and

Closing Summary
The Anderson 3-4H was drilled from north to south in the Middle Bakken
Dolomite Member with the target consisting of fifteen vertical feet beginning 8' below the
Upper Bakken Shale and extending to 22' feet above the Lower Bakken Shale. The well
bore is estimated to have been in the target zone 97% of the lateral. The overall dip was
89.6°. Ninety foot (90') surveys were taken throughout the turn in the lateral to total
depth (TD).
Formation descended at an average dip rate of 89.71° between the intermediate
casing shoe and approximately 16700' MD, where the formation started a steeper
descent at an approximate dip rate of 89.3°. Near 18100' MD the formation again
flattened out through 21100' MD to an average of 89.8°- 90.0°. At 21100' MD the
formation began to descend again at an average dip rate of 89.5° for the rest of the
lateral. The majority of the lateral was drilled in the middle to lower section of the target
zone for the Anderson 3-4H.
Through the first half of the lateral section, from casing to 16700' MD, there was
an average of 16.0% C3, 11.8% C4 and 5.3% C5 gases with background gas averaging
1909u and ranging from a low of 33u to a max of 6772u. The wellbore was drilled in the
top, middle and lower sections of the target zone for this section of the lateral. The
notable attributes of this section were seeing both the top gamma marker, marker 1,
and the bottom gamma marker, marker 2, as well as the heat up in counts at the bottom
of the target zone.
At about 16700' MD the expected down dip of 89.3° started and continued until
approximately 18100' MD. Through this section there was an average of 12.9% C3,
8.1% C4 and 3.6% C5 gases with background gas averaging 2464u and ranging from a
low of 14u to a max of 6479u. The wellbore was in the middle of the target zone for this
section of the well, as planned, in order to see the marker above or below the wellbore if
there was a change in formation dip. The notable attribute of this section was slightly
higher gas readings for roughly 200' MD.
From 18100' MD to about 21100' the descent of the formation lessened to 87.8°
to 90.0°. For this section there was an average of 13.9% C3, 8.5% C4 and 3.2% C5
gases with background gas averaging 2021u and ranging from a low of 16u to a max of
5691u. The notable attribute of this section was the planned gradual climb up in TVD in
the target zone for the expected up dip in formation.
At approximately 21100' MD to 21493' MD (TD), the formation unexpectedly
exhibited a downward dip around 89.5°. The cool gamma marker, marker 1, was used
to tell that the formation was in fact dipping down. For this section there was an average
6
7
of 11.9% C3, 7.9% C4 and 3.4% C5 gases with background gas averaging 2034u and
ranging from a low of 235u to a max of 5224u. The notable attribute of this section was
the confirmation that the formation was dipping down at 89.5° rather than dipping up at
the expected 90.5°.
Nearly all samples exhibited good, even-scattered, moderately yellow/green
fluorescence when examined under a fluoroscope. Oil cuts were commonly immediate,
diffuse/flash milky blue in color, leaving residual rings on the porcelain thumb dish. A
majority of samples were contaminated with petroleum-based lubricant, which was used
to achieve adequate bit weight to drill and to slide. This contamination masked some
fluorescence and oil cuts in some of the samples.
Throughout the Anderson 3-4H the gas readings were considered fair to good.
The most consistent gamma markers for this lateral were found ten feet below the
Upper Bakken Shale and at the bottom of the target zone, twenty-three feet below the
Upper Bakken Shale. The former was a cool marker with readings in the low to mid 30's
(API) to low 40's (API). The latter was a hotter gamma marker that read in the high 60's
(API) to the mid 70's (API). After total depth was reached at 21493' MD on August 19,
2014, Brara Geologic Consulting remained onsite for monitoring gas for the wiper trip
and through running and setting the production liner.
Conclusions
1.) The Continental Resources, Inc. Anderson 3-4H was a successfully drilled two-
run lateral well in the Middle Bakken Dolomite Formation from a surface location
in Section 4, T152N, R100W (235' FNL & 967' FWL) to a total depth (TD) of
21493' MD, 10760.03' TVD, (-8857' SS), with a bottom hole location in Section 9,
T152N, R100W (236' FSL & 678' FWL) in Williams County, North Dakota. The
lateral was drilled from the intermediate casing shoe to TD in one run. Total
depth was reached at 13:35 hours on August 19, 2014. The Anderson 3-4H
required fifteen total days of drilling and setting intermediate casing.
2.) Ultraviolet sample shows in this lateral would be rated as fair to good with slow to
moderately fast milky cuts with residual rings. Total gas shows and overall
porosity would also be rated as poor to fair. Wellbore placement was within the
target zone for approximately 97% of the total 10397' MD of lateral section, which
should allow for excellent production opportunities.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.

Continental Resources standard CCL, CBL, 4-1/2" liner running and testing procedures
Continental Resources' standard practice for running the cement bond log and casing
caliper log is to run both logs immediately after coming out of the hole after TD, prior to running
the 4-1/2" liner, to the deepest depth obtainable; however, if there are well control concerns that
require us to run the liner sooner, only the CBL will be run and the CCL will be run after setting
the liner.
Based on the CCL results, we determine the actual API minimum burst allowance for the 7"
casing. If the downgraded API burst pressure is below our minimum required frac pressures, we
will run a 4-1/2" frac string; if severe wear or holes are found in the casing, we will run a 5"
cemented, to surface, tie back string.
The CBL log is run in order to determine the top of cement, as required by the NDIC.
Our current 4-1/2" liner program for a 1280 unit is 30, evenly spaced, stages with 29 swellable
packers. The liner shoe is set approximately 180 feet off bottom. The shoe stage below the last
packer has 2 joints, a double valved float, one joint, and a ported guide shoe ­ appx 130 ft. The
liner is run using a running tool on the end of 4" DP. The 7" packer/hanger is set about 40 ft
above KOP between two casing collars but conditions occasionally occur that require setting
higher, either through unexpected failure or in order to isolate casing wear close to KOP.
Recently we have tried 40 stage liners and the trend to explore the optimum stage count will
continue.. Once the liner is at depth, a ball is dropped through the DP, the ball is pressured up
against the setting tool to approximately 2500 psi, and the 7" packer/hanger is set.
A push pull test is done to confirm the hanger has set. Then, a 4500 psi pressure test is
completed on the back side of the 4" DP to confirm the packer has set. The setting tool is then
backed off and the 4" DP/running tool is laid down.
Immediately after the rotary rig has been moved off the well location, the 7" csg and liner
packer/ hanger are tested to the frac pressure. The testers will rig up and test the tubing head to
5000 psi. Next a test plug will be run and set, using wire line, in the top of the 7" packer/hanger.
Testers will pressure up to our frac pressure, typically 8500 psi, to confirm the 7" is ready for
completion.
