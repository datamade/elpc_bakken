GEOLOGICAL SUMMARY & CONCLUSIONS
OBJECTIVE AND LOCATION
The objective of Continental Energy's Robert 1-13H was to successfully drill a horizontal
production well through the Mississippian Middle Bakken porosity zone. Drilling was
successfully completed on the Robert 1-13H on September 17, 2014. The Robert 1-13H is
located approximately 270 feet from the North line (FNL), 1,920 feet from the west line (FWL)
of Section 13, Township 155 North, Range 100 West of Williams county North Dakota. The
intermediate casing point was established 746 feet (FNL), 1,960 feet FWL of Section 13, while
the final projected bottom hole location is estimated at 236 feet FSL; 1,983 feet FWL of Section
13, Township 155 North, Range 100 West.
OPERATIONS
VERTICAL AND CURVE
The Cyclone 25 crew spud from 9-5/8" surface casing set at 2,222 feet measured depth (MD) at
00:30 hours early the morning of September 4th, 2014. Using an 8.75 inch diameter bit the crew
drilled ahead to 2,237 feet where they stopped to perform a Formation Integrity Test which
yielded an effective mud weight of 12.1ppg. Drilling resumed but was soon interrupted to allow
the crew to repair an O-ring on the drill pipe. Drilling then resumed in earnest at 07:00 hours
later the morning of the September 4th and continued without incident to the planned kick-off
point of the 13° per 100 foot curve at 10,306 feet measured depth (MD) reached at 12:00 hours
the afternoon of September, 8th 2014.
Drilling commenced on the curve at 04:00 hours the morning of September 9, 2014. Drilling
continued without incident on the 13° per one hundred foot curve through to the landing point of
11,115 feet (MD), 10,790 feet true vertical depth (TVD) reached at 10:00 hours the morning of
September, 10th, 2014. The curve landed approximately 16 feet into the Middle Bakken roughly
7 feet into the target zone and 4 feet below the proposed landing point elevation. The proposed
dip of the well was estimated at 89.67°.
NorAm Wellsite Services began geological supervision of the Robert 1-13H at 8,500 feet (MD)
the evening of September 6th, 2014. Lagged total gas, calibrated to a one percent Methane in air
standard, and chromatography data were monitored and recorded using a Continental
Laboratories Mini-pac model 2300 total gas detector coupled with a Varian Model 490 Micro
Gas Chromatograph. Geo-steering and bore hole guidance were accomplished with the aid of
lagged total gas data, lithological samples, a Pason EDR Workstation, gamma ray logging and
directional drilling surveys. Measurement while drilling and directional drilling services were
performed by Leam Drilling Systems, LLC. Drilling fluids consisted of inverted diesel from
spud through the curve followed by brine in the lateral. The mud was engineered and maintained
by Geo Drilling Fluids, Inc.

GEOLOGICAL SUMMARY & CONCLUSIONS (CONT.)
LATERAL
After spending an extra day drilling 2,768 feet of cement, the lateral began at 15:30 hours the
afternoon of September 14th, 2014. From the shoe, the directional driller's had difficulty
controlling the drilling assembly as the well path traversed upward out of the target zone to
11,560 feet MD, 10,787 feet TVD before finally being directed back downward. The well path
re-entered the target zone at approximately 12,100 feet measured depth but not before the MWD
tool failed at 12,043 feet which necessitated a short trip to replace the tool the morning of
September 15, 2014. Drilling continued the evening of September 15th at 12,043 feet and
continued in the target to 15,085 feet MD where high gamma ray readings in excess of 200api
counts necessitated stopping to circulate bottoms-up to obtain a formation sample as the gamma
indicated Shale was present. After confirming there was no shale in the bottoms-up sample,
drilling resumed through to the end of the lateral; a driller's TD of 15,368 feet 10,830 feet TVD.
For more information, please refer to the enclosed geo-steering cross-section.
Drilling operations were completed at 22:30 hours on September 17, 2014. Approximately 92
percent of the lateral was completed within the target. The overall average dip of the well path is
estimated at 89.5°
ZONES OF INTEREST
The top of the Charles Salt was estimated, based on sample analysis and gamma ray signature, at
8,527 feet TVD (-6,339 feet) approximately 25 feet low to the prognosis. The base of the last
salt was picked at 9,230 feet TVD (-7042 feet), 9 feet low to the prognosis. No shows were
encountered in the Charles. A formation gas of 144 units recorded at 9,412feet MD was the
maximum observed gas value observed while drilling through the Charles. Mud weights through
the Charles averaged 9.7 ppg.
The Mission Canyon was estimated, based on gamma ray signature, at 9,475 feet TVD, (-7,287
feet) approximately 27 feet low to the prognosis. A maximum formation gas of 296 units
recorded at 9,991 feet MD was liberated from a gray tan to gray brown argillaceous Limestone.
As drilling progressed through the upper portion of the Mission Canyon the chromatograph
began to record measurable quantities of heavier hydrocarbons C2 and C4. Mud weight through
the Mission Canyon averaged 9.8 ppg. No other significant shows were observed through the
Mission Canyon.
The Lodgepole Formation, as observed also from gamma ray signature, was encountered at
10,123 feet (TVD) (-7,935 feet), approximately 91 feet high to the prognosis. The maximum gas
observed in the Lodgepole was liberated at 10,783 feet MD and measured 162 units. Mud
weight through the Lodgepole averaged 10.0 ppg.
The upper Bakken Shale came in at 10,895 feet MD, 10,755 feet TVD (-8,567 feet), 13 feet low
to the prognosis. At this location, the Upper Bakken is characterized as a very dark gray to dark
brown gray, earthy to chalky, massive petroliferous Shale. A maximum gas of 8,249 units
lagged up at 10,895 feet MD was the maximum recorded gas in the upper Bakken Shale. Mud
weight through the Upper Bakken measured 10.0 ppg.

GEOLOGICAL SUMMARY & CONCLUSIONS (CONT.)
The Middle Bakken was observed beginning at 10,950 feet MD, 10,774 feet TVD (-8,586 feet),
13 feet low to the prognosis. At this locale, the Middle Bakken was observed as a light gray, to
gray brown friable, dolomitic, argillaceous Siltstone with very poor to poor interparticle porosity
Shows were generally classified as fair and generally exhibited no fluorescence with a pale to
dull to moderate diffuse blue white cut. The maximum observed gas through the Middle Bakken
lateral was a 9,248 unit short trip gas recorded at 12,043 feet; the gas was accompanied by a 6
foot to 12 foot flare. Beginning at 14,020 feet MD and through to the well's TD gas was
circulated through the gas buster with a 2 foot to 3 foot constant flare. Mud weights through the
Middle Bakken generally ranged from 9.7 to 9.75 ppg.
In summary, the Robert 1-13H was another successfully completed southward trending Middle
Bakken horizontal well occupying section13 of Township 155 North, Range 100 West of
Williams County, North Dakota.

Filter Socks and Other Filter Media

Oil and Gas Wells
Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.

Continental Resources standard CCL, CBL, 4-1/2" liner running and testing procedures
Continental Resources' standard practice for running the cement bond log and casing
caliper log is to run both logs immediately after coming out of the hole after TD, prior to running
the 4-1/2" liner, to the deepest depth obtainable; however, if there are well control concerns that
require us to run the liner sooner, only the CBL will be run and the CCL will be run after setting
the liner.
Based on the CCL results, we determine the actual API minimum burst allowance for the 7"
casing. If the downgraded API burst pressure is below our minimum required frac pressures, we
will run a 4-1/2" frac string; if severe wear or holes are found in the casing, we will run a 5"
cemented, to surface, tie back string.
The CBL log is run in order to determine the top of cement, as required by the NDIC.
Our current 4-1/2" liner program for a 1280 unit is 30, evenly spaced, stages with 29 swellable
packers. The liner shoe is set approximately 180 feet off bottom. The shoe stage below the last
packer has 2 joints, a double valved float, one joint, and a ported guide shoe ­ appx 130 ft. The
liner is run using a running tool on the end of 4" DP. The 7" packer/hanger is set about 40 ft
above KOP between two casing collars but conditions occasionally occur that require setting
higher, either through unexpected failure or in order to isolate casing wear close to KOP.
Recently we have tried 40 stage liners and the trend to explore the optimum stage count will
continue.. Once the liner is at depth, a ball is dropped through the DP, the ball is pressured up
against the setting tool to approximately 2500 psi, and the 7" packer/hanger is set.
A push pull test is done to confirm the hanger has set. Then, a 4500 psi pressure test is
completed on the back side of the 4" DP to confirm the packer has set. The setting tool is then
backed off and the 4" DP/running tool is laid down.
Immediately after the rotary rig has been moved off the well location, the 7" csg and liner
packer/ hanger are tested to the frac pressure. The testers will rig up and test the tubing head to
5000 psi. Next a test plug will be run and set, using wire line, in the top of the 7" packer/hanger.
Testers will pressure up to our frac pressure, typically 8500 psi, to confirm the 7" is ready for
completion.
