INTRODUCTION
Located in Juniper field, Buffalo Wallow play, Hess Bakken Investments II, LLC.'s BW-Heidi
Ho-149-99-3526H-4 [SE SW Sec. 35, T149N, R99W] oil well is approximately 9 miles south of
Watford City, North Dakota. It is planned to be a double section, horizontal oil well that will
target the Middle Member of the Bakken Formation. This is the fourth lateral drilled to total
depth in a northern direction by Precision Drilling rig #564 on this location, as seen in Figure 1.
Previously, the BW-Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-1 well was drilled to total depth on June 1, 2013.
This well, along with the Heidi Ho H-2 and H-3, will be used for close correlation for the H-4
curve landing and lateral expectations.
GEOLOGIC EVALUATION
A two person geology team from Sunburst Consulting, Inc. provided well site geologic
supervision and logged drill cuttings, monitored gas, and provided geosteering services
throughout the drilling operation. Wellbore landing into the target zone was maximized by
collaboration and communication between the Hess operations geologist, the Baker Hughes
directional team, and the well site geologists.
Lithology
The Middle Bakken was the target formation for lateral production for this well and lithology
descriptions will be focused here. All details, including formation tops for the vertical portion of
the well, can be found in addendum to this evaluation. The Middle Bakken Member was
encountered at 11,399' MD (11,147' TVD) with a marked decrease in gamma and ROP. A
brownish gray-tan to off white siltstone was observed at the top of the Middle Bakken Member
Figure 2. Sample cuttings within the Middle Bakken lateral (left), and of the Upper Bakken Shale (right).
and is estimated to be around 3-5' thick. Samples below this siltstone are a light to medium gray
calcareous silty sandstone. The Middle Bakken can be dolomitic in part, although locally is not.
The silty sandstone is very fine grained, moderately well sorted, moderately well cemented with
calcite cement, and has an earthy-granular texture. The formation also contains traces of
disseminated pyrite and intergranular porosity, as well as traces of brown, spotty oil staining as
shown in Figure 2. Throughout the lateral, the Middle Bakken samples varied only slightly in
color, cementing, and rounding. The lower half of the Middle Bakken is what is referred to as the
Lower Middle Bakken Facies. This rock is less porous, and therefore, more compacted and does
show some bioturbation. It is marked by a characteristic gamma signature that reads a higher,
tight range of API units, typically between 90 and 100.
Hydrocarbon Shows
Background gases throughout the lateral remained low, averaging just over 350 units. There
were common gas shows ranging from 450 units to some over 1,000 units (Figure 3). Gas
readings while on the buster may be slightly lower than the true value. There were some
instances where lube used in lateral drilling operations affected the gas detection. The gas trap
was moved to another shaker to relieve this issue. Average background gas and gas shows were
low, much like they were while drilling the BW-Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-3.

GEOSTEERING
The BW-Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-4 lateral was drilled along the western hardline of the double
section drilling unit. The lateral was expected to have an overall 0.2° down dip to TD, based on
the offsets. Total depth was expected to be at roughly 11,200' TVD, while the starting TVD was
11,170'. The curve was landed low, at the bottom of the target window, and immediately out of
casing was the warm, consistent gamma characteristic of the base of the target window and
below, seen at approximately 600' VS in Figure 4.
This provided a good marker to start off with right away. Formation dip trended downward at
approximately 0.3° to around 7,200' VS. From there it leveled off to TD. These matched
expectations based on the 3 laterals drilled previously on this location, and the dip profile in
Figure 5 closely compares to the Heidi Ho H-1, H-2, and H-3 dip profiles.
Gamma ray readings within the target zone were erratic, leaving only a few markers to calculate
formation dip. However, the lower Middle Bakken facies, very evident in Figure 4 beyond
7,500' VS, was a good marker towards the bottom of the target zone. When encountered, the
Middle Bakken facies was used to consistently track formation dip. There was also a good
Figure 5. Sunburst Consulting, Inc. dip profile (Chase Noakes, Wellsite Geologist).
marker at the top of the target zone, with API values between 120-150, which were quite a bit
higher than gamma values found anywhere else in the Middle Bakken target zone.
The overall apparent formation dip was 0.19° down, gaining approximately 30' TVD, varying
only slightly from the expectations based on the surrounding offsets and structure map. The BW-
Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-4 was drilled to total depth at 21,303' MD in 16 drilling days. The
bottom hole location is 9,706.18' north and 1,783.06' west of the surface location or
approximately 503.45' FNL & 563.94' FWL, NW NW Sec. 26, T149N, R99W. Overall the
lateral was drilled 65% within the target window.
SUMMARY & CONCLUSIONS
The formations encountered during the vertical portion of the BW-Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-4
were encountered very close to prognosis. This was, in part, due to the well being the fourth
vertical and curve to be drilled on this location. It was also the fourth well for lateral drilling to
be completed to the planned total depth of 21,303' MD on the location, with the BW-Heidi Ho-
149-99-3526H-1 already producing. These things provided good control and formation
expectations while drilling. For the lateral section of the BW-Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-4, drilling
within the lower portion of the target window was much more efficient than drilling higher in the
zone. The overall formation dip was consistent with expectations based on the other wells drilled
previously on location. Background gases overall were low with values between 100 and 400
units, but the values were consistent with the other wells on the Heidi Ho pad.
Drill cuttings within the Middle Bakken target window showed little variation throughout the
lateral. However, there were good gamma ray markers at the top and bottom of the target
window, both of these making for good indication of wellbore positioning. Formation dip varied
minimally from expectations. Overall the BW-Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-4 was drilled
successfully and shows good potential for production.

Flaring Alternatives:
Mobile Gas Treatment Units:
GTUIT is a mobile flare gas capture technology for remote natural gas liquids (NGLs) extraction and gas
conditioning processes. The systems strips out NGLs from the gas stream and refrigerates them for
storage reducing volatile organic compounds (VOCs) thereby mitigating environmental impacts by
significantly decreasing emissions from flare stacks. The Units can be combined and scaled up to handle
high volumes of gas.

Hess has contracted with GTUIT to provide mobile gas treatment units (MTUs). As of 6/23/2014, Hess has
five GTUIT units being utilized in the field and expects delivery of three additional units by 8/1/2014. The
units have capacities ranging from 500 MSCFD to 1,000 MSCFD and can be relocated from one site to
another. The units also can be linked onsite to provide a larger daily capacity intake.

Hess is developing methodologies that will result in an order-of-merit list of candidate sites at which to
deploy the MTUs, based on environmental, regulatory, economic, and operational factors. There are
commercial constraints present which pertain to Hess's commercial agreement with GTUIT; wells must
produce at least half of the MSCFD unit capacity and cover the initial connection fees in order for use of
the GTUIT unit to be economic.
Natural Gas Generators:

Natural Gas Generators are an alternative to diesel generators to provide electricity to well pads located
in remote areas. The units are tied directly into the natural gas production onsite and supplement liquid
propane for use during hourly production fluctuation. Natural gas generators require liquid propane to be
used when gas is not being produced from the well. As production of gas increases and decreases hourly,
the units may switch from propane to natural gas and then back to propane.

Hess performed two pilot programs to evaluate performance characteristics of natural gas generators
from two manufacturers. Both manufacturers require further development to increase their reliability and
performance of units.

Economics favor diesel generators over natural gas generators at this time due to cost and reliability. Pilot
program was not able to produce consistent results to move forward with field implementation at this
time. Natural gas generators will continue to be evaluated as an alternative in the future.
Bi-Fuel (Blended) Diesel Generators:

Bi-Fuel diesel generators are diesel generators with natural gas "makeup" kit (blend kit) allowing units to
use produced gas onsite to power the facility.

Hess is currently in the conceptual phase of evaluation of Bi-Fuel Diesel Generators.
