Introduction
The GN-Halseth-158-97-3130H-3 is a double-section horizontal Middle Bakken well
located in New Home field on a four well pad. Drilling of the vertical and curve portions
of the well were completed by rig H&P #309 on September 16, 2014. During drilling of
the lateral portion of GN-Halseth-158-97-3130H-3, directional drilling and geo-steering
techniques were employed to maximize drilling efficiency and borehole exposure to the
target zone.
Lateral Operations
H&P drilling rig #293 (Figure 1) re-entered the GN-Halseth-158-97-3130H-3 on
December 3, 2014 to drill the lateral section of the well. Drilling began at a measured depth
(MD) of 10,648' in a saltwater drilling fluid. Right after drilling began, issues became
apparent, with the wellbore direction being uncontrollable and surveys not correlating with
the slide directions. At 10,833' MD, H&P 293 tripped out of the hole to troubleshoot the
problems. The scribe was 13° different than previously recorded, and the decision to
sidetrack was made. The original wellbore finished at a projected bottom-hole location of
143.72' north and 927.12' west of surface location or approximately 601.72' FNL &
1,466.12' FEL; SW SE Sec. 31, T158N, R97W. Sidetrack #1 began at 10,675' MD and was
completed at a total depth (TD) of 20,900' MD on December 9, 2014 with a closure
azimuth of approximately 348.90° and a closure distance of approximately 9,980.17'. The
bottom-hole location (BHL) is estimated to be 9,793.45' north and 1,921.51' west of the
surface location or approximately 306.62' FNL and 2,460.51' FEL, NW NE Sec. 30,
T158N, R97W in Williams County, North Dakota. The lateral was drilled in two BHA
(bottom hole assembly) runs, totaling 152.10 drilling hours.
Prospective Pay Zone
The Middle Bakken Member was encountered at 10,265' TVD (true vertical depth) and
8,014' mean sea level, and is approximately 55' thick. It is characterized by reduced
gamma values and drilling rates from the Upper Bakken Member. The top of the Middle
Bakken drilling target was encountered roughly 12' TVD below the base of the Upper
Shale Member. Gamma signature indicated a layer of limestone deposited ~9' TVD below
the base of the Upper Shale Member which comprised the upper 2' TVD of the targeted
pay zone. Sample returns from this upper 2' of target zone consist of very fine-grained,
slightly pyritic, silty sandstone with dolomite and calcite cement as well as limestone with
occasional to common amounts of oil staining and characteristic ooids present (Figure 2).
The middle section of the target consists of light gray-brown silty sandstone as opposed to
the bottom of section which contains very fine grained light to medium gray-brown silty
sandstone with rare amounts of disseminated pyrite and rare to common light brown spotty
oil stain (Figure 3).

Geo-steering
Baker Hughes provided equipment and personnel for MWD and directional services for
the lateral part of the well. Directional drillers, MWD field engineers, Sunburst Consulting,
Inc. well site geologists, and the Hess operations geologist were in close communication
throughout the lateral to make decisions regarding optimal geosteering targets. The lateral
target was defined as a 10' thick zone within the Middle Bakken Member, where the middle
of the zone lies ~17' below the base of the Upper Bakken Shale. The geological target zone
consisted of gamma counts ranging from 40 to 110 API units. The very upper portion of
the target window consisted of anywhere from 40 to 80 API units while the middle to upper
portion consisted of a higher gamma set ranging from 90 to 110 units. The lower part of
the target zone was characterized by 65 to 93 API units. Below the targeted pay zone we
saw mainly counts in a consistent, tight 70-95 API range, an ambiguous gamma trend.
Overall gross apparent dip was calculated at 1.09° up, with local dips ranging from
approximately 1.00°-1.20° up (Figure 4). The wellbore was calculated to be 49% within
the ideal geologic window. A "hard streak" positioned just below the bottom of the
geological window was identified by deflections in bit inclination that trended closely with
formation dip. For the majority of the lateral, the Hess target line hugged the bottom of the

Hydrocarbon Shows
Mud weights on GN-Halseth-158-97-3130H-3 ranged between 9.75 and 10.05 parts per
million (ppm) throughout the entire lateral. No oil or flares were observed at the shakers at
any time while drilling. Sample cuts were examined throughout the lateral displaying a
pale yellow color, diffuse to streaming fluorescence with speeds varying from very slow to
moderate.
Total gas was recorded as units (100 units = 1% methane) and chromatography of C1-C4
components were recorded in parts per million. Many gas shows were encountered
throughout the lateral. Some of the largest shows measuring 1,766, 1,786, and 1,811 units
were seen at 18,204', 20,475', and 17,559' MD consecutively. These high shows were
seen at the bottom of the targeted pay zone, and were possibly affiliated with lubricant
sweeps, and/or from fracture zones within the Middle Bakken member. Background gas
varied throughout the lateral with units ranging from 16 units to 1,811 units. The high gas
shows seen throughout the lateral indicate zones of fracturing within the Middle Bakken
member (Figure 5).

RE: Filter Socks and Other Filter Media
Leakproof Container Required
Oil and Gas Wells
Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
Thank you for your cooperation.
