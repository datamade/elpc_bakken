Introduction
Raphael Stroh 3-13-24H-143-97 is located approximately 20 miles Northwest of Dickinson in Dunn
County, North Dakota. Raphael Stroh 3 is one of 3 horizontal Middle Bakken wells which will be drilled
on this location. It will consist of one 20,727' MD (measured depth) long lateral drilled to the South
(184.81º). The vertical hole was drilled to a kick off point depth of 10,260' MD. The Curve section of
the well landed at a measured depth of 11,245' MD, 10,840' TVD (total vertical depth), with 15'of rat
hole. Intermediate 7"casing was set at 11,230' MD. Following pre-lateral meeting, drilling of the lateral
section began on 9/11/2014. To isolate the targeted reservoir, a 6" bit was utilized while drilling the
lateral section. Upon landing the curve, directional drilling and geo-steering techniques were employed
to maintain maximum exposure to the target reservoir.
Off Set Wells
MWD Gamma Data was used to determine marker and formation tops during the vertical hole and
during the curve. During both the vertical and curve-build sections, tops were compared to the Raphael 3
pilot hole to ensure successful landing within the targeted zone. The true vertical depth (TVD) of the
landing target was calculated by assuming comparable formation thicknesses to the pilot hole and was
adjusted as necessary until landing. Formation dips were predicted but not always accurate compared to
Evelyn Kary 2 lateral.
Engineering
Vertical Operations
The Raphael Stroh 3-13-24H-143-97 was spud on August 27th, 2014 by H&P 329. An 8 ¾" PDC
MM65D (bit #1) was used in front of a 1.5º fixed Hunting mud motor that drill from surface casing of
2,358' MD to 8,790', for a total of 6,432'. At 8,790' tripped out of the hole due to slow ROP, and
changed out BHA (bottom hole assemble). Tripped back in with an 8 3/4" Baker Talon T505 (bit #2)
motor that drilled to 9,689' MD just before KOP. Kick off point at 10,260' was reached on September
5th, 2014. DHI geologists commenced logging at 8,000'MD.
Curve Build
Curve building started on September 6th, 2014 at 0430hrs with an 8 ¾" PDC Smith (bit#3) and a 2.38
degree motor which drilled to landing TD of 11,245'MD in 38 hours. The MWD tool assemblies were
provided by Baker Hughes which recorded survey and gamma ray data. Gamma correlation was good
with some natural variation from the cross-borehole references. The wellbore successfully penetrated the
Middle Bakken formation at 11,099'MD (10,826'TVD). The Curve was landed at 11,245'MD (10,840'
TVD) approximately 14' into target zone. Upon completion of the landing, the drill string and BHA
were tripped out of the hole. 7" Casing was set at 11,230'MD.

Lateral
Drilling of the lateral on the Raphael Stroh 3-13-24H-143-97 began on September 11th, 2014 using brine
water. The target interval chosen for this well was 8-16' below the Upper Bakken Shale within a 24'
zone. Drilling of the lateral portion of the well began at a depth of 11,245'MD, with Bit #5 and drilled to
a depth of 11,445'MD for a total of 200'. The call was made to trip out of the hole due to lost ROP. Bit

Middle Bakken formation and 72.8% within the 9' target area of the payzone which is discussed more
under geo-steering section. (Page 9)
Gas Readings
Methods
One well-site geologist and two logging geologists, contracted by DHI Services, Inc. conducted the
geologic analysis for Raphael Stroh 3-13-24H-143-97. DHI installed and monitored a flame ionization
detector (FID) Total Hydrocarbon Analyzer (THA) and chromatograph interfaced with a DHI Services, Inc.
Drillsense Electronic Data Recording System (EDR). The EDR provided depth, drilling rate and pump
stroke data to the Clarity-Lite gas-monitoring software which returned gas data in the form of total gas units
and the hydrocarbon components C1 through C5 (methane, ethane, propane, butane and pentane)
concentrations expressed in parts-per-million. Total Gas and the individual components were calibrated
daily to a known calibration gas. (Figure 1)

DHI's chromatograph reads 7 gases: C-1 methane, C-2 ethane, C-3 propane, iC-4 isobutane, nC-4 normal
butane, iC-5 isopentane, and nC-5 normal pentane. The chromatograph runs continuously automatically,
injecting gas samples and sending the results to clarity light for computation. The results are all tagged with
a time which is then used to populate the database with chromatograph data that is correctly lagged to the
correct foot base on what the lag depth was at the particular time the sample was taken. The chromatograph
uses a coiled column which separates the gases based on molecular weights and is run thru a flame
ionization detector which causes an electrical charge on each carbon atom that is read by the detector and
sent to the clarity light program. The electrical response is compared to the known calibration values. The
chromatograph has various settings that can be change for hi or low gas values.
DHI's THA (Total Hydrocarbon Analyzer) uses the same type of flame ionization detector but detects the
constant stream of gas sample being brought back to the machine via agitator and poly flow sample line.
Because the THA and GC are using the same gas stream there is a constant comparison of each machines
readings. Generally the amount read by the GC, if combined, is roughly the same methane equivalent as
detected by the THA, although there are other gases read by the THA that are not being computed on the
chromatograph such as acetylene and any gas above C-5. This being said the THA will read slightly more
gas compared the methane equivalent of all the gases reported on the mud log's chromatograph data.
Gas samples were pulled through ¼" poly-flow tubing after agitation in DHI's gas trap installed in the
possum belly. DHI personnel caught lagged samples under the supervision of well-site geologists in 10' to
30' intervals from 8,000' to 20,727' TD. Cuttings were then repeatedly washed with Dawn dish detergent
and water until clean. Dry cuttings were examined under a binocular microscope. Cuttings collected from
within the Middle Bakken Formation were evaluated for hydrocarbon fluorescence using a fluoroscope,
"cut" and residual ring.
If more than one carbonate type is present then they can be determined separately using the values from the
multiple pressure peaks and formulas supplied by the instrument manufacturer. Two sets of dry cuttings
were collected, with one set sent to the North Dakota Geological Survey Core Library and the second set
sent to the project geologist overseeing operations.
Hydrocarbons
The #5 BHA from 11,245' MD to 11,445'MD had gas increase right away in the lateral section with
average background gas between 2,900 to 5,200 units of gas and connection gas up to 8,500 units. Gas also

cuttings also. Noted oil and gas shows are on the mudlog.

The Mississippian Kibbey Formation typically consists of red-
brown to red-orange sand, siltstone and shale with a limestone
zone near the middle. The upper portion typically consists of
sands inter-bedded with shale while the middle portion is mainly
white to light tan limestone. In this area, the limestone member is
predominant. Typical sample descriptions from this formation are
as follows: LIMESTONE ­dark red to red brown to red gray,
microcrystalline to very fine crystalline, varying hardness from
friable to moderately firm, earthy to dense texture with SHALE
stringers.
Kibbey Formation
Below the Kibbey is the Mississippian Charles Formation
(Madison Group). This formation consists of inter-bedded thick
to thin beds of limestone anhydrite, and salt. Note: Very
important formation in the Williston Basin. Pay particular
attention to the Base of the last salts for target changes. Typical
sample descriptions from this formation are as follows:
LIMESTONE­ light gray to gray to tan gray, cryptocrystalline to
microcrystalline to crystalline, varying hardness from friable to
hard, earthy to dense texture commonly containing anhydrite and
clear crystalline salt.

dolomitic in part.

LIMESTONE­light to medium gray with trace tan, dark gray

The "False Bakken" Marker (Mississippian Lodgepole Formation) is described as follows:
SHALE: black to brown black, soft to friable, earthy texture, slightly calcareous. This Marker is important for
making final adjustments to the landing TVD. Any drastic change here allows time for the directional drillers to
adjust their approach to landing the curve.
The Mississippian-Devonian Bakken Formation is comprised of two black carbonaceous and petroliferous
shales containing a silty, calcite and dolomite-cemented sandstone middle member with some inter-crystalline
porosity. This lithology is characterized by high gamma and elevated gas shows. A typical sample is described as
follows: SHALE - black, firm, sub blocky, earthy texture, common disseminated pyrite, calcareous,
carbonaceous, petroliferous, and no visible porosity.
Upper Bakken Shale
The Upper Bakken Shale has two distinctive peaks with the
lower peak ranging between 350 ­ 500 API. The samples showed
a blocky, very dark gray to black shale with no discernible flow or
depositional pattern.

gamma signature. Close inspection of the samples under a
microscope consistently showed a distinctive light colored, fine
grained silt stone.

Summary
1) The Oxy USA Inc. Raphael Stroh 3-13-24H-143-97 consists of drilling a vertical, curve and
lateral section in the Middle Bakken formation.
2) Liners were run post TD.
3) Raphael Stroh 3-13-24H-143-97 was spud on August 27th, 2014. Curve section was started

vertical and curve section then brine water was used to drill the lateral.
5) Percentage in zone 100%
6) Gas throughout the well averaged 2,900 units.
7) Geo-steering was accomplished via sample evaluation and gamma markers. Dip estimations

successfully utilized to maintain contact between the well bore and the target zone.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.

These wells will be connected to ONEOK's low pressure gathering system in Dunn County,

scheduled to be drilled in the foreseeable future. In addition, Oxy and ONEOK have conference
calls, usually bi-weekly, to discuss connection status of wells and changes to Oxy's drilling and
completion schedules.
Gathering System
Gas from these wells will be processed at ONEOK's Grassland Processing Plant located in
Section 36, T146N, R105W, McKenzie County, North Dakota. ONEOK's gathering system in
this area is a dynamic system that has multiple compressor stations with common suction lines.
Gas produced from this pad will have a primary flow path to ONEOK's Little Knife compressor
station and secondary flow paths to ONEOK's Killdeer and Lodgepole compressor stations. The
actual flow of the gas will be based on compression operating parameters and gathering system
pressures.
In addition to ONEOK, Hiland Partners owns and operates a low pressure gathering line less
than one-quarter of a mile from the ONEOK tie-in point for this pad. Oxy owns and operates an
oil gathering system that will be connected to the tank facilities at this pad location which has an
extra line that could be used to gather gas to increase capacity in the vicinity of this well. Petro-
Hunt has a low pressure gathering system approximately three miles west of the K ­ R Stroh pad
location.

Flowback Strategy
After the fracture treatment, these wells will be flowed through a choke to test production tanks.
Choke size will depend on final shut in pressure post frac and well pressure prior to opening the
choke. The choke will be adjusted up or down based on flowing pressure, liquid rates and sand
4
returns, to prevent clogging manifold chokes. During flowback, the water chlorides and sand
content will be monitored. When the water chlorides are greater than 100,000 ppm and the
produced fluids contain no sand, the wells will be turned to production facilities. Since the
pipeline is already at the location of the pad and Oxy will have its production facilities
constructed before flowback starts, and gas sales should start as soon as the wells start flowing
through the production facilities, unless there are capacity issues on ONEOK's system at that
time. Based on current information, it is our belief the system can take this gas upon completion
of the three wells.
The typical well on this pad will initially flow 700 barrels of oil and 300 thousand cubic feet of
gas per day based on Oxy's production type curve of this area. Based on current drilling and
completion plans, these wells will be completed in the same timeframe. For the three wells, Oxy
projects a peak oil rate of 2,100 barrels and a peak gas rate of 900 thousand cubic feet per day
from the pad. It is anticipated these wells will decline sharply after initial production as do the
majority of horizontal wells in the Williston Basin.
