Introduction
Elroy Kadrmas 6-3-10H-143-96 is located North of Dickinson in Dunn County, North Dakota. Elroy
Kadrmas 6-3-10H is a single horizontal Middle Bakken well. It will consist of one 20,932' MD
(measured depth) long lateral drilled to the South (178.99º). The vertical hole was drilled to a kick off
point depth of 9,875' MD. The Curve section of the well landed at a measured depth of 11,008' MD,
10,568' TVD (total vertical depth), with 16'of rat hole. Intermediate 7"casing was set at 10,992' MD.
Following pre-lateral meeting, drilling of the lateral section began on 12/23/2014. To isolate the targeted
reservoir, a 6" bit was utilized while drilling the lateral section. Upon landing the curve, directional
drilling and geo-steering techniques were employed to maintain maximum exposure to the target
reservoir.
Off Set Wells
MWD Gamma Data was used to determine marker and formation tops during the vertical hole and
during the curve. During both the vertical and curve-build sections, tops were compared to the Elroy
Kadrmas 5 hole to ensure successful landing within the targeted zone. The true vertical depth (TVD) of
the landing target was calculated by assuming comparable formation thicknesses to the type log and was
adjusted as necessary until landing. Formation dips were predicted but not always accurate compared to
Elroy Kadrmas 5 lateral.
Engineering
Vertical Operations
The Elroy Kadrmas 6-3-10H-143-96 was spud on December 8th, 2014 by H&P 329. An 8 3/4"Smith
MDSi616 (bit #2) was used in front of a 1.5º fixed Hunting mud motor that drill from surface casing of
2,150' MD to 7,724', for a total of 5,574'. At 7,724' tripped out of the hole due to slow ROP, and
changed out BHA (bottom hole assemble). Tripped back in with an 8 3/4" Smith MDSi616 (bit #3) that
drilled to Kick off point of 9,733', for a total of 2,009'. DHI geologists commenced logging at
8,000'MD.
Curve Build
Curve building started on December 19th, 2014 with an 8 ¾" Smith MDi516 (bit#4) and a 2.38 degree
motor which drilled to landing TD of 11,008'MD in 35.5 hours. The MWD tool assemblies were
provided by Baker Hughes which recorded survey and gamma ray data. Gamma correlation was good
with some natural variation from the cross-borehole references. The wellbore successfully penetrated the
Middle Bakken formation at 10,829'MD (10,551' TVD). The Curve was landed at 11,008'MD (10,568'
TVD) approximately 17' into target zone. Upon completion of the landing, the drill string and BHA
were tripped out of the hole. 7" Casing was set at 10,992'MD.

Gas Readings
Methods
One well-site geologist and two logging geologists, contracted by DHI Services, Inc. conducted the
geologic analysis for Elroy Kadrmas 6-3-10H-143-96. DHI installed and monitored a flame ionization
detector (FID) Total Hydrocarbon Analyzer (THA) and chromatograph interfaced with a DHI Services,
Inc. Drillsense Electronic Data Recording System (EDR). The EDR provided depth, drilling rate and
pump stroke data to the Clarity-Lite gas-monitoring software which returned gas data in the form of total
gas units and the hydrocarbon components C1 through C5 (methane, ethane, propane, butane and
pentane) concentrations expressed in parts-per-million. Total Gas and the individual components were
calibrated daily to a known calibration gas. (Figure 1)
Figure 1 ­ DHI Services, Inc. Calibration Chromatography

DHI's chromatograph reads 7 gases: C-1 methane, C-2 ethane, C-3 propane, iC-4 isobutane, nC-4 normal
butane, iC-5 isopentane, and nC-5 normal pentane. The chromatograph runs continuously automatically,
injecting gas samples and sending the results to clarity light for computation. The results are all tagged with
a time which is then used to populate the database with chromatograph data that is correctly lagged to the
correct foot base on what the lag depth was at the particular time the sample was taken. The chromatograph
uses a coiled column which separates the gases based on molecular weights and is run thru a flame
ionization detector which causes an electrical charge on each carbon atom that is read by the detector and
sent to the clarity light program. The electrical response is compared to the known calibration values. The
chromatograph has various settings that can be change for hi or low gas values.
DHI's THA (Total Hydrocarbon Analyzer) uses the same type of flame ionization detector but detects the
constant stream of gas sample being brought back to the machine via agitator and poly flow sample line.
Because the THA and GC are using the same gas stream there is a constant comparison of each machines
readings. Generally the amount read by the GC, if combined, is roughly the same methane equivalent as
detected by the THA, although there are other gases read by the THA that are not being computed on the
chromatograph such as acetylene and any gas above C-5. This being said the THA will read slightly more
gas compared the methane equivalent of all the gases reported on the mud log's chromatograph data.
Gas samples were pulled through ¼" poly-flow tubing after agitation in DHI's gas trap installed in the
possum belly. DHI personnel caught lagged samples under the supervision of well-site geologists in 10' to
30' intervals from 8,000' to 20,932' TD. Cuttings were then repeatedly washed with Dawn dish detergent
and water until clean. Dry cuttings were examined under a binocular microscope. Cuttings collected from
within the Middle Bakken Formation were evaluated for hydrocarbon fluorescence using a fluoroscope,
"cut" and residual ring.
If more than one carbonate type is present then they can be determined separately using the values from the
multiple pressure peaks and formulas supplied by the instrument manufacturer. Two sets of dry cuttings
were collected, with one set sent to the North Dakota Geological Survey Core Library and the second set
sent to the project geologist overseeing operations.

The Mississippian Kibbey Formation typically consists of red-
brown to red-orange sand, siltstone and shale with a limestone
zone near the middle. The upper portion typically consists of
sands inter-bedded with shale while the middle portion is mainly
white to light tan limestone. In this area, the limestone member is

(Madison Group). This formation consists of inter-bedded thick
to thin beds of limestone anhydrite, and salt. Note: Very
important formation in the Williston Basin. Pay particular

sample descriptions from this formation are as follows:
LIMESTONE­ light gray to gray to tan gray, cryptocrystalline to

hard, earthy to dense texture commonly containing anhydrite and
clear crystalline salt.

LIMESTONE­light to medium gray with trace tan, dark gray

SHALE: black to brown black, soft to friable, earthy texture, slightly calcareous. This Marker is important for

The Mississippian-Devonian Bakken Formation is comprised of two black carbonaceous and petroliferous

porosity. This lithology is characterized by high gamma and elevated gas shows. A typical sample is described as
follows: SHALE - black, firm, sub blocky, earthy texture, common disseminated pyrite, calcareous,
carbonaceous, petroliferous, and no visible porosity.
Upper Bakken Shale
The Upper Bakken Shale has two distinctive peaks with the
lower peak ranging between 350 ­ 500 API. The samples showed
a blocky, very dark gray to black shale with no discernible flow or
depositional pattern.

The gamma signature of the zone was typically a 50 ­ 150 API

grained silt stone.

Interpreted Cross Section
The target zone was chosen based on density porosity logs from other wells within the manning Field.
Stratigraphic location of the target was based primarily on gamma ray values but gas shows, sample
observations, and drill rates were also utilized. The presence of distinct gamma ray indicators within the
Middle Bakken Formation help provide for dip calculations when present. The Limestone stringer which
is a clear low gamma marker seemed to always be present which allowed for accurate dip calculations.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.

Pipeline Notification
These wells will be connected or are already to ONEOK's low pressure gathering system in
Dunn County, North Dakota. Oxy's acreage that comprises the spacing unit (SU) for the Elroy
Kadrmas 6-3-10H-143-96 well is dedicated to ONEOK. Under terms of the contract, ONEOK is
obligated to install the facilities that are required to connect this well to its gathering system.
Oxy provides (on a monthly basis) to ONEOK a drilling, completion and estimated first
production date for all wells that are scheduled to be drilled in the foreseeable future.
In
addition, Oxy and ONEOK have conference calls, usually bi-weekly, to discuss connection
status of wells and changes to Oxy's drilling and completion schedules.
Gathering System
ONEOK's gathering system in this area is a dynamic system that has multiple compressor
stations with common suction lines. Gas produced from this pad will have a primary flow path
to ONEOK's Lodgepole compressor station and secondary flow paths to ONEOK's Killdeer and
Little Knife compressor stations. The actual flow of the gas will be based on compression
operating parameters and gathering system pressures. Gas gathered on this part of ONEOK's
gathering system is processed at ONEOK's Grassland Processing Plant located in Section 36,
T146N, R105W, McKenzie County, North Dakota.
In addition to ONEOK, PetroHunt has a low pressure gathering line five miles north of the Elroy
Kadrmas SESE 34 pad location and Hiland has a low pressure line five miles southwest of this
pad location. PetroHunt's and Hiland's lines, along with ONEOK's, are shown on the attached
map.
The production facilities for the Elroy Kadrmas 6-3-10H-143-96 will be located less than 150'
from ONEOK's existing 8.625" outside diameter (OD) low pressure gathering line.
The
connection to ONEOK will require less than 150' of 4.50" OD low pressure gathering line and
related facilities.
Existing Lines
3
At the terminus of the metering, associated piping at the well's production facilities and the 150'
of 4.50" OD low pressure gathering line noted above, a connection will be made to an existing
8.625" OD gathering line. This 8.625" OD gathering line extends approximately 4 miles before
connecting to a 16" OD gathering line. The 16" OD gathering line extends approximately 35
miles and then connects to the inlet station piping at ONEOK's Lodgepole compressor station.
This will be the primary flow path of gas delivered to ONEOK from this pad. As noted under
"Gathering System", above, the gas will have secondary flow paths due to the dynamics of
ONEOK's gathering system. The table below shows the capacities and current flows of the
primary gathering lines connecting this well.
ONEOK's low pressure gathering system is near capacity in this area due to continuing
development of the fields in this area. It is projected that the system will have capacity when this
well is ready for production due to decline on the existing wells gathered on this segment of the
system and ONEOK's continuing efforts to maximize flow on its gathering system(s).
ONEOK is in the process of adding compressors at its Lost Bridge compressor station in Section
14, T147N, R96W, Dunn County and installing a 16" OD low pressure gathering line that will
start in T145N, R94W, Dunn County (Dunn Center) and end at the inlet of the Lost Bridge
compressor station. Construction of these facilities will redirect gas that currently flows on the
primary and secondary flow paths of the Elroy Kadrmas SESE 34 Pad to flow on the new
facilities. Although this does not directly increase the capacity of the primary and secondary
flow paths of the Elroy Kadrmas SESE 34 Pad, it redirects gas that currently flows on the same
pipelines to a different compressor station, in essence reducing volumes transported on the flow
paths for this well. ONEOK is also planning on constructing a new discharge line from its Lost
Bridge compressor station that will deliver some of the gas currently processed at ONEOK's
Grasslands Processing Plant to ONEOK's Garden Creek Processing Plant(s).
ONEOK has announced plans to construct the Bear Creek natural gas processing plant and
related natural gas and natural gas liquids infrastructure in Dunn County, North Dakota. The
Bear Creek natural gas processing plant and related facilities are expected to be operation during
the second quarter of 2016. In addition, ONEOK Demicks Lake natural gas processing plant,
located in McKenzie County, North Dakota, is expected to be operational during the third quarter
of 2016.
Flowback Strategy
4
After the fracture treatment, this well will be flowed through a choke to test production tanks.
Choke size will depend on final shut in pressure post frac and well pressure prior to opening the
choke. The choke will be adjusted up or down based on flowing pressure, liquid rates and sand
returns, to prevent clogging manifold chokes. During flowback, the water chlorides and sand
content will be monitored. When the water chlorides are greater than 100,000 ppm and the
produced fluids contain no sand, the well will be turned to production facilities. The gathering
pipeline and Oxy's production facilities should be constructed before flowback starts allowing
gas sales to start as soon as the well starts flowing through the production facilities, unless there
are capacity issues on ONEOK's system at that time. Based on current information, it is our
belief the system can take this gas upon completion of this well.
This well will initially flow 700 barrels of oil and 300 thousand cubic feet of gas per day based
on Oxy's production type curve of this area. It is anticipated this well will decline sharply after
initial production as do the majority of horizontal wells in the Williston Basin.
