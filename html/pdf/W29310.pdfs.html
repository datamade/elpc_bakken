Well Evaluation
Introduction
Liberty Resources Management Company, LLC ND State 158-95-21-28-2MBH [SW SW
Section 16, T158N, R95W] is located approximately 7.3 miles north-northwest of Tioga, in
McGregor Field of Williams County, North Dakota. The ND State 158-95-21-28-2MBH is
designed as a horizontal Middle Bakken well situated along the western flank of the Nesson
anticline of the Williston Basin. The curve would be kicked off in the Lodgepole Formation and
landed within the Middle Bakken. Directional drilling and geo-steering techniques will then be
applied to maintain maximum exposure to a 20' target zone over the course of one approximately
10,373' long lateral drilled to the south (177.58°).
The Williston Basin is a structural and sedimentary basin located at the western edge of the
Canadian Shield. Within the basin, the Bakken Formation (Late Devonian ­ Early Mississippian)
consists of four members: the Upper Bakken Shale, the Middle Bakken, the Lower Bakken and
the Pronghorn. The Middle Bakken sits between the pyritic, organic-rich Upper and Lower Bakken
shales. The Pronghorn Member, a gray siltstone, sits below the three other `Bakken' members.
Through exploration and study the fine grained, silty sandstone Middle Bakken has been
developed as an extremely viable oil and natural gas resource by way of horizontal drilling and
hydraulic fracturing technology.
Offset Wells
The following existing offset wells provided stratigraphic information to optimize planning for
curve landing. The formation tops and landing true vertical depth (TVD) of these wells were used
as control data against the formation tops chosen in the vertical and curve of the ND State 158-95-
21-28-2MBH. This allowed for target landing TVD to be periodically updated to ensure accuracy
in the landing target (Figure 1).

Engineering
The ND State 158-95-21-28-2MBH was spud on September 20, 2014 by a surface rig. Freshwater
drilling fluid was used in the surface hole, diesel invert mud for the vertical and curve, and
saltwater fluid for the lateral.
Three bottom hole assemblies were used in the vertical. The first two vertical BHAs included PDC
bits and fixed mud motors with 1.5° bends. One trip was made during the vertical at 6,307' MD
due to bit failure. A second trip was made at 8,319' MD due to motor failure, at which time a fixed
mud motor with a 1.50° was implemented. The curve was accomplished in one 12.3 hour run with
a PDC bit and a fixed mud motor holding a 2.38° bend. The curve was landed at 9,875' MD with
a projected inclination of 90.0°. Following the landing, 7" intermediate casing was set and
cemented to 9,861' MD.
The lateral was completed using three BHAs, all including PDC bits and fixed mud motors with
1.5° bends. Trips were made at 12,023', 19,301' and 20,103' MD for a bit failure, a motor failure,
and completion of the lateral, respectively. A total depth of 20,103' MD was reached on January
1, 2015 at 08:23 hours CST. The bottom hole location is 10,835.80' south and 471.36' east of
surface location, or approximately 252.20' FSL and 1,617.36' FWL SE SW, Section 28, T158N,
R95W.
Geology
Sunburst well-site geologists and rig hands caught lagged samples at 30' intervals beginning at
8,050' MD, just prior to the Base of Last Salt. Beginning at the False Bakken, samples were caught
at 10' intervals, and throughout the lateral they were caught at 50' intervals to total depth. Rock
sample analysis was performed via binocular microscopy. Formation tops were picked using
gamma data throughout the well. Further accuracy was achieved using ROP and drilling
parameters in the vertical.
Hydrocarbon components, methane (C1), ethane (C2), propane(C3) and butane (C4), measured in
parts-per-million (ppm) concentrations were monitored by Sunburst Geologists using the
Mudlogging Systems, Inc. chromatograph to record possible viable plays throughout the vertical
and curve. Hydrogen sulfide (H2S) was also monitored for safety and geologic records. Throughout
the vertical, background gas (C1-C2) remained above 40 units and averaged around 121 units.
While drilling the Ratcliffe, gas shows reached 534 units against 9.55 to 9.85 ppg diesel-invert
mud. Gas shows within the Lodgepole "B" taken while drilling the curve reached 140 units against
9.90 ppg diesel-invert mud. No H2S was detected during the lateral.
Target Zone
A 20' target zone was chosen for the ND State 158-95-21-28-2MBH by Liberty Resources
Management Company, LLC geologists that began 7' below the base of the Upper Bakken Shale
and ended 30' from the top of the Lower Bakken Shale. The target's thickness was estimated using
control well logs, structure maps and isopach maps to determine a potential pay zone for the extent
of the lateral, which would in turn, be utilized to determine the curve landing target. The ND State
158-95-21-28-2TFH type log was used as a guide for expected gamma ray markers while drilling
the lateral because of its complete record of the local Middle Bakken gamma signatures (Figure
2). The target zone is characterized by two distinct gamma zones; the upper portion, a cool gamma
zone (approximately 25-40 API) and the lower portion, a warm gamma zone (approximately 75-
100 API).

Target Zone Lithology
The targeted Middle Bakken (Mississippian-Devonian) was drilled at 9,560' TVD (-7,075'
MSL). The general lithology of the formation consisted of off white, light gray and tan colored,
fine grained silty sandstone. The formation showed possible intergranular porosity and trace
spotty brown oil staining (Figure 3).
Figure 3. Middle Bakken cuttings from the ND State 158-95-
21-28-2MBH lateral.
Target Zone Oil and Gas Show
During the course of the lateral, background gas (C1-C4) ranged from 100 to 700 units with a show
of 2,906 units at 15,414' MD (Figure 4). Higher gas shows were generally associated with the
base of the cool gamma zone. Trace spotty brown oil staining was observed throughout the target
zone with hydrocarbon cut fluorescence primarily being weak, pale yellow or yellow, diffuse and
streaming when observed. No free oil was observed at shakers.
Figure 4. Cross sectional representation of lateral target zone and wellbore versus gas, gamma and ROP.
Geo-Steering
The original curve landing target of 9,584' TVD at 90° was calculated by assuming comparable
isopach thicknesses with offset wells extending from the Lodgepole to the landing target gamma
signature. The Lodgepole "X" top indicated a higher landing target was necessary and the landing
target was raised to 9,582' TVD. No further adjustments were made to the landing target, and the
wellbore was landed at a TVD of 9,582'.
The original prognosis dip profile was developed using subsurface maps of the region, as well as
the full dip profile from the McGregor 24-16H-2128-15895- MB. These resources indicated that
formation would be down dipping throughout the lateral with isolated flat sections. Structurally,
the formation dropped 45' from initial Middle Bakken contact to lateral TD. This resulted in an
overall apparent dip of 0.25° down (Figure 5).
Figure 5. Cross sectional representation of lateral target zone as drilled.
Summary
The Liberty Resources Management Company, LLC ND State 158-95-21-28-2MBH was drilled
by Patterson-UTI 286 from spud to total depth in 22 days. The well reached a total depth of 20,103'
MD on January 1, 2015. Geologic data obtained during the ND State 158-95-21-28-2MBH indicate
a hydrocarbon rich system in the Middle Bakken target. Gas shows (C1-C4) within the target zone
reached 2,906 units within the center of zone. Samples from the target zone consisted primarily of
off white, light gray and tan silty sandstone with possible intergranular porosity and trace spotty
oil staining. Geo-steering techniques exposed 89% of uncased borehole to the 20' Middle Bakken
ideal target.

ND State 158-95-21-28-2MBH
Rig crews caught samples in 30' intervals from 8,050' to 9,850' MD and 50' intervals from 9,850' to 20,103' MD
(TD). Formation tops and lithologic markers have been inserted into the sample descriptions below for reference.
Sample descriptions begin in the Mississippian Charles Formation. Samples were examined wet and dry under a
binocular microscope. Sample fluorescence cuts are masked by invert mud through intermediate casing. Quantifiers
in order of abundance are trace, rare, occasional, common and abundant.

North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all

Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.

states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
