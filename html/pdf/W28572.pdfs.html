INTRODUCTION
The American Eagle Energy Corporation Massive State 3-1N-163-102 [Lot 3 Section 1,
T163N, R102W] is located 200' east of the Crestone State 3-1N-163-102, 1.05 miles east of the
Shelly 3-2N-163-102 and 0.79 miles west of the Tangedal 13-31-164-102 in Divide County,
North Dakota. The well was designed as a Three Forks horizontal, and was drilled to develop
Section 36 and Section 25 within 200' north and south setbacks and 500' east and west setbacks.
The well plan called for a vertical hole to be drilled into the Lodgepole Formation to 7,299' true
vertical depth (TVD), where the curve would be kicked off with a 10º/100' build rate. At
approximately 8,417' MD and 7,876' TVD, the curve was to be landed at an azimuth of 38.4°
with an inclination of 90°, in Section 36 within the Three Forks Formation. The lateral was then
to be drilled at a northerly azimuth from the SW SE quarter of Section 36 to Lot 2 of Section 25
(Figure 2). Directional drilling and geo-steering would be employed to land in the Three Forks
horizon and maintain maximum exposure to a 10' ideal target.

OFFSET WELLS
The primary offset wells used for depth correlation during curve operations for the Three Forks
target were the American Eagle Energy Corporation Crestone State 3-1N-163-102, the American
Eagle Energy Corporation Shelly 3-2N-163-102 and the American Eagle Energy Corporation
Tangedal 13-31-164-102.
1.) The Crestone State 3-1N-163-102 [Lot 3 Section 1, T163N, R102W] is located
approximately 200' west of the Massive State 3-1N-163-102. This horizontal well was
drilled in May of 2014 to a total measured depth of 14,240', and data provided by the
North Dakota Industrial Commission states that the hole is on confidential status.
2.) The Shelly 3-2N-163-102 [Lot 3 Section 2, T163N, R102W] is located approximately
1.05 miles west of the Massive State 3-1N-163-102. This horizontal well was drilled in
March of 2014 to a total measured depth of 14,430', and data provided by the North
Dakota Industrial Commission states that the hole is on confidential status.
3.) The Tangedal 13-31-164-102 [Lot 4 Section 31, T164N, R101W] is located
approximately 0.79 miles east of the Massive State 3-1N-163-102. This horizontal well
was drilled in December of 2013 to a total measured depth of 14,030', and data
provided by the North Dakota Industrial Commission states that the hole has produced
over 21 thousand BO, over 10 thousand MCFG and over 39 thousand BW.
These wells were essential in curve operations. The formation thicknesses expressed by gamma
signatures in these offsets were used to assist in curve landing. A successful Three Forks landing
was accomplished by comparing gamma signatures from the aforementioned wells with
particular emphasis on the Crestone State 3-1N-163-102 and data from the Sunburst target
projection file to the measurement-while-drilling (MWD) gamma ray data at the Massive State 3-
1N-163-102. The ideal casing TVD was periodically updated to ensure accurate landing of the
curve. Data used in this successful landing effort are found in an appendix to this report.
ENGINEERING
Vertical Operations
A 13 ½" hole was spud from surface with freshwater on June 15, 2014, cased to 1,533' MD and
cemented to surface. Diesel invert with a weight of 9.4 to 9.7 ppg was used as drilling fluid for
vertical and curve operations. The vertical was completed to 7,300' MD in 31.1 hours with one
motor and one PDC bit. The bit accomplishments are as follows: Bit #1 drilled 5,767' in 31.1
hours for an average rate of penetration of 185 ft/hour.
Directional Operations
Millenium Directional Services Ltd. provided personnel and equipment for MWD services and
directional drilling. The directional drillers, MWD and Sunburst Consulting geologists worked
together throughout the project to evaluate data, land the curve, make steering decisions to
maximize the amount of wellbore exposure to the targeted zone, and increase the rate of
penetration (ROP) in the formation.
The hole was kicked off from 7,300' MD and slid with a target build rate of 10º/100'. MWD
tools and an 8 ¾" Security MMD55M bit attached to a 2.12° mud motor were tripped in for the
curve. The curve was then drilled in 16.2 total bit hours and landed at 8,418' MD, 7,876' TVD
(projected) with an inclination of 89.9 ° (projected) at casing depth. According to Millenium
data, there are 19' of Upper Bakken and 199' of Lower Bakken Shake exposure. During June 19
and 20, 2014, 65 joints of 32# HCL-80 and P-110 and 122 joints of
29# HCL-P-110 7"
intermediate casing were run into the hole and successfully set to 8,406' MD. On June 20, 2014,
7" intermediate casing was cemented in place by Sanjel. MWD tools and a 6" Security MM73
PDC bit attached to a Cougar 1.5º bent mud motor were tripped into the hole that same day.
Casing cement was drilled out early on June 21, 2014. The lateral was then drilled with saltwater
mud (9.2 to 9.7 ppg) to TD in two runs. The first lateral BHA drilled from 8,418' to 10,800' MD
in 18.1 bit hours at which point (June 22, 2014), it was tripped out due to motor failure. An
identical BHA, save for bit (6" Security DP307 PDC), was tripped in late the same day. The
lateral was then drilled from 10,800' to 14,555' MD (TD) in 23.7 bit hours. The bottom hole
location (BHL) is 6,602.07' north and 875.69' east of the surface location or approximately
1,035.75' from the south line and 2,355.83' from the east line, within Lot 2 Section 25, T164N,
R102W. The hole was prepared for 4 ½" liner and multi-stage fracture stimulation.

throughout the wellsite. Gas data were fed from Sunburst's digital gas detector and total gas
chromatograph, through PASON for dissemination. Hydrocarbon constituents (C1-C4) are
expressed in part-per-million concentrations. Gas samples were drawn through ¼" poly tubing
after agitation in a Sunburst gas trap at the shakers. Rig crews caught time lagged samples at the
direction of the wellsite geologist. Samples were collected at 30' intervals and by the geologist at
select intervals of inquiry. Rock cuttings were analyzed in wet and dry conditions under a 10x45
power binocular optical microscope (for detailed lithologic descriptions see an addendum to this
report). Cuttings were sent to American Eagle Energy Corporation and the North Dakota
Geologic Survey's Core Library. Rock samples, rate of penetration and gamma ray data were
also used in geologic analysis to aid geo-steering and dip calculations.
Prospective Pay Zones
The Middle Bakken horizon was drilled from 7,889' MD, 7,793' TVD (-5,555') to 8,040' MD,
7,848' TVD, (-5,610'). Based upon gamma ray analysis and drill cuttings, the Middle Bakken is
composed of light to medium gray, poorly sorted, predominantly massive to poorly laminated
and calcite cemented silty sandstone (Figures 3 & 4). The sample lithology varied only slightly
throughout the curve except for minor visible changes in the proportion of calcite cement vs.
clastics. The data suggested predominantly poor intergranular porosity with slightly oily samples
and a maximum gas reading (C1) of 26 units recorded at 8,021' MD.
Figure 3 (left dry) & Figure 4 (right wet): Photographs of silty sandstone of the Middle
Bakken prospective pay zone (Andrew Kunz, Sunburst Consulting).
The Three Forks horizon was drilled from 8,239' MD, 7,870' TVD (-5,632') to 14,555' MD,
7,850' TVD, (-5,612'), well TD. Based upon gamma ray analysis and drill cuttings, the Three
Forks is composed of light gray to off white, massive to weakly laminated, strong to moderately
dolomite cemented dolomitic argillite (shale) with stringers of light pink to off white argillaceous
dolomite (Figure 5 & 6). Based on local gamma peaks and hand samples, lithology varied only
slightly in the Three Forks target zone except for locally visible changes in the proportion of
dolomite vs. dolomitic argillite in samples. The data suggested very limited porosity within the
ideal target with more porous dolomite and much greater gas values immediately above. A
maximum gas peak of 196 units (Figure 7 & 8) with typical Three Forks crude-associated gas
containing C1 to C4 was circulated up at 11,809' MD. Trace oil cut was observed in the Three
Forks and chromatograms suggested the presence of petroleum, but no live oil was observed in
samples or at the shakers.

Gas and Oil Show
Gas tracking provided evidence of a hydrocarbon reservoir during drilling of the Massive State
3-1N-163-102.
Stable and low background gas (C1) was recorded from the Charles to the
Lodgepole with a maximum gas value of 44 units recorded in the Mission Canyon. A maximum
gas peak of 68 units was recorded in the Upper Bakken Shale and heavy oil was observed in
samples. During the drilling of the curve, background gas produced a maximum show of 26
units within the Middle Bakken, and 65 units within the Lower Bakken Shale. At curve landing,
background gas within and slightly above the Three Forks target window was recorded between
35 and 45 units. Once invert was displaced with salt water mud, there was a drop in background
gas from 35 to 9 units. Relatively low background gas was seen throughout the remainder of the
lateral except while drilling at or above the top of the ideal target window (see localized gas
shows 11,020'-11,340' MD; 11,740'-12,410' MD).
The lateral gas (C1-C4) increased
dramatically while drilling above target just below the Lower Bakken Shale, the highest peak
(196 units) being recorded at 11,812' MD. From landing to TD, gas shows were highest while
drilling in the argillaceous dolomite (low gamma marker) above the target window.
When drilling commenced with saltwater mud only trace amounts of oil were observed in
samples, and no oil was subsequently observed throughout drilling. No oil stain was visible in
the ideal target zone but trace oil stain was observed directly above the top of target. Samples
exhibited trace diffuse blue white cut fluorescence which was visibly more pronounced above
target, but tended to disappear as the lateral was drilled deeper into the target zone. Cuts were
locally compromised by the presence of borehole lubricants.
Geo-steering
At the top of the Three Forks horizon in the Massive State 3-1N-163-102 (Figure 9) between
7,870' and 7,875' TVD is a zone of argillaceous dolomite with gamma readings from 50-80 API.
From the top of the ideal target zone at 7,875' to 7,885' TVD is a zone of interbedded dolomitic
argillite (shale) and argillaceous dolomite with gamma readings from 92-165 API.
A calculation for the landing target based on subsea values of the surrounding wells was 7,876'
TVD. The objective was to land slightly below the low gamma zone at the top of the prognosed
target zone, then drill downward to the bottom profiling the target window.
Repeated
comparison of TVD gamma ray data to the Crestone State 3-1N-163-102, Tangedal 13-31-164-
102 and Shelly 3-2N-163-102 offset log data allowed for ongoing adjustments to the landing
target. This was further facilitated by the target projection worksheet provided by Sunburst.
Actual landing was 1' below the top of target at 8,418' MD 7,876' TVD. This put the window 5'
below the Lower Bakken Shale. Based on data supplied by the client, it was anticipated that the
target zone of the Massive State 3-1N-163-102 would rise at an average 0.67° to the end of the
lateral.

SUMMARY
1) Massive State 3-1N-163-102 is a successfully drilled 6,137' long Three Forks horizontal well.
It was drilled from spud to TD in 10 drilling days and within the ideal target zone for 92% of its
length.
2) The vertical hole was spud from surface on June 15, 2014, drilled to 1,533' MD in 13 hours
with one bit, cased to 1,533' MD and cemented in place. The vertical was completed to 7,300'
MD in 31.1 hours with one PDC bit and one motor. The curve was kicked off at 7,300' MD and
drilled to 8,418' using one PDC bit and one motor in 16.2 hours. On June 19-20, 2014
intermediate casing was run to 8,406' MD and cemented in place by Sanjel. The lateral was
drilled with two motors and two PDC bits in 41.8 hours. A total depth of 14,555' MD was
reached on June 24, 2014.
3) Diesel invert drilling fluid (9.4-9.7 ppg) and saltwater brine (9.2-9.3 ppg) were used to
maintain stable hole conditions, minimize washout through the salt intervals and permit adequate
analysis of mud gas concentrations. Lubrication was used to assist sliding and to grease the hole
for the liner during drilling of the lateral.
4) Samples from the Three Forks target zone consisted of interbedded argillaceous dolomite with
dolomitic argillite (shale). Oil shows varied significantly by stratigraphic position with no to
trace disseminated blue white cut fluorescence in the target zone and in the overlying low
gamma.
5) Gas tracking provided evidence of a hydrocarbon reservoir during drilling of the Massive
State 3-1N-163-102. Stable and low background gas (C1) was recorded from the Charles to the
Lodgepole with a maximum gas value of 44 units recorded in the Mission Canyon. A maximum
gas peak of 68 units was recorded in the Upper Bakken Shale and heavy oil was observed in
samples. During the drilling of the curve, background gas produced a maximum show of 26
units within the Middle Bakken, and 65 units within the Lower Bakken Shale. At curve landing,
background gas within and slightly above the Three Forks target window was recorded between
35 and 45 units. Once invert was displaced with saltwater mud, there was a drop in background
gas from 9 to 35 units. Relatively low background gas was seen through the remainder of the
lateral except while drilling above target just below the Lower Bakken Shale where gas (C1-C4)
increased up to 196 units.
6) The American Eagle Energy Corporation Massive State 3-1N-163-102 awaits a multi-stage
fracture stimulation to determine its ultimate production potential.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
