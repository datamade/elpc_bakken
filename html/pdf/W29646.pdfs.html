INTRODUCTION
The SM Energy Company Virgil 2B-16HN [NW NE Section 16, T163N, R100W] is
located approximately 5.25 miles northeast of Fortuna in Divide County, North Dakota.
The subject well was designed to drill north at a heading of 2.62°, from [434' FNL and
2,274' FEL] Section 16 to Lot 2 Section 4, T163N, R100W by the Nabors #687 drilling
rig (Figure 1). The Virgil 2B-16HN is intended to develop oil production from the
Bakken Formation in the West Ambrose Field.
OFFSET WELL CONTROL
Virgil 2B-16HN offset control was provided by the Kaiser-Francis Oil Company.,
Spyglass 3-8 [SW NE Section 8, T163N, R100W], the SM Energy Company., Wolter 13-
9H [SW SW Section. 9, T163N, R100W] and the Louisiana Land & Exploration Co.
State Lacher 13-3 1 [NW SW Section 3, T163N R100W] (Figure 2). The Spyglass 3-8 is
located approximately 1.15 miles northwest of the Virgil 2B-16HN, and was drilled in
January of 2006. The Spyglass 3-8 is a Duperow vertical well and has produced in
excess of 29 thousand barrels of oil over the course of its life. The Wolter 13-9H is
located approximately 0.42 miles west-northwest of the Virgil 2B-16HN, and was drilled
in July of 2011. The Wolter 13-9H is a Three Forks horizontal well and to date has
produced over 264 thousand barrels of oil. The State Lacher 13-3 1 is located 1.54 miles
north-northeast of the Virgil 2B-16HN, and was drilled in September of 1985. It is
currently a non-producing Duperow vertical well. Open-hole logs from these wells were
used in forecasting formation tops as well as the landing target for the Virgil 2B-16HN.
The Wolter 13-9H was used to provide the closest landing projection through the Middle
Bakken.
Figure 2) Map showing the location of the SM Energy Company Virgil 2B-16HN and
the location of the three wells used for offset control (NDIC Oil & Gas Division, Arc
IMS online).
ENGINEERING & OPERATIONS
On December 7th, 2014, the Virgil 2B-16HN was re-entered at 1,560' measured depth
(MD) by Nabors #687. The surface hole was isolated with 9 " surface casing (36# J-
55). The straight hole was drilled to a KOP (kick-off point) of 7,510' MD with a NOV 8
¾" DS616M PDC bit and a Hunting NBR 1.50º mud motor. The curve assembly,
including a NOV 8 ¾" E1202-A11B PDC bit and a Sperry 2.25º motor, was tripped in at
7,510' MD and drilled to landing at 8,278' MD on December 9th, 2014. Diesel invert
drilling fluid was used through the vertical hole and curve build sections (1,560' to 8,278'
MD) maintaining a 9.55-9.70 ppg. mud weight for stable hole conditions. Sperry-
Halliburton provided night tour directional drilling and measurement-while drilling
(MWD) services. Surveys from the vertical hole provided tie-in orientation of the
wellbore to begin the curve build and lateral drilling. Sunburst Consulting, Inc. provided
onsite geologic consultation.
After curve landing, 7" intermediate casing (29# HCL-80 and 32# HCL-80) was set and
cemented to 8,258' MD. Drilling fluid was changed over to saltwater ranging between
9.65-9.80 ppg. Lateral operations began on December 10th, 2014. The lateral BHA
included a Varel 6" 6VM713 PDC bit, a Hunting 1.5° fixed mud motor, and MWD
survey and gamma tools. This BHA was used to drill the entire two-section lateral from
intermediate casing to 18,363' MD.
The Virgil 2B-16HN was drilled to a total depth (TD) of 18,363' MD, 8,000' true vertical
depth (TVD), on December 14th, 2014. Projected to TD, the bottom hole coordinates are
10,790.91' north and 495.05' east of surface location or approximately 215.59' FNL and
1,778.95' FEL Lot 2 Section. 4, T163N, R100W. At TD the wellbore has a closure
distance of 10,802.26' at a closure direction of 2.63°.
GEOLOGIC EVALUATION
Geologic supervision was provided by Sunburst Consulting, Inc. for the Virgil 2B-16HN.
The geology team monitored gas, logged drill cuttings and provided onsite geo-steering
consultation. Sample logging and gas monitoring began at 7,510' MD. The wellsite
geologists caught drill cuttings at 30' time lagged intervals through the curve and 100'
intervals through the lateral. Drill cuttings were examined under a binocular microscope,
and complete lithologic descriptions are provided in the appendix of this report. Dried
cuttings were organized and shipped to SM Energy Company and the North Dakota
Geological Survey. A Pason digital gas detector was interfaced with a Pason electronic
data recorder (EDR). The Pason system distributed hole depth, bit depth, pump rates and
total gas readings across location. Total gas was recorded in units (100 units = 1%
methane equivalent in air).
Lithology & Hydrocarbons
Sample logging began 26' into the Lodgepole Formation [Mississippian, Madison
Group], which is generally indicated by a transition from light brown limestone to a
medium to dark brown argillaceous limestone. The Lodgepole was penetrated at 7,459'
TVD (-5,273'), and was 15' high to prognosis. The Lodgepole top is further indicated by
an increase in rate of penetration and an increase in gamma. Limestone and argillaceous
limestone compose the Lodgepole Formation. Background gas readings within this
interval were between 33 to 82 units, with significant gas shows as high as 150 units.
Contact with the Upper Bakken Shale [Mississippian] occurred at 7,894' TVD
(-5,708'), and was 11' high to the prognosis. The top of the Upper Bakken Shale was
indicated by sample returns of dark brown to black, carbonaceous, petroliferous and
pyritic shale. Gas shows were as high as 403 units while gamma counts were recorded to
685 API.
The Middle Bakken Member [Mississippian/Devonian] was entered at 7,907' TVD
(-5,721'), and was 10' high to the prognosis. The Middle Bakken top was marked by an
abrupt decrease in both gamma API, and rate of penetration as the wellbore passed from
shale to siltstone and silty sandstone. This silty sandstone was logged as very light gray
to light gray tan in color, calcite cemented and containing trace amounts of disseminated
pyrite (Figure 3). Additionally, trace poor intergranular porosity and trace spotty light
brown oil stain were present.
Figure 3) Calcite cemented silty sandstone from within the
preferred target interval; wet, 20X magnification (Andrew
Kunz, Wellsite Geologist, Sunburst Consulting, Inc.).
An interval of highly calcite cemented silty sandstone lies approximately 16' below the
base of the Upper Bakken Shale. Thickness was estimated to be 6' at curve landing. It is
described as very light to medium gray in color, and contains trace amounts of
disseminated pyrite (Figure 4). Also, trace poor intergranular porosity and trace spotty
light brown oil stain were present. Being locally distinct for it's significantly `cooler'
(40-50 API) gamma signature, the base of the highly calcite cemented silty sandstone was
set as the top marker for the Virgil 2B-16HN drilling target.
Figure 4) Highly calcite cemented silty sandstone from
within the cool gamma interval above the preferred target
interval; wet, 20X magnification) (Andrew Kunz, Wellsite
Geologist, Sunburst Consulting, Inc.).
Hydrocarbon gas shows were recorded throughout the target zone as well as within the
overlying cool gamma facies. Gas measurements as high as 165 units at 11,828' MD and
background gas ranging from 30 to 70 units were recorded while drilling in the cool
gamma facies,. Connection gas shows within the low API interval reached 154 units at
11,902' MD. Hydrocarbon shows and background gas present at the base of target was
similar on average to shows above target, though these were noticeably higher than the
background and gas shows throughout the middle of target. Gas shows reached 338 units
at 16,070' MD at the base of the target interval, and connection gases reached 127 units.
Background gas ranged from 6 to about 80 units.
GEO-STEERING
Figure 5) Type log of the Middle Bakken gamma signatures as recorded in the SM Energy Company,
Wolter 13-9H open-hole logs compared with the Virgil 2B-16HN (Andrew Kunz, Wellsite Geologist,
Sunburst Consulting, Inc.).
The target interval within the Middle Bakken was 8' thick, beginning approximately 23'
below the lower contact of the Upper Bakken Shale. Target interval identification was
based on the TVD gamma ray log of the offset control well, SM Energy Company,
Wolter 13-9H (Figure 5). Gamma ray readings within the 8' target interval ranged from
74-103 API. Landing depths were adjusted as formation markers were traversed.
Calculations from markers within the Lodgepole were confirmed with the Upper Bakken
Member and provided a target landing depth of 7,930' TVD, but due to lower than
expected motor output, the directional drillers landed the curve at 8,150' TVD. MWD-
gamma ray data correlated adequately with the Wolter 13-9H, which was also used as the
most accurate offset for geo-steering. The steering team maintained 84% of the exposed
10,105' wellbore within the target interval (Figures 6 & 7). Based upon intersections
with the cool gamma marker at the upper target boundary, an overall apparent dip was
estimated at 0.71° up.

SUMMARY
The SM Energy Company Virgil 2B-16HN was re-entered on December 7th, 2014 by
Nabors #687. It is a two-section Middle Bakken horizontal well within the West
Ambrose Field of Divide County, North Dakota. 84% of the lateral was drilled within the
ideal 8' primary target interval. A mud program consisting of diesel invert (9.55-9.70
ppg) during the vertical and curve build sections, and saltwater (9.65-9.80 ppg) during the
lateral was employed. The target zone was an 8' thick interval that lies beneath a highly
calcareous silty sandstone unit, approximately 23' below the top of the Middle Bakken.
The silty sandstone displayed poor intergranular porosity and trace spotty very light
brown oil staining. Background gas ranged from approximately 6 to about 80 units. The
Virgil 2B-16HN awaits completion to determine its full production potential.

Lithology
Virgil 2B-16HN
Sunburst Geologists caught lagged samples in 30' intervals through the curve and 100' intervals through
the lateral. A detailed list of sampling intervals is included in the Well Data Summary page. Gamma ray
marker tops have been inserted in the sample descriptions below for reference. Samples were examined wet
and dry under a stereo zoom Binocular microscope. The drilling fluid was oil based invert from surface
casing to an intermediate casing point of 8,278' MD, and saltwater brine was used throughout the lateral

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
