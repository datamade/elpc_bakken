INTRODUCTION
The North Plains Energy, LLC, Stevens 160-100-14-23-11-14A-1H [250' FSL & 2,265' FWL;
SE SW Section 11, T160N, R100W] lies within Smokey Butte Field and is located
approximately 40 miles north of Williston, North Dakota in Divide County (Figure 1). The well
follows several other Three Forks horizontal, dual-section wells designed to develop oil
production in this area. The surface will be drilled with freshwater, while the vertical hole and
curve build section will be drilled using a diesel invert mud system. Directional plans called for
the vertical section to be drilled to a kick-off point of 8,605' measured depth (MD), build a curve
at 12°/100' and land in the lower half of the Three Forks target interval. After casing the vertical
and curve, the lateral will be drilled using a produced salt-water mud system. The lateral was
planned to drill at an azimuth of 180.09°, from the southeast octant of the southwest quadrant of
Section 11 [T160N, R100W] to the southeast octant of the southwest quadrant of Section 23
[T160N, R100W]. Upon anticipated completion of the well, the hole will be prepared for casing
and production evaluation using multi-stage fracture stimulation with packers and sleeves as
needed.
OFFSET WELLS
Three primary offset wells were used to develop a well-rounded prognosis of formation tops to
be used as control for the Stevens 160-100-14-23-11-14A-1H (Figure 2). These wells include:
-The Roy Hagen 1 [SW NE Sec. 12, T160N, R100W] which is located
approximately 1.5 miles northeast of the Stevens 160-100-14-23-11-14A-1H in
Smokey Butte Field. Drilled in 1969-1970 by Miami Oil Producers, Inc., it is a
vertical Red River well that is currently inactive.
-The State 160-100-3-10-36-15E-1H [SE SW Sec. 36, T161N, R100W] which is
located approximately 2.5 miles northwest of the Stevens 160-100-14-23-11-14A-
1H in Smokey Butte Field. Drilled in September of 2013 by North Plains Energy,
LLC, it is a horizontal double-section unit, Three Forks well that is currently
active.
-The Knudsvig 12-1-160-100H 1PB [SW SE Sec. 12, T160N, R100W] which is
approximately 1.2 miles east of the Stevens 160-100-14-23-11-14A-1H in
Smokey Butte Field. Drilled in August of 2011 by Baytex Energy USA, LLC, it
is a horizontal double-section unit, Three Forks well that is currently active.
Gamma signatures from the State 160-100-3-10-36-15E-1H MWD TVD gamma logs were used
to provide log character for the Stevens 160-100-14-23-11-14A-1H target interval section.
Complete formation tops are included as an appendix to this report.

ENGINEERING
The Stevens 160-100-14-23-11-14A-1H well was spud at 00:00 CDT, on September 7, 2014 by
Ensign rig #118. A 13 ½" hole was drilled using freshwater, with one tri-cone bit in 9.5 drilling
hours to 1,735' MD, at which point a casing string consisting of 9 " (36#) J-55 casing joints
were set and cemented to surface. After casing, a diesel invert mud system was used with mud
weights ranging from 8.60-10.00 pounds/gallon and associated viscosities ranging from 27-77
seconds/quart. The hole was then drilled to a kick-off point (KOP) of 8,590' MD, in just under
45 drilling hours with two 8 ¾" PDC bits over two bottom-hole assembly (BHA) runs. The
kick-off point of 8,590' MD was reached at 15:00 CDT on September 12, 2014, after which the
hole was circulated in preparation to run electric logs with Schlumberger. Schlumberger arrived
on location and rigged up logging tools on the evening of September 12, 2014. The logging
consisted of one successful run of a combined Triple Combo: Compensated Neutron, Lithology
Density, and Gamma Ray XY-Caliper run from 8,601' MD to 1,720' MD (logger's TD and
casing depths); along with gamma-ray run to surface. The Triple Combo tool was laid down the
next morning of September 13, 2014.
The curve started at 13:00 CDT on September 13, 2014 with an 8 ¾" PDC bit in front of a 2.38°
fixed motor. The 856' curve was drilled, using one bit over one assembly, to 9,446' MD in 15
drilling hours, landing at 12:20 CDT on September 14, 2014. A 7" casing string, consisting of
7" (32#) HCL-80 and 7" (26#) HCP-110 casing joints, was then set and cemented to 9,399' MD.
The lateral portion of the well began at 13:00 CDT on September 16, 2014, using a produced
brine mud system with mud weight ranging from 9.30 ­ 9.70 pounds/gallon and viscosities
ranging from 28-29 seconds/quart. The lateral BHA consisted of a 6" PDC bit, 1.5° fixed mud
motor, MWD survey tool, and MWD gamma tool. A trip was required at 18,983' MD to replace
the mud motor, bit and MWD tool. The entire lateral was drilled using two bits over two BHA
assemblies, drilling the entire lateral in 95.4 drilling hours. The Stevens 160-100-14-23-11-14A-
1H reached a total depth (TD) of 19,430' MD on September 22, 2014 at 00:30 CDT, after 17
days from spud. A total of six drilling bits were used to drill the well from surface to total depth
in 164.6 total drilling hours. The bottom-hole coordinates are estimated to be 10,571.43' south
and 2.35' west of the surface location or approximately 254.05' FSL and 2,267.35' FWL, SE SW
Section 23, T160N, R100W. Projected to total depth, the well-bore is at an inclination of 92.23°,
a closure azimuth of 179.99° and a true vertical depth (TVD) of 9,212.70', resulting in a closure
distance of 10,571.43'.
GEOLOGICAL EVALUATION
Methods
A two-person geology team logged drill cuttings, monitored mud gas and provided guidance for
steering. Sample and gas evaluation began in the Kibbey Formation at 6,900' MD. Rig crew
members caught 30' lagged samples under direction of the well-site geologists. Wet and dry
cuttings were examined under a binocular microscope. Cuttings were also examined for
hydrocarbon `cut' fluorescence by immersion in Entron solvent under ultraviolet light. One set
each of dry cuttings were collected and sent to North Plains Energy, LLC and the North Dakota
Geological Survey. A digital gas detector was interfaced with a Pason electronic data recorder
(EDR). The Pason provided hole depth, bit depth and pump stroke information to the gas
detection program (MController) and distributed the detector's total gas information for viewing
across location. Total mud gas was recorded in units (100 units = 1% methane equivalent in air)
and chromatography of C1 ­ C4 components were calculated in parts-per-million.
Lithology of Prospective Formations & Hydrocarbon Shows
Note: All depths while drilling the curve are referenced to driller's depth; refer to composite geology log
for electric log tops.
Sample logging began in the Kibbey Formation, right above the Charles Formation. Samples
from the Charles consisted of limestone (primarily mudstone) and anhydrite, along with varying
amounts of argillaceous and dolomitic limestone, as well as beds of argillaceous dolomite.
Background gas remained slightly elevated, between 20 and 40 units, with one gas show of 43
units at 7,772' MD. Trace to rare spotty and dead black oil staining and trace of intercrystalline
porosity were visible within the upper Charles Formation. The Base of Last Salt, an interval of
the Charles, was encountered at 7,587' MD [7,586' TVD (-5,392' subsea)], and was used for
landing depth estimations.
The Mission Canyon Formation was marked by a transition from argillaceous and dolomitic
limestone to a clean limestone at 7,858' MD [7,857' TVD (-5,663' subsea)]. Samples consisted
of limestone, primarily mudstone with occasional wackestone, to slightly dolomitic and
argillaceous limestone. Some of the samples throughout this formation contained traces of fossil
fragments, algae material and were mottled in part. No oil stain was observed within the upper
part, but a trace of dark brown spotty oil staining was observed within the center and lower half
of this formation, but no visible porosity was observed. Background gas ranged from 30 and 40
units, increasing to 60 and 80 units, with connection gases up to 64 and 86 units and gas shows
reaching 61 and 105 units.
The Lodgepole Formation was encountered at 8,550' MD [8,549' TVD (-6,355' subsea)]. The
Lodgepole Formation top was indicated by a slight increase in gamma readings in reaction to the
transition from limestone to argillaceous limestone. The Lodgepole was approximately 428'
thick and consisted of variably argillaceous, lime mudstone. While drilling the Lodgepole
Formation, background gas stayed slightly elevated at 20 and 30 units, increasing towards 40
units. Connection and slide gases reached 40 units. The basal ~11' of the Lodgepole is
comprised of the informal "False Bakken" and underlying Scallion subintervals. The "False
Bakken" is ~5' of medium to dark gray-brown, calcareous shale while the Scallion is ~6' of
gray-brown, fossiliferous lime mudstone and partial wackestone. Background gas was elevated
to 20 units in the False Bakken and in the Scallion, with no survey or connection gases. No
significant oil staining was observed throughout the Lodgepole, but rare traces of intercrystalline
porosity were observed within the upper half.
The Upper Bakken Shale Member, of the Bakken Formation, was encountered at 9,038' MD
[8,977' TVD (-6,783' subsea)]. This member consists of ~12' of black, carbonaceous and
petroliferous shale with anomalously high gamma-ray log response (>300-400 API) and
increased rate of penetration (ROP). Background gas in the Upper Bakken Shale remained
consistent at 10 units with a connection gas reaching 12 units, likely due to mechanical problems
with the gas trap. The Middle Member [Devonian-Mississippian] was encountered at 9,060' MD
[8,989' TVD (-6,795' subsea)]. Decreased penetration rates and siltstone in sample returns
indicated entry into this interval. Three distinct lithologic zones were recognized in cuttings
while drilling through the Middle Bakken: siltstone, limestone and silty sandstone. The siltstone
comprises the upper ~15' of the Middle Bakken. Underlying the siltstone is a ~3' thick
limestone bed classified as oolitic packstone. The limestone generally contains sparry calcite
and micrite, but contains no visible porosity. Beneath the limestone is ~37' of fine-grained and
moderately dolomite and calcite-cemented silty sandstone. Small amounts of pyrite, in
disseminated and nodular form, were also observed as an accessory mineral throughout the
Middle Bakken, with more nodular pyrite towards the lower section of the member. A trace of
spotty light brown oil stain was also present in the upper and lower halves of the silty sandstone
interval. While drilling the curve background gas measured 10 units in the siltstone, 10 units in
the limestone, and 100 units in the silty sandstone, again due to issues with the gas trap. The
Lower Shale Member [Devonian] was encountered at 9,182' MD [9,044' TVD (-6,850' subsea)].
This member consists of ~22' of black, carbonaceous and petroliferous shale similar to the
Upper Bakken Shale with anomalously high gamma ray log response (>600-700 API).
Background gas in the Lower Bakken Shale varied between 100 and 600 units, with a connection
gas of 408 units.
The Three Forks Formation was encountered at 9,268' MD [9,066' TVD (-6,872' subsea)].
This formation consists of interbedded silty dolomite, dolomite-cemented silty sandstone and
shale. A trace of intergranular and intercrystalline porosity was observed in this interval. The
pale green shale indicated no visible porosity. Pyrite (disseminated, nodular and euhedral form)
was also a fairly common accessory mineral in this section of formation. The target zone, as
established by the North Plains Energy, LLC geologist, was a 15' interval which included the
aforementioned interbedded dolomite, silty shale and sparse silty sandstone. The target interval
consisted primarily of sandy dolomite, described as:
DOLOMITE: mudstone, tan to light brown, off white to cream, friable to firm, microcrystalline, sucrosic texture,
sandy in part, trace sparry calcite, trace disseminated and nodular pyrite, trace intercrystalline porosity, trace
light brown spotty oil stain (Figure 3).
Disseminated and nodular pyrite was also an accessory mineral within the dolomite. Traces of
intercrystalline porosity and a trace spotty light brown oil staining were also observed.
Interbedded within this sandy dolomite is silty shale commonly described as:
SHALE: pale green, blue-green, friable to soft, earthy to chalky texture, trace to rare disseminated pyrite,
occasional euhedral pyrite (Figure 3).
Also interbedded throughout the target interval and primarily at the base and below the target
was dolomite-cemented silty sandstone, commonly described as:
SILTY SANDSTONE: gray, off white to light gray, very fine grained, friable to firm, sub-angular to sub-rounded,
moderately sorted, dolomite cemented, silty in part, trace disseminated pyrite, possible intergranular porosity.
Pyrite, in disseminated and nodular form, is also an accessory mineral in this interval and trace
intergranular porosity but no visible oil stain was observed.

Geo-steering
Independent consultants provided well-site supervision, while Weatherford provided directional
drilling and MWD services. Sunburst Consulting, Inc. provided well-site geology consulting.
Drilling fluids were provided for and analyzed daily by Mi Swaco.
The well plan called for a landing of ~20' into the Three Forks Member, in the center of the 15'
target interval, at a projected TVD of 9,081' and an inclination of approximately 89.3°. While
drilling the vertical, the landing target was adjusted to 9,087' TVD after correlating real time
gamma-ray from the tops of the Lodgepole markers and the False Bakken, in comparison to the
Knudsvig 12-1-160-100H 1PB offset well. The actual landing was at 9,085' TVD, putting the
well-bore 11' below the top of the 15' target, entering the target zone at 9,317' MD. The top of
the 15' target came in at 9,074' TVD and the bottom of the target came in at 9,089' TVD.
The target zone is a 15' thick interval, approximately 9' below the base of the Lower Bakken
Shale Member. The target interval was based on gamma-ray markers from all three offset wells
and the Stevens 160-100-14-23-11-14A-1H MWD curve landing gamma log. Gamma-ray
readings crossing through the top of the 15' target ("D" marker) were typically around 105 (API)
counts, 30-130 (API) counts through the center ("F" marker), and 65-120 (API) counts through
the base ("H" marker) of the 15' target zone. The majority of the lateral was drilled within the
center of the target zone with gamma-ray readings of 30-125 (API) counts. Real time MWD
gamma-ray signatures correlated very well with the gamma data from the Stevens 160-100-14-
23-11-14A-1H curve landing (Figure 4).
Figure 4.) Type log of gamma signatures recorded on the Stevens 160-100-14-23-11-14A-1H curve landing
that were used to provide correlation for the North Plains Energy, LLC, Stevens 160-100-14-23-11-14A-1H.
Transitions from high to low gamma-ray markers, and amplitude of gamma counts provided
comparative points from which dip estimates and steering decisions were made. Gas readings
and sample observations also helped make dip estimates by determining the position of the well-
bore within the stratigraphic interval. Offset wells also contributed to excellent structure control,
since the State 160-100-3-10-36-15E-1H Three Forks well lies directly to the north, the
Knudsvig 12-1-160-100H 1PB Three Forks well lies directly to the east, and the previously
drilled Amundson 160-100-22-15-13B-1H Middle Bakken well lies directly south; all reflect
similar structure trends.
Structure trends were typically downwards ranging from 0.30º - 1.30º throughout the entire
lateral. A gross apparent down-dip of 0.71° was observed upon completion of the lateral. The
well-bore drilled at the base of the target interval at the start of the lateral and then twice more
half-way through, falling below the target interval, for approximately 480', then again at the end
of the lateral. The majority of the lateral was drilled within the center of the 15' target interval,
then towards the lower 10' for the remaining 600'. Steering was successful in maintaining the
well-bore in the desired target interval for approximately 91% of the 10,113' lateral section
(Figure 5).

Hydrocarbons
Average background gas in the lateral portion of the well was approximately 400 to 1,800 units
against heavy salt water drilling fluid, weighing 9.30 to 9.70 pound/gallon. Gas components (C1-
C4) were observed throughout the course of the lateral. Upon exiting the shoe, background gas
averaged between 200 and 400 units, with connection and slide gases reaching up to 800 units.
As the well progressed, background gas increased upwards between 800 and 1,800 units with
connection gases reaching upwards of around 2,400 and 2,800 units (Figure 4). A trip was
required at 18,983' MD, resulting in a trip gas of 5,244 units. The trip also resulted in a trip flare
of 10-20', which from point on, flow was vented through the gas buster/flare system to TD.
Trace of dark green oil was observed at shakers during rig service down-time at 11,977' MD,
and again during the connection at 12,624' MD. Common dark green foamy oil was observed at
the shakers during the trip at 18,983' MD. Samples contained between 70-90% yellow to pale
yellow hydrocarbon fluorescence in the center and upper section of the target interval, decreasing
to 20-40% below the center line and less than 10-20% below the target interval. No back-
pressure was held while drilling the Stevens 160-100-14-23-11-14A-1H.
Total gas readings were fairly similar than the previously drilled State 160-100-3-10-36-15H and
Knudsvig 12-1-160-100H 1PB Three Forks wells, that lie directly north and east, which had an
average background gas of 160 to 600 units with mud weights ranging 9.30-9.50 pounds/gallon.
No back pressure was held during either well but the gas buster/flare system was utilized for a
majority of each lateral.

SUMMARY DISCUSSION
The North Plains Energy, LLC, Stevens 160-100-14-23-11-14A-1H is a double-section,
horizontal Three Forks well within Smokey Butte Field in Divide County, North Dakota. The
well spud on September 7, 2014 and was drilled to a depth of 8,590' MD for kick-off point. The
curve entered the top of the target interval at 9,317' MD, and was landed at 9,085' TVD, putting
the well-bore 11' below the top of the 15' target. Intermediate 7" casing was run into the Three
Forks target interval reaching over the legal hard-line boundaries. The well was later maintained
within the target for the remaining 91% of the lateral. Samples from the target zone consisted of
interbedded silty dolomite, dolomite-cemented silty sandstone and shale, with fair intergranular
and intercrystalline porosity and trace spotty light brown oil staining. After penetrating the
Three Forks Formation, gas values ranged from 400 to 1,800 units with trace of dark green free
oil in over the shakers during rig services and connections. The single lateral well reached a total
depth of 19,430' MD on September 22, 2014, after 15 drilling days from spud. The North Plains
Energy, LLC, Stevens 160-100-14-23-11-14A-1H awaits completion to determine its full
production potential.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
