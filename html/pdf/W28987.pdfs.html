GEOLOGICAL SUMMARY & CONCLUSIONS
Continental Resources, Inc. drilled the Debrecen 4-3H1 as a single lateral well targeting the
Devonian Three Forks formation in Stark County, North Dakota. The surface hole location is in
the Northeast one-quarter of the Northeast one-quarter of Section 3, Township 140 North, Range
99 West (350' FNL & 810' FEL). The well then traverses south to a final bottom hole location
approximately 230' from the South section line and 679' from the East section line of section 10.
Continental Resources began drilling operations using H&P rig 257 on September 13, 2014.
Nine and five eights inch diameter surface casing was cemented in place at 2332' prior to the
arrival of H&P 257. NorAm Wellsite Services began geological supervision at 8700' MD on
August 16, 2014. Lagged total gas, calibrated to a one percent Methane in air standard, and
chromatography data were monitored and recorded using a NorAm Wellsite Services Advanced
Gas Detector (AGD) #7, a combination total gas detector and high speed micro-gas
chromatograph (GC). Geosteering and borehole guidance were accomplished with the aid of
lagged total gas data, lagged geological samples, a RigSense EDR Workstation, and gamma ray
logging coupled with directional drilling survey data. MS Energy provided the Directional
Drilling and MWD Services.
The build section began on the afternoon of September 18, at 10280' MD. Gamma data from the
Debrecen 2-3H1 (~0.25 miles west), and the Epsom 1-31H (<0.25 miles northwest) were used to
calculate average thicknesses of markers within the Lodgepole formation to the Three Forks
formation top to land the curve. The bit was landed 15' into the Three Forks formation. The 7"
diameter intermediate casing was installed and cemented in place at 11013' MD, 10741' TVD
(805' FNL & 726' FEL) on September 20. The well path was then curved south along a general
180.0-degree South azimuth.
The geosteering cross section of the Debrecen 2-3H1 as well as the Upper Bakken structure map
were used to help guide the lateral portion of the well. The target zone was defined as the upper
25' of the Three Forks formation. The gamma profiles in the area change within each mile. The
one consistent marker is a low gamma peak ~26' into the Three Forks formation. Below this
marker is a darker and bluer Shale. There is a gamma low below the Lower Bakken Shale that
thins and thickens approximately 10'-16' in the area. The Kordonowy ST#31-10 showed a
thinning to 14' from 10' about 1 mile south and was reflected on the geosteering cross section.
Three Forks samples in this area are a light gray to gray brown microcrystalline, partly silty,
Dolomite with layers of blue gray to blue green shale. Pyrite is scattered throughout the target
section. Under the fluoroscope, samples show a dull yellow fluorescence and a moderately fast
blue white solvent cut. A constant trickle of drilling lube was pumped into the well throughout
the lateral. From around 17500' to 21584', lube sweeps were pumped on top of the constant
lube trickle. Total gas averaged 778 units with shows recorded over 4500 units. Interpretation
of the wetness and balance ratios, clarified by the character ratio, indicates a good, denser oil
show from approximately 11000' to 13500' and fairly good to 17800'. Interpretation of the
remainder of the well, however, shows spotty, residual oil as the recorded wetness ratio was
around 70.
A total depth of 21584' was reached at 19:56 MST on September 26, 2014. Personnel from
NorAm Wellsite Services were retained until after liner was installed.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
