Geologic Summary
The PARSHALL 151-1608H was drilled as a vertical and horizontal wellbore, targeting the Middle
Member of the Bakken Formation. The surface location of the well is in Section 16, T152N, R90W, of
Mountrail County, North Dakota. The vertical whole location was four-hundred, seventy feet (470') from
the south section line, and sixteen hundred feet (1600') from the west section line.
Fresh water was used to drill from the surface to 1995' measured depth in the vertical hole. Invert mud
was used to drill from 1995' measured depth until we set casing at 9580' MD. Fresh water was used to
drill the horizontal hole from 9580' MD until TD at 18417' MD.
Well site geological supervision began September 10, 2014. Logging began above the Charles formation
at a measured depth of 7680'. Joshua Wold & Brad Hill logged vertical and horizontal hole. Thirty foot-
lagged samples were caught during the curve portion of the well, increasing to one-hundred foot lagged
samples during the horizontal portion of the well. The Parshall 59-1608H, Parshall 75-2127H and the
Hovda 1-08H were used as correlates in steering the curve.
The 3rd Shale Marker and False Bakken member of the Lodgepole formation appear to be relatively
reliable markers for isopach thickness to target. The Upper Bakken shale came in at 9286.21' true
vertical depth. The Middle Bakken dolomite came in at 9309.92' true vertical depth.
The Parshall 151-1608H lateral portion of drilling began as most laterals do. We drilled out of casing and
commenced to shoot for our designated "landing target". Our landing was set at 9800' MD and a
"window" of 9324-9334' TVD. We had an extremely smooth landing, with no sliding necessary until
after we reached our landing target measured depth of 9800.
Our first Gamma Ray Marker was seen at 9810' MD, 9322.91' TVD. This was not out of place as it
came in about 11-13' from the Middle Bakken Dolomite top (variance given due to the steeper down dip
at the beginning of the hole which made the distance from the Dolomite top to the Gamma Ray Marker
appear thicker than it actually was).
From here, we began to see a rapid drop in TVD, indicating the formation was dipping down at an
extremely quicker pace than anticipated based on structural maps, and the correlate Parshall 59-1608H
well previously drilled right next to our well, and on the same pad.
As we continued drilling, we encountered the Gamma Ray Marker several more times, all indicating a
massive drop in dip (at points reaching as low as 88.2). While positionally we were fine relative to the
gamma ray marker (i.e. in no danger of striking either the upper or the lower Shales), it posed a quite a
challenge to explain why we were seeing such a huge difference compared to any other wells that had
been previously drilled in our area.
Drilling continued, along with several more dip changes and target window changes due to the continual
drop in formation dip. At 13483' MD, the motor lost a large amount of differential pressure and the
decision was made to TOOH for both the bit and the motor. Upon getting "on bank", we found that we
had left approximately 41-42' feet of BHA & the bit down hole. It was decided to back up to 13174' MD
and open hole sidetrack.
We tripped back into the hole with the new BHA, and had planned on taking several "check shot" surveys
for comparison to see if there was any major deviation between those surveys and the surveys taken with
the previous MWD tool. Unfortunately, a lack of communication resulted in us tripping all the way to

We tripped 10 stands out of the hole to take comparison surveys, and found that in just 10 stands
(approximately 1000' MD), we had a deviation of 8' of TVD (placing us much higher than originally
thought). This began to explain why we were so low relative to other wells in the area; the first MWD
tool used in the lateral had not been tracking the correct inclinations. At this point, the decision to trip all
the way back to the shoe was made, with all surveys to be re-shot throughout the entire lateral.
Upon completion of all the surveys in the lateral, it was found that we were approximately 35' HIGHER
than originally indicated by the first MWD tool run in the lateral. The new TVD and position fell into
line with the structural maps and projected dips, and it was decided to continue drilling.
We backed up to 13144' MD, and commenced time drilling. The remaining lateral was drilled with no
further complication.
The PARSHALL 151-1608H reached a total measured depth of 18417' (feet) on September 23, 2014, 14
days after spud.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
