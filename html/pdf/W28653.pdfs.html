Synopsis
Oasis Petroleum Kline Federal 5300 41-18 11T2 [SW SW Sec. 18 T153N R100W] is located
~15 miles Southwest of the town of Williston, North Dakota. Kline Federal 5300 41-18 11T2 is
situated within the Baker Field. A single lateral leg trending 90° was proposed to be drilled from
the SW SW corner of Section 18 to the SE SE corner of Section 17 targeting the porous
dolostone in the second bench of the Three Forks. The well was originally spud on September
11, 2014.
Control Wells
Two completed wells were used as the control offsets to the Kline Federal 5300 41-18 11T2.
The primary offset considered was the Kline Federal 5300 41-18 13T2X [SW SW Sec. 18
T153N, R100W] which is a horizontal well drilled by Oasis Petroleum. Kline Federal 5300 41-
18 13T2X is located approximately 66 feet south of Kline Federal 5300 41-18 11T2.
The other offset considered was the Kline Federal 5300 41-18 12TX [SW SW Sec. 18 T153N,
R100W] which is a horizontal well drilled by Oasis Petroleum. Kline Federal 5300 41-18 12TX
is located approximately 33 feet south of Kline Federal 5300 41-18 11T2.
During the curve, the gamma ray that was produced was repeatedly compared to the Kline
Federal 5300 41-18 13T2X and the Kline Federal 5300 41-18 12TX wells. Gamma ray was used
to help determine a proper landing depth.
To assist in the choosing of the landing target, an isopach table (Table 1) was constructed to
measure the distance of formation tops to the target depth determined from the Kline Federal
5300 41-18 13T2X and Kline Federal 5300 41-18 12TX offset wells. Comparison of gamma ray
markers showed the most consistent isopach for up-hole formations to be the Lower Bakken.

Geologic Assessment
Methods
Geologic supervision of Kline Federal 5300 41-18 11T2 was provided by experienced RPM well
site geologists. Gas and chromatograph levels were measured using NOV WellSight Gas
Detection real time gas detector and chromatograph system. The WellSight gas detection system
uses non-dispersive infrared and chemical sensor gas detection to evaluate gases liberated from
the formation by the drill bit and carried to the surface by the drilling fluid.
The gas detector was interfaced with a RigWatch electronic data recorder system. RigWatch
provided drill rate, on-off bottom and pump strokes to the gas detector and received total gas
information from the gas detector for viewing on location and remotely.
Under the direction of RPM well site geologists, rig crews were instructed to catch lagged drill
cutting samples at 30' intervals in the vertical hole and curve build from 8,350' to 11,232' MD
and 50' samples from 11,232' to TD (20,660').
Sampled drill cuttings were examined wet and dry under a binocular microscope using both plain
(broad spectrum) and transmitted light. Cuttings were evaluated for hydrocarbon "cut" by
immersion in Acetone and inspection under a UV fluoroscope. 10% hydrochloric acid and
alizarin red were used to determine the calcareous and dolomitic content of rocks and cementing.
RPM Geologic, Inc. (RPM) well site geologists also closely examined MWD gamma-ray
information and penetration rates to aid in steering decisions and dip degree estimations.
Vertical Operations
Overview
The Kline Federal 5300 41-18 11T2 was spud for surface drilling on 11 September 2014
utilizing Nabors 486 as the drilling contractor. Prior to commencement of RPM geological
services, a 13 1/2" hole was drilled with fresh water to depth of 2,128' and isolated with 13 3/8"
54# J-55 casing cemented from 2,115' to surface. After drilling to 6,150', a 9 5/8" 40# HCL 80
intermediate casing was installed at 6,131' to prevent drilling fluid loss into the porous and
permeable Dakota formation.
RPM well site geologists began logging the vertical interval at 8,350'.
One PDC bit was used to drill out of surface casing to the first intermediate casing. PDC bit #2
(12 ¼" Halliburton Varel R616PDG2UX) was used to drill from 2,128' to 6,150' and averaged
an ROP of 121.14 ft/hr in 33.2 hours of use.
After completion of the first intermediate casing, PDC bit #3 was installed (8 ¾" SEC MM65D
15506747) was used to drill from 6,150' to 10,316' and averaged an ROP of 74.66 ft/hr in 55.8
hours of use.
Diesel invert drilling fluid with a mud weight ranging from 10.05-11.50 ppg was used for the
remainder of the vertical hole and in the curve builds sections. The vertical interval was drilled
to a kick off point (KOP) of 10,316', at which point vertical operations were ceased.

Curve Build
Overview
Two PDC bits were used to build the curve section. Bit #4 (8 ¾" SEC MMD55C 12560734) was
a PDC bit used to drill the curve from a measured depth of 10,316' to 10,427' in 12.4 hours,
averaging 8.95 ft/hr. Bit #4 was pulled due to poor build rates and low ROP. Bit #5 (8 ¾" SEC
Z613 12674852) was a PDC bit used to drill the remainder of the curve from a measured depth
of 10,427' to 11,232' in 36.0 hours, averaging 22.36 ft/hr. 7" HCP-110 32 lbs casing was set to
11,212' MD upon completion of the curve.
A Chart was constructed for the curve (Chart 1) that compared the isopach of certain easily
recognizable gamma markers from the Kibbey Lime to the Three Forks Second Bench on the
control wells Kline Federal 5300 41-18 13T2X and Kline Federal 5300 41-18 12TX to the Kline
Federal 5300 41-18 11T2 as gamma marker picks were made. This chart shows the Lower
Bakken top being the closest in thickness to target based on the offset well, and was the main
point used to make changes to the target landing depth during the curve. A table (Table 2) was
constructed comparing Kline Federal 5300 41-18 11T2 to the offset formation tops as well as an
average of the estimated landing point based on the offset wells. This chart was used to
corroborate the landing point, but isopach thickness was the main consideration in choosing a
landing point. The curve was completed at a measured depth of 11,232'and TVD of 10,866', less
than 1 foot into the claystone below the second bench of the Three Forks.

Lateral
Overview
Drilling fluid consisting of open system salt water brine (9.95-10.05 ppg) was used while drilling
the lateral section up to the TD of 20,660'. One 6" PDC bit was used to drill the lateral.
Bit #6 (SEC Z613 JK1579) was a PDC bit used to drill the lateral from 11,232' to 20,660' and
averaged an ROP of 47.14 over 200 hours of use.
Drilling operations were concluded at 13:45 CST on January 13, 2015 at a bottom hole location
of 226.90' N & 9,989.21' E of surface location or approximately 693.90' FSL & 262.94' FEL, SE
SE Sec. 17, T153N, R100W.
Lithology

gamma signature from the curve landing of the Kline Federal 5300 41-18 11T2 (Diagram 1). The
warmer gamma (80 api ­ 100 api) near the center of the zone, as well as the warm gamma
signature characteristic of the first and second Claystones, appeared to be reliable and served as
the main markers for steering.

Gas and hydrocarbon Shows
It was found that the gas and hydrocarbon shows were a poor indicator of placement in Zone
throughout the lateral. Gas remained between 100 and 500 units while in the Three Forks (2nd
Bench).
The NOV MD Totco Wellsite Gas WatchTM gas chromatograph showed components of methane
and propane present through the entire lateral. Sample cuttings throughout the lateral showed oil
stain and a fast white diffuse cut when submerged in Acetone solution and observed with a UV
fluoroscope. Trace oil was observed in the sample wash water.
Summary
1) The Kline Federal 5300 41-18 11T2 was initially spud for surface drilling on 11th of
September, 2014 in the Baker field of McKenzie County, North Dakota. The well was
spud by Nabors 486 and the vertical hole was drilled to a total depth (KOP) of 10,316'.
2) A mud program consisted of diesel invert (10.05-11.50 ppg) after surface casing through
the curve build sections. The drilling mud was successful in maintaining stable hole
conditions and minimizing washout through the salt intervals. Brine water with a weight
of 9.95-10.05 ppg was used during lateral drilling operations to maintain hydrostatic
balance until a TD of 20,660'.
3) One 12 ¼" Varel PDC bit was used to drill out of surface casing to the first intermediate
casing that isolated the Dakota formation at 6,150'. One 8 ¾" PDC bit was used to drill
from 6,150' to KOP of 10,316'. Two 8 ¾" PDC bits were used to drill the curve to
11,232' MD. One 6" PDC bits was used to drill the lateral to 20,660' TD.
4) The lateral was targeting the Three Forks (2nd Bench) ranging from about 3' below the
base of the first Claystone down to approximately 3' above the second Claystone.
5) Gas levels between 100 and 500 units were maintained through the lateral drilling
operations while in the Three Forks.
6) The Three Forks target consisted of one observed gross lithology. The Three Forks was
observed to be a dolostone with trace disseminated and nodular pyrite and apparent
intergranular porosity. Calcareous claystone was often interbedded.
7) Trace to moderate oil stain was observed in zone associated with a fast, white, diffuse
cut. Trace oil washed out of the samples into the wash water when they were cleaned.
8) Drilling operations were concluded at 13:45 CST on January 13th, 2015 at a bottom hole
location of 226.90' N & 9,989.21' E of surface location or approximately 693.90' FSL &
262.94' FEL, SE SE Sec. 17, T153N, R100W.
9) Exposure to target zone was ~80% of the lateral. Oasis Petroleum's Kline Federal 5300
41-18 11T2 awaits completion operations to determine its ultimate production potential.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
