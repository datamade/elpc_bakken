INTRODUCTION
XTO Energy, Inc. Ranger 21X-18E [NE NW Section 18, T151N, R99W] is located approximately 6
miles north-northeast of Arnegard, in Tobacco Garden Field of McKenzie County, North Dakota (Figure
1). The well is designed as a Three Forks horizontal well to develop Sections 18 and 19. Directional
drilling and geo-steering will be employed to land in the Three Forks reservoir and maintain maximum
exposure to a 20' target zone.
GEOLOGY
Methods
Geologic analysis for the Ranger 21X-18E was provided by a Sunburst Consulting, Inc. geology team.
Sunburst well site geologists caught lagged samples at 50' intervals from 2,235' to 5,000', then 100'
intervals to 9,500' and at 30' intervals for the remainder of the well. Rock samples, rate of penetration
and gamma ray data were used in geologic analysis to aid in geo-steering and dip calculations.
Prospective Lithology
The Niobrara Formation came in at 4,351' MD / 4,351 TVD, 4' low to Tobacco Garden 41-18SH. The
Niobrara consists of light to dark gray shale which has an earthy texture and contains trace amounts of
disseminated pyrite and bentonite. Background gas through this formation rose to a 400 units with a mud
weight of 9.60 ppg.
The Carlile Formation was logged at 4,507' MD / 4,507' TVD, 12' low to Tobacco Garden 41-18SH.
This formation consists of medium to dark gray shale which has an earthy to waxy texture, bentonite was
also seen with in the Carlile. A gas spike of 478 units was seen in the middle part of this formation
against a 9.60 ppg mud weight.
The Greenhorn Formation was observed at 4,830' MD / 4,830' TVD, 30' low to prognosis and 5' low
to the Tobacco Garden 41-18SH. The same light to dark gray shale was witnessed here also however the
Greenhorn tended to contain more of the slightly darker shale. Bentonite was also seen in this formation.
A gas spike of 540 units was seen at the top of the Greenhorn while drilling with the same 9.60 ppg mud
weight.
A section of silty sandstone in the center of the Piper Formation increased background gas to 289 units
against a 9.90 ppg mud weight. This formation came in at 6,690' MD / 6,689' TVD, 1' low to Tobacco
Garden 41-18SH. The Piper contains shale, limestone, siltstone, silty sandstone and also had a layer of
anhydrite.
The Tyler Formation was logged at 8,157' MD / 8,156' TVD, 4' high to prognosis and 8' high to
Tobacco Garden 41-18SH. The lithology here was mostly light to medium gray shale which was sub-
bulky with earthy texture and showed trace disseminated pyrite. The Tyler also had orange to brown
siltstone. Two gas shows were witnessed in this formation, a 288 unit show and a 247 unit show shortly
after, against a mud weight of 9.55 ppg.
The Otter top came in at 8,374' MD / 8,373' TVD, 1' high to Tobacco Garden 41-18SH, the Otter
consists of dark gray shale and off-white limestone mudstone. A gas show of 393 units was witnessed
while drilling this formation with a mud weight of 9.60 ppg.
The Mission Canyon Formation was drilled at 9,777' MD / 9,776' TVD, 7' low to prognosis and 17'
high Tobacco Garden 41-18SH. The Mission Canyon consists primarily of clean, argillaceous and
dolomitic limestone. The upper Mission Canyon is primarily friable to firm, light to medium gray
argillaceous limestone. While the lower lithology consisted primarily of off white to medium gray
microcrystalline limestone mudstone. Hydrocarbon shows ranged from 91 to 196 units in 9.70 to 9.85 ppg
drilling mud.
The argillaceous limestone signifying the top of the Lodgepole Formation was penetrated at 10,301'
MD / 10,300' TVD which is 3' low to prognosis and 22' high to Tobacco Garden 41-18SH. This slope
marine deposit consisted of mostly gray to brown argillaceous mudstone that was firm to hard,
microcrystalline and contained rare disseminated pyrite. Also present were relatively thin beds of clean
limestone that were light gray to brown, microcrystalline and had an earthy texture. Fracture gases ranged
from 1,044 units to 4,290 units against 10.1 ppg drilling mud.
The False Bakken shale was drilled at 11,175' MD / 11,094' TVD which was 11' low to prognosis and
3' high to Tobacco Garden 41-18SH. This relatively thin lithology consists of medium to dark gray brown
shale. A single gas show of 2,430 units was witnessed against a 10.15 ppg mud weight.
The Bakken Formation has four members, two of which are organic-rich pyritic shale, separated by a
silty sandstone middle member. The carbonaceous Upper Bakken Shale was penetrated at 11,192' MD /
11,103' TVD, 4' low to prognosis and 5' high to Tobacco Garden 41-18SH. Gas shows through the shale
read upwards of 2,173 units (C1 through C4) before penetrating the Middle Bakken at 11,229' MD /
11,122' TVD, 5' low to prognosis and 1' high to Tobacco Garden 41-18SH. The Middle Member
consisted of light gray to light brown silty sandstone. Background gas here dropped as low as 805 units
against 10.15 ppg mud. Oil staining was common along with disseminated pyrite. The Lower Bakken
Shale came in at 11,328' MD / 11,164' TVD, 4' low to prognosis and 1' low to Tobacco Garden 41-
18SH and was similar to the Upper Bakken Shale, with a slightly browner color. Gas shows here got as
high as 1,673u (C1 through C4). The final member, The Pronghorn, came in at 11,377' MD / 11,181'
TVD, 4' low to prognosis and even to Tobacco Garden 41-18SH. This member consists of a medium gray
to brown siltstone.
The targeted Three Forks Formation was observed at 11,402' MD / 11,188' TVD, 6' low to prognosis
and even to Tobacco Garden 41-18SH. The observed lithology consisted of a cream to brown dolomite,
which was crystalline with an earthy texture. This facies also exhibited laminations and contained
disseminated pyrite. The targeted Three Forks lithology also contained a very light, mint green to pale
blue shale that was occasionally off white. This lithology was soft to friable and contained disseminated
and nodular pyrite. Spotty light brown oil staining and intercrystalline porosity were present throughout.
The 20' target zone was 10' below the top of the Three Forks Formation (Figure 2), and consisted of the
same argillaceous dolomite and shale. Gas shows ranged from 597 units to 3,007 units.
Figure 2: Wet cuttings of the upper target Three Forks dolomite and shale (left) & lower target dolomite and shale right).
Lateral Gas and Oil Shows
The background gas for the Ranger 21X-18E ranged from 125 to 2,664 units during the course of the
lateral. The highest gas show was 3,007 units at 17,233' MD against 10.20 ppg mud. Higher gas shows
were generally witnessed in the middle part of the target zone. The gas buster was venting throughout the
lateral and a 10' trip flare was witnessed after re-entering the hole at 17,182'.
Geo-steering
A 20' target zone was chosen for the Ranger 21X-18E by XTO Energy geologists that began 10' below
the Pronghorn and ended 6' from the top of the Second Bench of the Three Forks. The thickness was
estimated using control well logs. A well drilled previously in Section 18, the Clouse 7-18, provided
guidance for expected gamma ray markers while drilling the lateral (Figure 3). The top of the target zone
would have gamma readings relatively high at about 96 API. Below this gamma would drop to 75 API
before rising back up to its highest peak of 120 API. Below this high streak was the middle target zone
low gamma where gamma would read around 50 API, gamma would then increase to 95 API. The
wellbore was never drilled lower than right below the middle target zone cool streak.

Bottom-Hole Location
Total depth resulted in a closure azimuth of 181.70° and a closure distance of 9,814.32'. The bottom-hole
location is projected to be 9,809.99' south and 291.37' west of the surface location or approximately
253.41' FSL & 1,108.63' FWL, Lot 4 Section 19, T151N, R99W. The hole was prepared for liner and
multi-stage fracture stimulation directly after drilling operations concluded.
SUMMARY
The XTO Energy, Inc. Ranger 21X-18E was spud on October 18, 2014 by H&P 456. The well efficiently
reached TD in 18 total drilling days on November 4, 2014. Samples from the Three Forks target
contained oil staining that was described as trace and spotty with visible intergranular porosity. MWD
gamma ray markers, sample analysis, and ROP were successfully utilized to expose 100% of the lateral
borehole to the Three Forks target zone. The Ranger 21X-18E awaits completion operations to maximize
its production potential.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
