GEOLOGICAL SUMMARY & CONCLUSIONS
Continental Resources, Inc. drilled the Mary 1-16XH as a single lateral well targeting the
Mississippian-Devonian Middle Bakken formation in McKenzie County, North Dakota. The
surface hole location is in the Southeast one-quarter of the Southwest one-quarter of Section 16,
Township 146 North, Range 98 West (460' FSL & 2100' FEL). The well then traverses north to
a final bottom hole location 805' from the north section line and 1973' from the west section line
of section 4.
Continental Resources began drilling operations using H&P rig 257 on October 11, 2014. Nine
and five eights inch diameter surface casing was cemented in place at 2485' on October 14.
NorAm Wellsite Services began geological supervision at 9000' MD on October 19, 2014.
Lagged total gas, calibrated to a one percent Methane in air standard, and chromatography data
were monitored and recorded using a NorAm Wellsite Services Advanced Gas Detector (AGD)
#7, a combination total gas detector and high speed micro-gas chromatograph (GC). Geosteering
and borehole guidance were accomplished with the aid of lagged total gas data, lagged
geological samples, a RigSense EDR Workstation, and gamma ray logging coupled with
directional drilling survey data. MS Energy provided the Directional Drilling and MWD
Services.
The build section began on the night of October 22 , at 10812' MD. Gamma data from the
Heiser Family #1 (~1.5 miles southeast), the Orf #1-17H (~1.33 miles northwest), and the
Brockmeir David (~2.9 miles northeast) were used to calculate average thicknesses of markers
within the Lodgepole formation to the Middle Bakken formation top to land the curve. The bit
was landed approximately 16' into the Middle Bakken formation. The 7" diameter intermediate
casing was installed and cemented in place at 11743' MD, 11258' TVD (846' FSL & 2127'
FWL) on October 25. The well path was then curved north along a general 359.7-degree North
azimuth.
The target zone, over time, became defined as the entire Middle Bakken formation with zone
boundaries at the Upper and Lower Bakken Shales. The gamma profiles in the area change
within each mile. The two consistent markers are a low gamma peak ~11' into the Middle
Bakken formation and a high gamma ~19' into the Middle Bakken.
Middle Bakken samples in this area are a gray brown, silty, argillaceous Dolomite with rare
disseminated pyrite. Samples after ~13600' became increasingly contaminated with drilling
lube. The brightness and abundance of solvent cut also increased after 13600'. Total gas
averaged 1847 units with shows recorded over 4700 units with the highest gas near the Upper
Bakken Shale.
At approximately 25390' MD, a high gamma was encountered usually associated with the Upper
Shale. Action was taken to slide down for 25' and circulate bottoms up samples. Samples were
circulated for over 6 hours with no Shale. It is unclear whether the gamma probe was showing
false readings or the bit was in the Upper Shale as there was no Shale in the samples. The
bottom-hole assembly became stuck and later freed. It was decided to call total depth at 25518'.
Total depth was reached at 15:00 MST on November 6, 2014. Personnel from NorAm Wellsite
Services were retained until after liner was installed and cemented.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
