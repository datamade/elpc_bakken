INTRODUCTION
The Oxy USA, Inc. Shuck 6-33-28H-144-97 [SW SW Sec 33, T144N, R97W] is located approximately 10
miles west of Manning, North Dakota. The subject well was drilled in Dunn County, ND by the Nabors X-
18 rig in the Little Knife Field of the Bakken pool. The subject well was planned with one approximately
8,935' [Intermediate casing to projected total depth (TD)] long northerly lateral bore with intent to intersect
regional hydraulic fracture trends that might enhance reservoir quality within the Middle Bakken. OXY
USA intends to develop oil production in the late Devonian Middle Bakken target porosity zone with a
stratigraphy consisting of siltstone, silty sandstone and limestone with depositional lithofacies ranging
from onshore terrestrial to offshore marine deposits.
ENGINEERING
Surface, Vertical & Curve Operations:
A 9 " surface hole was drilled on June 23, 2014 by the Nabors X-18 rig and surface casing was
set at 2,225'. The vertical hole operations commenced on June 27, 2014 with Weatherford providing
equipment and personnel for MWD and directional drilling services.
The first vertical (2nd overall) Bottom Hole Assembly (BHA), consisting of MWD tools, 1.50° bend
motor and Tercel MR616 PDC bit, drilled from 2,235' to 7,912' and was pulled out due to low ROP. The
third BHA was then picked up with a Smith MDSi516 bit and 1.50º Hunting motor and drilled to a KOP of
10,318', making 2,406' in 48 hours. A trip was then made to pick new tools. No E-logs were run due to the
close proximity of the other four wells on the pad.
The curve assembly (4th BHA) consisted of an NOV E1202 bit, 2.38º bend motor, IncSonde,
survey, and gamma tools. This BHA drilled to 10,976' and was pulled out due to MWD failure. Following a
trip out, the fifth BHA was picked up and consisted of the same model bit and motor. The curve TD was
reached at a depth of 11,230', landing the well-bore approximately 13' into a 24' thick (shale to shale)
Middle Bakken zone and 4' into the 10' thick silty sandstone target interval.
The curve was drilled in a total of 75 hours and on-bottom for 23 hours. Directional tools were
then pulled out of the hole and a string of 7" intermediate casing was run, set, and cemented at a depth of
11,216' MD, after which true lateral operations commenced. Diesel invert drilling fluid was used
throughout the vertical hole and curve build sections maintaining a 9.6-10.1 ppg mud weight for stable
down-hole conditions.
Lateral Operations:
Weatherford again provided equipment and personnel for MWD and directional drilling services.
The directional drillers and Payzone geologists worked closely together throughout the project to make
steering decisions in order to maximize the amount of borehole exposure to the pay zone.
A six bladed 6" Security MMD54 bit with 5x20 jets, 1.50º bend Hunting 5683 mud motor (1.0
rev/gal), and MWD tools comprised the first lateral BHA (6th BHA overall). This BHA was run in the hole
on 4" drill pipe from 11,216' MD to 13,573' MD, when a trip out was made due to MWD failure. The
Security bit drilled 2,343' in 24 hours. The MMD54 bit and Hunting motor were re-run with new MWD tools
and drilled to 18,016', when they were pulled out of the hole for low ROP. A new Security MMD54,
Hunting 1.50º bend motor and MWD tool we're sent down-hole and reached at TD of 20,151', drilling
2,135' in 24 hours. The lateral was drilled in 6 days, two trips, and only 11 slides. Spud to TD was 22
days. The lateral resulted in a TD of 20,151' MD (10,848.77' TVD) at a final azimuth of 0.31º, resulting
in a closure vertical section of 9,704.72'. The BHL is 9,704.68' North & 51.70' East of surface location or
approx. 234.43' FNL & 648.1'0 FWL, NW NW Section 28, T144N, R97W (Figure 2). Salt water drilling
fluid was used from the intermediate casing to TD with mud weights throughout the lateral varying from
9.6-10.1 ppg.

GEOLOGIC EVALUATION
Methods:
Geologic supervision of the well was provided by Payzone Consulting, Inc. with two well site geologists.
Brandon Lutz and Bob Troutman drilled the vertical, curve, and lateral sections. A wireless Totco Gas
WatchTM gas chromatograph was interfaced with a Rigwatch and NOV electronic data recorder system
and provided total gas, C1, and C3 information for viewing around location. Lagged samples were caught
in 30' intervals from 8,000' to 11,230' MD and 100' samples for the lateral (11,230'-20,151' TD). Cuttings
were examined under a binocular microscope and complete lithologic descriptions are provided in the
lithology document within this evaluation. Two sets of dry cuttings were collected, with one set sent to the
North Dakota Geological Survey Core Library and the second set sent to Scott Hoag, the project
geologist overseeing operations.

LATERAL GEOLOGY
Gamma Signature:
Within the Middle Bakken silty sandstone/siltstone/limestone target interval, gamma counts varied
between 30 and 85 API (Figures 8 and 9). A 2.5' thick packstone (30-45 API) was encountered upon
landing but was not penetrated again throughout the lateral. Because the base of the packstone occurred
only 5' below the base of the Upper Bakken Shale, the 10' target interval was set lower than in the last
four wells, which designated the top of target as the base of the packstone. The top of target was set to
7.5' below the base of the Upper Bakken Shale, 1.5' below the base of the packstone. The upper half of
the 10' targeted interval consists of predominantly silty sandstone with intervals of sandy siltstone. This
zone displays a characteristic "ratty" or noisy gamma signature, with large swings from 48-78 API units.
Between the center of target and 1.5' below, the gamma signature steadfastly trends downward with
readings between 39-60 API.
Between the low gamma signature at and just below center and the base of target, there are three distinct
intervals of silty sandstone. The upper zone (1.5-2.5' below; Figure 8) has a "high and tight" gamma
signature that trends between 77-88 and 60 API units. Below that, from 2.5' below center to the base of
target, the gamma signature is tight and trends between 69-81 API units. Right at the base of target, the
gamma signature is "high and tight" and ranges between 77-88 API units. The lowest point (just of the 10'
targeted interval) the well-bore reached was at ~15,000' MD, where the gamma signature dropped off
from 70's-60's API.
The well bore was able to track with minimal sliding between these tight and "high and tight" zones
(Figure 10: 12,500' MD- 16,100' MD). ***This suggests that the "high and tight" zones act as hard
streaks, deflecting the well-bore up or down to maintain an average inclination comparable to formation
dip. Through this interval, the well-bore also tracked above the upper (1.5-2.5' below center) hard streak.
Another reliable tracking zone was present in the upper part of zone, suggesting hard streaks were
present there as well. At 11,250', 11,700', and 18,000' MD (Figure 10), the well-bore appears to have
deflected off the center of the target zone and tracked in the ratty/noisy upper half of the target (11,250'-
12,250' MD; 16,500'-20,151' TD).

Geo-steering:
The entire Shuck 6-33-28H-144-97 well was drilled in between the 24' projected interval between the
upper and lower Bakken shales. Consistent gamma detection and continuous Inc Sonde updates
provided by Weatherford MWD were key tools to a smooth and productive well-bore. 99% of the 20,151'
well-bore (8,871 of 8,921' rat hole out of intermediate casing to TD) was drilled within the 10' targeted
interval of the middle Bakken.
TVD for the center of the 10' targeted interval increased 40.36' over 8,886' (first survey out of intermediate
casing to TD projection at bit) resulting in a gross apparent dip of 0.27º down or 89.73º. Similar to the
Shuck 4 and Shuck 5, formation dip flattened toward the northern end of section 28. This consistent
flattening of dip in the area may be a useful piece of data for future prospects in the area.
A total depth of 20,151' was reached on July 14, 2014 at 14:00 MST. Total depth resulted in 100% overall
exposure within the Middle Bakken Member of the Bakken Formation and 99% within the defined 10'
targeted interval. (Figure 10).

Hydrocarbons:
During the drilling of the lateral, gas readings were recorded, imported/exported, and analyzed via a
Totco gas detection system. The line was hooked up to the gas sniffer and hard-lined into the system
below the shakers which resulted in diminishing returns for the hydrocarbons. By having the trap set far
away from the possum bel y (enclosed possum belly was not conducive to any gas trap/agitator
configuration), the hydrocarbons were significantly cut, thus values of the gas released from the reservoir
did not depict an accurate reading. Moreover, the mud flow was circulating through the gas buster at
various intervals throughout the lateral. Despite inaccurate values, the trends of concentrated gases were
extremely accurate when the well bore entered various stratigraphic sections of the Middle Bakken.
Background gas varied significantly from 5-195 units with peaks scattered minimally throughout the target
zone. The highest background gas values were encountered during the second half of the well and just
above the center of target. These elevated backgrounds gases (25-70 units) produced continuous to
intermittent flares that ranged from 2-6' in height. Connection gases reached as high as 210 units with an
average of 40 units at connections. The lowest gas concentrations were recorded in the bottom half of the
targeted interval and consisted of connection gases as high as 32 units and background gases ranging
from 5-35 units. The zone 0-1' above the center of target exhibited the highest gas concentrations
typically ranging from 45-75 units. Elevated gas levels in this zone may depict a more fractured interval or
higher depositional/diagenetic porosity/permeability. A maximum trip gas of 450 units was recorded at
13,573' MD. Hydrocarbon content was cut significantly due to the mud weight. Oil show in samples
consisted of even to spotty golden brown oil stain.

Summary

The Nabors X-18 drilling rig successfully drilled a two-section horizontal wellbore within the
Middle Bakken Member of the Bakken Formation at the OXY USA, Inc. Shuck 6-33-28H-144-97.

A mud program consisting of diesel invert (9.6-10.1+ ppg) was used from spud to curve landing
with salt water drilling fluid used from the intermediate casing to 20,151' MD (TD). Salt water mud
(9.8-10.1 ppg) was efficient for maintaining stable down hole conditions and permitted adequate
analysis of gas concentrations in the lateral. Background gas concentrations ranged from 5-195
units with connections as high as 218 units. These elevated gas levels were continuous
throughout the lateral and the highest concentrations were recorded at the top of the targeted
interval.

Steering was accomplished by sample evaluation and by following correlative drilling rate and
MWD gamma ray markers; making dip estimations based on intersections of these markers with
the well bore. Projections of dip were successfully used to maintain the well bore within the
Middle Bakken. A net of 8,871 (99%) of gross 8,921' of the lateral was drilled in the ideal 10' thick
Middle Bakken target with 100% exposure within the 24' shale to shale Middle Bakken Member of
the Bakken Formation.

Samples from the target consisted of white to off-white, light brown, trace light gray, and light gray
to dark gray siltstone/silty sandstone. Traces of intergranular porosity were seen throughout the
lateral. Samples from the ideal zone contained a spotty to even brown oil stain.

The transitional packstone was present upon landing but not drilled during the lateral section.
Three hard streaks were present: just above the center of target, 2.5' below center, and at the
base of target. These hard streaks permitted the well bore to track excellently. Directional only put
in 11 slides throughout the entire lateral section. Continuous tracking in multiple intervals
throughout the target zone is also attributed to the use of a Security MMD54 bit, which has been
known to maintain inclination better then other bits used on this pad and X-20.

The Shuck 6-33-28H-144-97 was a successful drilling effort as measured by gross exposure to
the target. The well will be fitted with a 4 ½" production liner and swell packers in preparation for a
fracture stimulation that will determine the true commercial value of the well.

Shuck 6-33-28H-144-97 Lithology
Rig crews caught samples in 30' intervals from 8,000' to 11,230' and 100' intervals from
11,230' to 20,151' (TD). Formation tops and lithologic markers have been inserted into
the sample descriptions below for reference. Sample descriptions began in the Amsden
Formation. Samples were examined wet and dry under a binocular microscope and were
checked for hydrocarbon cut fluorescence during the lateral. Hydrocarbon cut was
masked by invert mud through 11,216' (intermediate casing) where the invert mud was
exchanged to a saltwater mud for the duration of the lateral. Quantifiers in order of
increasing abundance are trace, rare, occasional, common and abundant. Subsea is
delineated as the negative numbers within the parentheses located next to the measured
depth or true vertical depth. Sample quality was excellent to good. Overall, the samples
within the Middle Bakken target consisted of silty sandstone with grains that varied from
off-white, white, light to medium brown with traces of tan to light cream, and light grey
in color.
Siltstone and limestone were present above the top of the target window.
Variances occurred throughout the lateral and are noted below.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
