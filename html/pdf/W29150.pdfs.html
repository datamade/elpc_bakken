WELL SUMMARY
The Hart #1-11H was spud on September 7, 2014, at a surface location 160' FSL
and 1980' FEL in Section 11, T158N, R96W, Wil iams County, North Dakota.
After dril ing the surface and setting a 9 5/8" casing string with AES rig #4,
Cyclone rig #1 was moved onto the Hart location to dril the remainder of the wel ;
it dril ed out of the 9 5/8" casing shoe on September 15, 2014. The Hart is part of
a series of wel s dril ed by Continental Resources in the Temple field area
targeting the Mississippian/Devonian middle Bakken and the Devonian Three
Forks in the Wil iston Basin of North Dakota. The Temple field lies in Wil iams
County in northwestern North Dakota and currently consists of at least 13
productive "Bakken pool" wel s and 5 Devonian Winnipegosis wel s. Some minor
production also comes from the Devonian Duperow, Mississippian Madison and
the Ratcliffe zone of the Mississippian Charles.
In addition, the Devonian
Dawson Bay, Ordovician Red River, and Silurian Stonewal have been productive
within the Temple field in the past.
The wel plan shows a build section started in the Mississippian Lodgepole with a
12°/100' build rate to a landing within the middle Bakken, approximately 20' below
the base of the Mississippian/Devonian upper Bakken shale. The lateral wel plan
shows one lateral leg dril ed to the north approximately 9600' and ending near the
north section hardline in the northwest of the northeast quarter of Section 2,
T158N, R96W.
Correlation logs were provided by two vertical wel s in the Temple field area. The
Northwest Exploration Sundhagen #2 (SE SE 10-T158N-R96W) was a dry hole
dril ed into the Ordovician Red River in 1980 and is located about one mile west
of the Hart #1-11H surface location. The Conquest Exploration Biwer 43-12 (NE
SE 5-T161N-R95W) was a Devonian Winnipegosis test dril ed in 1985 that was
briefly productive from the Winnipegosis. It is located about 1¼ miles northeast
of the Hart surface location.
BUILD SECTION:
The middle Bakken target of the Hart build section was original y picked at 9640'
TVD based upon the prognosis and correlation of uphole dril ing times with the
offset wel s. The build section was kicked-off at a depth of 9175' on September
2818, utilizing a 2.0º mud motor and a PDC bit. A trip to replace the mud motor
was made at 9308', with a 2.25° mud motor used to dril the rest of the curve.
The target TVD was moved downward 30' to 9670' while dril ing the curve, based
upon correlation of gamma ray markers within the lower Lodgepole and upper
Bakken. The curve was completed on September 20 at a total depth of 10047'.
The projection to the bit at the end of the curve included an inclination of 90.6°
and a TVD of 9670' (within the middle Bakken target section). During the build
section, the Bakken shale was topped at 9813' (9639' TVD) and the middle
Bakken at 9874' (9652' TVD).
LATERAL LEG:
Dril ing of the Hart #1-11H lateral leg began on September 22, 2014, at a
measured depth of 10047' (9670' TVD), about 18' below the base of the upper
Bakken shale and within the targeted section of the middle Bakken (entered at
9962' MD, 9667' TVD). After dril ing out of the 7" intermediate casing shoe, the
lateral was dril ed with brine water in a northerly direction (359.81°) utilizing a
PDC bit and a 1.5° mud motor.
A total depth of 19665' was reached on
September 25, 2014 with 9618' of lateral hole dril ed, entirely within the middle
Bakken. No trips were required in the lateral as the bit and mud motor finished
the leg in 101½ dril ing hours.

No oil shows were noted at the shaker while dril ing the Hart #1-11H horizontal.
Total gas readings (as monitored by Noram Wel site Services equipment) were
relatively moderate for most of the lateral (typical y 400-800 units). The best gas
shows were recorded in a 2500'-interval between 13800' and 16300' as the
average background climbed to 750-1000 units. A peak gas reading of 2078
units was recorded at 11703' and appeared to be liberated formation gas.
Connection gas peaks were irregularly recorded and were typical y no more than
250 units over background. The weight of the brine water utilized to dril the
lateral was kept near 9.9 lbs/gal for the majority of the leg.
Poor sample shows were displayed by the argil aceous to silty, dolomitic
limestone of the middle Bakken as the cuttings typical y displayed little
fluorescence and yielded occasional pale diffuse cut fluorescence.
Visible
porosity was poor in the cuttings, with the best porosity appearing to be within the
"clean" gamma interval within the upper portion of the target zone in the middle
Bakken. Sample quality was fair for most of the lateral leg; with depth, much lube
material began to be seen in the cuttings samples.
Based upon Bakken structure maps of the area, the Bakken was expected to
climb about 90' over the length of the Hart lateral leg (apparent dip of 0.5°).
While dril ing the Hart #1-11H lateral, the formation appeared to ascend at a rate
greater than the anticipated dip, with an estimated change of 138' observed, an
average apparent dip of 0.8°. Unlike the much of the middle Bakken of the
Wil iston Basin where inconsistent gamma ray markers and lateral gamma ray
changes are not unusual, the middle Bakken of the Temple field area appears to
have a relatively consistent marker bed in the "clean gamma" interval found
approximately 15' below the base of the upper Bakken shale. Contact with the
top or bottom of this zone al ows for the calculation of a reasonably accurate
estimate of formation dip.

RE:
Filter Socks and Other Filter Media
Leakproof Container Required
Oil and Gas Wells
Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
Thank you for your cooperation.
