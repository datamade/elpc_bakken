GENERAL:
Newfield Production Company's double section Sand Creek Federal 153-96-21-28-2H
was "spudded" as a Middle Bakken Formation Double lateral well on September 18th, 2014. The
subject well is being drilled in the Westburg field, located about 10 miles North West of Keene
in McKenzie County, North Dakota. Surface location of the Sand Creek Federal 153-96-21-28-
2H is 328' FNL, 827' FEL, NENE Section 21-T153N-R96W. The Sand Creek Federal 153-96-
21-28-2H was planned with the ultimate goal of oil production in the dolomite porosity of the
Middle Bakken formation. The horizontal well-path was planned to follow structure with an
estimated 90° average formation dip throughout the lateral to total depth at 20,410' MD. The
well bore was planned with one 5 degree turn and finished with drilling to the South following
an azimuth along 180.0°.
ENGINEERING:
The Sand Creek Federal 153-96-21-28-2H was drilled with Patterson UTI 346. Ground
level of the Sand Creek Federal 153-96-21-28-2H was 2,057' above mean sea level with the
Kelly bushing located at 2,082' above mean sea level, 25' above ground level.
The Sand Creek Federal 153-96-21-28-2H was drilled with fresh water from surface to
1,691' MD. A switch was then made and oil base mud, and was used from 1,691' MD to
10,468'MD. Once horizontal, the well was switched over to salt brine from 10,468' MD to total
depth at 20,175' MD.
The main lateral of Newfield Production Company's Sand Creek Federal 153-96-21-28-
2H began on November 1st, 2014. It was drilled from 10,320' MD (10,132' TVD) to a TD at
20,175' MD (10,258.14'TVD, est). At TD a 5" liner was run in the vertical and 4.5" liner was
ran through the lateral and fracture stimulation was planned to enhance the well.
The final lateral profile for the Sand Creek Federal 153-96-21-28-2H showed a
net drop of 103' TVD over a span of 9826' of vertical section drilled bringing the average dip for
the whole lateral to approximately 89.39 deg. The bottom hole location for the Sand Creek
Federal 153-96-21-28-2H is 251' FSL, 536' FEL of SESE Sec 28 T153N R96W.

into Pason's EDR system was gamma data provided by Baker Hughes. Pason's EDR provided
drill rate, total gas, gamma and circulation data for viewing on location. Lagged samples for the
well were caught by the rig crews in 30' intervals from 8,000' through 10,468' and then 100'

The Charles Salt came in at 7,846' MD 7,831' TVD -5,749' sub-sea-level. The Charles
Salt consisted predominately of salts, limestone, and anhydrite. Salts from the Charles formation
were typically clear to translucent to translucent red in color, hard, massive, cubic and euhedral
crystalline structure, vitreous to sub glassy textures. Lithology below the salts in the Charles
formation consisted of limestone and anhydrites. Limestone's above the Ratcliffe interval within
the Charles formation were typically light gray to medium gray to bluish gray in color, firm to
brittle to hard, predominantly coarse to poor crystalline, wackestone, argillaceous, with poor
visible fracture porosity, no stain, no fluorescence, no odor, or cut fluorescence. No oil or gas
shows were observed while drilling the Charles Formation. No problems were encountered while
drilling the Charles Formation. The Base of the last Charles Salt was observed at 8,561' MD,
8,547' TVD and -6,465' sub-sea level.
The Mission Canyon formation top came in at 8,753' MD, 8,749' TVD and -6,667' sub-
sea level. The Mission Canyon formation consisted of gray limestones, moderately hard to hard
in hardness, cryptocrystalline wackestone to packstone in structure. Common shale inter-bedding
was observed. Poor to moderate oil show accompanied it in samples. Occasional lost circulation
intervals are found in the Mission Canyon. No other problems were encountered while drilling
the Mission Canyon formation.
The Lodgepole Formation top was recorded and observed at 9,377' MD, 9,363' TVD and
-7,281' sub-sea level. The Lodgepole Formation consisted of medium gray to gray to dark brown
to dark gray brown limestone, hard and microcrystalline to cryptocrystalline packstone and
wackestone in structure. No problems were encountered while drilling the Lodgepole Formation.
The False Bakken top was recorded and observed at 10,262' MD, 10,110' TVD and -
8,028' sub-sea level. The False Bakken formation consisted of dark brown to dark gray limey
shale's. The shale's of the False Bakken were typically firm to brittle, sub-platy in structure, and
silty to earthy in texture. A 4,137 unit gas show was encountered while drilling the False
Bakken and had an oil show consisting of scattered faint yellow fluorescence with fast streaming
blue white cut fluorescence. No problems were encountered while drilling the False Bakken
formation.
The Upper Bakken Shale formation top was recorded and observed at 10,290' MD,
10,121' TVD and -8,039' sub-sea level. The Upper Bakken Shale's consisted of very dark gray
to black shale's, firm to soft to occasionally brittle in hardness, sub-blocky ­ blocky in structure,
carbonaceous to earthy in texture. Oil show was noted with drill cuttings of the Upper Bakken
Shale exhibiting a flash blue white cut fluorescence. A background gas increase was observed
while drilling the Upper Bakken Shale with a peak gas recorded of 4,592 units. Casing hung up
in the Upper Shale and was not able to progress past 10,320' MD (~10,132' TVD) the decision

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
