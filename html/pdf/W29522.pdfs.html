INTRODUCTION
The Oasis Petroleum North America, Twobins 5501 41-20 6T [SW SW Section 20, T155N,
R101W] is located approximately 5 miles northwest of Williston in Williams County, North
Dakota. The Twobins 5501 41-20 6T is a horizontal first bench Three Forks well within the
Williston Basin consisting of one 9,283' lateral drilled toward the north. Geologically, the
Williston Basin is an intracratonic, structural, and sedimentary basin located at the western
edge of the Canadian Shield. Within the basin, the Three Forks Formation (Late Devonian)
consists of four members: the basal Pronghorn Member (formerly recognized as the Sanish
Siltstone) lies at the base of Bakken Formation (Late Devonian ­ Early Mississippian). The
Three Forks consists of mixed carbonate and clastic rocks.
OFFSET WELLS
Offset well data used for depth correlation during curve operations are found in the `Structure'
section appended to this report. Offset well control was essential in curve operations to
successfully land within the Middle Bakken. Formation thicknesses expressed by gamma ray
signatures in these wells were compared to gamma data collected during drilling operations in
order to successfully land the curve. The target landing true vertical depth (TVD) was updated
twice in the Bakken Formation to ensure accurate landing of the curve.
ENGINEERING
The Twobins 5501 41-20 6T was spud on November 15, 2014 by Nabors 169. Drilling fluid
was diesel invert mud for the vertical and curve, and salt water mud for the lateral. The curve
was landed at 11,212' MD (10,828' TVD) with an inclination of 89.5°. While 7" casing was
being ran the casing got stuck in the Lower Bakken Shale. After a lengthy time of try to free
the casing from the shale the decision was made to cut the casing, lay the casing down, cement
back the curve and drill a new curve. Then the new curve was kicked off the existing wellbore
at a depth of 10,600' on December 1, 2014. The new curve was landed in a more ideal portion
of the target interval, at 11,195' MD (TVD of 10,832' with an inclination of 89.8°). Following
the landing, 7" intermediate casing was set and cemented to 11,175' MD. To start the lateral, a
6 Baker Hughes PDC bit, Ryan Directional Services MWD tool, and a 1.5° adjustable Baker
Hughes mud motor were used. The complete BHA accomplishments are tabulated in an
appendix (BHA Record) to this report. The well reached total depth (TD) at 20,458' MD at
22:30 hrs CST on December 11, 2014, generating an exposure of 9,283' linear feet of 6 hole
through the Three Forks target interval. The bottom hole location (BHL) lies 9,865.46' north,
130.53' west of surface location or approximately 318.14' FNL, 584.47' FWL, NW NW Sec.
17, 155N, 101W.
GEOLOGY
Lithology
Sample logging began at 8,300' MD within the Kibbey. The main zones of interest drilled at
the subject well include the Ratcliffe, Mission Canyon, Lodgepole and Bakken Formations.
The Ratcliffe interval of the Charles Formation (Mississippian - Madison Group) was drilled
at 9,297' TVD (-7,002'), 13' high to the Twobins 5501 42-20H. Samples observed during this
interval of drilling consisted of an argillaceous limestone and occasionally a clean limestone.
The argillaceous limestone was a medium to dark gray, light gray, occasionally light brown,
These limestones were observed as both mudstone and wackestone. The argillaceous limestone
was microcrystalline with a firm, friable, and occasionally hard. Occasionally, throughout this
interval was a clean limestone that was cream and light gray in color. The cleaner limestone
was more commonly friable than firm and had an earthy texture. While drilling this portion a
medium brown dead oil staining was from time to time observed in samples.
The Mission Canyon Formation (Mississippian -Madison Group) was drilled at 9,490' TVD
(-7,195'), 5' high to the Twobins 5501 42-20H. Sunburst geologists observed cuttings that
consisted of a clean limestone that was cream, medium-gray, light brown in color. This
limestone was friable to firm and had an earthy texture. Samples were described as
microcrystalline, banded limestone are rarely displayed trace medium brown dead oil staining.
The Lodgepole Formation (Mississippian -Madison Group) was drilled at a TVD of 10,069'
TVD (-7,774'), 16' high to the Twobins 5501 42-20H. The Lodgepole consisted of a medium
gray argillaceous limestone. Cuttings were comprised of both mudstone and wackestone that
had an earthy texture. There were no promising oil staining or visible porosity throughout the
Lodgepole Formation.
While drilling the second curve the Upper Bakken Shale was drilled at 10,826' MD, 10,746'
TVD (-8,451'), 1' high to the Twobins 5501 42-20H. The Upper Bakken Shale was composed
of black to very dark brown, carbonaceous and petroliferous shale, characterized by very high
gamma ray (>500api).
The Middle Bakken Member was drilled at 10,853' MD, 10,761' TVD (-8,466'), 2' high to
the Twobins 5501 42-20H. The Middle Bakken displayed gamma counts ranging from 90-140
API. The middle member was composed of a light brown gray to light brown, silty sandstone.
The silty sandstone was fine grained, moderately cemented with calcite and on rare occasion
dolomite. The Middle Bakken displayed a trace spotty and occasionally even, light to medium
brown oil stain and poor intergranular porosity. Samples throughout this interval contained
rare amounts of disseminated and nodular pyrite.
The Lower Bakken Shale was drilled at 10,954' MD, 10,802' TVD (-8,507'). The Lower
Bakken Shale consisted of a black, carbonaceous and petroliferous shale had an earthy texture
and contained rare amounts of both disseminated and nodular pyrite.
The Pronghorn Member top was confirmed at 10,988' MD, 10,814 TVD (-8,519'). The
Pronghorn interval consisted of medium gray siltstone that was cub-blocky and had an earthy
texture. The siltstone was moderately cemented with calcite. Both nodular and disseminated
pyrite were observed throughout this interval. The Pronghorn displayed trace spotty and
occasionally even, light to medium brown oil stain and poor intergranular porosity. The
Pronghorn was associated with the warm, 190-210 API gamma signature seen just below the
Lower Bakken Shale.
Target Three Forks Formation
The top of the Three Forks was confirmed at 11,025' MD, 10,822' TVD (-8,527'). While
drilling the Twobins 5501 41-20 6T only the upper Three Forks bench was targeted. The upper
Three Forks bench consisted of two lithologies. Most commonly observed was a dolomite
mudstone. The dolomite was associated with cool gamma markers ranging from 80-45 API.
The mudstone was cream and pink in color and was most commonly firm to friable but on
occasion relatively hard. The mudstone often displayed trace to rare spotty oil staining.
Throughout the dolomite there were trace amounts of disseminated pyrite and rare to
occasional amounts of nodular pyrite. The dolomite throughout the Three Forks first bench
displayed trace to rare intergranular porosity. Slightly less commonly seen in samples was
light green to light blue green shale and associated with warm 100-140 API gamma signatures.
The shale was firm and occasionally soft with an earthy and waxy texture. The shale displayed
a sub-blocky texture. There were trace amounts of both disseminated and nodular pyrite, less
than that seen throughout the dolomite intervals. At the very base of the target zone was a
claystone interval. This claystone interval is what separates Oasis Petroleum's first and second
bench of the Three Forks Formation. The claystone was associated with a relatively warm 160-
190+ API gamma signature. The claystone was off white to light gray in color. This portion of
the formation displayed an earthy texture that was firm and occasionally firm. There were trace
amounts of disseminated pyrite and rare amounts of nodular pyrite. This interval displayed the
least amount of oil staining seen throughout the Three Forks upper bench.
Gas and Oil Shows
Hydrocarbon constituents (C1 through C4) are expressed in parts-per-million (ppm)
concentrations. Diesel-invert mud was used to drill the vertical and curve, while salt-water
mud was used to drill the lateral. During the vertical, gas shows ranged from 20-54 units above
a background of ~20-30 units against a 9.7-10.8 lb./gal diesel-invert mud weight. Methane and
Ethane (C1-C2) were observed in the Charles Formation. Gases heavier than C2 were not
observed. Methane, Ethane, and Propane (C1-C3) were logged from the Charles Base Last Salt
to the Scallion. While drilling in the Ratcliffe interval shows ranged from 25-33 units.
Occasionally, samples throughout the Ratcliffe displayed a medium to light brown dead oil
staining. The most promising hydrocarbon shows of the vertical were observed in the lower
portion of the Mission Canyon. While drilling this portion hydrocarbon shows regularly
exceeded 40 units. On rare occasion, samples displayed a medium brown dead oil staining.
While drilling the Lodgepole shows ranged from 30-85 units. The most promising shows was
observed after the first curve was cemented and we began to dress cement. The trip gas was
just under 500 units. The first significant gas show was observed within the Scallion interval.
A 2,400 unit survey gas was recorded at a depth of 10,818'. While drilling the Upper Bakken
Shale two gas shows of 1,047 units and 596 units were observed. The Upper member displayed
a dark brown to black even oil stain. Other than connection and survey gasses there were no
significant hydrocarbon shows observed throughout the Middle Bakken. The connection and
survey gasses ranged from 389-825 units. Samples throughout the Middle Member regularly
displayed medium brown spotted, occasionally even oil staining. While drilling throughout the
Lower Bakken Shale the background gas remained consistent, staying near 400 units. The
Lower Member displayed a dark brown to black even oil stain. The Pronghorn member
exhibited one survey gas of 451 units.
Over the course of the lateral hydrocarbon shows were fairly promising. Background gasses
peaked at 1,200 units, but were more often near 900 units while drilling the lateral portion.
Connection and survey gasses regularly exceeded 1000 units regularly regardless of the
wellbores position in the target interval (excluding the claystone interval). The intervals that
were predominantly dolomite associated with cool gamma markers displayed slightly better
oil staining than the samples with more shale. The oil staining was described as being light
brown and spotty. The worst oil staining was saw within the claystone interval. The claystone
not only displayed poor amounts of staining, but hydrocarbon shows also decreased while
drilling this lower zone. Fluorescent cuts were poor to moderate throughout the lateral. The
most promising fluorescent cuts was observed early in the lateral and were described as being
slow, white to cream streaming, becoming diffuse. There was one trip in the lateral at a depth
of 18,600'. This trip resulted in a 4,767 unit trip gas.
GEOSTEERING
The potential pay zone of the Twobins 5501 41-20 6T was identified by evaluating electric logs
of the Sonat Exploration Co., Glen 1-30, and the lateral logs of the Oasis Petroleum, Twobins
5501 42-20H, and Oasis Petroleum, O M Erickson 5501 42-19H. The electric logs displayed
a 9-10' Pronghorn member overlying the upper Three Forks bench. At the base of the upper
bench is a 8' interval of claystone, this would be the base of the 9' target interval (Figure 2).
The plan for steering the lateral portion was to stay as near as possible to the warm gamma
signature near the top of zone. The reason being that steering off this interface would have
more favorable hydrocarbon shows in comparison to the underlying claystone.

Structure maps provided by Oasis Petroleum geologists indicated that over the course of the
structure would trend upward at a steady inclination of .4°. Since the wellbore was near the
top of the target interval upon reaching casing point the plan for drilling forward was to slowly
descend down to the claystone. In doing this, gamma markers throughout the target interval
would be cataloged. The claystone was identified at a depth of 11,340'. While drilling through
the target zone it appeared that there was reliable shale interval (~120-130 API) overlying a 4'
clean dolomite (~40-50 API) interval before reaching the claystone. After seeing this the plan
for drilling forward was to drill within the dolomitic interval and steer of the base of the warm
gamma near the center of the target zone. This steering strategy worked remarkably well
throughout the lateral.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
