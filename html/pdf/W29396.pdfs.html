Introduction
State Gresz 6-35-26H-143-98 is located North of Dickinson in Dunn County, North Dakota. Oxy State
Gresz is a single horizontal Middle Bakken well. It will consist of one 20,604' MD (measured depth)
long lateral drilled to the North (2.17º). The vertical hole was drilled to a kick off point depth of 10,328'
MD. The Curve section of the well landed at a measured depth of 11,257' MD, 10,892' TVD (total
vertical depth), with 15'of rat hole. Intermediate 7"casing was set at 11,242' MD. Following pre-lateral
meeting, drilling of the lateral section began on 10/29/2014. To isolate the targeted reservoir, a 6" bit
was utilized while drilling the lateral section. Upon landing the curve, directional drilling and geo-
steering techniques were employed to maintain maximum exposure to the target reservoir.
Off Set Wells
MWD Gamma Data was used to determine marker and formation tops during the vertical hole and
during the curve. During both the vertical and curve-build sections, tops were compared to the State
Gresz 1 hole to ensure successful landing within the targeted zone. The true vertical depth (TVD) of the
landing target was calculated by assuming comparable formation thicknesses to the Gresz 1 hole and
was adjusted as necessary until landing. Formation dips were predicted but not always accurate
compared to State Gresz 1 and the Davis Dvorak 14-22 lateral.
Engineering
Vertical Operations
The State Gresz 6-35-26H-143-98 was spud on October 20th, 2014 by H&P 329. An 8 3/4"Smith
MDSi616 (bit #1) was used in front of a 1.5º fixed Hunting mud motor that drill from surface casing of
2,441' MD to 8,045', for a total of 5,604'. At 8,045' tripped out of the hole due to slow ROP, and
changed out BHA (bottom hole assemble). Tripped back in with an 8 3/4" Smith MDSi616 (bit #2) that
drilled to 9,735' which was 588' before Kick off point at 10,328', for a total of '. DHI geologists
commenced logging at 8,000'MD.
Curve Build
Curve building started on October 26th, 2014 with an 8 ¾" Smith MDi516 (bit#3) and a 2.38 degree
motor which drilled to landing TD of 11,257'MD in 28 hours. The MWD tool assemblies were provided
by Baker Hughes which recorded survey and gamma ray data. Gamma correlation was good with some
natural variation from the cross-borehole references. The wellbore successfully penetrated the Middle
Bakken formation at 11,199'MD (10,888'TVD). The Curve was landed at 11,257'MD (10,892' TVD)
approximately 4' into target zone. Upon completion of the landing, the drill string and BHA were
tripped out of the hole. 7" Casing was set at 11,242'MD.

Lateral
The State Gresz 6-35-26H-143-98 lateral began on October 29th, 2014 using brine water. The target
interval chosen for this well was 9.5-16.5' below the Upper Bakken Shale within a 19' zone. Drilling of
the lateral portion of the well began at a depth of 11,257'MD, with Bit #4 and drilled to a total depth of
20,604'MD in a single run. The well bore finish 100% in the Middle Bakken formation and 68.4%
within the 7' target area of the payzone which is discussed more under geo-steering section. (Page 9)
Gas Readings
Methods
One well-site geologist and two logging geologists, contracted by DHI Services, Inc. conducted the
geologic analysis for State Gresz 6-35-26H-143-98. DHI installed and monitored a flame ionization
detector (FID) Total Hydrocarbon Analyzer (THA) and chromatograph interfaced with a DHI Services, Inc.
Drillsense Electronic Data Recording System (EDR). The EDR provided depth, drilling rate and pump
stroke data to the Clarity-Lite gas-monitoring software which returned gas data in the form of total gas units
and the hydrocarbon components C1 through C5 (methane, ethane, propane, butane and pentane)
concentrations expressed in parts-per-million. Total Gas and the individual components were calibrated
daily to a known calibration gas. (Figure 1)
Figure 1 ­ DHI Services, Inc. Calibration Chromatography
DHI's chromatograph reads 7 gases: C-1 methane, C-2 ethane, C-3 propane, iC-4 isobutane, nC-4 normal
butane, iC-5 isopentane, and nC-5 normal pentane. The chromatograph runs continuously automatically,
injecting gas samples and sending the results to clarity light for computation. The results are all tagged with
a time which is then used to populate the database with chromatograph data that is correctly lagged to the
correct foot base on what the lag depth was at the particular time the sample was taken. The chromatograph
4 | P a g e
uses a coiled column which separates the gases based on molecular weights and is run thru a flame
ionization detector which causes an electrical charge on each carbon atom that is read by the detector and
sent to the clarity light program. The electrical response is compared to the known calibration values. The
chromatograph has various settings that can be change for hi or low gas values.
DHI's THA (Total Hydrocarbon Analyzer) uses the same type of flame ionization detector but detects the
constant stream of gas sample being brought back to the machine via agitator and poly flow sample line.
Because the THA and GC are using the same gas stream there is a constant comparison of each machines
readings. Generally the amount read by the GC, if combined, is roughly the same methane equivalent as
detected by the THA, although there are other gases read by the THA that are not being computed on the
chromatograph such as acetylene and any gas above C-5. This being said the THA will read slightly more
gas compared the methane equivalent of all the gases reported on the mud log's chromatograph data.
Gas samples were pulled through ¼" poly-flow tubing after agitation in DHI's gas trap installed in the
possum belly. DHI personnel caught lagged samples under the supervision of well-site geologists in 10' to
30' intervals from 8,000' to 20,604' TD. Cuttings were then repeatedly washed with Dawn dish detergent
and water until clean. Dry cuttings were examined under a binocular microscope. Cuttings collected from
within the Middle Bakken Formation were evaluated for hydrocarbon fluorescence using a fluoroscope,
"cut" and residual ring.
If more than one carbonate type is present then they can be determined separately using the values from the
multiple pressure peaks and formulas supplied by the instrument manufacturer. Two sets of dry cuttings
were collected, with one set sent to the North Dakota Geological Survey Core Library and the second set
sent to the project geologist overseeing operations.

The Mississippian Kibbey Formation typically consists of red-
brown to red-orange sand, siltstone and shale with a limestone
zone near the middle. The upper portion typically consists of
sands inter-bedded with shale while the middle portion is mainly

LIMESTONE­ light gray to gray to tan gray, cryptocrystalline to

carbonaceous, petroliferous, and no visible porosity.

Interpreted Cross Section
The target zone was chosen based on density porosity logs from other wells within Fayette Field.
Stratigraphic location of the target was based primarily on gamma ray values but gas shows, sample
observations, and drill rates were also utilized. The presence of distinct gamma ray indicators within the
Middle Bakken Formation help provide for dip calculations when present. The Limestone stringer which
is a clear low gamma marker seemed to always be present which allowed for accurate dip calculations.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
