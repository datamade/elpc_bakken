Ho-149-99-3526H-2 [SE SW Sec. 35, T149N, R99W] oil well is approximately 9 miles south of
Watford City, North Dakota. It is a double section horizontal oil well that will target the Middle
Member of the Bakken Formation. This is the second lateral drilled to total depth in a northern
direction by Precision Drilling rig #564 (Figure 1) on this location. Previously, the BW-Heidi
Ho-149-99-3526H-1 well was drilled to total depth on June 1, 2013. This well will be used for
close correlation for the H-2 curve landing and lateral expectations. The Heidi Ho H-1 lateral
runs parallel to the BW-Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-2 lateral.
GEOLOGIC EVALUATION
A two person geology team from Sunburst Consulting, Inc. provided well site geologic
supervision and logged drill cuttings, monitored gas, and provided geosteering services
throughout the drilling operation. Wellbore landing into the target zone was maximized by
collaboration and communication between the Hess operations geologist, the Baker Hughes
directional team, and the well site geologists.
Lithology
The Middle Bakken was the target formation for lateral production for this well and its lithology
will be focused here. All formations up-hole can be found in addendum to this evaluation. The
Middle Bakken was encountered at 11,346' MD (11,162' TVD) with a marked decrease in
gamma and ROP. A brownish gray-tan to off white siltstone was observed at the top of the
Middle Bakken Member and is estimated to be around 3'-5' thick. Samples below this siltstone

are a light to medium gray calcareous silty sandstone. The silty sandstone is very fine grained,
moderately well sorted, and moderately well cemented with calcite cement. The Middle Bakken
sample cuttings also displayed an earthy to granular texture, and contained traces of disseminated
pyrite and intergranular porosity. Primarily, rare brown spotty oil staining was seen throughout
the lateral as shown in Figure 2. As the lateral progressed, the Middle Bakken samples varied
only slightly in color, cementing, and rounding. The lower half of the Middle Bakken is referred
to as the lower Middle Bakken facies. This rock is less porous; and therefore, more compacted,
and does show some bioturbation. This facies is marked by a characteristic "high and tight"
gamma signature, ranging between 85 and 105 API units.
Hydrocarbon Shows
Background gases throughout the lateral ranged from 400 units to over 900 units, averaging
around 750 units. Commonly, gas shows ranged from 1,200 units to 1,600 units. Gas levels were
generally higher when the wellbore was near the top of the target zone. Figure 3 shows the gas
curve for the extent of the lateral. The gas buster was utilized throughout the duration of the
lateral, and gas readings while on the buster may be slightly lower than the true value. Flares
ranging from 3'-5' were commonly seen.

GEOSTEERING
The BW-Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-2 lateral was drilled east of the Heidi Ho H-1 lateral, providing
good control for the Middle Bakken structure map. The lateral was expected to have a 0.4° to
0.6° down dip for the first 6,000'-7,000' of the vertical section. It was then expected to flatten to
nearly flat and have a 0.1° to 0.2° down dip for the remainder of the lateral. The curve was
landed at the top of the target window and immediately out of casing the wellbore dropped in
section to reveal the majority of the ideal target window. For the first 6,500' vertical section
(VS) of the lateral, the dip trended downward at approximately 0.25°, with some flattening
locally. Formation dip from 6,500' VS to total depth then averaged 0.1° down. The distinct "high
and tight" gamma of the lower Middle Bakken facies was encountered multiple times and can
been seen at 2,000' VS and again at 6,000' VS as examples in Figure 4. In reference to the
currently producing Heidi Ho H-1 well, formation dips encountered while drilling were very
close to expectations and the dip profile in Figure 4 closely compares to the BW-Heidi Ho-149-
99-3526H-1 dip profile.
Gamma ray readings within the target zone were rather erratic. However, the lower Middle
Bakken facies, very evident in Figure 4 beyond 5,500' VS, was a good marker towards the
bottom of the target zone. There was also a fairly good marker with higher gamma between 100
to 120 API at the top of the target zone.
The overall apparent formation dip was 0.16° down, decreasing approximately 28' TVD, varying
only slightly from the expectations based on the surrounding offsets and structure map. The
BW-Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-2 was drilled to total depth at 21,498' MD in 16 drilling days. The
bottom hole location is 9,900.63' north and 1,261.29' east of surface location or approximately
302.66' from the north line and 1,596.39' from the east line, NW NE Section 26, T149N, R99W.
The lateral was drilled 70% within the target window.

SUMMARY & CONCLUSIONS
The BW-Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-2 was re-entered on September 25, 2014 and reached a TD of
21,498' MD on October 9, 2014. The formation depths encountered during the vertical portion of
the BW-Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-2 were very close to prognosis. This was, in part, due to the
proximity of this well to the BW-Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-1 previously drilled on this location,
and currently producing. During the lateral section background gases ranged from 400 to 900
units, with shows up to 1,600 units. Oil staining was typically rare spotty brown.
Drill cuttings within the Middle Bakken target window showed little variation throughout the
lateral. However, there were good gamma ray markers at the top and bottom of the target
window, both of these making for good indication of wellbore positioning. This should prove
useful for the next lateral section on this location. Formation dip varied minimally from
expectations. Overall the BW-Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-2 was drilled successfully and shows
good potential for production.

below for reference. Sample descriptions begin in the Mississippian Kibbey formation and end in the Devonian

microscope fitted with a UV light right for hydrocarbon `cut' fluorescence and 3MP camera for a photo sample log.
Cuttings were examined for `cut' by immersion in Carbo-Sol (trichloroethylene) and inspection under the UV light.
Cut speeds in order of increasing speed are very slow, slow, moderate, fast and instant. Quantifiers in order of
increasing abundance are trace, rare, occasional, common and abundant.

Flaring Alternatives:
Mobile Gas Treatment Units:
GTUIT is a mobile flare gas capture technology for remote natural gas liquids (NGLs) extraction and gas
conditioning processes. The systems strips out NGLs from the gas stream and refrigerates them for
storage reducing volatile organic compounds (VOCs) thereby mitigating environmental impacts by
significantly decreasing emissions from flare stacks. The Units can be combined and scaled up to handle
high volumes of gas.

Hess has contracted with GTUIT to provide mobile gas treatment units (MTUs). As of 6/23/2014, Hess has
five GTUIT units being utilized in the field and expects delivery of three additional units by 8/1/2014. The
units have capacities ranging from 500 MSCFD to 1,000 MSCFD and can be relocated from one site to
another. The units also can be linked onsite to provide a larger daily capacity intake.

Hess is developing methodologies that will result in an order-of-merit list of candidate sites at which to
deploy the MTUs, based on environmental, regulatory, economic, and operational factors. There are
commercial constraints present which pertain to Hess's commercial agreement with GTUIT; wells must
produce at least half of the MSCFD unit capacity and cover the initial connection fees in order for use of
the GTUIT unit to be economic.
Natural Gas Generators:

Natural Gas Generators are an alternative to diesel generators to provide electricity to well pads located
in remote areas. The units are tied directly into the natural gas production onsite and supplement liquid
propane for use during hourly production fluctuation. Natural gas generators require liquid propane to be
used when gas is not being produced from the well. As production of gas increases and decreases hourly,
the units may switch from propane to natural gas and then back to propane.

Hess performed two pilot programs to evaluate performance characteristics of natural gas generators
from two manufacturers. Both manufacturers require further development to increase their reliability and
performance of units.

Economics favor diesel generators over natural gas generators at this time due to cost and reliability. Pilot
program was not able to produce consistent results to move forward with field implementation at this
time. Natural gas generators will continue to be evaluated as an alternative in the future.
Bi-Fuel (Blended) Diesel Generators:

Bi-Fuel diesel generators are diesel generators with natural gas "makeup" kit (blend kit) allowing units to
use produced gas onsite to power the facility.

Hess is currently in the conceptual phase of evaluation of Bi-Fuel Diesel Generators.
