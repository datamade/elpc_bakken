INTRODUCTION
QEP Energy Company's P. Levang 4-14-23BH [NW NW, Section 14, T150N, R95W] is
located approximately 29 miles to the east of Watford City, North Dakota. This well is permitted
as a 1,280 acre spacing unit. The P. Levang 4-14-23BH is proposed as a double section 10,000'
lateral targeting the Middle Member of the Bakken Formation within the Grail Field of the
Williston Basin. The lateral will be drilled to the south within section 14. This well will be the
third vertical hole to be drilled on the P. Levang 4-14x Pad.
OFFSET WELLS
The primary offset well used for adjusting the curve landing was QEP's P. Levang 4-14-23H
drilled 43 feet to the south. The other offset wells used for correlation were the QEP Energy
Company's Jones 16-14H, located 1.1 miles to the southeast, and QEP Energy Company's Jones
4-15-22H located approximately 1.2 mile to the west southwest. All three offset wells drilled
horizontally into the Three Forks, providing good data for isopach thickness and structural dip
estimations.
ENGINEERING
The P. Levang 4-14-23BH was spud on November 16, 2014 by Unit Drilling Rig #121, where a
13 ½" surface hole was drilled with fresh water to 1,885' then isolated with 9 5/8" casing and
cemented to surface. The drilling fluid was changed to diesel invert for the vertical and curve
build. A vertical hole was drilled to kick off point at 10,130' MD. Halliburton provided the
directional drilling and MWD personnel and tools, including gamma ray and surveys. The curve
assembly was picked up at the kick off point, and the curve was completed in 18.5 drilling hours
at 10,874' MD, on November 22, 2014. The lateral section of the P. Levang 4-14-23BH was
proposed to be drilled in the Middle Bakken Formation north to south for approximately 9,800'
MD.
GEOLOGY
Methods
Geologic analysis for P. Levang 4-14-23BH was provided by Sunburst Consulting, Inc.
Information was networked through Pason's electronic data recorder system. This information
network provided depth, drilling rate, pump strokes and total gas units throughout the well site.
Gas data was gathered using Pason's gas detection system and disseminated across the location.
Total gas was measured in units, using a 10,000 unit scale. Beginning at 6,500' MD, within the
Spearfish Formation, rig crews caught lagged samples at the direction of the well site geologists.
Samples were collected at 30' intervals throughout the vertical and curve. Two sets of dry
cuttings were collected for QEP Energy Company, and the North Dakota Geological Survey. The
sample cuttings were analyzed in wet and dry conditions under a 20x60 power binocular
microscope.
Prospective Intervals
The Mission Canyon Formation was drilled for 618' TVD. The upper section varied from
wackestone to mudstone and contained dark brown algal laminations. As is common throughout
the upper portion of this formation were stringers of anhydrite, and dolomite. The middle to
lower sections consists of argillaceous limestone described as a firm mudstone, light gray to a
dark brown with an earthy texture. Trace light brown oil staining was found in sample cuttings
throughout the Mission Canyon. Background gas throughout the formation fluctuated from 150
to 850 units. An increase in the porosity of the formation at 9,290' MD yielded a gas show
which reached 876 units.
The Lodgepole Formation was drilled for 816' TVD and consisted of argillaceous mudstone and
wackestone. This rock is described as light to medium gray, soft and earthy. Also noted was a
firm to moderately hard wackestone with a very fine waxy texture. These samples also included
traces of algal laminations and disseminated pyrite. Indications of increased fracture porosity
within the upper half of the Lodgepole occurred at 9,980' MD where background gas increased
from 200 units to nearly 800 units. Our gas remained consistently elevated throughout the
remainder of this vertical portion of the well. Once our kickoff point was reached, and we
resumed drilling in the lower portion of the Lodgepole, the background gas settled back to
between 190 to 250 units with slight elevations during connections.

GEO-STEERING
Curve/Landing
The kick off point for the curve build was reached at 10,130' MD on November 21, 2014 while
drilling in the Lodgepole Formation. This kick off matched the P. Levang 3-14-23BH kick off
point, also on this pad, to the exact MD. The landing was proposed to be 10' to 20' TVD into
the Middle Bakken. Our prognosis tops corresponded perfectly while calculating our landing
target until we reached the Lodgepole #2 marker. Here we were forced to move the landing up a
full 10' TVD due to our marker appearing earlier than expected. The curve was landed without
any problems at 10,874' MD, and 10,597' TVD in the Middle Bakken on November 22, 2014 at
3:26 CT.
Lateral Target Zone
The target zone defined by QEP was a 20' target window from the top of the lower dolomite
(base of low gamma marker) to 10' above the Lower Bakken Shale. Dip expectations provided
by QEP, structure maps, and nearby offset wells suggested an overall continuous 0.5° regional
structural dip to TD. The prominent marker used to accurately steer within our zone was the low
gamma marker associated with the Shoal. Attempts were made to correlate repeating gamma
signatures for dip calculations throughout, however using the Shoal encounter at 15,100' MD our
projections to that point were fully solidified. Moving forward from that point we were able to
continuously calculate accurate local dips based on similar gamma characteristic in relation to
the well bore's path through the formation. The overall structure ended up being extremely close
to QEP's pre-drill estimation of 0.5°, at 0.47°. This is shown on the cross sectional profile
below.
Samples collected throughout the lateral within the target zone were generally described as: silty
sandstone from brown to light brown and light gray to dark gray in color with traces of nodular
pyrite and calcite crystals. Samples collected from the "Shoal" were described as: off white to
very light gray limestone, firm to hard, and containing an abundance of oolitic fossil fragments.

SUMMARY
The P. Levang 4-14-23BH was the first fully completed Middle Bakken well on this pad. Due to
the proximity of the offset wells, our projections proved to be quite accurate, and aside from the
10' change to the landing, nothing else came in as a surprise.
The overall gas units as observed were very normal for a Middle Bakken well, and throughout
the target zone, spotty brown to light green oil was noticeable over the shakers. The lateral was
drilled from Intermediate Casing Point to Total Depth in 7 drilling days. This made up a total of
16 drilling days from spud to TD at 20,675' MD on December 2, 2014.
Overall the P. Levang 4-14-23BH was drilled 100% within the designated target zone, and shows
by all signs shows exceptional potential for oil production.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.

NOTES ON 9-5/8" SURFACE CASING DESIGN:
The key point in designing surface casing is to extend the shoe of the surface casing beyond the top of
the Pierre Shale. This ensures that all potable or usable water zones are covered. At this point in the well
design, the drilling fluid is changed to oil base mud to contend with salt sections that occur at
approximately 6,550' TVD and 8,950' TVD. The 36 ppf J55 casing accomplishes these criteria easily.
A higher burst rating is not necessary based on industry standards throughout the Williston Basin.
In the event the surface casing burst rating was increased, the normally pressured and sub normally
pressured zones all the way thru the Madison Group at approximately 8,250' TVD has the potential to
break down. Therefore the increased pressure rating of surface casing is not warranted. It is not feasible
to set the surface casing point at a greater depth because the shut in surface pressures would not
appreciably increase. The Dakota formation (4,900' TVD) is frequently weak. Typically, we drill the
intermediate section with 10.5 ppg OBM to the 7" casing point. In the event we lost circulation in the
exposed intermediate interval, the wellbore will not be entirely evacuated and the surface casing can be
shut in without compromising the pressure rating of the 36 ppf J-55.

A. Oil-base mud will be mixed in the closed circulating system and transferred to 500-bbl tanks on

and production section of the hole is drilled, the cuttings transported to the surface with the drilling
fluid will be mechanically separated from the drilling fluid as waste by shale-shakers and then
cleaned/dried via a mud cleaner and/or centrifuge. These separated cuttings will be transferred to a
container, dried and stabilized, and then transported to an approved disposal site or, if a cuttings pit
is approved, the solidified and dried cuttings will be stored in the pit.
B. Plastic material will underlay the rig, oil base mud/diesel storage tanks and mud pits. All tanks on
location will be placed inside of berms. Any oily waste fluids and sediments generated at the work
site during drilling operations or when cleaning the fluid containment system after drilling will also
be stabilized and then transported to an approved disposal site or, if a cuttings pit is approved, it will
be solidified and stored in the cuttings pit.
C. All rig ditches will be lined and directed to a lined sump for fluid recovery. A drip pan will be
installed on the BOP stack, a mud bucket will be utilized as needed on connections and a vacuum
system will be used on the rig floor for fluid recovery in those areas.
