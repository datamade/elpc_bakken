Introduction
The Kirkland 6-7-1-12T2HD is one of the two wells which will be drilled on the pad. It's
located in Croff Field of Mackenzie County ND, or more specifically 269' FSL & 285' FWL,
[SW SW Sec. 7, T149N, R95W.] This oil well is approximately 19 miles south east Watford
City, North Dakota. It's a planned double section, horizontal well targeting the "2nd Bench" of
the Three Forks Formation, and is the first vertical/curve sections to be drilled. The Kirkland 6-
7-1-12T2HD will be the only well drilled within the Three Forks formation.
Offset Wells
Offset wells are used in depth and structure correlation throughout the vertical and curve. This
data is crucial in order to establish and correct measurements made throughout the build and
landing of the curve. The Primary offsets used to establish these figures were QEP Energy's
Moberg 3-20-21TH, QEP Energy's Kirkland 13-23/14H, and Texaco's Lyle Henderson 1.
Engineering
The Kirkland 6-7-1-12T2HD was spud February 16, 2015, and drilled entirely by Unit Drilling
Rig #121. This included a 13 ½" surface hole drilled with fresh water to 2,185', and cased to
2,175' with 9 5/8" casing.
The drilling fluid was then changed to diesel invert to resume the vertical portion of the well. On
February 24th at 10,671' MD the kick off point to begin the curve was reached while drilling in
the lower section of the Lodgepole formation. The curve was then drilled to completion on Feb.
26th at 11,432' MD, with a projection to bit of 11,150' TVD, and an inclination of 89.38°. This
placed the well bore approximately 10' below the top of the "Three Forks Second Bench," which
will also serve as the top of the target window to begin the lateral. Intermediate casing was set to
a depth of 11,421' MD.
The directional and MWD personnel and tools are all provided by Sperry-Halliburton. This
equipment and personnel combined with collaborated efforts and communication between QEP
and Sunburst, create the ability to accurately place our wellbore into the target zone.
Geologic Evaluation
A two man geology team provided by Sunburst Consulting, Inc. provided the on-site real time
data analysis. Streaming well information is networked through Pason's electronic data recorder
system. This network provides all pertinent data such as depth, drilling rate, total gas, and
gamma ray units throughout the well site, and remotely. The gas data is gathered using Pason's
gas detection and trap system. Total gas is measured using a 10,000 unit scale and logged
without the use of a chromatograph. Beginning at 6,000' MD while drilling in the Swift
formation, rig crews caught 30' samples at the direction of the well site geologists. Two sets of
dry cuttings are collected and labeled for both the QEP Energy Company, and the North Dakota
Industrial Commission. These cuttings were analyzed in wet and dry conditions under a 20x60
power binocular microscope.
Petrography and Hydrocarbon Shows
Formations above the Lodgepole Formation showed minimal characteristics for secondary
potential. Due to fracture porosity, the lower Lodgepole is where the first signs of abundant
petroleum were recorded. At 10,642' MD the gas units steadily rose to 5,687. The sample
description for this area was argillaceous limestone; described as dark to light grey mudstone,
firm and earthy, including traces of algal laminations and disseminated pyrite.
The next major area containing significant oil and gas shows is the Upper Bakken Shale (Figure
2), which was drilled at 11,035' MD, 10,994' TVD (8,461') SS. The UBS was composed of
black, carbonaceous and petroliferous shale, gamma ray values eclipsing 400 API, and a gas
show over 1500 units.
Figures 2: Upper Bakken Shale (Left), 3: Middle Bakken sandstone (Center), 4: Lower Bakken Shale (Right).
The Middle Bakken was encountered at 11,064' MD, 11,015' TVD, (8,482') SS, and is marked
with a sudden, drastic, yet temporary decrease in gamma ray, ROP, and gas due to the Lower
Bakken Shale's quick introduction shortly thereafter. The Middle Bakken however does
generally maintain two distinct facies within Croff Field. The first, a dark gray-tan siltstone
observed at the top, and is estimated to be around 3-5' thick. A light to medium brown silty
sandstone (Figure 3) makes up the remainder of the formation. This rock is fine grained,
moderately well sorted, moderately well calcite cemented, and having an earthy texture. Traces
of disseminated pyrite, intergranular porosity, and rare, brown, spotty oil staining did exist
The Lower Bakken member (Figure 4), was logged at 11,124' MD, 11,055' TVD, (8,522') SS.
Similar to the UBS, the Lower Bakken consisted of black, carbonaceous and petroliferous shale,
gamma ray values above 650 API, and a gas show above 1500 units.
The Three Forks Formation 11,177' MD, 11,086' TVD, (8,553') SS, and primarily its Second
Bench, is the location for our lateral drilling target window.
The First Bench of the Three Fork Formation contains interbedded firm, pale green-light pink
blocky shale, having an earthy texture with trace to rare disseminated pyrite; and white to off
white, light brown, very fine crystalline, microsucrosic dolomite containing trace disseminated
pyrite and rare amounts of spotty, medium brown oil staining (Figure 5).
Figures 5: Interbedded Three Forks shale & dolomite (Left), 6: Second Bench shale (Right).
The Second Bench logged at 11,267' MD, 11,125' TVD, (8,592') SS of the Three Forks
Formation consists of very fine crystalline, microsucrosic dolomite having trace light brown oil
staining (Figure 6).
Curve Landing & Summary
The projected data from QEP operations geologists indicate a landing target 10' below the Three
Forks second bench. The curve was landed for the Kirkland 6-7-2-1THD with a projection to bit
of 11,150' TVD which places us at the top of our target window when we drill out for the lateral.
This lateral section of the well is expected to have an initial 90° inclination, so with the projected
89.3° angle at the bit, all data indicates we are in a fantastic position to move forward. Being
slightly below dip we should be able to log more data as it pertains to the remaining portion of
the zone.

LATERAL INTRODUCTION
The Kirkland 6-7-1-12T2HD is proposed as a double section 10,000' lateral, drilling from south
to north within sections 7 and 6 and targeting the 2nd Bench of the Three Forks, which is a
prospective secondary oil play within the Three Forks Formation. The Kirkland 6-7-1-12T2HD
will be the only Three Forks lateral drilled and completed on the pad.
ENGINEEERING
Prior to drilling the lateral, casing was set and cemented from surface to Intermediate Casing
Point with 7" HCL80 32# casing to 11,358' MD. Following casing operations, the drilling fluid
was changed from diesel invert to salt water. The lateral reached TD using a total of three
BHA's. The Kirkland 6-7-1-12T2HD lateral was completed on March 26, 2015 at 2:30 CST
which reached a total depth of 21,223' MD and 11,157' TVD in 8 drilling days. The bottom hole
location is 10,007.31 N & 147.16 W of surface location or approximately 250.61' FNL &
137.84' FWL, NW NW Sec. 7, T149N, R95W. Overall the lateral was drilled 100% within the
target zone.
GEOSTEERING
The lateral section of the Kirkland 6-7-1-12T2HD was geo-steered by the QEP Geology team.
Dip expectations were provided by QEP along with structure maps and nearby offset wells, all of
which suggested an approximate 0.03° regional structural dip. Attempts were made to correlate
repeating gamma signatures for dip calculations. The prominent markers used for steering were
the "Cool Gamma" at the top or target zone, "Ratty Gamma in the middle of zone and "Hot
Gamma" markers near the bottom. These markers were encountered repeatedly during the lateral
section providing strong points for geo-steering decision making. The Cool Gamma consisted of
an approximate 3' thick bed of tan and moderately soft dolomite. The Ratty Gamma signature
was made up of interbedded siltstone and dolomite, which was the prominent rock encountered
throughout the target zone. The Hot Gamma markers primarily consisted of 1' thick shale beds
with thin siltstone laminations. Initially a 0.03° structural dip was assumed from surface to
bottom hole. Actual dip followed QEP's pre-drill plan closely, very slight adjustments were
made throughout the lateral. The gross overall dip for the Kirkland 6-7-1-12T2HD was 0.03°
upward (Figure 2).
TARGET ZONE
The target zone defined by QEP was a 20' target window measured by 7' below the 2nd Bench to
9' above the 2nd Bench Shale. The designated target window primarily consisted of interbedded
dolomite and siltstone. The prominent rock within the target zone was described as; dolomite tan
in color, moderately soft and very fine crystalline texture, with interbedded siltstone. Siltstone
samples were typically described as; brown to gray in color, firm, and very fine grained with
dolomitic cement. Present in the upper portion of the target zone was an approximately 3' thick
tan to light brown dolomite, moderately soft to firm with traces of unconsolidated sand grains,
very similar to the "Massive Dolomite" which was encountered during the curve landing. In the
lower section near the bottom of target zone, alternating beds of very thin shale and dolomite
were present. The shale beds were approximately 6" to 1' in thickness while the dolomite was
approximately 1' to 2' in thickness. The shale samples were described as; light gray to light blue,
calcareous, firm and platy with traces of pyrite.
Figure 1: Light gray shale in the lower portion of target window.
Background gas consistently increased throughout the lateral when the wellbore was present in
the lower portion of target zone and most commonly when the well bore was in or near the thin
shale beds. Throughout the lateral background gas remained between 500 and 1,000 units while
in the silty dolomite intervals and between 1,500 and 2,000 units while in or near the shale
intervals.

SUMMARY
The Kirkland 6-7-1-12T2HD was the second well on this pad to be completed, and the only
Three Forks well. The pre-drill profile estimation was very close to actual structure, resulting in
only slight dip changes throughout. The overall gas units as noted were significantly higher near
the bottom of target zone. Throughout the target zone, spotty brown to light green oil staining
was present within the rock cuttings, as well as noticeable oil indications over the shakers. The
lateral was drilled from Intermediate Casing Point to Total Depth in 8 drilling days for a total of
19 drilling days from spud to finish and reached TD at 21,223' MD on the 26th of March, 2015.
Overall the Kirkland 6-7-1-12T2HD was drilled approximately 100% within the designated
target zone, and shows great potential for oil production.

North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.

NOTES ON 9-5/8" SURFACE CASING DESIGN:
The key point in designing surface casing is to extend the shoe of the surface casing beyond the top of
the Pierre Shale. This ensures that all potable or usable water zones are covered. At this point in the well
design, the drilling fluid is changed to oil base mud to contend with salt sections that occur at
approximately 7,000' TVD and 9,400' TVD. The 36 ppf J55 casing accomplishes these criteria easily.
A higher burst rating is not necessary based on industry standards throughout the Williston Basin.
In the event the surface casing burst rating was increased, the normally pressured and sub normally
pressured zones all the way thru the Madison Group at approximately 8,700' TVD has the potential to
break down. Therefore the increased pressure rating of surface casing is not warranted. It is not feasible
to set the surface casing point at a greater depth because the shut in surface pressures would not
appreciably increase. The Dakota formation (5,300' TVD) is frequently weak. Typically, we drill the
intermediate section with 10.5 ppg OBM to the 7" casing point. In the event we lost circulation in the
exposed intermediate interval, the wellbore will not be entirely evacuated and the surface casing can be
shut in without compromising the pressure rating of the 36 ppf J-55.
