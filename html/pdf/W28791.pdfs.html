Geologic Summary
To analyze the geology present in the wellbore, multiple tools were utilized. The Bloodhound
gas detection unit was used to collect and record gas values present when drilling. The gas was
fed from the possum belly to the Bloodhound using 1/4" and 3/8" polyline. The gas is then
analyzed by the Bloodhound's chromatograph to determine total gas present, as well as the
heavier gas constituents (C1 through C4). This data is then routed to the iBall software to display
these values in a gas chart. Pason's electronic data recorder was also utilized to receive depth,
rate of penetration, pump strokes, and other related values. Formation tops were chosen by
analyzing the rock cuttings, as well as using rate of penetration and gamma ray data.
Samples were originally collected at 30' intervals between the kick-off point and the landing
point (10,400'-11,470' MD). They were then collected at 50' intervals throughout the lateral,
but returned to 30' intervals whenever issues occurred (ex. shale spikes, gas results out of
order). Cuttings were then analyzed and saved to be sent to Abraxas Petroleum and the North
Dakota Geologic Survey.
Cut was very rarely seen in samples collected (about 10% of samples). Whenever cut was seen
it was generally a very poor blue cut with no residual ring. Fluorescence was seen in only a
couple of samples, and on those occasions the fluorescence may have been due to lube used to
lubricate the bit while drilling. The lack of cut and fluorescence suggests that this well probably
will not produce as well as the other wells on this location.
Note: A gas buster was used for the majority of the well which decreased the actual gas values
to the gas detector by up to 50%.
Lodgepole Formation
The Lodgepole 1 came in at 10,596' TVD (10,617' MD, -8,187' SS), and consists of massive open
marine limes. Research performed suggests there are naturally occurring fractures that are
filled with calcite, glauconite, and pyrite. The Lodgepole is separated into 8 different intervals
that range between 10,596' TVD and 10,966' TVD. All of the Lodgepoles are:
Limestone: grey to dark grey, mudstone to wackstone, microcrystalline, microsucrosic
texture, some detrital texture with micro fossil fragments, mudcrack/geopetal structure,
inter-crystal fabric, no visible intercrystalline porosity, tight.
False Bakken
The False Bakken came in at 10,966' TVD (11,193' MD, -8,557' SS). This is a very thin bed of
shale seen below the Lodgepole 8 that is approximately 3' thick. The False Bakken has
characteristics of a dysanaerobic shale and is similar to the Upper Bakken. The False Bakken is:
Shale: dark grey to grey, dull luster, smooth texture, hard to firm, blocky to sub-blocky,
calcareous matrix, micromicaceous, some free organized pyrite crystals, occasional
finely laminated, dense in part, trace of tabular calcite with calcite filled fractures.

Scallion
The Scallion came in at 10,969' TVD (11,217' MD, -8,560' SS). This is a relatively thin bed of
limestone that is approximately 8' thick. The Scallion (or Lower Lodgepole) has characteristics
of an outer ramp carbonate and is a slightly darker grey than the Lodgepole formation above.
Limestone: grey to dark grey, mudstone to wackestone, microcrystalline, non-crysalline,
microsucrosic texture, some detrital with micro fossil fragments, mudcrack/geopetal
structure, inter-crystal l fabric.
Upper Bakken Shale
The Upper Bakken Shale came in at 10,977' TVD (11,226' MD, -8,568' SS). The Upper Bakken is
approximately 25' thick, and is recognizable by the high gamma readings recorded (max of 612
C/s). This shale is dysanaerobic in environmental origin.
The Upper Bakken is:
Shale: : dark grey to grey, dull luster, smooth texture, hard to firm, blocky to sub-blocky,
calcareous matrix, micromicaeous, some free organized pyrite crystals, occasional finely
laminated, dense in part, trace of tabular calcite with calcite filled fractures
Middle Bakken Member
The Middle Bakken Member was determined to come in at 11,002' TVD (11,317' MD, -8,593'
SS), and consists of both intra-tidal greywacke clusters and siltstone. The Middle Bakken
Member is separated as follows:
Sandstone: Mainly quartzose. Also referred to as greywacke having more than 15%
matrix, cream to light grey calcareous cement with trace of calcite cement, very fine to
silt size grains, very well sorted, rounded with occasional well rounded, trace frosted
texture, mostly disaggregated, some very friable clusters, salt and pepper clusters with
abundant embedded black and green fragments, abundant banded black bit flour, trace
embedded bioclast fossil remnants, occasional visible primary effective open porosity.
Siltstone : Massive, dense, somewhat laminated, mottled light grey to grey cement, silt
size grains, milky in part, occasional weathered, possibly bioturbated, round to sub-
round, very well sorted, mostly disaggregated, friable clusters, with moderately weak to
strong calcareous cement, trace pyrite, poor to no visible porosity.

Daily Logging Summary
February 13, 2015:
Drilled approximately 1,640' of the lateral at an average ROP of 135 ft/hr. Samples consisted primarily of
light gray to gray siltstone with occasional cream to light cream sandstone clusters. Samples were very
fine grained, argillaceous, well sorted, round to sub-round, friable to moderately hard, and had a
moderately strong calcareous cement. Cuts were generally poor to non-existent with trace dull yellow
fluorescence in some samples. For about 100 feet there was no mud through the trap due to rig hand
meddling.
February 14, 2015:
Drilled 2,057' today at an average ROP of 132 ft/hr. The sample has very highly cemented matrix with a
silt size grain. The grains are milky, well rounded and well sorted. The clusters are somewhat weathered
and are very argillaceous. Samples also contain significant amounts of light gray to gray siltstone that is
also has a calcareous cement. Very few cuts during the day that have been poor, but generally samples
have had no cut. At 14,560' MD power was cut from the gas trap so that the mud was not being
agitated. This resulted in about 90 feet of inaccurate gas data.
February 15, 2015:
Drilled 504' today at an average ROP of 61 ft/hr. Samples remained relatively consistent with samples
from the previous day. Had to TOOH at 6:00 PM to change out the motor.
February 16, 2015:
Drilled 1,145' today at an average ROP of 100 ft/hr. Resumed drilling at 2:00 PM at 15,671' MD. Samples
were dominated by light grey to grey siltstone with only traces of light cream greywacke clusters. No
cuts were seen in samples today, and only trace fluorescence. At approximately 15,850' MD the
background baseline gas dropped down to approximately 450u. Good connection gases and shows
continued to show for 500 feet before the baseline dropped down further to 250u, suggesting a
depletion of formation gases.
February 17, 2015:
Drilled 1,706 feet today at an average ROP of 90 ft/hr. Samples were dominantly grey to light grey
siltstone with trace amount of light cream greywacke clusters. Gas values greatly increased around
18,280' MD because of the reduced drilling speeds and larger amount of down time gas.
February 18, 2015:
Drilled 191 feet today at an average ROP of 38 ft/hr. Samples remained consistent with the previous
day. Had to TOOH at 1:30 PM due to the motor failing, which explains the reduced drilling speeds and
increased down time gas seen.

February 19, 2015:
Drilled 1,187 feet today at an average ROP of 91 ft/hr. Resumed drilling at 10:30 pm with a new motor
and same bit. Samples have had more greywacke sandstone in them today than siltstone, and cut and
fluorescence continue to be absent. At approximately 18,690' MD the shakers were being bypassed due
to the screens being replaced. Because of this one sample was not collected and gas data is inaccurate
for about 60 feet. There was also a time today when our gas trap lost power so the mud was not being
agitated; however this was fixed quickly enough that the inaccurate gas data is not obvious on the log.
February 20, 2015:
Drilled 601 feet today at an average ROP of 64 ft/hr. Samples continue to be dominantly greywacke
sandstone with some siltstone intermixed, and cut remains absent from samples. Had to TOOH for a
new motor at 10:00 PM.
February 21, 2015:
Drilled 290 feet today at an average ROP of 64 ft/hr. Resumed drilling at 8:00 PM for an all-around trip
of 22 hours. Samples have been approximately half light cream greywacke clusters and grey siltstone.
Rock cuttings continue to be friable, moderately argillaceous, and has a calcareous cement. Cut
fluorescence are still absent from samples.
February 22, 2015:
Drilled 665 feet today at an average ROP of 58 ft/hr. Reached a total depth of 21,365' at 1:00 AM on
2/23/15. Samples on this last leg were dominantly grey siltstone with some cream greywacke clusters.
Cut and fluorescence continued to be absent from samples.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
