GENERAL:
On October 19, 2014 Continental Resource s Annapolis 6-29H1 was spudded as a Three
Forks formation, 2560 spaced, lateral well. The subject well is being drilled in the Dollar Joe field,
located about 10 miles south southwest of Ray, in Williams County in North Dakota. Ray is located in
the north central portion of the Williston Basin. Surface location of the Annapolis 6-29H1 is 310 FSL
& 875 FEL, SESE of Section 20, T155N - R97W. The Annapolis 6-29H1 was planned with an
ultimate goal of oil production in the 1st Bench of the Three Forks formation. The horizontal well-path
was planned following structure with an estimated 89.63° average formation dip to total depth at
21,678 MD. The well bore was planned to be drilled to the south across southern part of section 20
and across sections 29 and 32 following an azimuth of 182.39°. The Annapolis 6-29H1well is located
on the same pad as the Annapolis 7-29H, Raleigh 6-20H1 and Raleigh 7-20H wells.

ENGINEERING:
The Annapolis 6-29H1 surface hole was drilled to 2,286 MD with another drilling rig. Pad
height of the Annapolis 6-29H1 was 2,405 above mean sea level with the Cyclone #10 kelly bushing
located at 2,425 above mean sea level, 20 above ground level.
Annapolis 6-29H1 was drilled with fresh water from surface to 2,286 MD. From surface
casing, the Annapolis 6-29H1 was drilled with oil based mud. Oil based mud was used from 2,286
MD to 11,425 MD. A switch was then made to brine mud from 11,425 MD to a total depth of
21,655 MD.
Initially, the Madison 6-28H1was drilled as a 13 ½ hole to 2,286 MD, and 9 5/8 36# J-55
casing was set to a measured depth of 2,286 through the Fox Hills formation into the Pierre formation.
The Annapolis 6-29H1 then progressed as an 8 ¾ hole from the upper part of the Pierre formation
until landing of the curve in the Three Forks formation. Kick off point (KOP) was reached in the
Lodgepole formation on October 22, 2014, 2131 hrs at 10,648 MD (10,646.06 TVD). At KOP, a trip
was made to pick up a LEAM mud motor with a 2.5° fixed bend and a new PDC bit. Directional work
during the curve was performed by Robert Horton and Collin Bennett from LEAM directional
services. The curve from vertical to horizontal began on October 23, 2014 at 0943 hrs and was drilled
starting from 10,648 MD (10,646.06 TVD) and landed horizontal (88.12°) at 11,425 MD
(11,118.89 TVD). The curve was drilled holding an average build rate of 11.34° per 100 feet and was
landed on October 23, 2014 at 2134 hrs. No mechanical problems were encountered while drilling the
curve.

GEOLOGICAL EVALUATION:
Geologic supervision of the Continental Resource s Annapolis 6-29H1well was provided by
Geo-Link, Inc with a two-man mudlogging / geo-steering consulting service. Geologic
evaluation began just above the Charles formation and continued until TD at the end of the
lateral in the Three Forks formation. MSI s Red Box (chromatograph) was used to record gas
levels at the drilling rig s shakers. The Red Box gas data was integrated into Pason s EDR
(Electronic Data Recorder) system. Also integrated into Pason s EDR system was gamma ray
data provided by Phoenix Technologies MWD services. Pason s EDR provided drill rate, Red
Box gas data, gamma and circulation data for viewing on location and remote via Pason s
Datahub. Lagged samples for the well were caught in 30 intervals from 8,750 measured depth
until total depth was reached at 21,655 measured depth. Cuttings were examined under a
binocular microscope and UV fluoroscope. Cuttings were tested with hydrochloric acid and
alizarin to distinguish between carbonates. Acetone was used to examine oil cut from the
samples. The samples were logged in MPlot, MSI s mudlogging program. After sample
evaluation, one set of dried cuttings was saved for the State of North Dakota.
Sample evaluation for the Annapolis 6-29H1 began just above the Charles Salt formation at a
measured depth of 8,750 . The Charles Salt came in at 8,768 measured depth, 8,767 total
vertical depth and -6,342 mean sea level. The Charles Salt consisted mostly of salts. Salts from
the Charles formation were typically clear to translucent to milky to translucent red in color,

The Mission Canyon formation came in at 9,673 measured depth, 9,671 total vertical depth

structure. No shows were present while drilling the Mission Canyon formation. No problems

The Lodgepole formation came in at 10,276 measured depth, 10,274 total vertical depth and
-7,849 mean sea level. The Lodgepole formation consisted of gray brown to gray to dark
brown to dark gray brown limestones, hard in hardness and microcrystalline to
cryptocrystalline packstones and wackestones in structure. No significant oil/gas shows were
observed while drilling the Lodgepole formation. No problems were encountered while drilling
the Lodgepole formation.
The Upper Bakken shale member came in at 11,067 measured depth, 11,004 total vertical
depth and -8,579 mean sea level. The Upper Bakken shales consisted of dark brown to dark
gray brown to black shales, firm to brittle in hardness, sub platy to sub blocky structure, non
calcareous, carbonaceous to earthy in texture. Oil show was noted with drill cuttings of the
Upper Bakken shale exhibiting yellow fluorescence and bright yellowish blue cut fluorescence.
A 310 unit background gas increase was observed while drilling the Upper Bakken shale with a
peak gas recorded of 4404 units. No problems were encountered while drilling the Upper
Bakken shale member.
The Middle Bakken limestone came in at 11,101 measured depth, 11,026 total vertical depth
and -8,601 mean sea level. The Middle Bakken consisted of gray to tan to gray brown silty
limestones. The Middle Bakken limestones were typically hard to firm and occasionally soft in
hardness, no to poor visible porosity, very fine crystalline packestones and wackestones in
structure, very slightly dolomitic in part, slightly argillaceous and commonly sandy. Pyrite was
rare in the upper part of the formation. Drill cuttings of the Middle Bakken showed fair to good
oil shows with patchy yellow fluorescence, slight hydrocarbon odors, weak milky yellow and
blueish white cut fluorescence. A 274 unit increase in background gas was observed while

The Lower Bakken shale member came in at 11,207 measured depth, 11,075 true vertical

The Three Forks formation came in at 11,288 measured depth, 11,103 true vertical depth and
-8,678 mean sea level. The Three Forks consisted of dolomite with interbedded shales and
siltstones. The dolomites were typically light brownish gray to dark brownish gray to gray, hard

very poor visible porosity and predominantly packestones in structure. Pyrite was abundant in
the upper part of formation. The shales were pale green to pale greenish gray, soft to
moderately hard, waxy grading to silty to occasionally sandy in the lower part of formation.
Disseminated pyrite was common throughout the interbedded shales. Drill cuttings of the Three

moderate diffuse milky bluish to slow streaming yellowish green cut fluorescence. Background
gas levels varied from 700 units to 7800 units throughout the lateral.

Continental Resource s Annapolis 6-29H1 well was drilled successfully as a 2560 spaced,
Three Forks 1st Bench lateral well. The Annapolis 6-29H1 took 13 days to drill from spud to TD.
The lateral was within the 13 target zone for 75% of the MD drilled. The lateral was within the 1st
Bench of the Three Forks formation for 100% of the time.

Filter Socks and Other Filter Media
Leakproof Container Required
Oil and Gas Wells
Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.

Continental Resources standard CCL, CBL, 4-1/2" liner running and testing procedures
Continental Resources' standard practice for running the cement bond log and casing
caliper log is to run both logs immediately after coming out of the hole after TD, prior to running
the 4-1/2" liner, to the deepest depth obtainable; however, if there are well control concerns that
require us to run the liner sooner, only the CBL will be run and the CCL will be run after setting
the liner.
Based on the CCL results, we determine the actual API minimum burst allowance for the 7"
casing. If the downgraded API burst pressure is below our minimum required frac pressures, we
will run a 4-1/2" frac string; if severe wear or holes are found in the casing, we will run a 5"
cemented, to surface, tie back string.
The CBL log is run in order to determine the top of cement, as required by the NDIC.
Our current 4-1/2" liner program for a 1280 unit is 30, evenly spaced, stages with 29 swellable
packers. The liner shoe is set approximately 180 feet off bottom. The shoe stage below the last
packer has 2 joints, a double valved float, one joint, and a ported guide shoe ­ appx 130 ft. The
liner is run using a running tool on the end of 4" DP. The 7" packer/hanger is set about 40 ft
above KOP between two casing collars but conditions occasionally occur that require setting
higher, either through unexpected failure or in order to isolate casing wear close to KOP.
Recently we have tried 40 stage liners and the trend to explore the optimum stage count will
continue.. Once the liner is at depth, a ball is dropped through the DP, the ball is pressured up
against the setting tool to approximately 2500 psi, and the 7" packer/hanger is set.
A push pull test is done to confirm the hanger has set. Then, a 4500 psi pressure test is
completed on the back side of the 4" DP to confirm the packer has set. The setting tool is then
backed off and the 4" DP/running tool is laid down.
Immediately after the rotary rig has been moved off the well location, the 7" csg and liner
packer/ hanger are tested to the frac pressure. The testers will rig up and test the tubing head to
5000 psi. Next a test plug will be run and set, using wire line, in the top of the 7" packer/hanger.
Testers will pressure up to our frac pressure, typically 8500 psi, to confirm the 7" is ready for
completion.
