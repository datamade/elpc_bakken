Introduction
Kenneth Stroh 4-12-1H-143-97 is located approximately 20 miles Northwest of Dickinson in Dunn
County, North Dakota. Kenneth Stroh 4 is one of 3 horizontal Middle Bakken wells which will be
drilled on this location. It will consist of one 21,204' MD (measured depth) long lateral drilled to the
North (0.64º). The vertical hole was drilled to a kick off point depth of 10,122' MD. The Curve section
of the well landed at a measured depth of 11,228' MD, 10,837' TVD (total vertical depth), with 3'of rat
hole. Intermediate 7"casing was set at 11,225' MD. Following pre-lateral meeting, drilling of the lateral
section began on 10/6/2014. To isolate the targeted reservoir, a 6" bit was utilized while drilling the
lateral section. Upon landing the curve, directional drilling and geo-steering techniques were employed
to maintain maximum exposure to the target reservoir.
Off Set Wells
MWD Gamma Data was used to determine marker and formation tops during the vertical hole and
during the curve. During both the vertical and curve-build sections, tops were compared to the Raphael 3
pilot hole to ensure successful landing within the targeted zone. The true vertical depth (TVD) of the
landing target was calculated by assuming comparable formation thicknesses to the pilot hole and was
adjusted as necessary until landing. Formation dips were predicted but not always accurate compared to
Raphael 3 and 4 lateral.
Engineering
Vertical Operations
The Kenneth Stroh 4-12-1H-143-97 was spud on August 3rd, 2014 by H&P 329. An 8 ¾" PDC
MDSi616 (bit #1) was used in front of a 1.5º fixed Hunting mud motor that drill from surface casing of
2,346' MD to 7,471', for a total of 5,115'. At 7,471' tripped out of the hole due to slow ROP, and
changed out BHA (bottom hole assemble). Tripped back in with an 8 3/4" Security MM65D (bit #2) that
drilled to 9,624' MD just before KOP. Kick off point at 10,122' was reached on August 9thth, 2014. DHI
geologists commenced logging at 8,000'MD.
Curve Build
Curve building started on August 9th, 2014 at 1146hrs with an 8 ¾" Security MM65D (bit#3) and a 2.38
degree motor which drilled to landing TD of 11,228'MD in 45 hours. The MWD tool assemblies were
provided by Baker Hughes which recorded survey and gamma ray data. Gamma correlation was good
with some natural variation from the cross-borehole references. The wellbore successfully penetrated the
Middle Bakken formation at 11,165'MD (10,831'TVD). The Curve was landed at 11,228'MD (10,837'
TVD) approximately 11' into target zone. Upon completion of the landing, the drill string and BHA
were tripped out of the hole. 7" Casing was set at 11,225'MD.

Lateral
Drilling of the lateral on the Kenneth Stroh 4-12-1H-143-97 began on October 6th, 2014 using brine
water. The target interval chosen for this well was 8-17' below the Upper Bakken Shale within a 24'
zone. Drilling of the lateral portion of the well began at a depth of 11,228'MD, with a HC PDC Bit #4
and drilled to a depth of 20,115'MD for a total of 8,887'. The call was made to trip out of the hole due
to tool failure. Bit # 5 a HC PDC finished off the lateral drilling to a total depth of 21,204'. The well
bore finish 100% in the Middle Bakken formation and 88.3% within the 9' target area of the payzone
which is discussed more under geo-steering section. (Page 9)
Gas Readings
Methods
One well-site geologist and two logging geologists, contracted by DHI Services, Inc. conducted the
geologic analysis for Kenneth Stroh 4-12-1H-143-97. DHI installed and monitored a flame ionization
detector (FID) Total Hydrocarbon Analyzer (THA) and chromatograph interfaced with a DHI Services, Inc.
Drillsense Electronic Data Recording System (EDR). The EDR provided depth, drilling rate and pump
stroke data to the Clarity-Lite gas-monitoring software which returned gas data in the form of total gas units
and the hydrocarbon components C1 through C5 (methane, ethane, propane, butane and pentane)
concentrations expressed in parts-per-million. Total Gas and the individual components were calibrated
daily to a known calibration gas. (Figure 1)
Figure 1 ­ DHI Services, Inc. Calibration Chromatography
4 | P a g e
DHI's chromatograph reads 7 gases: C-1 methane, C-2 ethane, C-3 propane, iC-4 isobutane, nC-4 normal
butane, iC-5 isopentane, and nC-5 normal pentane. The chromatograph runs continuously automatically,
injecting gas samples and sending the results to clarity light for computation. The results are all tagged with
a time which is then used to populate the database with chromatograph data that is correctly lagged to the
correct foot base on what the lag depth was at the particular time the sample was taken. The chromatograph
uses a coiled column which separates the gases based on molecular weights and is run thru a flame
ionization detector which causes an electrical charge on each carbon atom that is read by the detector and
sent to the clarity light program. The electrical response is compared to the known calibration values. The
chromatograph has various settings that can be change for hi or low gas values.
DHI's THA (Total Hydrocarbon Analyzer) uses the same type of flame ionization detector but detects the
constant stream of gas sample being brought back to the machine via agitator and poly flow sample line.
Because the THA and GC are using the same gas stream there is a constant comparison of each machines
readings. Generally the amount read by the GC, if combined, is roughly the same methane equivalent as
detected by the THA, although there are other gases read by the THA that are not being computed on the
chromatograph such as acetylene and any gas above C-5. This being said the THA will read slightly more
gas compared the methane equivalent of all the gases reported on the mud log's chromatograph data.
Gas samples were pulled through ¼" poly-flow tubing after agitation in DHI's gas trap installed in the
possum belly. DHI personnel caught lagged samples under the supervision of well-site geologists in 10' to
30' intervals from 8,000' to 21,115' TD. Cuttings were then repeatedly washed with Dawn dish detergent
and water until clean. Dry cuttings were examined under a binocular microscope. Cuttings collected from
within the Middle Bakken Formation were evaluated for hydrocarbon fluorescence using a fluoroscope,
"cut" and residual ring.
If more than one carbonate type is present then they can be determined separately using the values from the
multiple pressure peaks and formulas supplied by the instrument manufacturer. Two sets of dry cuttings
were collected, with one set sent to the North Dakota Geological Survey Core Library and the second set
sent to the project geologist overseeing operations.
Hydrocarbons
The lateral section had an average background gas between 580 and 650 units of gas and connection gas up
to 800 units. Oil shows contained bright yellow to white florescence with immediate streaming to blooming
white cuts with a flare height averaging 3-8' high. Trip gas after the pulling BHA #4 was 1,200 units.
Throughout the lateral cuttings were tan-brown-dark grey sandstones and limestones with visible porosity.
Noted oil and gas shows are on the mudlog.

The Amsden Formation typically consists of red-brown to red-
orange Limestone, siltstone and shale. It ranges in thickness from
300 ­ 400 feet thick. Typical sample descriptions from this
formation are as follows: SHALE ­ dark red to reddish brown to
green, soft to very soft and platy to sub platy, slightly calcareous,
trace of siltstone.
Amsden Formation
Tyler Formation ­ SHALE- dark red to reddish brown- dark
gray- green-yellow, soft to very soft, and platy to blocky, slightly

Tyler Formation

The Mississippian Kibbey Formation typically consists of red-
brown to red-orange sand, siltstone and shale with a limestone
zone near the middle. The upper portion typically consists of
sands inter-bedded with shale while the middle portion is mainly
white to light tan limestone. In this area, the limestone member is
predominant. Typical sample descriptions from this formation are
as follows: LIMESTONE ­dark red to red brown to red gray,
microcrystalline to very fine crystalline, varying hardness from
friable to moderately firm, earthy to dense texture with SHALE
stringers.
Kibbey Formation
Below the Kibbey is the Mississippian Charles Formation
(Madison Group). This formation consists of inter-bedded thick
to thin beds of limestone anhydrite, and salt. Note: Very
important formation in the Williston Basin. Pay particular
attention to the Base of the last salts for target changes. Typical
sample descriptions from this formation are as follows:
LIMESTONE­ light gray to gray to tan gray, cryptocrystalline to
microcrystalline to crystalline, varying hardness from friable to
hard, earthy to dense texture commonly containing anhydrite and
clear crystalline salt.
First Charles Salts
The Mississippian Mission Canyon Formation (Mississippian
Madison Group) is typically composed of mostly massive brown
to gray limestone, argillaceous, fossiliferous and oolitic;
dolomitic in part.
Mission Canyon Formation
The Mississippian Lodgepole Formation (Mississippian
Madison Group) is typically described thin bedded argillaceous
fossiliferous limestone. Typical sample descriptions from this
formation are as follows:
LIMESTONE­light to medium gray with trace tan, dark gray
and black, cryptocrystalline to microcrystalline, varying

Lodgepole Formation

The "False Bakken" Marker (Mississippian Lodgepole Formation) is described as follows:
SHALE: black to brown black, soft to friable, earthy texture, slightly calcareous. This Marker is important for

adjust their approach to landing the curve.
The Mississippian-Devonian Bakken Formation is comprised of two black carbonaceous and petroliferous

porosity. This lithology is characterized by high gamma and elevated gas shows. A typical sample is described as
follows: SHALE - black, firm, sub blocky, earthy texture, common disseminated pyrite, calcareous,
carbonaceous, petroliferous, and no visible porosity.
Upper Bakken Shale
The Upper Bakken Shale has two distinctive peaks with the
lower peak ranging between 350 ­ 500 API. The samples showed
a blocky, very dark gray to black shale with no discernible flow or
depositional pattern.
Middle Bakken:
The Middle Bakken siltstones are consistent within the first 3-5'.
The gamma signature of the zone was typically a 50 ­ 150 API
gamma signature. Close inspection of the samples under a
microscope consistently showed a distinctive light colored, fine
grained silt stone.
