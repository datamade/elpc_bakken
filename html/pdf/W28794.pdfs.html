Geologic Summary
To analyze the geology present in the wellbore, multiple tools were utilized. The Bloodhound
gas detection unit was used to collect and record gas values present when drilling. The gas was
fed from the possum belly to the Bloodhound using 1/4" and 3/8" polyline. The gas is then
analyzed by the Bloodhound's chromatograph to determine total gas present, as well as the
heavier gas constituents (C1 through C4). This data is then routed to the iBall software to display
these values in a gas chart. Pason's electronic data recorder was also utilized to receive depth,
rate of penetration, pump strokes, and other related values. Formation tops were chosen by
analyzing the rock cuttings, as well as using rate of penetration and gamma ray data.
Samples were originally collected at 30' intervals between the kick-off point and the landing
point (10,400'-11,350' MD). They were then collected at 50' intervals throughout the lateral,
but returned to 30' intervals whenever issues occurred (ex. shale strikes). Cuttings were then
analyzed and saved to be sent to Abraxas Petroleum and the North Dakota Geologic Survey.
Cut was seen in the rock cuttings of close to 75% of the samples. Cuts in the samples were poor
to fair, and ranged from blue to blue green to milky blue in color with faint to poor blue residual
rings. Trace dull fluorescence was also seen occasionally in samples.
Lodgepole Formation
The Lodgepole formation was determined to begin at 10,104' TVD (10,164' MD, -7,695' SS), and
consists of massive open marine limes. Research performed suggests there are naturally
occurring fractures that are filled with calcite, glauconite, and pyrite. The Lodgepole is
separated into 8 different intervals that range between 10,535' TVD and 10,911' TVD. All of the
Lodgepoles are:
Limestone: generally light grey to grey to grey-brown, mudstone to wackestone, blocky,
microcrystalline, firm to moderately hard, moderately argillaceous, has no visible inter-
granular porosity. Oolites and bioclasts were occasionally seen in Lodgepole samples.
False Bakken
The False Bakken came in at 10,911' TVD (11,068' MD, -8,502' SS). This is a very thin bed of
shale seen below the Lodgepole 8 that is approximately 2' thick. The False Bakken has
characteristics of a dysanaerobic shale and is similar to the Upper Bakken. The False Bakken is:
Shale: dark grey to grey, hard to firm, blocky to sub-blocky, has trace calcareous cement.
Scallion
The Scallion came in at 10,913' TVD (11,072' MD, -8,504' SS). This is a relatively thin bed of
limestone that is approximately 8' thick. The Scallion (or Lower Lodgepole) has characteristics
of an outer ramp carbonate and is a slightly darker grey than the Lodgepole formation above.
The Scallion is:
Limestone: grey to dark grey, mudstone to wackestone, blocky, microcrystalline, firm to
moderately hard, moderately argillaceous, has no visible inter-granular porosity.

Upper Bakken Shale
The Upper Bakken Shale came in at 10,921' TVD (11,089' MD, -8,512' SS). The Upper Bakken is
approximately 20' thick, and is recognizable by the high gamma readings recorded. The Upper
Bakken is:
Shale: dark grey to grey, ranges from hard to firm, blocky to sub-blocky, silty, has trace
calcareous cement.
Middle Bakken Member
The Middle Bakken Member was determined to come in at 10,940' TVD (11,133' MD, -8,531'
SS), and consists of both inter-tidal greywacke clusters and siltstone. The Middle Bakken
Member is separated as follows:
Sandstone: cream to light cream, very fine grained occasionally fine grained, round to
sub-round occasionally well rounded, well sorted, moderately indurated to friable, has
moderately strong to weak calcareous cement, poor to fair visible inter-granular
porosity.
Siltstone: light grey to grey, very fine grained, round to sub-round, very well sorted,
friable, has moderately weak to weak calcareous cement, trace pyrite, poor to no visible
inter-granular porosity.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
