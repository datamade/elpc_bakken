Slawson Explorations Jore 2-12H [NW NW Section 12, T151N, R95W] is located ~12 miles

Control Wells

target depth determined from the offset wells. The table was then used to assist in the choosing
of the landing target. Comparison of the gamma ray markers showed the most consistent isopach
for up-hole formations to be the False Bakken.

Geologic Assessment
Methods
Geologic supervision of Jore 2-12H was provided by two experienced RPM well site geologists.
Gas and chromatograph levels were measured using iBall Instruments BloodhoundTM real time
gas detector and chromatograph system. The Bloodhound gas detection system uses non-
dispersive infrared and chemical sensor gas detection to evaluate gases liberated from the
formation by the drill bit and carried to the surface by the drilling fluid.
The Bloodhound was interfaced with a Pason electronic data recorder system. Pason provided
drill rate, on-off bottom and pump strokes to the Bloodhound and received total gas information
from the Bloodhound for viewing on location and remotely.
Under the direction of RPM well site geologists, rig crews were instructed to catch lagged drill
cutting samples at 30' intervals in the vertical hole and curve build from 8,100' to 10,924' MD
and 50' samples from 10,924' to TD (15,110').
Sampled drill cuttings were examined wet and dry under a binocular microscope using both plain
(broad spectrum) and transmitted light. Cuttings were evaluated for hydrocarbon "cut" by
immersion in Entron (N-Propyl Bromide) and inspection under a UV fluoroscope. 10%
hydrochloric acid and alizarin red were used to determine the calcareous and dolomitic content
of the rocks and the cementation of the rocks.
RPM Geologic, Inc. (RPM) well site geologists also closely examined MWD gamma-ray
information and penetration rates to aid in steering decisions and dip degree estimations.
Vertical Operations
Overview
The Jore 2-12H was spud for surface drilling on 15 July 2014 utilizing Key 887 as the drilling
contractor. Prior to commencement of RPM on-site geology services, a 13 1/2" hole was drilled
with a PDC bit and fresh water to depth of 2,082' and isolated with 9 5/8" 36# J-55 casing
cemented from 2,061' to surface.
RPM well site geologists began logging the vertical interval at 8,100'.
Three PDC bits were used to drill out of surface casing to vertical TD. PDC bit #1 (8 ¾"
Halliburton MM65D) was used to drill out of the surface casing shoe from 2,082' to 7,047' and
averaged an ROP of 86.3 ft/hr in 57.5 hours of use. PDC bit #2 (8 ¾" Halliburton FX65D)
replaced bit #1 in preparation for drilling through the Mississippian age limestones. It was used
from 7,047' to 8,959' and averaged an ROP of 46.1 ft/hr in 41.5 hours of use. PDC bit #3 (8 ¾"
Halliburton MM65D) was installed after a motor failure. It was used from 8,959' to 10,090 and
averaged an ROP of 40.4 ft/hr in 28 drilling hours.
Diesel invert drilling fluid with a mud weight ranging from 8.80-11.40 ppg was used for the
remainder of the vertical hole and in the curve builds sections. The vertical interval was drilled
to a kick off point (KOP) of 10,090', at which point vertical operations were ceased.
Once KOP was reached, electronic log (e-log) operations began. Triple combo open hole logs
were built; neutron-density porosity logs from KOP to the top of the Kibbey lime, induction
resistivity logs from the KOP to the base of surface casing, and gamma ray logs from KOP to the
ground surface. The hole condition was good, and logging was completed without and technical
problems or delays.

geologists worked closely with RPM directional as well as Enseco MWD teams to formulate
steering decisions in order to maximize the footage of borehole in the pay zone.
Curve Build
Overview
One PDC bit was used to build the curve section. Bit #4 (8 ¾" Halliburton MMD55M) was used
in front of a 2.38° fixed mud motor to drill the curve from a measured depth of 10,090' to
10,924' in 41 hours, averaging 20.3 ft/hr. 7" LTC P-110 32 lbs casing was set to 10,911' MD
upon completion of the curve.
A Chart was constructed for the curve (Chart 1) that compared the isopach of certain easily
recognizable gamma markers from the Base of the Last Salt to Middle Bakken Sandstone on
the control wells Olive 1-6 and Clark's Creek 14-18-19H to the Jore 2-12H as gamma marker
picks were made. The False Bakken marker was the main point used to make changes to the
target landing depth during the curve, being similar in thickness to target based on the offset
well. This marker should serve as a good reference point for future curve landings in the
immediate area. The curve was completed at a measured depth of 10,924'and TVD of 10,597', ~
13' below the base of the Upper Bakken Shale.
Chart 1: Comparing distances from gamma markers on the offset wells to markers in gamma in the curve.
Lithology
The top of the "False Bakken" [Mississippian ­ Devonian] was penetrated at 10,672' MD,
10,550' TVD (-8,354') 60' low to the Olive 1-6. Due to the low angle of penetration this thin
interval was not largely observed in sample.
The Upper Shale of the Bakken formation [Mississippian ­ Devonian] was drilled at 10,722'
MD, 10,568' TVD (-8,372') with sample returns of black, carbonaceous, and petroliferous shale
(Figure 5) characterized by gamma ray values in excess of 275 API counts. The Upper Shale was
62' low to the Olive 1-6. Samples were described as:
SHALE: black, firm, earthy to waxy texture, pyrite, carbonaceous, platy, visible
fracture porosity

The Middle Member of the Bakken formation was logged at 10,787' MD, 10,584' TVD (-
8,388'), 59' low to the Olive 1-6. The Middle Member contained layers of siltstones, sandstones
and silty sandstones with varying amounts of porosity and oil stain (Figure 6). Samples from the
Middle Member were described as:
SILTY SANDSTONE: common salt and pepper appearance, occasional light gray to
medium gray, visible white, very fine grained, soft to firm, moderately to well sorted,
rounded to subrounded, moderately to poorly cemented, visible calcareous filled

intergranular porosity, visible light brown spotty to even oil stain

Lateral
Overview
Drilling fluid consisting of diesel invert drilling fluid (11.00-11.40 ppg) was used while drilling
the lateral section up to the TD of 15,110'. Two 6" PDC bits were used to drill the lateral.
Bit #5 (6" Halliburton MM64) was a PDC bit used to drill the start of the lateral from 10,924' to
11,437', where it was pulled after a trip for motor failure. It was used for 17 hours at an average
drilling rate of 30.2 feet per hour.
Bit #6 (6" Halliburton MM64) was a PDC bit used to drill the remaining section off lateral from
11,437' to 15,110', averaging a drilling rate of 35 feet per hour for 105 hours.
Drilling operations were concluded at 12:35 am on August 11, 2014 at a bottom hole location of
4,721.06' S & 112.79' E of surface location or approximately 299.66' FSL & 1,356.79' FWL, SE
SW Sec. 12 T151N, R95W.
Lithology
To aid in communications while drilling the lateral, a type log was created using a common
gamma signature from the Clark's Creek 14-18-19H (Diagram 1). The goal was to land above
the cool gamma shoal in the Middle Bakken and drill out below projected formation dip tagging
the cool gamma shoal to confirm position within the target window. The cool gamma of "E"
marker (50api ­ 60api) appeared to be reliable and served as the main marker for steering. The
cool "C" marker at the top of the target zone was also commonly used for steering purposes.
Diagram 1: Type log created from gamma log of the Clark's Creek 14-18-19H.
The target Zone of the Middle Bakken consisted of a Calcareous Silty Sandstone with very
apparent intergranular porosity, abundant gas, and moderate oil show (Figure 7). Samples from
the target Zone were generally described as:
SILTY SANDSTONE: common salt and pepper appearance, occasional light gray to
medium gray, visible white, very fine grained, soft to firm, moderately to well sorted,
rounded to subrounded, moderately to poorly cemented, visible calcareous filled
fractures, common disseminated pyrite, possible fracture porosity, common
intergranular porosity, visible light brown spotty to even oil stain, slow yellow
streaming cut, moderately dull yellow mineral fluorescence

Gas and hydrocarbon Shows
It was found that the gas and hydrocarbon shows were a poor indicator of placement in Zone
throughout the lateral. Gas consistently remained between 1,500 and 3,000 units while in the
Middle Bakken member, with occasional connection peaks maxing out at 7,260 units.
The Bloodhound gas chromatograph showed components of all C1-C4 present through the entire
lateral. Sample cuttings throughout the lateral showed a trace oil stain and a slight to moderate
white streaming cut when submerged in Entron solution and observed with a UV fluoroscope.
Trace oil was observed in the sample wash water.

RE: Filter Socks and Other Filter Media
Leakproof Container Required
Oil and Gas Wells
Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.

Slawson Pre-Frac Safety and Testing Procedure
7" casing testing procedure
1) Logging
a. Run bond log to top of L/H/P and ensure adequate cement coverage (at least 500' above top of zone to
be fractured)
b. Run casing inspection log in 7" casing to top of L/H/P or 100' beneath cement top
i. De-rate casing for any significant wear based on remaining wall thickness.
ii. Set maximum treating pressure at no more than 85% of the normal or de-rated pressure rating -
whichever applies.
2) Casing testing
a. RU wireline and set a test plug in the top of the Liner/Hanger/Packer.
b. Pressure test the 7" casing and wellhead as follows:
i. In new areas where treating pressures are unknown, test to 7500# for 30 minutes with less than
a 5% loss
ii. In areas with established operations, test to 500# above fracturing pressures seen in that area for
30 minutes with less than a 5% loss
iii. In the case of worn casing, if 85% of the de-rated pressure rating results in a maximum
allowable treating pressure that is less than 500# greater than the expected treating pressure ­
run a tie-back string
3) Pressure relief valve ­ an adequately sized pressure relief valve must be installed on the treating lines from
the pump to the wellhead and set to limit the line pressure to no more than the casing test pressure less 100#.
4) Surface casing valve ­ the surface casing valve must be left open and a diversion line run to a pit or
containment vessel; in order to enable relief of any pressure encountered in the surface casing/intermediate
casing annulus during fracture operations.
