INTRODUCTION
The Oxy USA, Inc. Sharon Rainey 3-17-20H-142-96 [SESE Sec 8 , T142N, R96W] is located
approximately 6 miles Southwest of Manning, North Dakota. The subject well was drilled in Dunn County,
ND by the Nabors X-20 rig in the Manning Field of the Bakken pool. The subject well was planned with
one approximately 9,977' [Intermediate casing to projected total depth (TD)] long southerly lateral bore
with intent to intersect regional hydraulic fracture trends that might enhance reservoir quality within the
Middle Bakken. OXY USA intends to develop oil production in the late Devonian Middle Bakken target
porosity zone with a stratigraphy consisting of siltstone, silty sandstone and limestone with depositional
lithofacies ranging from onshore terrestrial to offshore marine deposits.
ENGINEERING
Surface, Vertical & Curve Operations:
A 9 " surface hole was drilled on October 21st, 2014 by the Nabors X-20 rig and surface casing
was set at 2,297'. The vertical hole operations commenced on November 1, 2014 with Weatherford
providing equipment and personnel for MWD and directional drilling services. The first vertical Bottom
Hole Assembly (BHA), consisting of MWD tools, 1.50° bend Hunting 7/8 5.7 motor and NOV DS616M-X1
PDC bit (6x16 jets), drilled to a depth of 7,870' measured depth (MD) and was pulled out due to low rates
of penetration (ROP). The second BHA had a Smith MDSi616BPX bit and a new motor. This BHA was
pulled for motor failure at 9,312' MD. KOP was not reached, but a curve assembly was run in the hole to
complete the vertical section.
The first curve BHA (BHA #4 overall) consisted of a 1.83º bend NOV 7/8 5.0 motor, 8 ¾" Security
MMD55M PDC bit, and new MWD tools. This assembly was pulled due to low build rates. The motor was
changed out for a 2.12 bend Hunting motor, and curve TD of 11,075' MD (~10,579' TVD) was reached.
This placed the well bore approximately 9' into a 17' thick (shale to shale) Middle Bakken zone, and 3'
into the targeted 6' thick silty sandstone targeted interval. This BHA drilled the 7.5°/100', 1,275' long curve
in 43 cumulative hours.
Directional tools were then pulled out of the hole and a string of 7" intermediate casing was run,
set, and cemented at a depth of 11,060' MD. The rig was then skid to Sharon Rainey 5-17-20H-142-97.
The hole was wire-lined by GYRO Data to decrease collision control ellipsoids. Lateral operations
continued on November 27th, 2014. Diesel invert drilling fluid was used throughout the vertical hole and
curve build sections maintaining a 9.8-10.1 ppg mud weight for stable downhole conditions.
Lateral Operations:
Weatherford again provided equipment and personnel for MWD and directional drilling services.
The directional drillers, MWD hands, and Payzone geologists worked closely together throughout the
project to make steering decisions. The Inc Sonde tool was paramount in successful steering, especially
during sections where distinct gamma markers were absent.
A 6" Security MMD54 PDC bit, 1.50º Hunting mud motor, and MWD tools comprised the first and
only lateral BHA (6th BHA overall) and was run in the hole on 4" drill pipe starting at 11,075' MD. This BHA
reached a TD of 20,697' in 100 hours, making a one run, 4.1 day lateral. Spud to TD was a total of 14
drilling days. The final closure azimuth was 182.07°, resulting in a closure vertical section of 10,549.16'.
The BHL is 10,542.32' South & 380.60' West from surface location or approx. 595.78' FSL & 794.60' FEL
SE SE Sec. 20, T142N, R96W (Figure 2). Salt water drilling fluid was used from the intermediate casing
to TD with mud weights throughout the lateral varying from 9.8-10.1 ppg.

GEOLOGIC EVALUATION
Methods:
Geologic supervision of the well was provided by Payzone Consulting, Inc. with two well site geologists.
Brandon Lutz and Kevin Jacks drilled the vertical/curve and lateral sections. A CanrigTM gas
chromatograph was interfaced with a Rigwatch electronic data recorder system and provided total gas
information for viewing around location. Lagged samples were caught in 30' intervals from 8,000'-10,730'
with 10' samples collected when landing the curve from 10,730'-11,075' MD. 100' lagged samples were
collected through out the lateral (11,075' -20,697' TD). In order to keep more solids in the drilling fluid, the
shakers were adjusted to a steep incline such that for long portions of the lateral, no drill cuttings were
obtainable. However, wherever possible, some sample was collected. Cuttings were examined under a
binocular microscope and complete lithologic descriptions are provided in the lithology document within
this evaluation. Two sets of dry cuttings were collected, with one set sent to the North Dakota Geological
Survey Core Library and the second set sent to Jennifer Califf, the project geologist overseeing
operations. 200' lagged gas samples were also collected during the lateral via an IsoTubeTM gas
manifold.

Geosteering and Tracking Assembly: TVD Calculation Discrepancies
The entire well bore was drilled in between the 17' projected shale to shale zone. Well developed gamma
signatures and trends (discussed above) allowed the well site and Houston geologists to make steering
decisions, thus keeping the wellbore in the 6.5' targeted interval for the majority of the well (65%).
Figures 9, 10, 11, and 12 depict the main gamma markers as seen on the Wellsite Horizontal log and
Payzone Interpretive Steer dip profiles, respectively.
Two geosteering profiles were maintained by the well site geologists: Inc Sonde Profile (Figure 12) and
Survey profile (Figure 11). When drilling 250 ft/hr for the majority of the well, Weatherford's continuous
inclination tool provides an update ~ every 20-30' MD. The survey tool only receives measurements ~
every 96'. The tendency of the lateral assembly to track resulted in the two profiles yielding a similar dip
(90.44 and 90.42, respectively).
The Survey profile from Sharon Rainey 5-17-20H-142-96 yielded an overall dip of 90.36. This was a
whole tenth of a degree different from the Inc Sonde Profile for that well (90.47). In essence, two
supposedly identical wells 200' apart can yield completely different overall dips. This proves that it is
absolutely necessary to steer off gamma markers during the lateral. The amount of error in TVD
calculations increases with distance from the SHOE and depends greatly on the behavior of the BHA (i.e.
whether it has the tendency to build, track, or fall). Our TVD calculations are solely a function of where the
survey is taken and do not take into account all the builds and drops in the assembly between surveys.

overall exposure within the Middle Bakken Member of the Bakken Formation and 65% within the defined
6.5' targeted interval (Figure 11).

Summary

The X-20 drilling rig successfully drilled a two-section horizontal wellbore within the Middle
Bakken Member of the Bakken Formation at the OXY USA, Inc. Sharon Rainey 3-17-20H-142-96.

A mud program consisting of diesel invert (9.8-10.1 ppg) was used from spud to curve landing
with salt water drilling fluid used from the intermediate casing to 20,697' MD (TD). Salt water mud
(9.8-10.1 ppg) was efficient for maintaining stable down hole conditions and permitted adequate
analysis of gas concentrations in the lateral. Background gas concentrations within the Middle
Bakken ranged from 30-175 units with connections/gas shows as high as 600 units.

Major lithologic/gamma markers used for steering were the limestone/siltstone interval, the center
of target high gamma, and the base of target high gamma. Dip calculations were made based on
the wellbore's repeated intersection with these markers.

The lateral was drilled 100% (9,622' MD) in the 17' shale to shale zone and 65% (6,254' MD) in
the ideal 6.5' targeted interval. Overall dip of formation depended on which method used for the
calculation. Using the Inc Sonde data to plot the wellbore, gross apparent dip was 0.44°. If only
surveys were used to plot the wellbore, overall dip was 0.42°. The similarity between the two
reflects a tracking assembly. However, the difference between this well and the Sharon Rainey 5-
17-20H-142-96 (drilled 200' to the east in the same zone) highlights the amount of error involved
in TVD calculations.

Samples from the target consisted of white to off-white, light-dark grey, and brown silty
sandstone. The center of target zone, when gamma counts were in the 90's, displayed distinct
light grey color and fissile tenacity. It became finer grained and better cemented. This section
within the target zone is interpreted to be a hard streak off which the wellbore deflected for the
last half of the well. Oil stains were spotty to even and light brown in color.

The Sharon Rainey 3-17-20H-142-96 was a successful drilling effort as measured by gross
exposure to the target. Additionally, the geosteering knowledge gained from drilling two wells next
to each other with different assemblies is vital for future operations. The well will be fitted with a 4
½" production liner and swell packers in preparation for a fracture stimulation that will determine
the true commercial value of the well.

Rig crews caught samples in 30' intervals from 8,000' to 10,730', 10' intervals from
10,730' to 11,075', and 100' intervals from 11,075' to 20,697' (TD). Formation tops and
lithologic markers have been inserted into the sample descriptions below for reference.
Sample descriptions began in the Amsden Formation. Samples were examined wet and
dry under a binocular microscope. Quantifiers in order of increasing abundance are
trace, rare, occasional, common and abundant. Subsea is delineated as the negative
numbers within the parentheses located next to the measured depth or true vertical depth.
Sample quality was good to fair. Overall, the samples within the Middle Bakken target
consisted of light-medium grey silty sandstone with common calcite cement and rare
disseminated pyrite. Oil stains were light brown and spotty. Gas samples were caught at
200' intervals throughout the lateral using an IsotubeTM sampling manifold.

Filter Socks and Other Filter Media
Leakproof Container Required
Oil and Gas Wells
Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
Thank you for your cooperation.
