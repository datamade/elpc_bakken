Introduction
Emerald Oil, Inc. successfully drilled the Joel Goodsen 6-32-29H horizontal well
at an average azimuth of 360º and an average inclination of 89.99°, targeting the Three
Forks Formation beginning in Section 32, T 149 N, R 102 W, (370' FSL & 1150' FEL) in
McKenzie County, North Dakota, drilling north to the northeast quarter of the northeast
quarter of Section 29, T 149 N, R 102 W, with a spacing unit consisting of 1,280 acres.
The well is located in the Moline Field in the southwest portion of the Williston Basin,
North Dakota. The main wells used for correlation were the vertical and curve sections
of the Joel Goodsen 7-32-29H, drilled on the same location, the Davis-Choctaw Cross
24-32 Section 32 T 149 N, R 102 W, and Haugen 13X-34 in Section 34, T 149 N, R 102
W.
Operations Summary
Vertical Section
The Joel Goodsen 6 was spud on August 31, 2014. Brara Geologic
Consulting arrived on site and began well site operations on September 5, 2014.
A Mudlogging Systems, Inc. Redbox gas detection system was used for
analyzing and recording total gas and chromatograph data. Surface drilling was
completed on September 4, 2014 and 9 5/8" casing set to a depth of 2147' (MD).
An invert mud system was used to drill out from surface throughout the landing of
the curve section.
The first BHA used in the vertical consisted of (Bit #2) an 8 ¾'' Security
MM75, a Pathfinder 1.5° mud motor, and a HDS-1 MWD tool which drilled out of
surface casing at 2147' (MD). This BHA drilled 7211' in 68 hours to 9358' (MD),
where it was pulled due to motor failure. The next BHA consisted of (Bit #3) an 8
¾'' Security MMD83M, a Pathfinder 1.5° mud motor, and a HDS-1 MWD tool
which drilled 1137' in 30 hours to the kick-off point (KOP) at 10495' MD, 10494'
TVD, (-8079' SS). The kick off point was reached at 18:41 on September 8, 2014
and a trip was made to pick up the curve assembly.
Curve Section
Drilling operations resumed on September 9, 2014 at 12:05 with the curve
assembly. This BHA included (Bit #4) an 8 ¾'' Security MMD55D, paired with a
Pathfinder 4/5 lobe configuration 7.0 stage, 2.5° and 5.58' bit to bend mud motor
and HDS-1 MWD/gamma tool. This assembly drilled 790' in 26 hours to the
landing of the curve at 11285' MD, 10977.44' TVD, (-8562.44' SS). The curve
was landed at 15:33 on September 10, 2014 at an Inclination of 89° and an
Azimuth of 352.5°, with a landing point location in Section 32, T 149 N, R 102 W,
(876.01' FSL & 1231.92' FEL).

Lateral Section
The intermediate casing shoe was drilled out on September 10, 2014 with
a new BHA and a 9.8 lb/gal salt water mud system. The first BHA used in the
lateral included (Bit #5) a 6" Security MMSI613 bit, with a Hunting 7/8 lobe
configuration, 1.5º and 5.00 bit to bend motor, an AWR LWD (Resistivity/Gamma
tool), ABS (Battery sub), non-mag pin by pin X/O, HDS-1L (Directional and back
up Gamma). This assembly drilled 1176' from casing to a depth of 12461' (MD),
where a Lower Bakken Shale strike occurred. A short trip was then made at
11608' (MD), 10975.8' TVD, (-8560.8'SS) to start a sidetrack.
Starting the sidetrack included time drilling with a consistent tool face of
165° left for eight minutes per inch for three feet, from 11608' (MD) to 11611'
(MD). At 11611' (MD) drill time was increased to five minutes per inch for five feet
to 11616' (MD), where drilling time increased again to four minutes per inch for
four feet to 11620' (MD). A controlled slide was then held at 90° left for six feet
from 11620' (MD) to 11626' (MD) to accomplish separation from the previous
wellbore. Separation was successful and the same assembly was used to drill to
14203' (MD), where a trip was made for a new bit. (Bit #5) drilled a total of 2918'
in 61.65 hours.
Upon inspection it was determined that the tool string had experienced
internal electrical failure. A new BHA was assembled which consisted of (Bit #6)
a 6" Security MD6I613, with a Hunting 7/8 lobe configuration, 1.5º and 5.00 bit to
bend motor, an AWR LWD (Resistivity/Gamma tool), ABS (Battery sub), non-
mag pin by pin X/O, and an HDS-1L (Directional and back up Gamma). This BHA
drilled a total of 6905' in 100 hours to total depth (TD) at 20712' MD, 11011' TVD,
(-8596' SS).
Total depth (TD) was reached at 21:15 on September 21, 2014 at an
inclination of 88.46° and an azimuth of 0.77°, in section 29, T 149 N, R 102 W,
(252' FNL & 1264' FEL).

Base of Last Charles Salt
The Base of Last Charles Salt was picked from samples and ROP at 9440' MD,
9438' TVD, (-7023' SS).
Base of Last Charles Salt Descriptions
Samples were caught in 30' intervals from 9440' MD to 9619' MD (Mission
Canyon Top) by manually collecting rock cuttings in sample bags.
Samples in the base of section contained abundant amounts of halite that
was off-white, semi-translucent to translucent and frosty, with coarse grains.
The limestone/dolomite in the samples was mudstone, light to medium
gray, very fine crystalline, slightly soft to occasionally firm, and occasionally
silted. Anhydrite in the formation remained predominantly white to off white with
occasional graying, microcrystalline, very soft but occasionally firm, amorphic
and massive.
Mission Canyon Formation
The Mission Canyon formation top was picked from samples at 9619' MD, 9618'
TVD, (-7203' SS). Formation background gas through the Mission Canyon varied from
15 to 40 units.
Mission Canyon Formation Lithology Descriptions
Composite samples were caught in 30' intervals from 9619' MD to 10164'
MD (Lodgepole Formation Top) through the Mission Canyon formation by
manually catching rock cuttings and collecting them in sample bags.
Limestone in the samples was light to dark gray, gray/brown, sub-blocky,
firm, argillaceous mudstone to wackestone, with trace microcrystalline limestone.

Lodgepole Formation
The Lodgepole top was picked from samples at 10164' MD, 10163' TVD, (-7748'
SS). The kick-off depth of 10495' MD was reached on September 8, 2014.
Lodgepole Formation Lithology Descriptions
Samples were caught in 30' intervals from 10163' MD to 11017' MD (False
Bakken Top) by manually catching rock cuttings and collecting them in sample
bags.
The Lodgepole samples were predominately comprised of argillaceous
limestone that ranged from being a mudstone to a wackestone, medium to dark
gray in color, mainly very fine crystalline, firm-hard with occasional laminations,
very earthy textures, and no visible porosity with differing amounts of silt through
the formation. Slow blue white cuts were observed but were possibly due to
residual invert drilling fluid.
False Bakken Shale
The False Bakken top was picked from samples, gamma ray, gas shows and
penetration rate at 11017' MD, 10913' TVD, (-8498' SS). Dull, even yellow/orange
fluorescence was observed in the samples when viewed under a fluoroscope. Cuts in
the False Bakken were moderately fast blue/white but were heavily contaminated by
invert drilling fluid.
False Bakken Shale Lithology Descriptions
Samples were caught from 11017' MD to 11045' MD (Upper Bakken Top)
in 30' intervals through the False Bakken formation by manually catching rock
cuttings and collecting them in sample bags. Samples were comprised of mainly
shale and limestone.
Shale in the False Bakken was mostly black, fissile in part, hard to brittle,
blocky-sub blocky, earthy textured with occasionally waxy surfaces. Limestone
returns were mudstone-wackestone, medium to dark gray with occasional
laminations, predominantly massive, platy-to sub platy, very earthy textured with
no visible porosity and occasional trace amounts of pyrite.

Middle Bakken Dolomite
The Middle Bakken top was picked from sample lithology, gamma and
penetration rate at 11078' MD, 10936' TVD, (-8521' SS).
Middle Bakken Lithology Descriptions
Composite samples were caught in 30' intervals through the Middle
Bakken from 11078' MD to 11176' MD (Lower Bakken Top) by manually catching
samples from the shakers.
Middle Bakken dolomite samples were light gray to gray, occasionally tan,
light gray brown, and mottled gray and light brown. Cuttings were blocky to sub-
platy, soft to firm, and microcrystalline to fine crystalline with a chalky matrix.
Samples were frequently argillaceous to silty, occasionally grading to sandy.
Cuttings were tight, exhibiting poor visible porosity. Even dull yellow/orange
fluorescence was observed in nearly all samples when examined under a
fluoroscope. Moderately slow to fast milky cuts were noted throughout the Middle
Bakken.

Lower Bakken Shale Lithology Descriptions
Composite samples were caught from 11176' MD to 11199' MD (Three
Forks Top) in 30' intervals through the Lower Bakken by manually catching
samples from the shakers. Samples were comprised of shale.
Shale in the Lower Bakken was black to dark gray, blocky to sub-blocky,
firm, moderately to very calcareous, waxy, and carbonaceous. No oil staining
was observed. Poor visible porosity was present. Even yellow/orange
fluorescence and slow, faint milky bright blue/white cuts were observed when
examined under a fluoroscope, most likely the result of invert drilling fluid in the
cuttings.
Three Forks Formation
The Three Forks top was picked from Gamma and penetration rate at 11199'
MD, 10970' TVD, (-8555' SS), one foot shallow to the estimated prognosis TVD. Gas
reached a maximum formation reading of 854 units.
Three Forks Lithology Descriptions
Composite samples were caught in 30' intervals through the Three Forks
target from 11285' MD to 20712' (MD/TD).
Three Forks dolomite samples were light gray to gray brown, cream,
occasionally tan, blocky to sub-platy, moderately firm, argillaceous to silty with a
chalky matrix. A few packstone to wackestone interbedded markers throughout
the center of the Three Forks target were used for dip correlation, and desired
target was two feet above the low-count gamma packstone in the bottom of
target which was the most distinctive maker used for dip correlation.
Milled flakes were commonly observed higher in the formation with thin
laminations observed in cuttings. There were also traces of pyrite throughout the
target zone. Cuttings exhibited no to poor visible porosity. Even yellow/orange
fluorescence was observed in samples when examined under a fluoroscope.
Fast milky cuts were noted throughout the Three Forks. Fluorescence and cuts
later in the lateral were most likely the result of petroleum based lube sweeps
used during slides which increased contaminated false flash fluorescence.
Closing Summary
The Joel Goodsen 6-32-29H was drilled from south to north in the Three Forks
Formation. The target consisted of 10 to 13 vertical feet, starting three feet below the
Three Forks top. The well bore is estimated to have been in target zone 97% of the

lateral. The overall dip was 88.0°. Surveys were taken throughout the turn in the lateral
at 30' intervals to 360º then in 90' intervals until total depth (TD).
Formation dip was estimated at 89.6° in the beginning of the lateral between the
intermediate casing shoe at 11285' MD to 12700' MD. This section had an average of
11% C3 and 4% C4 with background gas averaging from 50 to 1000 units increasing as
the wellbore traveled down in target, traversing the target zone before 13000' MD. At
13000' MD dip increased a half a degree to an estimated 90.17° with gas readings
ranging from 400 to 1200 units with an average of 15% C3 and 7% C4. Dip continued at
90° until 15600' MD where formation dip showed at 89.6°.
The well bore was primarily in the lower half of target zone during this interval. At
16700' MD overall dip increased to 90.7° until 19100' MD. Gas reading in this section
were from 200 to 800 units averaging 400 units with C3 averaging 30% and C4 2% to
10%, as the wellbore transitioned the entire target zone.
At 19100' MD dip decreased significantly, averaging 88.8° until TD was reached
at 20712' MD. Background gas averaged 100 to 400 units with 700 unit shows while
transitioning from the upper to lower half of target. Heavy gasses of C3 averaged 20%
and C4 10% with the well bore predominately in the top of the target zone.
Nearly all samples exhibited spotty to even yellow/orange fluorescence when
examined under a fluoroscope. Oil cuts were commonly slow to moderately fast, diffuse
to slow streaming and light blue in color, leaving occasional residual rings. Some
samples were contaminated with petroleum-based lubricant, which was used to achieve
adequate bit weight to drill and slide. This contamination masked some fluorescence
and oil cuts in some of the samples.
After total depth of 20712' (MD/TD) was reached on September 22, 2014, Brara
Geologic Consulting remained onsite to monitor gas while short-tripping back to the
shoe and returning to bottom. A final 1441 unit trip gas reading was detected upon
circulating bottoms up. On September 22, 2014 Brara Geologic Consulting was
released from the Joel Goodsen 6-32-29H location while the rig was preparing to run
production casing.
Target interpretation: starting at the top two feet of target, with 70 to 90 API count
gamma readings, samples were 50% translucent and very fine grained with light and
dark brown stain. Scattered very bright yellow orange mineral fluorescence was only
observed in this top section. Light yellow brown oil was commonly liberated from the
samples and seen as suspended drops in the drilling fluid.
Samples increased in chalky texture as the wellbore dropped and the gamma
climbed to the hottest peak in the target zone (120 to 150 API gamma) approximately
two to four feet from the top. Samples gathered during this peak showed mottling and

was predominately light gray with very little to fine grain or staining. A thin, very hard,
white cryptocrystalline layer was observed and associated with the tip of peak.
Gamma readings dropped back down to 80 to 90 counts an estimated four to six
feet from the top in, what is further referred to in this report as, the saddle. Light blue-
gray microcrystalline texture with disseminated pyrite was seen at the top of the saddle
with a very fine grain translucent grain in the middle of saddle. This very fine grain had

darker blue gray than other lithologies observed above with abundant pyrite. This lower
portion was noticeably harder than other lithologies associated with the saddle.
Between the saddle and the clean marker a thin, very well cemented, no porosity
sandstone, which was poorly sorted with fine and coarse grains. Very light brown lithic
fragments and carbonaceous inclusions were observed, the first carbonaceous samples
observed in the lateral.
A 60 count gamma marker was located approximately six to eight feet below the
top of the target. Samples observed in this lithology were predominately very fine
grained, stained very light brown. Sucrosic texture observed during the lateral was
unique to this minor lithology and was rarely observed. Thin black carbon was
commonly observed and occasionally was moderately firm and malleable in parts. This
middle clean also had traces of light green and green yellow cryptocrystalline dolomite
that were not reactive to fluorescence. Very light yellow brown oil was observed in the
drilling fluid and was liberated from samples during cleaning. Background gas doubled
in this middle section.
The lowest section, eight to 11 feet from the top of target, exhibited 70 to 90 count
gamma. No blue-gray coloring in samples was observed below the clean marker
described above. Samples exhibited off-white and brown/gray coloring which darkened
towards the bottom of the target zone. Loose disseminated pyrite was present at the top
of this section. Gas readings were the highest in while drilling at the bottom of zone,
though very low concentrations of C4 averaging three to five percent of total gas shows
was recorded.
Conclusions
1) The Emerald Oil, Inc. Joel Goodsen 6-32-29H was a successfully drilled three-
run horizontal well in the Three Forks Formation from a surface location in
Section 32, T 149 N, R 102 W (370' FSL & 1150' FEL) to a total depth (TD) of
20712' MD, 11011' TVD, (-8596' SS), with a bottom hole location in Section 29, T
149 N, R 102 W (25'2 FNL & 1264' FEL) in McKenzie County, North Dakota. The
well was drilled from the intermediate casing shoe to TD with two trips and a
sidetrack from the Upper Bakken Shale. Pulling back from the shale strike at
11577' MD to the sidetrack point prompted a short trip to the casing shoe. Slow
ROP prompted replacement of the BHA at 14203' MD. Total depth was reached
9
10
at 21:15 on September 21, 2014. The Joel Goodsen 6-32-29H required 22 total
days of drilling and setting intermediate casing.
2) Ultraviolet sample shows in this lateral were slow light blue cuts with residual
rings. Total gas shows were poor to fair ranging from 200 to 500 unit average to
between 1000 and 1200 unit average. Overall porosity was very poor. Wellbore
placement was within the target zone for approximately 98% of the total 9427
feet of lateral section, which should allow for excellent production opportunities.

Filter Socks and Other Filter Media

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
