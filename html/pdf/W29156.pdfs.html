Well Evaluation
Hanson 11X-12A [NW NW Section 12, T144N-R96W] is located approximately 6 miles south
west of Killdeer in Dunn County, North Dakota. The Hanson 11X-12A is a double-section
horizontal Middle Bakken well within the Williston Basin consisting of one lateral drilled to the
south. It expands the existing Murphy Creek Field. The curve would be kicked off in the Lodgepole
and landed within the Middle Bakken Formation. Directional drilling and geo-steering techniques
would then be used to maintain maximum exposure to the target zone.
GEOLOGY
Lithology
Lithologic descriptions and sample analysis began in the Charles Formation in order to log the
Base Last Salt for curve landing purposes.
The Middle Bakken Formation was logged at 10,758' TVD (-8,424' MSL). It consists primarily
of silty sandstone. Samples show trace to rare amounts of spotty to even brown oil staining. The
Middle Bakken was the target reservoir for the lateral portion of the well.
Gas Monitoring
Drilling fluid was circulated through the gas buster starting from 15,450' MD until TD.
Background gas fluctuated between 60u-1,700u and produced intermittent flares ranging from 1'-
20' high. The entire ideal target zone was observed to contain moderate gas concentrations and
consistent oil staining. The highest gas value recorded was a show at 16,521' MD of 3,235u
correlating with the top half of the target zone (Figure 1).
Figure 1. Profile of the Hanson 11X-12A well with background gas in the range of 60-1,700 units. (Miles Anson, Anson Geophysical well site
geologist)
Curve Landing
Data including rate of penetration (ROP), torque, Measurement-while-drilling (MWD), and
lagged samples were used to determine marker and formation tops during the vertical hole.
During both the vertical and curve, tops were compared to our primary offset wells, in order to
ensure a successful landing in the target zone. The true vertical depth (TVD) of our initial
landing target (10,773' TVD) was calculated by assuming comparable formation thicknesses
from the Base Last Salt to the landing target gamma signature. Directional successfully landed
the curve at 10,776' TVD.
Geosteering
The ideal target zone was defined as a 10' interval of silty sandstone, the top of which was initially
set at 10,771' TVD, 13' below the base of the Upper Bakken Shale (Figure 2). Offset wells
indicated that the Middle Bakken would be up-dipping approximately 0.37° from entry of the
formation to TD. The final gross overall change in TVD from entry of the Middle Bakken
Formation to the end of the lateral was estimated at -40' over 9,737' (0.24° up, Figure 2). The
bottom-hole location is estimated to be 10,030.53' south & 766.48' west of surface location or
approximately 249.05' FSL & 529.52' FWL, SW SW Section 13, T144N, R96W. Geo-steering
techniques exposed 100% of uncased borehole to the 10' Middle Bakken ideal target. The well
reached a TD of 20,702' on December 16, 2014.
Figure 2. Cross-sectional interpretation of the Hanson 11X-12A well showing high gamma associated with the silty
intervals and lower gamma within the cleaner sandstone intervals. (Miles Anson, Anson Geophysical well site
geologist)
SUMMARY
1)
The XTO Energy, Inc. Hanson 11X-12A was spud on October 13, 2014 and reached TD in
22 days on December 16, 2014.
2)
Samples from the Middle Bakken target consisted of primarily of silty sandstone, with fair
intergranular porosity. Oil shows in samples were visible as spotted trace to rare brown
oil stain yielding encouraging fluorescent cuts.
3)
Moderate gas shows were present throughout the well; most significant was a gas show of
3,235u at 16,521' MD while drilling the lateral.
4)
The Hanson 11X-12A awaits completion operations to maximize its production potential.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
