INTRODUCTION
The SM Energy Company Riley 2B-16HS [NW NE Section 16, T163N, R100W] is
located approximately 5.25 miles northeast of Fortuna in Divide County, North Dakota.
The subject well was designed to drill south at a heading of 178.85°, from [469' FNL and
2,239' FEL] Section 16 to SW SE Section 21, T163N, R100W by the Nabors #687
drilling rig (Figure 1). The Riley 2B-16HS is intended to develop oil production from the
Bakken Formation in the West Ambrose Field.
OFFSET WELL CONTROL
Riley 2B-16HS offset control was provided by the Kaiser-Francis Oil Company, Spyglass
3-8 [SW NE Section 8, T163N, R100W], the SM Energy Company, Wolter 13-9H [SW
SW Section 9, T163N, R100W] and the Louisiana Land & Exploration Co. State Lacher
13-3 1 [NW SW Section 3, T163N R100W] (Figure 2). The Spyglass 3-8 is located
approximately 1.15 miles northwest of the Riley 2B-16HS, and was drilled in January of
2006. The Spyglass 3-8 is a Duperow vertical well and has produced just over 29
thousand barrels over the course of its life. The Wolter 13-9H is located approximately
0.42 miles west-northwest of the Riley 2B-16HS, and was drilled in July of 2011. The
Wolter 13-9H is a Three Forks horizontal well and to date has produced over 264
thousand barrels of oil. The State Larcher 13-3 1 is located 1.54 miles north-northeast of
the Riley 2B-16HS, and was drilled in September of 1985. It is currently a non-producing
Duperow vertical well. Open-hole logs from these wells were used in forecasting
formation tops as well as the landing target for the Riley 2B-16HS. The Wolter 13-9H
was used to provide the closest landing projection through the Middle Bakken.
Figure 2) Map showing the location of the SM Energy Company Riley 2B-16HS and
the location of the three wells used for offset control (NDICOil & Gas Division, Arc
IMS online).
ENGINEERING & OPERATIONS
On November 23rd, 2014, the Riley 2B-16HS was re-entered at 1,562' measured depth
(MD) by Nabors #687. The surface hole was isolated with 9 " surface casing (36# J-
55). The straight hole was drilled to a KOP (kick off point) of 7,420' MD with a Baker 8
¾" PDC dill bit and a Hunting NBR 1.50º mud motor. The curve assembly, comprising
of a NOV 8 ¾" PDC bit and a Sperry 2.25º motor, was tripped in at 7,420' MD and
drilled to landing at 8,215' MD on November 27th, 2014. Diesel invert drilling fluid was
used through the vertical hole and curve build sections (1,562' to 8,215' MD)
maintaining a 9.70-9.90 ppg mud weight for stable hole conditions. Sperry-Halliburton
provided night tour directional drilling and measurement-while drilling (MWD) services.
Surveys from the vertical hole provided tie-in orientation of the wellbore to begin the
curve build and lateral drilling. Sunburst Consulting, Inc. provided onsite geologic
consultation.
After curve landing, 7" intermediate casing (29# HCL-80 and 32# HCL-80) was set and
cemented to 8,184' MD. Drilling fluid was changed over to saltwater ranging between
9.70-9.80 ppg. Lateral operations began at 0400 hrs CST, on November 29th, 2014. The
initial lateral BHA included a Varel 6" PDC bit, a Hunting 1.5° fixed mud motor, and
MWD survey and gamma tools. This BHA drilled from intermediate casing to12,638'
MD before it was tripped out for MWD communication failure. A second identical BHA
was tripped in on December 1st and used to drill the remaining lateral section.
The Riley 2B-16HS was drilled to a total depth (TD) of 17,574' MD, 8,046' true vertical
depth (TVD), on December 3, 2014. Projected to TD, the bottom hole coordinates are
9,885.41' south and 213.59' east of surface location or approximately 215.11' FSL and
2,025.41' FEL SW SE Section 21, T163N, R100W. At TD the wellbore has a closure
distance of 9,887.72' at a closure direction of 178.76°.
GEOLOGIC EVALUATION
Geologic supervision was provided by Sunburst Consulting, Inc. for the Riley 2B-16HS.
The geology team monitored gas, logged drill cuttings, and provided onsite geo-steering
consultation. Sample logging and gas monitoring began at 6,500' MD. The wellsite
geologists caught drill cuttings at 30' time lagged intervals through the curve and 100'
intervals through the lateral. Drill cuttings were examined under a binocular microscope,
and complete lithologic descriptions are provided in the appendix of this report. Dried
cuttings were organized and shipped to SM Energy Company and the North Dakota
Geological Survey. A Pason digital gas detector was interfaced with PASON, an
electronic data recorder (EDR). The PASON system provided hole depth, bit depth and
pump rates to the gas detector and distributed the detector's total gas readings across
location. Total gas was recorded in units (100 units = 1% methane equivalent in air).
Lithology & Hydrocarbons
Sample examination began in the Charles Formation [Mississippian, Madison Group]
above the last Charles salt. The Base of the Last Charles Salt was penetrated at 6,635'
TVD (-4,449'), which was 1' low to prognosis. Salt intervals were marked by an
increase in rate of penetration along with a decrease in weight on bit. Background gas
data above the Base of the Last Charles Salt ranged from 50 to 62 units, and had no
differentiable gas shows.
The Ratcliffe interval of the lower Charles Formation consists of argillaceous limestone,
limestone, dolomitic limestone and anhydrite. The top of the Ratcliffe was penetrated at
6,701' TVD (-4,515'), which was 11' high to prognosis. The Ratcliffe top is within a
limestone facie and just below dolomitic limestone and anhydrite. Gas shows within the
Ratcliffe reached as high as 93 units, and background gases were within 53 and 67 units.
The Mission Canyon Formation [Mississippian, Madison Group] consists of limestone,
argillaceous limestone and dolomitic limestone. The Mission Canyon top was penetrated
at 6,879' TVD (-4,693'), and was 5' high to prognosis. The top of the Mission Canyon
was marked by a transition from argillaceous limestone to a light gray to light brown
limestone, which can be identified by a decrease in gamma. Limestone intervals
throughout the Mission Canyon have dark algal laminations. Gas shows within the
Mission Canyon reached as high as 87 units, and background gases were within 53 and
71 units. Connection gases reached as high as 77 units.
A transition from light brown limestone to a medium to dark brown argillaceous
limestone marked the top of the Lodgepole Formation [Mississippian, Madison Group].
The Lodgepole was penetrated at 7,458' TVD (-5,272'), and was 16' high to prognosis.
The Lodgepole top is further indicated by an increase in rate of penetration and an
increase in gamma. Limestone and argillaceous limestone compose the Lodgepole
Formation. Background gas readings within this interval were 32 to 43 units, with no
significant gas.
Contact with the Upper Bakken Shale [Mississippian] occurred at 7,902' TVD
(-5,716'), and was 3' high to the prognosis. The top of the Upper Bakken Shale was
indicated by sample returns of dark brown to black, carbonaceous, petroliferous and
pyritic shale. Gas shows were as high as 92 units and gamma counts were recorded to
712 API.
The Middle Bakken Member [Mississippian-Devonian] was entered at 7,915' TVD
(-5,729'), and was 2' high to the prognosis. The Middle Bakken top was marked by an
abrupt decrease in both gamma API, and rate of penetration as the wellbore passed from
shale to siltstone and silty sandstone. This silty sandstone was logged as very light gray
to light gray tan in color, calcite cemented and containing trace amounts of disseminated
pyrite (Figure 3). Additionally, trace poor intergranular porosity and trace spotty light
brown oil stain were present.

An interval of highly calcite cemented silty sandstone lies approximately 16' below the
base of the Upper Bakken Shale. Thickness was estimated to be 4' at curve landing. It is
described as very light to medium gray in color, and containing trace amounts of
disseminated pyrite (Figure 4). Also, trace poor intergranular porosity and trace spotty
light brown oil stain were present. Being locally distinct for it's significantly `cooler'
(40-50 API) gamma signature, the base of the highly calcite cemented silty sandstone was
set as the top marker for the Riley 2B-16HS drilling target.
The greatest hydrocarbon shows recorded in the Middle Bakken occurred at the base of
the target interval. These included shows as high as 251 units at 13,776' MD, and
background gas ranging from 6 to 100 units. Connection gas shows at the base of the
target interval reached 341 units at 14,655' MD. Hydrocarbon shows within the low API
or `cool' gamma interval above target were lower on average than has typically been seen
in the West Ambrose Field of Divide County. Gas shows reached 237 units at 8,876' MD
within the low API interval, and connection gases reached 141 units. Background gas
ranged from 6 to about 80 units.

SUMMARY
The SM Energy Company Riley 2B-16HS was re-entered on November 23rd, 2014 by
Nabors #687. It is a two-section Middle Bakken horizontal well within the West
Ambrose Field in Divide County, North Dakota. 96% of the lateral was drilled within the
ideal 8' primary target interval. A mud program consisting of diesel invert (9.70-9.90
ppg) during the vertical and curve build sections, and saltwater (9.70-9.80 ppg) during the
lateral was employed. The target zone was an 8' thick interval that lies beneath a highly
calcareous silty sandstone unit, approximately 24' below the base of the Upper Bakken
Shale. The silty sandstone displayed poor intergranular porosity and trace spotty very
light brown oil staining. Background gas ranged from approximately 6 to about 100
units. The Riley 2B-16HS awaits completion to determine its full production potential.

Lithology
Sunburst Geologists caught lagged samples in 30' intervals through the vertical and curve and 100'
intervals through the lateral. A detailed list of sampling intervals is included in the Well Data Summary
page. Sample or gamma ray marker tops have been inserted in the sample descriptions below for
reference. Samples were examined wet and dry under a stereo zoom Binocular microscope. The drilling
fluid was oil based invert from surface casing to an intermediate casing point of 8,215' MD, and
saltwater brine was used throughout the lateral to a total depth of 17,574' MD. Samples were collected
from 6,500' to 17,574' MD (TD). Two sets of dried samples were boxed and distributed: one to SM
Energy Company and one to the state of North Dakota.

North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.

states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
