INTRODUCTION
Located in the Sandrocks field, Buffalo Wallow play, Hess Corporation's BW-Arnegard State-
151-100-3625H-6 [SW SW Sec. 36, T151N, R100W] oil well is approximately 3 miles north of
Arnegard, North Dakota. It is planned as a double-section horizontal oil well that will target the
Middle Member of the Bakken Formation. This is the first lateral drilled to total depth in a
northern direction by Precision Drilling rig #564 on this location (Figure 1), and fifth for the pad.
Previously, the BW-Arnegard State-151-100-3625H-2 well was drilled to total depth nearby.
This well, along with Pioneer Production Corp.'s State 1-36, was used for close correlation for
the subject well curve landing and lateral expectations.
GEOLOGIC EVALUATION
A two person geology team from Sunburst Consulting, Inc. provided well site geologic
supervision and logged drill cuttings, monitored gas, and provided geosteering services
throughout the drilling operation. Wellbore landing into the target zone was maximized by
collaboration and communication between the Hess operations geologist, the Baker Hughes
directional team, and the well site geologists.
Lithology
The Middle Bakken was the target formation for lateral production for this well and the in-depth
lithology evaluation will be focused here. All details, including formation tops for the vertical
portion of the well, can be found in addendum to this evaluation. The Middle Bakken was
encountered at 11,449' MD (11,166' TVD) with a marked decrease in gamma and ROP. A
brownish gray-tan to off white siltstone found at the top of the Middle Bakken Member is
Figure 2. Sample cuttings of the Upper Bakken Shale (left), and within the Middle Bakken lateral (right).
estimated to be around 3-5' thick. Samples below this siltstone are a light to medium gray
calcitic silty sandstone. The silty sandstone is very fine grained, moderately well sorted,
moderately well cemented with calcite cement, has an earthy to granular texture, and contains
traces of disseminated pyrite and intergranular porosity. Traces of brown, spotty oil staining
were also seen in the Middle Bakken samples throughout the lateral as shown in Figure 2.
Middle Bakken samples varied only slightly in color, cementing, and rounding as drilling
commenced through the lateral. The lower half of the Middle Bakken in this area has a
characteristic gamma signature that reads a somewhat high, tight range of API units, typically
between 90 and 100, compared to the high-low gamma signature that varies from 60 to 130 units
found in the top portion of the target zone. This tighter, less variable gamma is often referred to
as the Lower Middle Bakken Facies. This rock is less porous, and therefore, more compacted,
and shows some bioturbation.
Hydrocarbon Shows
Background gases throughout the lateral were quite high, averaging around 4,000 units. There
were common gas shows ranging from 5,000 units to some over 7,000 units (Figure 3). The gas
buster was used throughout the lateral, and there was typically a flare present that ranged from
2'-10'. The gas on this well was significantly higher than the gas levels on nearby offsets.

John Dye, Sunburst Consulting, Inc.).
Geosteering
The BW-Arnegard State-151-100-3625H-6 lateral was drilled along the western hardline of the
two section drilling unit. The lateral was expected to have an overall 0.2° up dip to TD. The
prognosis landing target was at 11,147' TVD, which was moved down to a final adjusted target
of 11,178' TVD during curve operations. The curve was landed high, approximately 5' above the
top of the target window, at 11,170' TVD. After the curve was landed, casing would not reach
bottom, so casing was landed early at 11,376', leaving 73' of open-hole shale exposure during
the drilling of the lateral. This open hole resulted in shale being present in some samples
throughout the lateral. Upon drilling out of casing the wellbore was brought down into the target
window, below the target line. The wellbore followed an up and down pattern throughout the
lateral, providing repeat sections which were good for constraining dip, despite not having
especially distinctive markers. Dip was initially near flat, beginning to up dip at ~0.2° up from
2,500' VS to 7,200' VS, where it flattened to approximately 0.1° to TD (Figure 4). At 5,300'-
5,600' VS very high gamma which indicated proximity to the shale was logged, but the quality
of this gamma ray is in question, and shale was never logged in sample, though a significant
increase in penetration rate was observed. Another trip at 6,400' VS was made and the new
gamma ray tool that was picked up read approximately 30 API cooler than the previous tools.
This made correlation with earlier gamma difficult. At ~7,700' VS the wellbore began to climb
through the target zone and out the top, and was difficult to roll over and bring back down. Prior
to a trip at 9,000' VS, high gamma ray was again encountered, but could not be reconciled with a
shale strike due to the shape of the wellbore. Re-logging the gamma with the new tool confirmed
that the high gamma was incorrect and that the Upper Bakken Shale was not encountered.
Figure 4. Gamma ray vs. vertical section for the BW-Arnegard State-151-100-3625H-6 lateral (as represented
in TerraVu, Terra Domain Consulting).
Gamma ray readings within the target zone were erratic, leaving only a few markers to calculate
formation dip. The use of three different gamma ray tools also made correlation difficult since
their readings were significantly different.
The overall apparent formation dip was 0.13° up, losing approximately 20.6' TVD, which is
somewhat flatter than surrounding offsets and structure map. The BW-Arnegard-151-100-
3625H-6 was drilled to total depth at 21,078' MD in 20 drilling days. The bottom hole location is
9,974.16' N and 339.71' W of the surface location or approximately 303.16' FNL & 609.71'
FWL, NW NW Sec. 25, T151N, R100W. Overall the lateral was drilled 69% within the target
window.
Figure 5. Sunburst Consulting, Inc. dip profile of the BW-Arnegard State-151-100-3625H-6 lateral based off of
changes in gamma, ROP, and gas values.
SUMMARY & CONCLUSIONS
The formations encountered during the vertical portion of the BW-Arnegard-151-100-3625H-6
were encountered 30' low to the prognosis. This is likely due to a pipe tally error. The lateral
section of the well was drilled at or above the target window, with one near shale strike. After the
near-miss, targets were lowered and the wellbore remained low in target in the latter half of the
lateral, before coming out the top of zone and then dipping back below zone at TD. Multiple
gamma ray tools made correlation of gamma throughout the lateral difficult. The overall
formation dip was consistent with expectations based on the other wells drilled previously on
location. Background gas and gas shows on this well were quite good, and this is likely owing to
the number of wells in the area that have already been stimulated. The poor gamma ray
correlation and spotty markers made steering a challenge, and also may have affected the quality
of the interpretations. That being said gross apparent dip varied minimally from expectations.
Overall the BW-Arnegard-151-100-3625H-6 was drilled successfully and shows good potential
for production.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
