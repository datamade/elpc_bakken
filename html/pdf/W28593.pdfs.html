1.0) Well Introduction and Location
1.1.1) Well Overview
Zavanna, LLC Simmental 2-11 3H [SW SE Section 35, T154N, R99W] is located approximately
14 miles southeast of Williston in Williams County, ND. The Simmental 2-11 3H is a horizontal
Middle Bakken well within the Williston Basin consisting of one lateral drilled towards the
south. Wellsite geologists will begin logging during vertical hole drilling, just above the Base
Last Salt marker in order to gather data to ensure a curve landing within the desired target. The
curve will be kicked off in the Lodgepole and landed in the Middle Bakken. Seven-inch
intermediate casing will then be set to isolate the target reservoir, after which directional drilling
and geo-steering techniques will be used to maintain maximum exposure to the target zone.

The TVD of the landing target, which represents the center of the target zone, was calculated by
assuming comparable formation thicknesses (isopach) between the subject well and offset wells
from the Base Last Salt Marker to the top of the target (Figure 4). As drilling progressed through
the various formation tops and markers, this target was continually monitored and updated as

after the final Lodgepole marker was logged. The curve was landed just above this at a depth of
11,151' TVD and 11,465' MD. (Figure 2)

2.0) Wellsite Geology observations and Geo-steering
2.1.1) Lithology
Lithologic descriptions and sample analysis began in the Charles Formation in order to log the
Base Last Salt for target landing purposes.
The late Devonian-early Mississippian Bakken Formation is thought to be a result of a
transgression-regression-transgression sequence, and is recognized as the start of the primary oil-
producing zones. There are three members to the Bakken Formation: 1) the Upper Bakken
member represents an anoxic deep water anoxic depositional environment 2) the Middle Bakken
member represents a high energy, shallow marine depositional environment 3) the Lower Bakken
member also represents an anoxic deep water marine depositional environment.
These interpretations are supported by the lithologic make up of each member. The Upper and
Lower Bakken members are described as black, organic-rich pyrite bearing shales. Both these
members are recognized as the source rocks for the petroleum system. The Middle Bakken
member is the target zone for oil-producing zone, and recognized as the reservoir for the
petroleum system. The Middle Bakken member is comprised of varying lithology. Samples taken
while drilling the Simmental 2-11 3H well were described in stratigraphic order as follows: 1)
light to medium gray very fine grained siltstone containing trace to occasional disseminated
pyrite as well as trace to rare light to medium oil-staining (Picture 3), 2) white to off white lime
packstone with microcrystalline to very fine crystalline texture, trace intercrystalline porosity,
and trace to rare yellow to medium brown oil-staining (Picture 4), 3.) cream to medium gray very
fine grained silty sandstone with dolomitic cement, trace intergranular porosity, trace to common
disseminated and nodular pyrite and trace to occasional light to medium brown oil-staining
(Picture 5). It is also important to note loose quartz grains observed in samples taken just below
the packstone, and were described as transparent to frosted in color, ranging from subangular to
rounded in shape.
2.2.1) Gas Monitoring
The overall gas trend of this well suggests the gas was the highest in the areas proximal to or
within the ideal target zone. Background gas averaged 3,800 units, with gas shows ranging from
approximately 3,500-8,000 units while drilling within this area compared to an average
background gas of 2,100 units, with gas shows ranging from approximately 1,300-3,100 units in
the areas above and below the ideal target zone. The largest gas reading was near end of the
lateral, just above target zone, and measured as 8,408 units at a measured depth of 21,304'.

2.2.2) Gas and Oil Shows Within the Vertical and Curve Drilling
The background gas averaged 29 units in the vertical Simmental 2-11 3H. The first significant
gas show for this well was observed while drilling the vertical hole at ~10,324'MD, and
measured 116 units. The spot sample caught at this gas show was described as a light to medium
brown microcrystalline mudstone limestone with earthy to mottled texture, rare algal material
and rare sparry calcite. It was located within the Mission Canyon Formation interval
approximately 141'MD above the Lodgepole Formation.
The next significant gas shows came while building the curve, and were fracture gas shows
observed from the False Bakken through the Upper Bakken interval that ranged from 668-2,705
units. Background gas for this interval averaged 604 units, up from the background gas average
of 32 units within the Lodgepole Formation.
It is also important to note that during gas monitoring operations, after the completion of drilling,
green oil was observed coming over the shakers (Picture 2). This oil show occurred during the
reamer run at ~19,845'MD, and coincided with a 6-10' intermittent flare.
Picture 2. .Simmental 2-11 3H green oil observed at shakers during reamer run after drilling was completed. (Jenn Torres, Sunburst wellsite
geologist)
2.3.1) Geo-steering by Gamma Signature
The Middle Bakken of the Simmental 2-11 3H is estimated to be 41' in thickness, and contains an
apparent low gamma marker (lime packstone) (Figure 4). The target zone was defined by Sunburst well-
site geologists and the Zavanna prospect geologist as 4' thick, and is noted in the below figure. The target
interval was based primarily on a slightly higher porosity zone and the ease of geo-steering.
Figure 4. Offset gamma signatures from the Simmental 2-11 2TFH for the Simmental 2-11 3H (Beverly Faraday, Sunburst wellsite geologist)
As drilling progressed it became apparent the Middle Bakken had localized thickness changes.
This interpretation is supported by the movement of the wellbore in relation to its change in TVD
as it passed through the gamma signatures defining the ideal target zone. These thickness
changes did not last for long course lengths, and were therefore determined to be localized within
the formation.

Offset wells indicated that the Middle Bakken structure should drop 20' from curve landing to
TD. However, gamma signatures and sample analysis suggest that the formation had an initial
structural gain of approximately 26' (TVD) over the first 4,710' (MD) of the lateral. This yielded
an apparent up dip 0.32° for this interval of the well. Over the next 5,243'(MD) of the lateral,
gamma signatures and sample analysis suggest that the formation had a structural drop of
approximately 40' (TVD), yielding an apparent down dip of 0.44° for the second half of the well.
Over the entire 10,610'-long lateral, there was an estimated drop in TVD of 15', yielding an
apparent down dip of 0.08°. Geo-steering techniques exposed 100% of the borehole to the
Middle Bakken, with 49% of the borehole exposure being within the 4' ideal target zone. The
well reached a TD of 21,490' on November 8, 2014.

3.0) Summary
The Zavanna, LLC Simmental 2-11 3H was spud on July 21, 2014, and reached TD in 23 total
drilling days on November 8, 2014. Samples from the Middle Bakken target indicated a siltstone,
lime packstone and silty sandstone of variable oil staining. The oil staining was described as
trace to occasional yellow to medium brown, and in some cases contained visible trace
intercrystalline and intergranular porosity. The vertical drilling yielded one significant gas show
of 166 units, which was observed within the Mission Canyon Formation. The curve drilling
revealed one significant interval of fracture gas shows from the False Bakken through the Upper
Bakken Shale. MWD gamma ray markers, sample analysis, and ROP were successfully utilized
to expose 100% of the lateral borehole to the Middle Bakken. The Simmental 2-11 3H awaits
completion operations to maximize its production potential.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
