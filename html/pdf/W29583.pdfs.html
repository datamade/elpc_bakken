Well Evaluation
Satter 21X-1F [Lot 3 Section 1, T150N, R98W] is located approximately 5 miles
northeast of Watford City in McKenzie County, ND. The Satter 21X-1F is a horizontal
Three Forks well within the Williston Basin consisting of one lateral drilled toward the
south. The curve will be kicked off in the Lodgepole and landed in the Three Forks.
Directional drilling and geo-steering techniques will then be used to maintain maximum
exposure to the target zone

GEOLOGY
Lithology
Lithologic descriptions and sample analysis began in the Charles Formation in order to
log the Base Last Salt for landing target purposes.
The Three Forks Formation was logged at 11,079' TVD (-8,997' MSL). It consists
primarily of dolomite with laminated intervals of pale green shale and contains trace to
rare amounts of spotty, medium brown oil stain. The Three Forks was the target reservoir
for the lateral portion of the well.
Gas Monitoring
A possible fracture gas of 3,707u was recorded while drilling the curve at 10,707' MD,
which is consistent with a similar gas show seen while drilling the Satter 21X-1C at
10,718' MD. The highest gas observed while drilling the vertical portion of the well was
182u at 10,272' MD. The highest gas value recorded for the well was a down time gas of
3,781u off the gas buster at 17,140' MD and a trip gas of 1,800u at 17,614' MD on the
gas buster accompanied by a 10-15' flare. Background gas fluctuated between 80-400u
throughout the lateral section while circulating through the gas buster (~75% of the
lateral) and was rarely accompanied by a 1-2' intermittent flare. Background gas off the
buster showed values ranging from 500-1,600u. The entire ideal target zone was observed
to contain moderate gas concentrations and consistent oil staining. The highest gas
concentrations were seen in the upper 10 feet of the ideal target zone (Figure 2).

Geo-steering
The ideal target zone was defined as a 19' interval, the top of which was initially set at
11,094' TVD, 20' below the base of the Lower Bakken Shale (Figure 1). The
superposition of a cool gamma marker reading 45-50 API just below a warm gamma
marker reading 100-115 API ~12' into the Three Forks target zone proved to be the most
useful for geo-steering. There was also a natural deflection point approximately 10-11'
from the top of the target zone which also aided in geo-steering, though it was not as
consistent as the gamma ray markers. Offset wells indicated that the Three Forks would
be dipping up slightly from landing to TD. The final gross overall change in TVD from
landing to the end of the lateral was estimated at 18.5' over 9,623' (0.11° up, Figure 3).
The bottom-hole location is estimated to be 9,986.33' south & 31.23' west of surface
location or approx. 257.51' FSL & 2,601.23' FWL, SE SW Sec. 12, T150N, R98W. Geo-
steering techniques exposed 83% of uncased borehole to the 19' Three Forks ideal target
and the well reached a TD of 20,875' on January 8, 2015.

SUMMARY
The XTO Energy, Inc. Satter 21X-1F was spud on October 26, 2014. Trinidad 226
moved onto the location November 26 and re-entered surface on December 21. TD was
reached in 22 total drilling days on January 8, 2015. The correlation of the gas shows
seen between 10,700' and 10,720' MD in both the Satter 21X-1F and Satter 21X-1C
suggests evidence of a possible fracture zone in the Lodgepole formation and will be
watched closely when drilling the Satter 21X-1B on this pad. Samples from the Three
Forks dolomite and laminated shale target contained oil staining that was described as
trace to rare with visible intercrystalline porosity. MWD gamma ray markers, sample
analysis, and ROP were successfully utilized to expose 83% of the lateral borehole to the
ideal Three Forks target. The Satter 21X-1F awaits completion operations to maximize its
production potential.

Filter Socks and Other Filter Media

Oil and Gas Wells
Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
