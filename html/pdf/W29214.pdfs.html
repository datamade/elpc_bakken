1.0 INTRODUCTION
RUUD 5493 41-23 6T is a North lateral Three Forks 1st Bench well, with a surface location of
235' FSL- 521' FWL T154N R93W Sec 23, Mountrail County, North Dakota. The primary pay
zone extended from 25.5' to 36.5' below the top of the Three Forks 1st Bench unit. This pay
zone extended 3' into the Claystone 1 unit. This pay zone was selected due its drilling
properties, its reliable gamma markers, its production potential, and quality of reservoir rock.
The objective was to steer the well within the defined pay zone and within the legal requirements
of the state. Directional and MWD services were performed by Pathfinder/Schlumberger. Chris
Haynes, Ben Huffman, and Justin Ferguson were the well site geologists providing geo-steering
and mud logging services. In the vertical portion of the well Chris Haynes, and Beach Hastings
provided logging services, and steering through the curve, all from Columbine Logging, Inc.

2.1.1 Geosteering
Columbine Logging arrived on location and rigged up for vertical drilling operations to provide
mud logging and geosteering throughout the curve and lateral. Our primary offset GR TVD logs
were from the RUUD 5493 42-23H well, located in 154N 93W 23 SE SW Mountrail County
ND. From this offset log a target zone of 11' was determined to be 25.5' to 36.5' below the top
of the Three Forks 1st Bench member. Within the lateral, the primary objective was to drill
within the "TVD drilling window" defined by the 11 foot section identified above. Gamma
markers were well defined throughout most of the well. An expected gamma cross section was
provided by Nathan Gabelman from Oasis Petroleum and can be seen as appendix B.

The primary marker targeted was located 3' above the E marker. This target was
identified in gamma as a localized "bump" raging from 55 to 65 api. This area was bracketed
above by an easily identifiable low then immediate high gamma sequence. The high with the
sequence was approximately 85-95api, with the corresponding low raging approximately 50-60
api. This sequence was identified as being the D marker. Below the target is also bracketed by
an extended area of low gamma. This gamma ranging from 40-50 api, and is identified as the E
marker. Below the E marker, is an area identified as the Claystone1 top. The window extended
3' into the claystone. The gamma values associated with this boundary range from 120-160 api.
This is identified as the F marker and the bottom of the TVD drilling window. The bracketing
characteristics of the target area, made the target window easily identifiable. Throughout the
lateral the only major change in lithology was associated with crossing the Claystone 1 unit
boundary. The major difference in lithology seen was a shift from the arenaceous, moderate to
poorly cemented, possibly dolomitic in nature, siltstone, seen through most of the drilling
window, i.e. D and E markers, to the argillaceous, poorly cemented, well sorted, grey in color,
claystone associated with the F marker. These slight differences in lithology were used to help
correlate the location within the target window but generally were not distinct enough to specify
exact location alone. Steering in the lateral was accomplished by calculating dip from relevant
gamma markers, as well as by using lithology, and ROP to determine our position within the
formation. The well reached its total depth (TD) approximately 2.5 feet above the center of the
"TVD drilling window".
2.1.2 Gamma and Surveys
Gamma and survey MWD services were provided by Pathfinder. The gamma ray peaked at the
upper extent of the F gamma marker. The F marker is located 8 feet below the top of the TVD
drilling window, at the Claystone1 member top, within the Three Forks Formation. This gamma
was approximately 120-160 api. The lowest values recorded throughout the lateral were
recorded just above the claystone interface. These values were associated with the localized
drop in values identified as being the E marker. The E marker starts approximately 1 foot below
the center of the TVD drilling window. The rest of the drilling window experienced small,
localized spikes and troughs that maintained an easily identifiable, reproducible pattern. While
drilling in the "TVD drilling window" gamma fluctuated primarily between 40 and 160 API.

2.2.1 Sample Examination
Samples were collected by Columbine Logging every 30 feet from 7900' TVD in the vertical to
7" casing point at 10,997' MD 10,656.21' TVD. While drilling the lateral, Columbine Logging
collected samples every 30 feet, when possible, and 60 ft when ROP was above 150 feet per
hour. Sample descriptions included; Lithology, color, firmness, grain size, structure, texture, oil
stain, and hydrocarbon fluorescence. Hydrocarbon fluorescence was determined using a
fluoroscope with a UV lamp. The ratings are based on 7 descriptors: none, slight trace, trace,
fair, moderate, good, and Excellent. The descriptor used is based on the loggers' observations
and best judgment of brilliance, color and longevity of the cut. Acetone was used as a cutting
agent.
2.2.2 Gas Detection
Gas was logged using a Bloodhound total gas chromatograph system. The gas detection system
uses an infra-red detector to measure total gas and the chromatograph separates and measures
gases C1 (methane), C2 (ethane), C3 (propane), iC4 (iso-butane) and nC4 (normal butane). Gas
was recorded in units where 1 unit equals 100 ppm. The gas detection system measured gases:
C1, C2, C3, IC4, NC4, H2S, O2 and CO2, but only C1 through a total value of C4 (iC4+nC4)
were logged.
The Bloodhound Gas Detection and Chromatograph system uses digital signal processing
techniques and non-dispersive infrared and chemical sensors for gas detection. The system uses
a proprietary chromatograph. This chromatograph has the capability to detect from 0 to 10,000
gas units. This translates as 0 to 100% typical naturally-occurring hydrocarbon gas mixtures.
Calibration is performed using National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) traceable
calibration gases. Lab calibration points include 0%, 2.5%, and 100% pure methane. Complete
immunity to saturation or damage in the presence of high concentrations of both light and heavy
hydrocarbon gases precludes the necessity of constant re-calibration or zero referencing. This
allows the Bloodhound to react to hydrocarbon based gases from zero to 100% in concentration
without dilution.
Lag time was approximated from a calculation of annular velocity based on: pump output, open
hole diameter, cased hole diameter, collar diameter, drill pipe diameter and bottom hole
assembly. Connection gases were monitored to confirm lag time calculations and to adjust lag
time when needed.

4.1 Oil Shows
Invert was used as a drilling fluid during the vertical and curve sections of the well. The use of
invert makes oil show calculations impossible during these sections. Oil was extensively present
throughout most of the lateral. Fluorescence in the cuttings was described as: bright to brilliant,
and the associated cuts were described as fast yellow to brown with the occasional bluish white.
The overall oil show was rated on 7 descriptors: none, slight trace, trace, fair, moderate, good,
and Excellent. The descriptor used, are based on the loggers' observations and best judgment of
brilliance, color and longevity of the cut. The descriptors used to describe the speed of the cut
which ranges from very slow to fast. Color descriptors are just that, the color observed by the
logger. During the lateral the speed ranged from moderately fast, to instant, and color was very
dull milky blue-white to dark yellowish brown. The overall ratings observed in the lateral
ranged from moderate to fast very light blue-ish white cut all the way to instantaneous brownish
yellow cut. Oil was consistently visible in the possum belly and on the shakers. Acetone was
used as a cutting solvent.

6.0 WELLBORE
The surface location of RUUD 5493 41-23 6T is 235' FSL- 521' FWL T154N R93W Sec
23, Mountrail County, North Dakota. The ground elevation is 2278' and KB elevation was
2303', referenced to the Kelly bushing of Ensign 158. The curve was landed roughly 4 feet
below the center of the target window, at 11,057'MD; 10,656' TVD, within the Claystone 1 unit
of the Three Forks Formation.
From the 7" shoe to TD the gamma markers throughout the formation
displayed a predominantly consistent pattern. The primary marker targeted was located 3' above
the E marker. This target was identified in gamma as a localized "bump" raging from 55 to 65
api. This area was bracketed above by an easily identifiable low then immediate high gamma
sequence. The high with the sequence was approximately 85-95api, with the corresponding low
raging approximately 50-60 api. This sequence was identified as being the D marker. Below the
target is also bracketed by an extended area of low gamma. This gamma ranging from 40-50 api,
and is identified as the E marker. Below the E marker, is an area identified as the Claystone1
top. The window extended 3' into the claystone. The gamma values associated with this
boundary range from 120-160 api. This is identified as the F marker and the bottom of the TVD
drilling window. The bracketing characteristics of the target area, made the target window easily
identifiable.
On 1/17/2015 at 17238 MD the second trip of the lateral, due to a mud motor failure
began. This trip was concluded when the BHA made it back to the drill floor. Once on the drill
floor it was discovered that the direction mud motor had come apart. This resulted in
approximately 4 feet of the motor, and the drill bit being left in the hole. Oasis engineers then
specified were we would sidetrack in order to avoid the down hole metal debris. 16,854' MD
was selected for the sidetrack operations.
The bit tended to climb throughout most of the well bore, with only a few exceptions.
This tendency to climb forced the directional team to constantly monitor and take corrective
action. The well bore exited the target window on 5 separate occasions. All the exits were out
the top of the window. The exits can be attributed to the bits tendency to climb and the
occasional "bounce" presumably from impacting a more competent material. All of the
excursions out of the TVD target window, we on average approximately 200 feet in length. The
total footage drilled outside the window was 1329'. The total length of the lateral from shoe to
TD was 9521 feet. The total footage drilled outside the window accounted for approximately 14
percent. Thus 86 percent was drilled within the target window. A TD of 20,518' MD, 10,598.5
TVD, and location NWNW 274' FNL, 962' FWL SEC 14-T154N-R93W, Mountrail County, ND,
USA, was reached at 16:09 CST on 1/21/2015.

November 20, 2014 from a surface location of 235' FSL- 521' FWL T154N R93W Sec 23, Mountrail
County, North Dakota. The lateral was drilled to a total depth of 20,518'MD, 10,598.5'TVD,
10060.44 VS, (projected) on January 21, 2015 with a projected bottom hole location of, NWNW 274'
FNL, 962' FWL SEC 14-T154N-R93W, Mountrail County, ND, USA.
The primary pay zone extended 11'from 25.5' to 36.5' below the top of the Three Forks 1st Bench
member. Per the Geo prog provided by Oasis Petroleum the anticipated dip of the formation was
90.30 degrees, based on a subsurface structure map provided by Oasis Petroleum. Our primary offset
was RUUD 5493 42-23H well, located in 154N 93W 23 SE SW Mountrail County ND.
The formation had a fairly consistent dip rate that varied from 89.14 degrees at its lowest, (granted this
was for a very short period and calculation could have been affected by local thinning of the
formation) to a 90.43 at its steepest. Average experienced dip of the formation was 90.31 degrees
which is slightly greater than the expected dip of 90.30 degrees. The total average ROP throughout
the entire lateral was approximately 80.3'/hr while drilling.
The lateral was completed in 3 BHA's. Two directional mud motor failures caused the trips in
order to change BHA's. On 1/17/2015 at 17238 MD the second trip of the lateral, due to a mud motor
failure began. This trip was concluded when the BHA made it back to the drill floor. Once on the
drill floor it was discovered that the direction mud motor had come apart. This resulted in
approximately 4 feet of the motor, and the drill bit being left in the hole. Oasis engineers then
specified were we would sidetrack in order to avoid the down hole metal debris. 16,854' MD was
selected for the sidetrack operations.
This well was completed with 6 BHA's. The 6th BHA was a reaming assembly due to the
sidetrack being difficult to pass. The well reached TD at 20,518' MD, 10,598.5' TVD, 10,060.44 VS
(projected) on January 21, 2015.
Currently the well is awaiting completion.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
