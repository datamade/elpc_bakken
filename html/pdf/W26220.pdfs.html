County, North Dakota. The lateral well was drilled North to South crossing from 250' FNL & 990' FEL of section 2, T151N,

3.2. Hydrocarbon Analysis in the Vertical and Build Section
The first indication of hydrocarbons was observed at around 10567' lagged with a gas show from 200 units,
increasing to around 915 units. From that point on, we had consistent connection gases as well as down time gases
averaging 398 units. The Upper Bakken Shale produced average background gas of 426 units and a max of 907 at
10675'. The Middle Bakken Member produced a background gas averaging 221 units with a max of 828 at 10726'. The
Lower Bakken Shale produced an average gas of 193 increasing to a max of 280 units. Once we entered the Three
Forks our gas averaged 138 units and had a max gas of 254 units.
4.
Lateral Well
This well is comprised of an extended reach single lateral that extends from North to South from the casing point. The
well bore lateral was drilled from a 7" casing point at 10983' MD 10742.4' TVD and terminated at 19276' MD 10757.10' TVD,
giving a total lateral distance of 8293' MD, 100% of which was in the Three Forks Formation. The initial dip was set at 89.60
degrees but once we reached a MD of approximately 12950', we observed a gamma signature that suggested a slight flatter
dip of 89.75. As we drilled from 14000 out to 15500, the gamma signature correlation showed a slightly flatter dip of 89.9.
From 15500 out to 18531 the dip remained at an average of 89.77. At 18531 we TOOH. We were forced to sidetrack at
17901. From the sidetrack out to TD our dip remained around an 89.78 with very little structural variation. We successfully
drilled the entire lateral in three runs over 11 days.

6.
Well Summary
The curve was landed roughly 5 and half feet low of the middle of the target zone. Coming out of casing, we drilled into
the lower target at a projected inclination of 89.6 degrees. For this well we decided to drill the middle of the target zone where
drilling rates averaged 130 to 160 ROP and frequent sliding was not necessary. The DD's did an amazing job avoiding dog
legs early in the lateral. We were able to see decent gamma signatures when transitioning from roughly 2 feet above to 4 feet
below the middle of the target. There was also a great gamma signature roughly two feet above the lower target. The overall
dip of the well averaged out to be an 89.97 degree dip rate with no significant structural variation. There were no shale strikes
during the entire lateral and we were able to complete the lateral in 11 days with three trips and one sidetrack in the lateral.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
