INTRODUCTION
The Oxy USA, Inc. Sharon Rainey 5-17-20H-142-96 [SESE Sec 8 , T142N, R96W] is located
approximately 6 miles Southwest of Manning, North Dakota. The subject well was drilled in Dunn County,
ND by the Nabors X-20 rig in the Manning Field of the Bakken pool. The subject well was planned with
one approximately 9,903' [Intermediate casing to projected total depth (TD)] long southerly lateral bore
with intent to intersect regional hydraulic fracture trends that might enhance reservoir quality within the
Middle Bakken. OXY USA intends to develop oil production in the late Devonian Middle Bakken target
porosity zone with a stratigraphy consisting of siltstone, silty sandstone and limestone with depositional
lithofacies ranging from onshore terrestrial to offshore marine deposits.
ENGINEERING
Surface, Vertical & Curve Operations:
A 9 " surface hole was drilled on October 19th and 20th, 2014 by the Nabors X-20 rig and
surface casing was set at 2,257'. The vertical hole operations commenced on November 11, 2014 with
Weatherford providing equipment and personnel for MWD and directional drilling services. The first
vertical Bottom Hole Assembly (BHA), consisting of MWD tools, 1.50° bend Hunting 7/8 5.7 motor and
Smith MDSi616 BPX PDC bit (6x16 jets), drilled to a depth of 7,865' measured depth (MD) and was
pulled out due to low rates of penetration (ROP). The second BHA had the same bit but a new Hunting
7/8 5.7 motor. This BHA drilled to a kick off point (KOP) of 9,932' MD.
The first curve BHA (BHA #4 overall) consisted of a 2.12º bend Hunting 7/8 5.0 motor, 8 ¾" Ultera
U516M PDC bit, and new MWD tools. This assembly reached a curve TD of 11,088' MD (~10,577' TVD).
This placed the well bore approximately 7' into a 17' thick (shale to shale) Middle Bakken zone, and 2'
into the targeted 6' thick silty sandstone targeted interval. This BHA drilled the 8.5°/100', 1,156' long curve
in 29 hours with no issues.
Directional tools were then pulled out of the hole and a string of 7" intermediate casing was run,
set, and cemented at a depth of 11,063' MD. No E-logs were run due to the close proximity of the Sharon
Rainey 2-17-20H-142-96 and Sharon Rainey 3-17-20H-142-96, one of which drilled all the way through
the Middle Bakken Formation. Lateral operations continued on November 17th, 2014. Diesel invert drilling
fluid was used throughout the vertical hole and curve build sections maintaining a 9.8-10.1 ppg mud
weight for stable downhole conditions.
Lateral Operations:
Weatherford again provided equipment and personnel for MWD and directional drilling services.
The directional drillers, MWD hands, and Payzone geologists worked closely together throughout the
project to make steering decisions. The Inc Sonde tool was paramount in successful steering, especially
during sections where distinct gamma markers were absent.
A five-bladed 6" bit (Hughes T406), 1.50º Hunting mud motor, and MWD tools comprised the first
lateral BHA (5th BHA overall) and was run in the hole on 4" drill pipe starting at 11,088' MD. This BHA
reached a TD of 20,966' in 122.5 hours, making a one run, 5.5 day lateral. Spud to TD was a total of 15
days. The final azimuth was 179.70º, resulting in a closure vertical section of 10,745.45'. The BHL is
10,740.41' South & 329.00' East from surface location or approx. 418.69' FSL & 92.00' FEL SE SE Sec.
20, T142N, R96W (Figure 2).
Salt water drilling fluid was used from the intermediate casing to TD with mud weights throughout
the lateral varying from 9.8-10.1 ppg.

GEOLOGIC EVALUATION
Methods:
Geologic supervision of the well was provided by Payzone Consulting, Inc. with three well site geologists.
Brandon Lutz and Michael Anderson drilled the vertical/curve, and Kevin Jacks joined the team for the
horizontal section. A CanrigTM gas chromatograph was interfaced with a Rigwatch electronic data
recorder system and provided total gas information for viewing around location. Lagged samples were
caught in 30' intervals from 8,000'-10,730' with 10' samples collected when landing the curve from
10,730'-11,088' MD. 100' lagged samples were collected through out the lateral (11,088' -20,966' TD)
Cuttings were examined under a binocular microscope and complete lithologic descriptions are provided
in the lithology document within this evaluation. Two sets of dry cuttings were collected, with one set sent
to the North Dakota Geological Survey Core Library and the second set sent to Jennifer Califf, the project
geologist overseeing operations.

LATERAL GEOLOGY: GEOSTEERING
Stratigraphic Relationships and Gamma Signatures:
The Middle Bakken contained three lithologies with distinct gamma signatures. From the top down they
are siltstone, limestone, and silty sandstone (Figures 8, 9). The limestone appears to shrink and swell
along the lateral section. In places where the limestone is thin, the gap is filled with silty sandstone and
siltstone above it (SR-5). Where the limestone is thick, the siltstone interval thins but is still present (SR-
2).
The 6' target window was established at the base of the limestone (Figure 8). Figure 9 is a geological
summary depicting interval thickness and interpreted lateral gamma signature for the Sharon Rainy 5
Middle Bakken. The main gamma markers used for geo-steering were the packstone/siltstone interval,
the 70's-80's API marker in the center of target, and the 70's gamma marker just below the base of target.
Between these markers, gamma signatures were difficult to discern. However, there is a subtle difference
in the tightness of the data below the center of zone (e.g., Figures 9, 11: 13,600'-14,150' MD; 14,600'-
15,000' MD) and the rattier gamma in the upper half. This pattern has been observed in various other
wells in the area. The low swing of gamma typically observed in the center of target appears to have
disappeared due to the thin (17') Middle Bakken.

Geosteeering and Building Assembly:
The entire well bore was drilled in between the 17' projected shale to shale zone. Well developed gamma
signatures and trends (discussed above) allowed the well site and Houston geologists make steering
decisions, keeping the wellbore in the 6' targeted interval for the majority of the well (97%). Figures 9, 10,
11, and 12 depict the main gamma markers as seen on the Wellsite Horizontal log and Payzone
Interpretive Steer dip profiles, respectively.
Two geosteering profiles were maintained by the well site geologists: Inc Sonde Profile (Figure 12) and
Survey profile (Figure 11). The Inc Sonde profile contains an order of magnitude more data to depict the
pathway of the wellbore. Weatherford's continuous inclination tool provides an update ~ every 10' MD,
while the survey tool only receives measurements ~ every 96'. The tendency of the lateral assembly to
build resulted in surveys that did not accurately depict the wellbore's true pathway. Specifically, inclination
would rise up above dip for the first half of the stand; then directional would slide down to control it. By the
time the stand was drilled to Kelly Down, the survey tool read inclination below dip because it was sitting
at the downward slide. Thus, its reading did not reflect how much TVD the assembly truly built for that
100' MD.
Both profiles used the same gamma correlation. The Inc Sonde Profile resulted in an 85.71' TVD
decrease over 9,892` MD, yielding a gross apparent dip of 90.47. The Survey Profile decreased TVD
64.82' over 9,865' MD, yielding a dip of 90.36. The Inc Sonde Profile provides a much more accurate
depiction of the wellbore's true pathway, making it an important tool for geosteering in thin and relatively
unexplored zones.
Toward the end of the well (17,800'-20,500' MD), it was realized that the assembly was deflecting up off a
hard streak in the center of zone. This corresponded to the changing gamma signature and a change in
samples. This marker began reading in the 90's and samples became better cemented, light grey, more
fissile, and finer grained. They broke off into platy cuttings as opposed to unconsolidated, fine grained silt
and sand.

Hydrocarbons:
During the drilling of the lateral, gas readings were recorded, imported/exported, and analyzed via a
CanrigTM gas detection system. The line was hooked up to the gas sniffer and hard-lined into the base of
the possum belly. This permitted consistent and accurate readings of the gas released from the reservoir.
Background gas ranged from 30-250 units. Connections and gas shows reached as high as 600 units.
Gas shows came at regular intervals (60-90'). However, this may have been the result of gas building up
in the drill string between small releases at connections.
Originally, it appeared that the gas values were higher in the upper half of the target zone (Figure 13;
11,000'-16,000' MD). This hypothesis was not necessarily supported throughout the remainder of the
lateral; the correlation is speculative.

Summary

The X-20 drilling rig successfully drilled a two-section horizontal wellbore within the Middle
Bakken Member of the Bakken Formation at the OXY USA, Inc. Sharon Rainey 5-17-20H-142-96.

A mud program consisting of diesel invert (9.8-10.1 ppg) was used from spud to curve landing
with salt water drilling fluid used from the intermediate casing to 20,966' MD (TD). Salt water mud
(9.8-10.1 ppg) was efficient for maintaining stable down hole conditions and permitted adequate
analysis of gas concentrations in the lateral. Background gas concentrations within the Middle
Bakken ranged from 30-250 units with connections/gas shows as high as 800 units.

Major lithologic/gamma markers used for steering were the limestone/siltstone interval, the center
of target high gamma, and the base of target high gamma. Dip calculations were made based on
the wellbore's repeated intersection with these markers.

The lateral was drilled 100% (9,892' MD) in the 17' shale to shale zone and 97% (9,597' MD) in
the ideal 6' targeted interval. Overall dip of formation depended on which method used to
calculate. Using the Inc Sonde data to plot the wellbore, gross apparent dip was 0.47°. If only
surveys were used to plot the wellbore, overall dip was 0.36°. This can be interpreted as the
result of a building assembly. Surveys were taken at locations where down-slides were placed to
control the build. Thus, inclination data received at these surveys did not represent the true build
of the wellbore.

Samples from the target consisted of white to off-white, light-dark grey, and brown silty
sandstone. The center of target zone, when gamma counts were in the 90's, displayed distinct
light grey color and fissile tenacity. It became finer grained and better cemented. This section
within the target zone is interpreted to be a hard streak off which the wellbore deflected for the
last half of the well. Oil stains were spotty to even and light brown in color.

The Sharon Rainey 5-17-20H-142-96 was a successful drilling effort as measured by gross
exposure to the target. The well will be fitted with a 4 ½" production liner and swell packers in
preparation for a fracture stimulation that will determine the true commercial value of the well.

Sharon Rainey 5-17-20H-142-96 Lithology
Rig crews caught samples in 30' intervals from 8,000' to 10,730', 10' intervals from
10,730' to 11,088', and 100' intervals from 11,088' to 20,966' (TD). Formation tops and
lithologic markers have been inserted into the sample descriptions below for reference.
Sample descriptions began in the Amsden Formation. Samples were examined wet and
dry under a binocular microscope. Quantifiers in order of increasing abundance are
trace, rare, occasional, common and abundant. Subsea is delineated as the negative
numbers within the parentheses located next to the measured depth or true vertical depth.
Sample quality was excellent to fair. Overall, the samples within the Middle Bakken
target consisted of light-dark grey/brown to off white silty sandstone with calcareous
cement, rare disseminated pyrite, and traces of intergranular porosity. Oil stains were
spotty to even and light brown in color.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
