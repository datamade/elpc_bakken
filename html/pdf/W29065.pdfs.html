INTRODUCTION
Located in the Juniper field, Buffalo Wallow play, Hess Bakken Investments II, LLC.'s BW-
Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-3 [SE SW Sec. 35, T149N, R99W] oil well is approximately 9 miles
south of Watford City, North Dakota. It is planned as a double-section horizontal oil well that
will target the Middle Bakken. This is the third lateral drilled to total depth in a northern
direction by Precision Drilling rig #564 on this location. Previously, the BW-Heidi Ho 149-99-
3526H-2 well was drilled to total depth on October 10, 2013. This well will be used for close
correlation for the current Heidi Ho H-3 curve landing and lateral expectations. The Heidi Ho H-
2 lateral runs parallel to the BW-Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-3 lateral.
GEOLOGIC EVALUATION
A two person geology team from Sunburst Consulting, Inc. provided wellsite geologic
supervision and logged drill cuttings, monitored gas, and provided geosteering services
throughout the drilling operation. Wellbore landing into the target zone was maximized by
collaboration and communication between the Hess operations geologist, the Baker Hughes
directional team, and the wellsite geologists.
Lithology
The Middle Bakken Member was the target formation for lateral production for this well and its
lithology will be focused on here. All formations up-hole can be found in addendum to this
evaluation.
The Upper Bakken Shale was penetrated by the bit at 11,292' MD (11,142' TVD). Drilling
rates through the Upper Bakken Shale were high, as were the API gamma counts. Samples taken
from the shale were described as black, petroliferous, earthy, and silty in part; as seen in the left
picture in Figure 2. The samples also contained common disseminated pyrite and traces of
fracture porosity. The Middle Bakken was encountered at 11,337' MD (11,155' TVD) with a
marked decrease in gamma and ROP. A brownish gray-tan to off white siltstone was observed at
the top of the Middle Bakken Member and is estimated around 3-5' thick. Samples below this
siltstone are a light to medium gray poor to well calcite-cemented silty sandstone.
The silty sandstone is very fine grained, with an earthy-granular texture, and contains traces of
disseminated pyrite and intergranular porosity (Figure 2, right picture). Throughout the lateral,
the Middle Bakken samples varied only slightly in color, cementing, and rounding. The lower
half of the Middle Bakken is referred to as the Lower Middle Bakken Facies. This rock is less
porous, and therefore, more compacted and does show some bioturbation. It is marked by a
characteristic "high and tight" gamma signature with little variation, ranging between 75 and 95
API units.
Figure 2. Sample cuttings within the Upper Bakken Shale (left), and of the Middle Bakken (right).
Hydrocarbon Shows
Background gases throughout the lateral ranged from 150 units to over 500 units, averaging
around 400 units. There were common gas shows ranging from 200 units when we were below
zone, to between 600-800 units in zone or above. Gas levels generally were higher when the
wellbore was higher in the target zone. Figure 3, below, shows the gas curve for the extent of
the lateral. Flares ranging from 3'-10' were commonly seen, and the gas buster was used through
the extent of the lateral.
Figure 3. Gas curve vs. vertical section for the BW-Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-3 lateral.
Middle Bakken samples displayed rare brown spotty oil staining in the target zone, with less oil
staining the further below the target zone the wellbore went. Oil staining wasn't noticeably
different when above the target zone.
GEOSTEERING
The BW-Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-3 lateral was drilled parallel to the Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-2
lateral just 50' east, providing good control for the Middle Bakken structure map. The lateral was
expected to dip down 0.4° to 0.6° for the first 6,000'-7,000' of the vertical section. It was then
predicted to nearly flatten, with a projected 0.1° to 0.2° down dip for the remainder of the lateral.
Figure 4. BW-Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-3 Sunburst Consulting, Inc. lateral dip profile with gamma overlay.
The curve was landed near the center of the target window and immediately out of casing the
wellbore rose in section to reveal the majority of the ideal target window. For the first 1,500'
vertical section (VS) of the lateral, the dip trended downward at approximately 0.55°. Formation dip
from 1,500' VS to approximately 3,800' VS trended at an approximate 0.20° down. From 3,800' to
5,500' VS the dip averaged 0.40° down. From 5,500' VS to TD the formation began to flatten, trending at
0.20° down until approximately 8,000' VS, 0.10° down until 9,000'VS, and finally nearly flat with a
slight local up dip the rest of the way to TD. The distinct "high and tight" gamma of the Lower Middle
Bakken Facies was encountered a few times and can been seen at 2,200' VS and again at 6,500' VS until
well TD. Examples of this gamma can be seen in Figure 4. In reference to the currently producing BW-
Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-2 well, formation dips encountered during the Heidi Ho H-3 were locally
steeper; however, the dip profile in Figure 4 closely compares to the Heidi Ho H-2 dip profile in regards
to the VS in which dip changed.
Gamma ray readings within the target zone were erratic, leaving only a few markers to calculate
formation dip. However, the lower Middle Bakken facies, very evident in Figure 4 beyond 6,500' VS,
was a good marker towards the bottom of the target zone. The directional team had a hard time leveling
the wellbore within the target zone. They saw considerable build or drop rates any time they tried to
orientate the bit within the target zone. The decision was made on location to let the BHA cruise slightly
below the target zone to avoid the addition of sizable doglegs to the wellbore.
The overall apparent formation dip of the entire lateral was 0.22° down, with a 38' TVD change
over 9,956' VS. The overall dip varied slightly more than expected from the surrounding offsets
and structure map. The BW-Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-3 was drilled to total depth at 21,420' MD
in 16 drilling days. The bottom hole location is 9,900.49' N and 1,050.11' W of surface location,
or approximately 309.14' FNL & 1329.89' FWL, NE NW Sec. 26, T149N, R99W. Overall the
lateral was drilled 44% within the target window.
SUMMARY & CONCLUSIONS
The formations encountered during the vertical portion of the BW-Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-3
were very close to prognosis depths. The well is the third vertical and curve and second lateral
drilled on this location, with the BW-Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-1 currently producing. During the
lateral section, drilling was very fast. Other than having a hard time staying within the target
zone, lateral drilling went smoothly. Background gases ranged from 150 to 500 units, and
samples throughout had rare spotty brown oil staining.
Drill cuttings within the Middle Bakken target window showed little variation throughout the
lateral. However, there were good gamma ray markers at the top and bottom of the target
window, both of these making for good indication of wellbore positioning, which should prove
useful for the next lateral section on this location. Formation dip varied slightly from
expectations. Overall the BW-Heidi Ho-149-99-3526H-3 was drilled successfully and shows
good potential for production.

Sunburst geologists caught samples in 30' intervals from 8,600' to 11,500' during the vertical and curve, and 100'
intervals from 11,500' to 21,420' TD during the lateral. Formation tops and lithologic markers have been inserted
into the sample descriptions below for reference. Sample descriptions begin in the Mississippian Kibbey formation
and end in the Devonian Bakken formation. Drill cutting samples were examined wet and dry under a 3.5x-90x stereo
zoom binocular microscope fitted with a UV light for hydrocarbon `cut' fluorescence and 3MP camera for a photo
sample log. Cuttings were examined for `cut' by immersion in Carbo-Sol (trichloroethylene) and inspection under the
UV light. Cut speeds in order of increasing speed are very slow, slow, moderate, fast and instant. Quantifiers in order
of increasing abundance are traces of, rare, occasional, common and abundant.

Flaring Alternatives:
Mobile Gas Treatment Units:
GTUIT is a mobile flare gas capture technology for remote natural gas liquids (NGLs) extraction and gas
conditioning processes. The systems strips out NGLs from the gas stream and refrigerates them for
storage reducing volatile organic compounds (VOCs) thereby mitigating environmental impacts by
significantly decreasing emissions from flare stacks. The Units can be combined and scaled up to handle
high volumes of gas.

Hess has contracted with GTUIT to provide mobile gas treatment units (MTUs). As of 6/23/2014, Hess has
five GTUIT units being utilized in the field and expects delivery of three additional units by 8/1/2014. The
units have capacities ranging from 500 MSCFD to 1,000 MSCFD and can be relocated from one site to
another. The units also can be linked onsite to provide a larger daily capacity intake.

Hess is developing methodologies that will result in an order-of-merit list of candidate sites at which to
deploy the MTUs, based on environmental, regulatory, economic, and operational factors. There are
commercial constraints present which pertain to Hess's commercial agreement with GTUIT; wells must
produce at least half of the MSCFD unit capacity and cover the initial connection fees in order for use of
the GTUIT unit to be economic.
Natural Gas Generators:

Natural Gas Generators are an alternative to diesel generators to provide electricity to well pads located
in remote areas. The units are tied directly into the natural gas production onsite and supplement liquid
propane for use during hourly production fluctuation. Natural gas generators require liquid propane to be
used when gas is not being produced from the well. As production of gas increases and decreases hourly,
the units may switch from propane to natural gas and then back to propane.

Hess performed two pilot programs to evaluate performance characteristics of natural gas generators
from two manufacturers. Both manufacturers require further development to increase their reliability and
performance of units.

Economics favor diesel generators over natural gas generators at this time due to cost and reliability. Pilot
program was not able to produce consistent results to move forward with field implementation at this
time. Natural gas generators will continue to be evaluated as an alternative in the future.
Bi-Fuel (Blended) Diesel Generators:

Bi-Fuel diesel generators are diesel generators with natural gas "makeup" kit (blend kit) allowing units to
use produced gas onsite to power the facility.

Hess is currently in the conceptual phase of evaluation of Bi-Fuel Diesel Generators.
