GEOLOGIST'S SUMMARY
INTRODUCTION
Continental Resources, Inc. Stangeland ­Hayes ecopad is a four well location approximately
6 miles southeast of Williston, North Dakota and within the boundaries of the Crazy Man
Creek Field. The Stangeland ­Hayes ecopad location and drilling procedures were
designed to allow efficient extraction of hydrocarbon resources from the Bakken and Three
Forks Formations lying beneath sections 6,7 and 18 of T153N, R99 and section 31 of T154N
R99W. Stangeland 3-7H1 was the first lateral drilled in the unit and targeted the First Bench
of the Three Forks Formation. Surface location coordinates are 300' FSL & 790' FWL, S6-
T153N R99W, Williams County, North Dakota. The Stangeland ­Hayes drilling unit consists
the aforementioned four sections. The Stangeland 2-7H and 3-7H1 are southerly transects
and the Hayes 2-6H and 3-6H1 are northerly transects. All holes are planned as ~10,000'
laterals. The completed wells will utilize hydraulic fracturing technology to enhance their
petroleum yields from the Middle member of the Bakken and First Bench of the Three Forks
Formations. Drilling the Stangeland 3-7H1 began September 22, 2014 and the hole reached
total depth of 21725' on October 10, 2014.

DRILLING OPERATIONS
Vertical and Curve
Cyclone Rig 33 spudded the Stangeland 3-7H1 on September 22, 2014. Prior to the Cyclone
rig moving onto the hole, a spudding rig drilled a 12-1/4" hole with fresh water to 2423'. The
surface hole was isolated with 9 5/8" 36# J55 STC casing and cemented to surface. A 8 ¾"
PDC bit, 1.83°mud motor and diesel invert mud were used to drill the vertical hole to kick off
point at MD 10760' in the Lodgepole Formation. Directional tools employing a 2.38°degree
bent assembly were used to land the build curve at MD 11523' with a projected TVD of
11223' and inclination of 89.7°. 7", 32# P110 LTC intermediate casing was then run to MD
11515', TVD 11223' and cemented. The Intermediate casing shoe location is 132' FNL &
976' FWL, S7-T153N-R99W, Williams County, North Dakota.
Lateral
Diesel invert mud used while drilling the vertical and curve portions of the hole was replaced
with 9.8# sodium chloride brine for the remainder of drilling operations. Two 6" PDC bits and
1.5° steerable motors were used to drill the lateral section. A single 10210' long southerly
transect was drilled in the First Bench of the Three Forks Formation on a 180°azimuth to MD
21725', TVD 11225', terminating 231' north of the unit boundary hard-line near the south end
of Section18. The initial structural interpretation assumed relatively flat formation dip to MD
16500 followed by crossing perpendicular to the axis of a subtle eastward trending local high
from MD 16500 to MD 19500. After crossing the subtle structural high, the formation dip was
anticipated to remain essentially flat to TD. Over the course of the lateral, the formation
dipped 4' upward resulting in an average formation dip of 90.02°.

CONCLUSIONS
Oil shows observed while drilling Stangeland 3-7H1 were fair with a spotty oil stain
persistently present in the arenaceous to silty dolomite of all target zone samples. Mud gas
shows climbed steadily as the laterals vertical section increased, indicative of the very low

Bakken laterals was adequate to land the curve and geosteer the lateral. The lateral was
drilled with a 2 BHA's. Trip gas of 5500 units after running the 4 ½" liner is a positive
indication of hydrocarbons. Stangeland 3-7H1 will be completed with a multi-stage fracture
stimulation after which its production potential will be revealed.

RE:
Filter Socks and Other Filter Media
Leakproof Container Required
Oil and Gas Wells
Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
