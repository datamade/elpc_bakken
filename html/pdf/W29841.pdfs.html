GEOLOGIST'S SUMMARY
INTRODUCTION
Continental Resources, Inc.'s Maynor ecopad is a three wel location approximately 5 miles
southeast of Williston, North Dakota and within the boundaries of the Last Chance Field.
The Maynor ecopad location and drilling procedures were designed to allow efficient
extraction of hydrocarbon resources from the Bakken and Three Forks Formations lying
beneath sections 35 and 36 of T154N, R100W. Maynor 3-35H was the first lateral drilled in
the unit and targeted the Middle Member of the Bakken Formation. Surface location
coordinates are 1737' FSL & 372' FWL, S35 T154N R100W, Williams County, North Dakota.
Maynor 3-35H, 2-25H1 and 1-35H are all ~10,000' easterly transects. The completed wells
will utilize hydraulic fracturing technology to enhance their petroleum yields from the Middle
Member of the Bakken and First Bench of the Three Forks Formations. Drilling the Maynor 3-
35H began January 6, 2015 and the hole reached total depth of MD 21147' TVD 11049' on
January 20, 2015.

GEOLOGICAL EVALUATION
Well site geological supervision of Maynor 3-35H was provided Monaco Services, Inc., with
one wel site Geologist and two mudloggers. Monaco personnel obtained gas quantification
utilizing Bloodhound Unit #5003 and a Monaco System 2200 electric gas extractor. WITS
information was imported from the drilling rig EDR system. Monaco personnel caught lagged
30' samples in the vertical and curve portions of the hole and 100' samples during the lateral.
Cuttings from the invert mud drilled portions of the hole were cleaned with PC Citrasolve.
Fresh water was used to clean cuttings form the lateral portion of the hole. Cleaned and
graded samples were examined damp with a 3x-30x stereo binocular microscope and
Geoscope UV fluoroscope. Acetone was used as the solvent for hydrocarbon cut evaluation.
Al processed samples were preserved and sent to the North Dakota Geological Survey in
Grand Forks, North Dakota. Gamma and survey data was provided by the Leam Drilling
Systems personnel on location.

DRILLING OPERATIONS
Vertical and Curve
Cyclone Rig 33 spudded the Maynor 3-35H on January 6, 2015. Prior to the Cyclone rig
moving onto the hole, a spudding rig drilled a 12-1/4" hole with fresh water. The surface hole
was isolated with 9 5/8" 36# J55 STC casing set at 2236' and cemented to surface. An 8 ¾"
PDC bit, 1.5° mud motor and diesel invert mud were used to drill the vertical hole to kick off
point at MD 10569' in the Lodgepole Formation. Directional tools employing a 2.38° degree
bent assembly were used to land the build curve at MD 11331' with a projected TVD of
11014' and projected inclination of 90.9°. 7", 32# P110 LTC intermediate casing was then
run to MD 11291', TVD 11013' and cemented. The Intermediate casing shoe location is
1360' FSL & 676' FWL, S35-T154N-R100W, Williams County, North Dakota
Lateral
Diesel invert mud used while drilling the vertical and curve portions of the hole was replaced
with 9.6# sodium chloride brine for the remainder of drilling operations. The lateral was drilled
with one 6" PDC bit and 1.5° steerable motor. A single 9856' long easterly transect was
drilled in the Middle Member of the Bakken formation on a 89.8° azimuth to MD 21147', TVD
11049', terminating 634' FSL and 231' FEL of S36 T154N R100W. Initial structural
interpretation assumed relatively consistent formation dip of 89.6° to approximately MD
14500 and then flattening to approximately 89.8° from MD 14500 to MD 18000' and finally
dipping downward again at approximately 89.6° from MD 18000 to TD. Over the course of
the lateral, the formation dipped 44' downward, resulting in an average formation dip of
89.74°.

LITHOLOGY AND HYDROCARBON SHOWS
Vertical and Curve
Geologic evaluation of Maynor 3-35H began at MD 9400' in the Charles Formation. The
Base of the Last Charles Salt was encountered at MD 9431' TVD 9431' (SS TVD -7187'), 1'
structurally low to Maynor 2-35H1 and 2' structurally low to Maynor 1-35H. The Ratcliffe
interval from MD 9485 to MD 9500 consisted of Argillaceous Mudstone to Wackestone light
gray/graybrown mottled firm microcrystalline some slightly sucrosic dolomitic trace
intercrystalline porosity trace dark brown to black oil stain even very dull yellow green
fluorescence trace interbedded anhydrite white to light gray soft cryptocrystalline. A slight
gas increase from 100 units to 164 units was observed in two distinct very thin slightly porous
horizons. It is unlikely that economic volumes of hydrocarbons are present. In the Ratcliffe
interval. The Midale interval was composed of compact argillaceous wackestone to
mudstone. No porosity or shows were observed in samples from the Midale interval.
The top of the Mission Canyon Formation was encountered at MD 9658' TVD 9658', (SS
TVD -7414), structurally flat to Maynor 2-35H1 and 1' structurally low to Maynor 1-35H.
Samples from the Rival interval (9660' to 9675') consisted of Mudstone light gray firm
microcrystalline tight slightly argillaceous with common white to light gray anhydrite
inclusions trace spotty to even dull yellow fluorescence faint pale yellow streaming cut.
Total gas remained static at approximately 150 units while the Rival was penetrated. With
the lack of significant shows or porosity development, it is unlikely that commercial volumes
of hydrocarbons are present in the Rival interval at this location. No other significant porous
zones or gas shows were observed while drilling through the Mission Canyon Formation.
The top of the Lodgepole Formation was encountered at MD 10234' TVD 10234' (SS TVD -
7990'), structurally flat to Maynor 2-35H1 and 1' structurally low to Maynor 1-35H. The curve
was "kicked­off" at MD 10569'. Four gamma markers were selected and used to calculate
the curve landing point while drilling through the Lodgepole. Lodgepole samples consisted of
compact ARGILLACEOUS MUDSTONE with a trace of black carbonaceous shale present at
the False Bakken Marker. Background gas began increasing from 125 units at MD 11031 to
a maximum of 2267 units at top of the Bakken Shale at MD 11104'. Samples from the
Scallion interval ( MD 11082' to 11104') consisted Mudstone to wackestone light graybrown
firm to sub hard microcrystalline occasional fossil fragments no visible porosity pale yellow
fluorescence no visible stain slow milky pale yellow cut. It is likely that fractures emanating
vertically from the Bakken Shale are the source of this hydrocarbon show.
The Bakken Formation Upper Shale was encountered at MD 11104', TVD 10974' (SS TVD
-8730'). All three Maynor holes are structurally flat at the top of the Upper Bakken Shale.
Samples consisted of CARBONACEOUS SHALE, black, firm blocky, non-calcareous, slightly
siltaceous in part, non-fluorescent, weak milky pale yellow cut. Total gas at the top of the
shale was 2508 units and decreased slightly to 2450 units at the base of the shale.
The Middle Member of the Bakken Formation was encountered at MD 11163', TVD 10993'
(SS TVD -8749), 1' structurally low to Maynor 2-35H1 and 3' structural y low to Maynor 1-
35H. Samples observed while drilling through the Middle Member of the Bakken Formation
were very consistent and consisted of argillaceous to siltaceous and slightly arenaceous
slightly dolomitic mudstone. Weak shows with a trace of dark brown stain pale yellow
fluorescence and immediate pale yellow milky cut were persistent in all samples. A single
higher gamma marker bed was observed, at the top of the lateral target zone, which would
aid in steering the lateral. Total gas was static and ranged from 2020 at the top of the
Middle Bakken and decreased slightly to 1700 units when the curve was landed.

TARGET ZONE IDENTIFICATION IN THE LATERAL
The Target Zone within the Middle Member of the Bakken Formation consists of an 11' thick
target zone in the Middle Member of the Bakken Formation. The target zone is
stratigraphically situated between 16' and 27' beneath the base of the Upper Bakken Shale.
Lithology within the target was consistent over the course of the lateral, when quality samples
were available. Target lithology was observed to be Siltaceous to slightly very fine
arenaceous slightly dolomitic mudstone, tight, with dull yellow fluorescence present in
samples with a granular to slightly sucrosic texture. Fluorescent samples also were observed
to have scattered medium to dark brown hydrocarbon stain and were silty to slightly
arenaceous. Cuttings from samples associated with subtly lower gamma count, slightly more
arenaceous beds were also noted to be slightly lighter gray. Gamma counts within the Target
ranged from 80 to 100 API. The single high gamma marker at the top of the target zone was
observed to have gamma counts rising rapidly from 100 to 124 API counts and samples were
observed to be slightly darker gray colored. The base of the target zone was only briefly
encountered between MD 16100 and MD 16400 and evidenced by a subtle gamma
decrease from 90 to 100 counts and a subtle color change to slightly darker gray.
LATERAL OIL AND GAS SHOWS
Oil and gas shows observed in the samples when the wel path was in the target zone were
weak, yet consistent over the course of the lateral. Pinpoint to even dull yellow fluorescence
was present in all samples that could be correlated to the thin beds associated with slightly
lower gamma counts. There were no obvious shows of oil in the drilling mud and no gas
flares noted during the lateral. Mud gas was noted to slowly increase from 1600 units at the
heel of the lateral to 2300 units at the laterals toe.
LATERAL DRILLING OBSERVATIONS AND GEOSTEERING
The lateral was drilled with a single BHA and no trip were required. Upon exiting the 7"
casing shoe at MD 11291', steering procedures were executed to evaluate all of the discrete
lithologic horizons in the target zone of the Middle Member of the Bakken Formation. No
single horizon within the target appeared to display a favorable advantage with respect to oil
shows or rate of penetration. Steering response was adequate to achieve targets for the
entire length of the lateral. Structural interpretation indicates formation dip of 89.6° from the
7" casing shoe to approximately MD 13000'. From MD 13000' to approximately MD 19200'
the formation flattened to an average dip of 89.8° and then dipped downward at
approximately 89.5° to the hole TD at MD 21147'.
Over the course of the lateral, the
formation dropped 46', yielding an average local dip of 89.73º. The 6" bore exposed 9856
lineal feet of hole through the Middle Member of the Bakken Formation. While drilling the
lateral, the BHA was slid 1025' (10%). The bore remained in the target zone for 9461' (96%)
of the lateral.

CONCLUSIONS
Oil shows observed while drilling Maynor 3-35H were weak slightly dolomitic granular beds
exhibiting scattered spotty oil stain and dull yellow fluorescence present throughout the
course of the lateral. Mud gas shows climbed slowly and steadily from 1600 to 2300 units
over the course of the lateral. Subsurface structural control, utilizing data from the adjacent
2-35H1 Three Forks lateral was adequate to land the curve and geosteer the hole. The
lateral was drilled very efficiently with a single BHA. Maynor 3-35H will be completed with a
multi-stage fracture stimulation after which its production potential will be revealed.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.

Continental Resources standard CCL, CBL, 4-1/2" liner running and testing procedures
Continental Resources' standard practice for running the cement bond log and casing
caliper log is to run both logs immediately after coming out of the hole after TD, prior to running
the 4-1/2" liner, to the deepest depth obtainable; however, if there are well control concerns that
require us to run the liner sooner, only the CBL will be run and the CCL will be run after setting
the liner.
Based on the CCL results, we determine the actual API minimum burst allowance for the 7"
casing. If the downgraded API burst pressure is below our minimum required frac pressures, we
will run a 4-1/2" frac string; if severe wear or holes are found in the casing, we will run a 5"
cemented, to surface, tie back string.
The CBL log is run in order to determine the top of cement, as required by the NDIC.
Our current 4-1/2" liner program for a 1280 unit is 30, evenly spaced, stages with 29 swellable
packers. The liner shoe is set approximately 180 feet off bottom. The shoe stage below the last
packer has 2 joints, a double valved float, one joint, and a ported guide shoe ­ appx 130 ft. The
liner is run using a running tool on the end of 4" DP. The 7" packer/hanger is set about 40 ft
above KOP between two casing collars but conditions occasionally occur that require setting
higher, either through unexpected failure or in order to isolate casing wear close to KOP.
Recently we have tried 40 stage liners and the trend to explore the optimum stage count will
continue.. Once the liner is at depth, a ball is dropped through the DP, the ball is pressured up
against the setting tool to approximately 2500 psi, and the 7" packer/hanger is set.
A push pull test is done to confirm the hanger has set. Then, a 4500 psi pressure test is
completed on the back side of the 4" DP to confirm the packer has set. The setting tool is then
backed off and the 4" DP/running tool is laid down.
Immediately after the rotary rig has been moved off the well location, the 7" csg and liner
packer/ hanger are tested to the frac pressure. The testers will rig up and test the tubing head to
5000 psi. Next a test plug will be run and set, using wire line, in the top of the 7" packer/hanger.
Testers will pressure up to our frac pressure, typically 8500 psi, to confirm the 7" is ready for
completion.
