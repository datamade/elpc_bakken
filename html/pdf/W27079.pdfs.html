Synopsis
The Hanover Federal 5300 44-11 5T [SE SE Sec. 11 T153N R100W] is located in the Willow Creek Field
in Williams County approximately eight miles East of Williston, North Dakota. The well plan proposed a
single lateral leg trending 180° south to be drilled from the SE SE corner of Section 11 to the SE SE
corner of Section 23. The lateral portion of the Hanover Federal 5300 44-11 3T targeted a zone in the
dolomite sandstone of the Three Forks formation, approximately 8 to 24 feet below the Pronghorn
formation.
Control Wells
One complete well, the Hanover Federal 5300 44-11 4B was used as the control offset for the Hanover
Federal 5300 44-11 5T. Throughout the curve progression, gamma-ray data from the Hanover Federal 4B
was utilized to help determine proper landing depth for the Hanover Federal 5300 44-11 5T
The Hanover Federal 5300 44-11 4B [SE SE Sec. 11 T153N R100W] was spud on 04 February 2015.
Initial production status is still to come.
A graph was constructed to determine which markers and formation tops could be accurately used in
landing the curve. Markers with more than 5 feet difference between the offsets were watched but not
utilized in decision for moving the TVD target of the curve.

Geologic Assessment
Methods
Geologic supervision of Hanover Federal 5300 44-11 5T was provided by two experienced RPM well site
geologists. Gas and chromatograph levels were measured using NOV Wellsite Gas Watch real time gas
detector and chromatograph system. The NOV Wellsite Gas Watch system uses non-dispersive infrared
and chemical sensor gas detection to evaluate gases liberated from the formation by the drill bit and
carried to the surface by the drilling fluid.
The NOV Wellsite Gas Watch was interfaced with a Pason electronic data recorder system Pason
provided rate of penetration, on-off bottom and pump strokes to the NOV Wellsite Gas Watch and
received total gas information from the NOV Wellsite Gas Watch for viewing on location and remotely.
Under the direction of RPM well site geologists, rig crews collected lagged drill cutting sample at 30'
intervals from 8,570' to the kick off point (KOP) at 10,570'. Samples were continuously logged at 30'
intervals during the curve and 50' intervals throughout the lateral section.
Sampled drill cuttings were examined wet and dry under a binocular microscope using plain
(broad spectrum) and transmitted light. Cuttings were evaluated for hydrocarbon "cut" by
immersion in carbo-sol (trichloroethylene) and inspection under a UV fluoroscope. 10%
hydrochloric acid and alizarin red were used to determine the calcareous and dolomitic content
of rocks and cementing.
Vertical Operations
Overview
The Hanover Federal 5300 44-11 5T was spud on 04 February 2015 using Patterson 330 drilling rig.
Prior to rig up of RPM mud logging services, a 9 5/8" hole was drilled with fresh water to a depth of
2,397' and isolated with 9 5/8" 36# J-55 casing cemented to surface. Bit #1, 2 & 3 (8 ¾") were used to
drill from 2,397' to KOP (10,570'). The curve was drilled with bit #4 starting at MD 10,570' and drilled
to MD 11,715' and landed the curve 13' into the Three Forks formation.
RPM well site geologists arrived at site on 04 February 2015 and began logging the vertical section at
8,570'.
Diesel invert drilling fluid with a mud weight of ranging from 9.65-10.25 ppg was used during remainder
of the vertical hole and in the curve builds sections.
Lithology
The top of the Kibbey Lime [Mississippian Madison Group] was logged at 8,606' (-6,359'), 5' low to the
Hanover Federal 5300 44-11 4B. Samples from this interval (Figure 1) were described as:
LIMESTONE: mudstone, light grey to off white, occasional light brown to reddish brown, rare
dark grey, microcrystalline, platy, firm to moderately hard, crystalline texture.

Hanover Federal 5300 44-11 4B. The Base Last Salt was drilled at 9,470' (-7,223'), 3' low to the
Hanover Federal 5300 44-11 4B. Samples from this interval (Figure 1) were described as:
SALT: translucent, milky, crystalline, brittle to hard, anhedral, subhedral, crystalline texture, vitreous,
interbedded, cream, red, anhydrite in part; ANHYDRITE: white, grey, soft, amorphic texture.

The top of the Mission Canyon formation of the Madison Group [Mississippian] was drilled at a measure
of depth 9,688' (-7,441'), 3' low to the Hanover Federal 5300 44-11 4B (Figure 2). Samples from this
interval were described as:
LIMESTONE: medium to dark brown, common dark grey, occasional light brown,
wackestone, fine crystalline, blocky to platy in part, mottled appearance, no visible
porosity.

ARGILLACEOUS LIMESTONE: mudstone to wackestone, light to medium grey, very fine

Directional Operations
RPM provided equipment and personnel for directional services. Ryan Energy Technologies
(Ryan) provided equipment and personnel for MWD services. RPM geologists worked closely with RPM
directional and Ryan MWD teams to formulate steering decisions to maximize exposure of borehole in
the pay zone. RPM well site geologists closely observed MWD gamma-ray information and penetration
rates to aid in steering decisions and dip degree estimations.
Curve Build
Overview
The curve began with bit #4 at MD 10,570' and drilled to MD 11,715' and landed the curve ~13' into the
Three Forks formation.
During the curve progression, isopach data of gamma-ray signatures from the Hanover Federal 5300 44-
11 4B was compared to gamma-ray readings from the MWD tool from the Base Charles Salt (Table 1).
Prospective markers were entered into Table 2 to determine the target landing point. Based on the marker
pick for the Base Charles Salt, the target TVD for the curve was kept the same due to very minor
variation from the off-set well. The curve was completed at a measured depth of 11,715'and a TVD of
11,098' in the Three Forks.
Table 1: Comparing distances from certain gamma markers on offset wells to establish final landing target.
The curve was landed at the middle of the Three Forks first bench, ~ 13' below the Pronghorn.
Intermediate casing (7") was set at measured depth 11,715' and a TVD of ~11,098'.
Lithology
The top of the "False Bakken" was penetrated at a measured depth of 11,084' (TVD 10,993') (-8,746')
suggesting a target depth of 11,096' TVD. Gas levels peaked at 40 units and remained low through the
False Bakken.
The Upper Shale of the Bakken formation [Mississippian ­ Devonian] was drilled at 11,107' (TVD
11,008') (-8,761') with sample returns of typical black, carbonaceous and petroliferous shale (Figure 4)
characterized by gamma-ray values in excess of 500 API counts. Gas levels remained fairly consistent
with average background gas of ~70 units, and peaking at 199 units. Samples were described as:
SHALE: black, dark grey to black, firm, sub-blocky, earthy texture, calcite cemented, petroliferous,
carbonaceous, abundant fine disseminated pyrite, laminations.
Figure 4: Photograph sample from the Upper Bakken Shale.
The Middle Member of the Bakken formation was penetrated at 11,143' MD (11,024' TVD) (-8,777').
The upper zone of the middle member contained a tight grey siltstone with little or no oil stain or visible
porosity (Figure 5). Gas levels averaged ~40 units through the Middle Member of the Bakken formation.
Samples from the Middle member were described as:
SILTY SANDSTONE: light to medium grey, off white to light grey, very fine grained, sub-angular
to sub-round, friable to firm, moderately to well sorted, moderately calcite cemented, occasional
disseminated pyrite, occasional calcite fracture fill, intergranular porosity, light brown spotty oil
stain
Figure 5: Photograph representative of the siltstone in the upper zone of the Middle Bakken.
The Lower Shale of the Bakken formation was penetrated at 11,234' MD, (11,066' TVD) (-8,819') with
sample returns of typical black, carbonaceous and petroliferous shale characterized by gamma-ray values
in excess of 800 API counts. Gas levels averaged over 40 units throughout the Lower Bakken formation.
Samples were described as:
SHALE: black, dark grey to black, firm, sub-blocky, earthy texture, calcite cemented, petroliferous,
carbonaceous, abundant fine disseminated pyrite, laminations.
Figure 6: Photograph representative of the Lower Bakken Shale just above the Three Forks formation
The top of the Three Forks formation was drilled at 11,296' (~11,085' TVD) (-8,838'). Samples from
this interval (Figure 7) were described as:
DOLOMITE: mudstone, pale green, tan to cream, very fine crystalline, friable to firm, semi-hard, sub-
angular to sub-round, dense to sucrosic texture, well sorted, dolomitic cement, trace nodular pyrite, trace
light brown spotty oil stain.
Figure 7: Photograph representative of the middle member of the Three Forks formation
Lateral
Overview
Drilling fluid consisting of a closed system salt water brine (9.65 ­10.25ppg) was used while drilling the
lateral section. Two 6" PDC bits were used to drill the lateral.
Bits #5 & 6 (6" Smith), both PDC bits in front of a 1.5° mud motor were used to drill the lateral from
11,715' to 21,595'. The average ROP for this bit was 80.5 ft/hr and drilled 127 hours.
Drilling operations were concluded at 01:15 hours on 10 April 2015 at a bottom hole location of
10,728.57' S & 201.42' E of surface location or approximately 302.54' FSL & 557.58' FEL, SE SE Sec. 23
T153N, R100W
Lithology
The Three Forks formation target zone is represented by a12 foot thick target zone situated 11 to 27 feet
below the base of the Pronghorn formation. The target zone was characterized by an interval of cold
gamma and consisted primarily of sandy dolomite. Every attempt was made to keep the well bore in the
target zone below this low gamma package for as much of the drilling operations as possible.
The Three Forks formation seen in drilling samples consisted of two primary facies. Samples observed
while drilling the upper target zone consisted of a cream to tan, occasionally to light pink sandy dolomite
with lenses of purple to gray sandy siltstone. Pale green siltstone with common disseminated pyrite was
present. Common to trace nodular & disseminated pyrite was present. Samples in the upper target zone
commonly exhibited a slow white diffuse cut with trace white streaming cuts when submersed as dry
samples in EnTron solvent.
DOLOMITE: mudstone, cream, tan, occasionally white, rarely light gray to gray, very fine crystalline,
friable, sub angular to sub rounded, sucrosic texture, well sorted, dolomitic cement, very sandy, occasional
pale green siltstone, trace nodular pyrite, occasional disseminated pyrite, common oil stain, trace
intergranular porosity, possible fracture porosity
Figure 8: Dolomite in the upper part of the Three Forks target zone
Samples observed within the lower target zone were dominantly composed of a cream to off white, light
brown colored silty Sandstone with trace to occasional pale green siltstone which contained moderate
amounts of disseminated pyrite and rare amounts of nodular pyrite. Slow white diffuse cuts with fast
white streaming cuts were observed in dry sample when submersed in EnTron solvent.
Figure 9: Silty sandy dolomite from lower "Target Zone" of the Three Forks formation
Samples observed below the target zone were dominantly composed of a cream to off white, light brown,
trace dark gray colored Claystone with trace to occasional silty sandstone, pale green siltstone which
contained trace amounts of disseminated pyrite and rare amounts of nodular pyrite.
Figure 10: Claystone from below the base of the target in the Three Forks formation
Gas and Hydrocarbon Shows
While drilling the curve through the Lodgepole formation, total gas levels were recorded with an average
background of ~ 50 units. Total gas stayed consistent through penetrating the "Upper Bakken Shale"
marker. Background gas levels stayed consistent through Lower Shale of the Three Forks formation at
~80 units. Background gas levels rose slightly upon penetration of the Middle Member of the Three
Forks formation with exhibiting significant peaks. Background gas levels maintained an average of 300
to 800 units through the lateral with peaks over 2000 units.
Hydrocarbon cuts using Entron solvent were moderate though the entire lateral. The target zone and
above was characterized by slow white to yellow diffuse cuts and common fast streaming cuts. No
correlation was found between the strength of the hydrocarbon cuts and the position of the bit in the 1st
Bench Three Forks.
Summary
1) The Hanover Federal 5300 44-11 5T was spud 04 February 2015 east of the town Williston in Williams
County, North Dakota. The vertical hole was drilled to a total depth of 10,570'.
2) A mud program consisting of diesel invert (9.65 ­ 10.25 ppg) after surface casing and
through the curve build sections. The drilling mud was successful in maintaining stable
hole conditions and minimizing washout through the salt intervals.
3) Three 8 3/4" PDC bits were used to drill the vertical hole from surface casing to a vertical depth of
10,570' in a total of 69.5 hours. One 8 3/4" bit was used to drill through the curve, landing at a measured
depth of 11,715' in 25.5 hours. The entire 10,728.57' lateral was drilled in 127 hours with two 6" PDC
bits.
4) The lateral was targeting an 11' thick section of the middle member of the Three Forks situated 11 to
28 feet below the Pronghorn.
5) Gas levels of between 300 and 800 units were maintained through most of the drilling
operations.
6) The target zone of the Three Forks formation consisted primarily of a cream to light brown sandy
dolomite, with occasional pale green siltstone containing disseminated pyrite, trace lenses of light red,
gray sandy siltstone and trace to rare amounts of nodular pyrite. Moderate white diffuse cuts with fast
white streaming cuts were observed in dry sample when immersed in EnTron solvent.
7) Drilling operations were concluded at 10:30 hours on 11 April 2015, with a bottom hole location of
10,728.57' S & 201.42' E of surface location or approximately 302.55' FSL & 557.58' FEL, SE SE
Section 23, T153N, R100W
8) Oasis Petroleum Hanover Federal 5300 44-11 5T awaits completion operations to determine ultimate
production potential.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
