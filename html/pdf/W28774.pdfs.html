INTRODUCTION
XTO Energy, Inc. Ranger 21X-18F [NE NW Section 18, T151N, R99W] is located approximately 6
miles north-northeast of Arnegard, in Tobacco Garden Field of McKenzie County, North Dakota (Figure
1). The well is designed as a Three Forks horizontal well to develop Sections 18 and 19. Directional
drilling and geo-steering will be employed to land in the Three Forks reservoir and maintain maximum
exposure to a 20' target zone.
GEOLOGY
Methods
Geologic analysis for the Ranger 21X-18F was provided by a Sunburst Consulting, Inc. geology team.
Sunburst well site geologists caught lagged samples at 30' intervals from 9,500' to TD (total depth). Rock
samples, rate of penetration and gamma ray data were used in geologic analysis to aid in geo-steering and
dip calculations.
Prospective Lithology
The Ratcliffe was drilled at 9,632' MD / 9,631' TVD, 21' low to prognosis and 4' low to Ranger 21X-
18G. This interval was mainly mudstone to wackestone limestone, which was cream to medium brown in
color, friable and with an earthy texture. It also consisted of anhydrite and dolomite. Gas shows ranged
from 146 units to 219 units against a 9.8 ppg mud weight.
The Mission Canyon Formation was drilled at 9,775' MD / 9,774' TVD, 5' low to prognosis and 5' low
Ranger 21X-18G. The Mission Canyon consists primarily of clean, argillaceous and dolomitic limestone.
The upper Mission Canyon is primarily friable to firm, light to medium gray argillaceous limestone.
While the lower lithology consisted primarily of off white to medium gray microcrystalline limestone
mudstone. Hydrocarbon shows ranged from 171 to 389 units in 9.85 ppg drilling mud.
The argillaceous limestone signifying the top of the Lodgepole Formation was penetrated at 10,298'
MD / 10,297' TVD which is even to prognosis and 2' high to Ranger 21X-18G. This slope marine
deposit consisted of mostly gray to brown argillaceous mudstone that was firm to hard, microcrystalline
and contained rare disseminated pyrite. Also present were relatively thin beds of clean limestone that
were light gray to brown, microcrystalline and had an earthy texture. Fracture gases ranged from 208
units to 1,842 units against 9.85 to 10.1 ppg drilling mud.
The False Bakken shale was drilled at 11,147' MD / 11,085' TVD which was 2' low to prognosis and 6'
high to Ranger 21X-18G. This relatively thin lithology consists of medium to dark gray brown shale. A
single gas show of 2,417 units was witnessed against a 10.1 ppg mud weight.
The Bakken Formation has four members, two of which are organic-rich pyritic shale, separated by a
silty sandstone middle member. The carbonaceous Upper Bakken Shale was penetrated at 11,165' MD /
11,096' TVD, 3' high to prognosis and 7' high to Ranger 21X-18G. Gas shows through the shale read
upwards of 2,024 units (C1 through C4) before penetrating the Middle Bakken at 11,197' MD / 11,114'
TVD, 3' high to prognosis and 5' high to Ranger 21X-18G. The Middle Member consisted of light gray
to light brown silty sandstone. Background gas here rose to 2,664 units against 10.1 ppg mud. Oil staining
was common along with disseminated pyrite. The Lower Bakken Shale came in at 11,287' MD / 11,157'
TVD, 3' high to prognosis and 5' high to Ranger 21X-18G and was similar to the Upper Bakken Shale,
with a slightly browner color. Gas shows here got as high as 2,912 units (C1 through C4). The final
member, The Pronghorn, came in at 11,330' MD / 11,174' TVD, 3' high to prognosis and 2' high to
Ranger 21X-18G. This member consists of a medium gray to brown siltstone.
The targeted Three Forks Formation was observed at 11,350' MD / 11,181' TVD, 1' high to prognosis
and 3' high to Ranger 21X-18G. The observed lithology consisted of a cream to brown dolomite, which
was crystalline with an earthy texture. This facies also exhibited laminations and contained disseminated
pyrite. The targeted Three Forks lithology also contained a very light, mint green to pale blue shale that
was occasionally off white. This lithology was soft to friable and contained disseminated and nodular
pyrite. Spotty light brown oil staining and intercrystalline porosity were present throughout. The 20'
target zone was 12' below the top of the Three Forks Formation (Figure 2), and consisted of the same
argillaceous dolomite and shale. Gas shows ranged from 144 units to 1,850 units.

Lateral Gas and Oil Shows
The background gas for the Ranger 21X-18F ranged from 56 to 1,378 units during the course of the
lateral. The highest gas show was 1,850 units at 14,708' MD against 10.00 ppg mud. Higher gas shows
were generally witnessed in the upper part of the target zone. The gas buster was only ran when
circulating after a trip and no flare was witnessed.
Geo-steering
A 20' target zone was chosen for the Ranger 21X-18F by XTO Energy geologists that began 12' below
the Pronghorn and ended 4' from the top of the Second Bench of the Three Forks. The thickness was
estimated using control well logs. A well drilled previously in Section 18, the Clouse 7-18, provided
guidance for expected gamma ray markers while drilling the lateral (Figure 3). The top of the target zone
had relatively moderate gamma reading API in the high 90s, just below this gamma marker was a higher
marker reading 120 API. A low hard streak at the upper part of target zone provided a great geosteering
marker and was just below this high gamma. This hard streak would produce gamma readings in the low
50 API range. Another high marker reading around 115 API was witnessed below the cool gamma before
entering a very thin zone of cool gamma that would read in the high 50 API range. Below this marker was
a higher marker reading in the 90 API range before entering a relatively thick zone of lower 70's gamma.
A thick zone of 110 to 118 API gamma was observed at the lower middle part of target before going into
another thick zone of low gamma, this time reading as low as 52 API. At the very bottom of target zone a
high gamma was witnessed reading into the 120 API range.

Bottom-Hole Location
Total depth resulted in a closure azimuth of 175.35° and a closure distance of 9,938.56'. The bottom-hole
location is projected to be 9,905.82' south and 806.05' east of the surface location or approximately
247.82' FSL & 2,206.05' FWL, SE SW Section 19, T151N, R99W. The hole was prepared for liner and
multi-stage fracture stimulation directly after drilling operations concluded.
SUMMARY
The XTO Energy, Inc. Ranger 21X-18F was spud on August 1, 2014 by Advanced Energy Rig #10 and
re-entered by H&P 456 on December 5, 2014. The well efficiently reached TD in 21 total drilling days on
December 22, 2014. Samples from the Three Forks target contained oil staining that was described as
trace and spotty with visible intercrystalline porosity. MWD gamma ray markers, sample analysis, and
ROP were successfully utilized to expose 96% of the lateral borehole to the Three Forks target zone. The
Ranger 21X-18F awaits completion operations to maximize its production potential.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
