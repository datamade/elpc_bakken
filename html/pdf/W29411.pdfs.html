INTRODUCTION
The Oxy USA, Inc. Sharon Rainey 2-17-20H-142-96 [SESE Sec 8, T142N, R96W] is located
approximately 15 miles Southwest of Manning, North Dakota. The subject well was drilled in Dunn
County, ND by the Nabors X-20 rig in the Manning Field of the Bakken pool. The subject well was
planned with one approximately 10,096' [Intermediate casing to projected total depth (TD)] long southerly
lateral bore with intent to intersect regional hydraulic fracture trends that might enhance reservoir quality
within the Pronghorn member of the Bakken Formation. OXY USA intends to develop oil production in the
middle to late Devonian Pronghorn target porosity zone with a stratigraphy consisting of siltstone and
sandy siltstone with depositional lithofacies ranging from onshore terrestrial to offshore marine deposits.
ENGINEERING
Surface, Vertical & Curve Operations:
A 9 " surface hole was drilled on October 23, 2014 by the Nabors X20 rig and surface casing
was set at 2,341'. The vertical hole operations continued on October 26, 2014 with Weatherford providing
equipment and personnel for MWD and directional drilling services.
The first vertical Bottom Hole Assembly (BHA 2), consisting of MWD tools, a Hunting 1.50° bend
motor and Smith ,MDi616BPX bit drilled to a depth of 8,643'. At this depth, a trip was made due to a low
rate of penetration (ROP). The second vertical BHA (BHA 3) consisted of a new Smith PDC bit. The mud
motor and MWD tools were re-run. This BHA reached a KOP of 9,800' and was pulled out for the curve
assembly.
The curve assembly (BHA 4) consisted of a 1.83º mud motor, 8 ¾" Ultera U516M bit and MWD
tools. This BHA drilled to 11,234' MD, landing the curve ~15' into the Pronghorn Member of the Bakken
Formation and in the center of our original target zone. Due to the proximity of the Sharon Rainey 3 (a
Middle Bakken well), the target was moved down 4' TVD to avoid collision. The 7°/100' curve was drilled
in 32 hours, totaling 1,434' of measured depth.
The curve assembly was then pulled out for casing, wire line, and rig-skid operations. 7"
intermediate casing was run, set, and cemented at a depth of 11,220' MD. Diesel invert drilling fluid was
used throughout the vertical hole and curve build sections maintaining a 9.7-10 ppg mud weight for stable
downhole conditions.
Lateral Operations:
Weatherford again provided equipment and personnel for MWD and directional drilling services.
The directional drillers and Payzone geologists worked closely together throughout the project to make
steering decisions in order to maximize the amount of borehole exposure to the pay zone.
A five bladed 6" Hughes T406 PDA bit (6x20 jets), 1.50º Hunting 6/7 8.0 mud motor and MWD
tools comprised the first lateral BHA (5th BHA overall). It was run in the hole on 4" drill pipe starting at
11,234' MD. This BHA drilled to a TD of 20,156' MD, making 9,922' and a one run, 5 day lateral.
Final TVD, closure azimuth, and vertical section were 10,527.90', 183.40º, and 10,881.57',
respectively. The BHL is 10,862.46' South & 644.83' West of surface location or approx. 296.64' FSL &
1,065.83' FWL, Section 20, T142N, R96W (Figure 2). Salt water drilling fluid was used from the
intermediate casing to TD with mud weights throughout the lateral varying from 9.8-10.1 ppg.

GEOLOGIC EVALUATION
Methods:
Geologic supervision of the well was provided by Payzone Consulting, Inc. with two well site
geologists. Brandon Lutz and Kevin Jacks drilled the vertical, curve, and lateral sections. A CanrigTM gas
chromatograph was interfaced with a Rigwatch electronic data recorder system and provided total gas in-
formation for viewing around location. Lagged samples were caught in 30' intervals from 8,000' to 10,880'
with 10' samples collected when landing the curve from 10,880'-11,234' MD. Cuttings were examined
under a binocular microscope and complete lithologic descriptions are provided in the lithology document
within this evaluation. Two sets of dry cuttings were collected, with one set sent to the North Dakota
Geological Survey Core Library and the second set sent to Jennifer Califf, the project geologist
overseeing operations.

GEOSTEERING
Gamma Signature and Hard Streaks:
The Pronghorn target zone displayed a vertically repetitive gamma signature indicative of
interbedded lithology of alternating radioactivity. After adjusting our target zone to 8' thick and moving it
down 4' per Houston's request, the gamma signature matched perfectly with the 5" TVD log from the
Delvin Dukart 3-30-31H-143-95 (Figure 5), which was a Three Forks well drilled ~8 miles NE of the
subject well.
Figure 5. Delvin Dukart 3-30-31H-143-95 5" TVD Log. Note: Excellent correlation with the Sharon Rainey 2 Dip Profile
There was a 70-80 API gamma marker at the top of our target zone, a 40-50 API gamma marker
in the center of zone, and a high gamma marker at the base of zone. Additionally, there was also low,
ratty gamma below our target zone. These gamma markers were used for determining formation dip and
depth within the target interval and permitted efficient geosteering. They are plotted on the Wellsite
horizontal log (Figure 6) and dip profile (Figure 7).
Hard streaks played a major role in geosteering the Sharon Rainey 2-17-20H-142-96. The
formation at the center of target and out the bottom of target (ratty 50's API) was considerably hard to
drill. Ratty ROP and spiking differential pressure were correlated to places in formation with abundant
nodular pyrite (Figure 3). This posed potential risks of damaging the bit. A similar hard streak was
encountered during the Delvin Dukart 5-30-31H-143-95. It ended up damaging the bit beyond repair,
requiring a trip during the lateral. DSM, directional, and the geologists decided to stay low in zone and
away from the ratty gamma near the center.

Geo-steering:
The Pronghorn was penetrated at 11,034' MD; 10,594' TVD and consisted of siltstone when
landing the curve. An 8' target window in an approximately 32' thick Pronghorn was selected. Samples
from the target zone varied in porosity, oil staining, color, cementation, and pyrite content. Using distinct
gamma markers and the crucial aid of Weatherford's Inc Sonde tool, Payzone geologists were able to
interpret the dip rate for the well.
The wellbore stayed within the 8' targeted interval for 8,942' or 90% (Pronghorn target entry to
TD) of the lateral and was also in the 32' thick Pronghorn Member for 100% of the lateral. The TVD of the
formation decreased 76.2' over 9,991' (Pronghorn entry to TD projection at bit) resulting in an overall up
dip of 0.44º or 90.44°. A total depth of 21,156' was reached on December 9, 2014 at 21:45 MST.

Hydrocarbons:
During the lateral section of the Sharon Rainey 2-17-20H-142-96, gas readings were recorded,
imported/exported, and analyzed via a CanrigTM gas chromotograph interfaced with the RigwatchTM EDR.
The trends of concentrated gases were extremely accurate judging by the system's ability to decipher
miniscule changes in gas concentrations at connections.
Background gas varied from 52-64 units with peaks scattered minimally throughout the target
zone. No flares were seen during the drilling of the lateral. Connection gases reached as high as 64 units
with an average of 54 units at connections. Highest gas values in the Pronghorn were near the center of
the target zone or above (Figure 8). Oil shows in samples were questionable to absent.

Summary

The Nabors X-20 drilling rig successfully drilled a two-section horizontal wellbore within the
Pronghorn Member of the Bakken Formation at the OXY USA, Inc. Sharon Rainey 2-17-20H-142-
96 lease.

A mud program consisting of diesel invert (9.7-10.0 ppg) was used from spud to curve landing
with salt water drilling fluid used from the intermediate casing to 21,156' MD (TD). Salt water mud
(9.8-10.1 ppg) was efficient for maintaining stable down hole conditions and permitted adequate
analysis of gas concentrations in the lateral. Background gas concentrations ranged from 52-64
units with connections as high as 64 units. The highest concentrations were recorded in the
center of the targeted interval.

Steering was accomplished by plotting the wellbore surveys vs. TVD and correlating MWD
gamma markers as the tool passed them. Formation dip projections were made based on these
markers and provided to the directional team. Projections of dip were successfully used to
maintain the well bore within the Pronghorn target zone. 90% of the well was drilled in the 8'
targeted Pronghorn interval.

Samples from the target consisted of light grey to off-white moderately cemented siltstone with
common disseminated pyrite. Samples from the target zone contained a questionable to absent
oil stain. Cuts displayed a very slow, white to light blue blooming oil fluorescence when exposed
to Carbosol. Gas sampling was measured every 200' in the lateral with Isotubes provided by
Isotech in Champaign, IL and will receive future evaluation by Weatherford personnel and OXY
geologists. These samples will be compared to the gas samples of the Sharon Rainey 3 Middle
Bakken well.

The Sharon Rainey 2-17-20H-142-96 was a successful drilling effort as measured by gross
exposure to the target. The well will be fitted with a 4 ½" production liner and swell packers in
preparation for a fracture stimulation that will determine the true commercial value of the well.

Sharon Rainey 2-17-20H-142-96 Lithology
Rig crews caught samples in 30' intervals from 8,000' to 10,880', 10' intervals from
10,880' to 11,234', and 100' intervals from 11,234' to 21,156'(TD). Formation tops and
lithologic markers have been inserted into the sample descriptions below for reference.
Sample descriptions began in the Amsden Formation. Samples were examined wet and
dry under a binocular microscope and were checked for hydrocarbon cut fluorescence
during the lateral. Sample cuts are masked by invert mud through 11,234' (intermediate
casing set to 11,220') where the invert mud was exchanged to a saltwater mud from
11,234' to 21,156'. Quantifiers in order of increasing abundance are trace, rare,
occasional, common and abundant. Subsea is delineated as the negative numbers within
the parentheses located next to the measured depth or true vertical depth.
Sample quality was good to fair. Overall, the samples within the Pronghorn target
consisted of light grey to off white siltstone with dolomitic/calcareous cement and
common disseminated pyrite. Oil stains were light brown and spotty. Cuts showed a very
slow white to light blue blooming oil fluorescence when exposed to Carbosol.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
