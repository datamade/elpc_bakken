Synopsis
The Drummond 5501 42-21 5B [SE SW Sec. 21 T155N R101W] is located in the Missouri
Ridge Field in Williams County approximately eight miles North of Williston, North Dakota.
The well plan proposed a single lateral leg trending 4.01° south to be drilled from the SE SW
corner of Section 21 to the NE NW corner of Section 16. The lateral portion of the Drummond
5501 42-21 5B targeted a zone in the porous silty sandstone of the Middle Bakken formation,
approximately 15 to 30 feet below the Upper Bakken Shale formation.
Control Wells
One complete well, the Drummond 5501 42-21 6T was used as the control offset for the
Drummond 5501 42-21 5B. Throughout the curve progression, gamma-ray data from the
Drummond 5501 42-21 6T was utilized to help determine proper landing depth for the
Drummond 5501 42-21 5B.
The Drummond 5501 42-21 6T [SE SW Sec. 21 T155N R101W] is located on the same pad as
the subject well and was drilled just before it.
A graph was constructed to determine which markers and formation tops could be accurately
used in landing the curve. Markers with more than 5 feet difference between the offsets were
watched but not utilized in decision for moving the TVD target of the curve.
Graph1. Distance from formation tops in control well was utilized to determine the initial drilling target.
Geologic Assessment
Methods
Geologic supervision of Drummond 5501 42-21 5B was provided by two experienced RPM well
site geologists. Gas and chromatograph levels were measured using NOV Wellsite Gas Watch
real time gas detector and chromatograph system. The NOV Wellsite Gas Watch system uses
non-dispersive infrared and chemical sensor gas detection to evaluate gases liberated from the
formation by the drill bit and carried to the surface by the drilling fluid.
The NOV Wellsite Gas Watch was interfaced with a Pason electronic data recorder system Pason
provided rate of penetration, on-off bottom and pump strokes to the NOV Wellsite Gas Watch
and received total gas information from the NOV Wellsite Gas Watch for viewing on location
and remotely.
Under the direction of RPM well site geologist, rig crews were instructed to catch lagged drill
cutting samples at 30' intervals from 8,340 to the kick off point (KOP) at 10,230'. Samples were
continuously logged at 30' intervals during the curve and 50' intervals throughout the lateral
section.
Sampled drill cuttings were examined wet and dry under a binocular microscope using plain
(broad spectrum) and transmitted light. Cuttings were evaluated for hydrocarbon "cut" by
immersion in carbo-sol (trichloroethylene) and inspection under a UV fluoroscope. 10%
hydrochloric acid and alizarin red were used to determine the calcareous and dolomitic content
of rocks and cementing.
Vertical Operations
Overview
The Drummond 5501 42-21 5B was spud on 17 November 2014 using Patterson 330 drilling rig.
Prior to rig up of RPM mud logging services, a 9 5/8" hole was drilled with fresh water to a
depth of 2,328' and isolated with 9 5/8" 36# J-55 casing cemented from 2,309' to surface. Bit #1
(8 ¾" Smith) was used to drill from 2,328' to KOP 10,230'. It was used for 61.5 hours and
drilled 7,902', averaging 128.5 ft/hr.
RPM well site geologists arrived at site on 17 November 2014 and began logging the vertical
section at 8,340'.
Diesel invert drilling fluid with a mud weight of ranging from 9.6-10.3 ppg was used during
remainder of the vertical hole and in the curve builds sections.

Directional Operations
RPM provided equipment and personnel for directional services. Ryan Energy Technologies
(Ryan) provided equipment and personnel for MWD services. RPM geologists worked closely
with RPM directional and Ryan MWD teams to formulate steering decisions to maximize
exposure of borehole in the pay zone. RPM well site geologists closely observed MWD gamma-
ray information and penetration rates to aid in steering decisions and dip degree estimations.

Lateral
Overview
Drilling fluid consisting of a closed system salt water brine (9.4 ­ 9.5 ppg) was used while
drilling the lateral section. Two 6" PDC bits were used to drill the lateral.
Bit #3 (6" Baker T406) was a PDC bit used in front of a 1.5° mud motor was used to drill the
lateral from 11,086' to 17,347'. The average ROP for Bit #3 was 107.9 ft/hr with 58 hours of
use. Bit #4 (6" Baker T406) was a PDC bit used in front of a 1.5° mud motor was used to drill
the lateral from 17,347' to 20,505'. The average ROP for Bit #3 was 101.9 ft/hr with 31 hours of
use.
Drilling operations were concluded at 00:50 hours on 31 December 2014 at a bottom hole
location of 9,890.36' N & 685.61' E of surface location or approximately 329.36' FNL &
2,470.87' FEL, NW NE Sec. 16, T155N, R101W.
Lithology
To aid in communications while drilling the lateral, a type log was created using the gamma
signature from the Glen 1-30 (Diagram 1). The target zone was an area marked roughly between
the "A" and "B" markers on chart 1. The attempt was made to keep the well bore in this region
for as much of the drilling operations as possible.

Upper Bakken Shale.
The Middle Member of the Bakken formation seen in drilling samples consisted of two primary
facies. The upper most was the off white, gray siltstone seen while landing the curve and
described above. The second was described as sandstone interbedded with a light brown silty
sandstone. This silty sandstone consisted of a siltstone that was sub angular and grey in color
interbedded with a light brown sandstone (Figure 6). There was a substantial amount of oil stain
present in the sandstone. Samples from the top of the Middle Bakken were described as:
SILTY SANDSTONE: light gray to gray, off white to white, very fine grain, friable to soft, sub
angular, moderately sorted, vitreous, moderate disseminated and trace nodular pyrite, moderately
calcareous, trace oil stain, trace intergranular porosity, possible fracture porosity
Figure 6: Photograph of silty sandstone with disseminated pyrite seen in the upper part of the target zone
As the bit approached the bottom of the target more silty sandstone was seen in sample (Figure
7). This silty sandstone was light to medium grey in color, containing a trace amounted of
sandstone and held together with a weaker calcite cementing. This silty sandstone comprised the
primary target zone and all attempts were made to keep the well bore there.
Figure 7: Photograph of silty sandstone with pyrite from lower "Target Zone" in the Middle Bakken formation
Gas and Hydrocarbon Shows
Total gas levels were recorded with an average background of ~40-50 units while drilling
the curve through the Lodgepole formation. Gas levels stayed consistent at the Upper Bakken
Shale with a peak of 50 units and an average background gas of ~ 40 units. Gas levels elevated
after the penetration of the Middle Member of the Bakken formation.
Total gas levels for the entire lteral were recorded with an average background gas of ~ 300 to
700 units with few notable peaks. Occasionally connection peaks exceeded 1,000 units. The
NOV gas chromatograph measured propane and methane all through the entire lateral.
Hydrocarbon cuts using trichloroethylene were weak to moderate though the entire lateral. The
target zone and above was characterized by weak white to yellow diffuse cuts and common weak
steaming cuts. No correlation was found between the strength of the hydrocarbon cuts and the
position of the bit in the Middle Bakken formation.
Summary
1) The Drummond 5501 42-21 5B was spud the November 17th 2014 approxinately 8 miles
north of the town Williston in Williams County, North Dakota. The vertical hole was drilled to a
total depth of 10,230'.
2) A mud program consisting of diesel invert (9.6 ­ 10.3 ppg) after surface casing and through
the curve build sections. The drilling mud was successful in maintaining stable hole conditions
and minimizing washout through the salt intervals.
3) One 8 3/4" PDC bit was used to drill the vertical hole from surface casing to a vertical depth
of 10,230' in a total of 61.5 hours. One 8 3/4" bit was used to drill through the curve, landing at
a measured depth of 11,086' in 18 hours. The entire 9,419' lateral was drilled in 89 hours with
two 6" PDC bits.
4) The lateral was targeting a 15' thick section of the Middle Member of the Bakken formation
situated 10' below the Upper Shale.
5) Gas levels of between 300 and 700 units were maintained through most of the lateral drilling
operations. Occasional connection peaks of 1,000 units were observed.
6) The Middle Bakken consisted of two gross lithology's. Directly below the Upper Bakken
Shale, was observed a dark gray/brown siltstone. Below this siltstone, a light grey siltstone
interbedded with light brown to white oil stained sandstone. The target zone consisted of off
white to white, light gray/ brown porous silty sandstone.
7) Hydrocarbon cuts were few to moderate through the lateral. Weak white to yellow diffuse cuts
and common weak steaming cuts were present in the target zone.
8) Drilling operations were concluded at 00:50 hours on 31 December 2014 at a bottom hole
location of 9,890.36' N & 685.61' E of surface location or approximately 329.36' FNL &
2,470.87' FEL, NW NE Sec. 16, T155N, R101W.
9) Exposure to target was 100% of the lateral. Oasis Petroleum's Drummond 5501 42-21 5B
awaits completion operations to determine ultimate production potential.

Oil and Gas Wells
Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
Thank you for your cooperation.
