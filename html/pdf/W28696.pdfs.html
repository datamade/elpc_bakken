Introduction
Emerald Oil, Inc. successfully drilled the Joel Goodsen 7-32-29H horizontal well
at an average Azimuth of 360.0º and an average Inclination of 89.99°, targeting the
Middle Bakken Formation beginning in Section 32, T 149 N, R 102 W, (370' FSL &
1100' FEL) in McKenzie County, North Dakota, drilling north into Section 29, T 149 N, R
102 W, with a spacing unit consisting of 1,280 acres. The well is located in the Moline
Field in the southwest portion of the Williston Basin, North Dakota. The main wells used
for correlation were the vertical and curve sections of the Davis-Choctaw Cross 24-32
Section 32 T 149 N, R 102 W, and Haugen 13X-34 in Section 34, T 149 N, R 102 W.
Operations Summary
Vertical Section
The Joel Goodsen 7-32-29H was spud on August 5, 2014. Brara
Geologic Consulting arrived on site and began well site operations on August 9,
2014. A Mudlogging Systems, Inc. Redbox gas detection system was used for
analyzing and recording total gas and chromatograph data. Surface drilling was
completed on August 6, 2014 and 9 5/8" casing was set at 2147' (MD). An invert
mud system was used to drill out from surface throughout the landing of the
curve.
The first BHA used consisted of (Bit #2) an 8 ¾'' Security PDC bit, a
Pathfinder 1.5° mud motor, and a HDS-1 MWD tool, which drilled out of surface
casing at 2147' (MD). This assembly drilled 7012' in 53.25 hours to 9159' (MD),
where a trip was made due to bit failure. The next BHA included (Bit #3) an 8 ¾''
Security PDC bit, a Pathfinder 1.5° mud motor, and a HDS-1 MWD tool. This
assembly drilled 1311' in 30.83 hours to the kick-off point (KOP) at 10470' MD,
10463.65' TVD, (-8048.63' SS). The KOP was reached at 16:00 on August 12,
2014 and a trip was made to pick up the curve assembly.
Curve Section
Drilling operations resumed on August 13, 2014 at 07:20 with the curve
assembly. This BHA included (Bit #4), a re-run of (Bit #3) an 8 ¾'' Security PDC
bit paired with a Pathfinder 4/5 lobe configuration 7.0 stage, 2.5° and 5.58' bit to
bend mud motor and HDS-1 MWD/gamma tool. This assembly drilled 66' in
14.17 hours before being pulled due to motor failure at 10536' (MD). The next
BHA was comprised of (Bit #5) an 8 ¾'' Security PDC bit, a Hunting 2.12° mud
motor, and HDS-1 MWD/gamma tool. This assembly drilled 824' in 28.35 hours
to the landing of the curve at 11360' MD 10955.84' TVD (-8540.84' SS). The
curve was landed at 16:45 on August 15, 2014 at an Inclination of 89.34° and an
Azimuth of 17.53°, with a landing point location in Section 32, T 149 N, R 102 W,
(949.51' FSL & 668.89' FEL).

Base of Last Charles Salt Formation
The Base of Last Charles Salt (BLS) was picked from samples and ROP at 9448'
MD, 9442' TVD, (-7027' SS). The BLS came in eight feet lower than prognosis.
Base of Last Charles Salt Formation Lithology Descriptions
Samples were caught in 30' intervals from 9150' (MD) to 9618' (MD)
(Mission Canyon Top) by manually catching rock cuttings and collecting them in
sample bags.
Samples in the base of the Charles contained abundant amounts of halite
that was off-white, semi-translucent, and frosty, with coarse grains.
The limestone/dolomite in sample was mudstone, light to medium gray,
very fine crystalline, slightly soft to occasionally firm, and occasionally silted.
Anhydrite in the formation remained predominantly white to off white with
occasional graying, microcrystalline, very soft but occasionally firm, amorphic
and massive.
Mission Canyon Formation
The Mission Canyon Formation top was picked from samples at 9618' MD, 9612'
TVD, (-7197' SS), four feet lower in TVD than prognosis. Formation background gas
through the Mission Canyon varied from 35 to 70 units. Faint yellow/white cuts were
observed, but were attributed to the presence of invert residue in the samples. No DST
was recommended or conducted.
Mission Canyon Formation Lithology Descriptions
Samples were caught in 30' intervals from 9618' (MD) to 10160'
(MD) (Lodgepole Formation Top) through the Mission Canyon formation by
manually catching rock cuttings and collecting them in sample bags.

Limestone in the samples was light to dark gray, gray/brown, sub-blocky,
soft firm, argillaceous mudstone to wackestone, with trace microcrystalline
limestone. Visible inter-granular porosity was absent to very poor. No oil staining
was observed.
Occasional dull yellow fluorescence was observed in samples when
viewed under a fluoroscope, though shows and rare faint yellow cuts were
attributed to residual invert mud in the cuttings.
Lodgepole Formation
The Lodgepole top was picked from samples at 10160' MD, 10154' TVD, (-7739'
SS), five feet low to prognosis. The kick-off depth of 10470' MD was reached on August
12, 2014.
Lodgepole Formation Lithology Descriptions
Samples were caught in 30' intervals from 10149' (MD) to 11154' (MD)
(False Bakken Top) by manually catching rock cuttings and collecting them in
sample bags.
The Lodgepole is predominately comprised of argillaceous limestone that
ranged from being a mudstone to a wackestone, shades of gray, mainly very fine
crystalline, firm-hard, occasional laminations, very earthy textures, and no visible
porosity with differing amounts of silt through the formation.
Very slow to slow diffuse white cuts were observed in the Lodgepole due
to residual invert mud and cleaning processes. Background gas ranged between
30 and 40 units with a trip gas of 4988 units observed at KOP.
False Bakken Shale
The False Bakken top was picked from samples, gamma ray, gas shows and
penetration rate at 11096' MD, 10917' TVD, (-8502' SS). Dull, even yellow/orange
fluorescence was observed in the samples when viewed under a fluoroscope. Cuts in
the False Bakken were moderately fast blue/white but were heavily contaminated by
invert drilling fluid.
False Bakken Shale Lithology Descriptions
Samples were caught from 11096' (MD) to 11119' (MD) (Upper Bakken
Top) in 30' intervals through the False Bakken formation by manually catching
rock cuttings and collecting them in sample bags. Samples were comprised of
shale and limestone.

Shale in the False Bakken was mostly very dark brown, fissile in part, hard
to brittle, blocky to sub blocky, and earthy textured with occasionally waxy
surfaces. Limestone returns were mudstone-wackestone with varying amounts of
silt, gray coloring ranging from light to dark, occasional laminations were
observed but showed predominantly massive characteristics. Platy to sub platy
breaking was observed, with very earthy texture, no visible porosity and
occasional trace amounts of pyrite.
Upper Bakken Shale
Upper Bakken Shale top picked from samples, gamma ray, and penetration rate
at 11119' MD, 10925' TVD, (-8510' SS). Dull, even, yellow-orange fluorescence was
frequently observed in the samples when viewed under a fluoroscope, cuts in the Upper
Bakken were moderately fast blue/white but were heavily contaminated by oil based
drilling fluid.
Upper Bakken Shale Lithology Descriptions
Samples were caught from 11119' (MD) to 11161' (MD) (Middle Bakken
Top) in 10' intervals through the Upper Bakken by manually catching samples.
Shale in the Upper Bakken was black, very dark brown, mainly fissile but
occasionally hard to brittle, blocky to sub-blocky, earthy, occasionally waxy,
carboniferous and petroliferous.
Middle Bakken Dolomite
The Middle Bakken top was picked from sample lithology, gamma and
penetration rate at 11161' MD, 10936' TVD, (-8521' SS).
Middle Bakken Lithology Descriptions
Samples were caught in 30' intervals through the Middle Bakken from
11161' (MD) to 20725' (MD/TD) by manually catching samples from the shakers.
Middle Bakken dolomite samples were light gray to gray, occasionally tan,
light brown, with rare mottling. Cuttings were blocky to sub-platy, soft to firm, and
microcrystalline to fine crystalline with a chalky matrix. Samples were frequently
argillaceous to silty, occasionally grading to sandy. Cuttings were tight,
exhibiting poor visible porosity.
Even dull yellow/orange fluorescence was observed in nearly all samples
when examined under a fluoroscope. Moderately slow to fast milky cuts were
noted throughout the Middle Bakken. Some cuts and fluorescence from samples

Closing Summary
The Joel Goodsen 7-32-29H was drilled from South to North in the Middle
Bakken Member. The target consisted of 10 to 13 vertical feet, starting 12 feet below
the Middle Bakken top and ranging to two feet above the Lower Bakken Shale. The well
bore is estimated to have been in target zone 96% of the lateral, with an average overall
dip of 89.99°. Surveys were taken every 30' throughout the turn in the lateral to 360º,
changing to 90' surveys through total depth (TD).
Formation dip showed as 90.5º in the beginning of the lateral between the
intermediate casing shoe and 11550' (MD). This section had an average of 24% C3 and
C4 with background gas averaging from 100 to 400 units. At 11550' (MD) dip leveled
out and then decreased to 89.4° until 12200' (MD), where dip increased to 90.2° and
stayed consistent until 14000' (MD). This section had an increase in average of C3 and
C4 to 35% with background gas averaging 300 to 1000 units. The well bore was
primarily located in the middle of target in this section.
At 14000' (MD) dip averaged about 90° until 16700' (MD). Background C3 and
C4 gasses averaged 37% in this section with total gas averaging from 400 to 1200
units. The well bore was primarily in the top of target zone during this interval. Dip then
increased to 90.7° until 17700' (MD). At 17700' (MD) dip decreased to 89.6° until 18400'
(MD) then increased to 90.2° until 18700' (MD). In this section gas averaged between
200 and 800 units and C3 and C4 averaged from 35% to 40%. The well bore was
primarily in the top half of target for this section. From 18700' (MD) dip decreased
significantly to 89.3° until total depth at 20725' (MD/TD). Background gas increased,
averaging from 400 to 1500 units while C3 and C4 remained high, averaging between
30% and 35% of the total. The well bore was predominately in the top of the target zone
in this section.
Nearly all samples exhibited spotty to even yellow/orange fluorescence when
examined under a fluoroscope. Oil cuts were commonly slow to moderately fast, diffuse
to slow streaming, with a light blue color, leaving occasional residual rings. Some
samples were contaminated with petroleum-based lubricant, which was used to achieve
adequate bit weight to drill and slide and reduce torque and drag. This contamination
masked some fluorescence and oil cuts in some of the samples.
After total depth (TD) was reached on August 26, 2014, Brara Geologic
Consulting remained onsite to monitor gas while short-tripping back to the shoe and
returning to bottom. A final 491 unit trip gas reading was detected upon circulating
bottoms up. On August 27, 2014 Brara Geologic Consulting was released from the Joel
Goodsen 7-32-29H location while the rig was preparing to run production casing.

Conclusions
1) The Emerald Oil, Inc. Joel Goodsen 7-32-29H was a successfully drilled nine-run
lateral well in the Middle Bakken Formation from a surface location in Section 32,
T 149 N, R 102 W (370' FSL & 1100' FEL) to a total depth (TD) of 20725' MD,
10964' TVD, (-8549' SS), with a bottom hole location in Section 29, T 149 N, R
102 W (255.81 FNL & 560.89' FEL) in McKenzie County, North Dakota. The well
was drilled from the intermediate casing shoe to total depth with three trips. The
first trip was due to tool failure at 11965' (MD). The second trip was due to tool
failure at 16753' (MD). The third trip was due to tool failure at 19873' (MD). A
total of 9395' was made in the lateral. Total depth was reached at 12:40 on
August 26, 2014. The Joel Goodsen 7-32-29H required 22 total days of drilling
and setting intermediate casing.
2) Ultraviolet sample shows in this lateral were slow light blue cuts with residual
rings. Total gas shows were poor to fair ranging from a 200 to 500 unit average
to a 1000 to 1200 unit average. Overall porosity was poor to fair, which is
common for the Middle Bakken Formation. Wellbore placement was within the
target zone for approximately 96% of the total 9395 feet of lateral, which should
allow for excellent production opportunities.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
