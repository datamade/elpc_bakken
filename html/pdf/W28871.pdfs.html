Hess Bakken Investments II, LLC
GO-Hodenfield-157-97-0607H-2 Lateral
INTRODUCTION
The Hess Bakken Investments II, LLC. GO-Hodenfield-157-97-0607H-2 (SE SE Section 31,
T158N, R97W), is located roughly 12 miles south of Wildrose in Williams County, North
Dakota. The GO-Hodenfield-157-97-0607H-2 is a double-section horizontal Middle Bakken well
located on a four well pad; it consists of one 10,743'-long lateral drilled towards the south
(183.87°). Drilling of the vertical and curve portions of the well was completed by H&P #309.
H&P #293 was moved onto the pad to complete the lateral portions of the wells. During drilling
of the lateral portion of the GO-Hodenfield-157-97-0607H-2, directional drilling and geo-
steering techniques were employed to maintain maximum possible exposure to the target
reservoir.
ENGINEERING
Lateral Operations
Lateral operations for the GO-Hodenfield-157-97-0607H-2 began on December 12, 2014.
Drilling began at a measured depth of 10,696' and was drilled in three runs totaling 94 drilling
hours. Salt water drilling fluid fluctuated in density between 9.4-9.9 ppg throughout the lateral.
The lateral was completed at a total depth (TD) of 20,714' MD on December 19, 2014 with a
closure azimuth of approximately 183.87° and a closure distance of approximately 10,743.36'.
The bottom-hole location is estimated to be 10,718.95' south and 724.45' east of the surface
location or approximately 309.19' FSL and 1,230.45' FEL, SE SE Section 7, T157N, R97W in
Williams County, North Dakota.
GEOLOGY
Prospective Pay Zone
The Middle Bakken Member was logged at 10,277' TVD (-8,026' MSL). The top of the Middle
Bakken drilling target was encountered roughly 18' TVD below the base of the Upper Shale
Member. A layer of limestone deposited 10' TVD below the base of the Upper Shale Member
with a thickness of approximately 5' TVD comprised the upper half of the targeted pay zone.
Sample returns of the targeted pay zone consisted of very fine-grained, slightly pyritic, silty
sandstone with dolomite and calcite cement as well as limestone with trace amounts of oil
staining and characteristic oolites present (Figure 1). Sample cuts were examined throughout the
lateral and displayed pale yellow streaming and diffuse fluorescence with speeds varying from
slow to fast. Sample returns from below the targeted pay zone consisted primarily of light to
medium gray colored silty sandstone with trace to common amounts of oil staining.
Figure 1. The Middle Bakken targeted pay zone, displaying oil stained silty sandstone (left) and limestone (right).
Oil and Gas Show
Total gas was recorded as units (100 units = 1% methane) and chromatography of C1 ­ C4
components was recorded in parts-per-million. Gas shows were encountered throughout the
entire lateral. The largest gas show encountered was a connection gas, measuring 2,434 units,
and was dominantly methane (C1) with a mud weight of 9.5 ppg. This show occurred at 13,706'
MD while the wellbore was roughly 2' below the targeted geologic window. Background gas
varied throughout the lateral, ranging from roughly 30 units at the beginning of the lateral to 250
units by the end of the lateral, as more open hole was exposed. In general, the highest gas shows
were observed when the wellbore was in the bottom 2' of the geologic window to roughly 3'
below the base of the geologic window. This 5' zone with the highest gas shows indicates a zone
of increased porosity (Figure 2). There was no flare or live oil observed during the drilling of the
GO-Hodenfield-157-97-0607H-2.
Figure 2. GO-Hodenfield-157-97-0607H-2 profile displaying ROP, gamma ray, and total gas data.
Geo-steering
Baker Hughes provided equipment and personnel for MWD and directional services for the
lateral part of the well. Directional drillers, MWD field engineers, Sunburst Consulting, Inc.
well-site geologists and the Hess operations geologist collaborated throughout the project to
make decisions regarding steering and exploration of formations for production of hydrocarbons.
The 10' thick target zone within the Middle Bakken was established by the Hess operations
geologist based on core analysis, electric logs from offset wells, and seismic data of the area.
The upper 4-5' of the targeted pay zone was characterized by cooler gamma signatures (40-70
API) that are associated with the presence of a limestone layer. The center of the targeted
geologic window was characterized by a warm gamma spike (95-105 API). The bottom half of
the targeted pay zone was characterized by warmer, more ambiguous gamma signatures (70-90
API). Overall gross apparent dip is calculated at 0.81° down (Figure 3). The GO-Hodenfield-
157-97-0607H-2 reached TD (20,714' MD) while staying within the ideal geologic window for
roughly 52% of the well. It should be noted that the drilling target line provided by the Hess
operations geologist was not always placed within the ideal geologic window.
Figure 3. Dip profile with estimated formation dips for GO-Hodenfield-157-97-0607H-2.
SUMMARY
1)
The Hess GO-Hodenfield-157-97-0607H-2 lateral was successfully drilled as a Middle
Bakken lateral at Ray field in 7 drilling days, reaching a TD of 20,714' on December 19,
2014.
2)
Samples from the Middle Bakken targeted pay zone consisted mostly of very fine-grained
silty sandstone, varying in color from off white to light-medium gray to light brown, with
dolomite and calcite cement and traces of disseminated pyrite and trace to occasional oil
staining. The limestone layer that was observed in the targeted pay zone displayed
wackestone composition displaying traces of oil staining and trace amounts of oolites.
3)
The highest gas shows were observed in a 5' zone that included the bottom 2' of the
targeted geologic window and roughly 3' below the geologic window. Background gas
values varied throughout the lateral ranging from 30-250 units. No flare was observed
while drilling GO-Hodenfield-157-97-0607H-2.
4)
Overall apparent dip of the GO-Hodenfield-157-97-0607H-2 was calculated at
approximately 0.81° down. The steering team successfully opened ~10,743' of wellbore
to the potentially productive Middle Bakken reservoir rock while staying within the ideal
geologic window for 52% of the lateral.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
