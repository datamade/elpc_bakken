1.0 INTRODUCTION
The Nelson 157-100-25A-36-2H is a south lateral Middle Bakken well with a SHL
located in SEC 25, T157N, R100W in Williams County, North Dakota. The primary pay
zone was 16'-26' under the top of the Middle Bakken. This pay zone was picked for its
production potential and quality of reservoir rock. The objective was to steer the well
within the defined pay zone and within the legal requirements of the state. Directional
drilling and MWD services were performed by Leam. Skylar Kjos, Patrick Haley, Aclam
K, and Adam Harris were the primary wellsite geologists, providing geo-steering and
mud logging from Columbine Logging, Inc.

2.0 SERVICES
2.1 Wellsite Geology
Geological consulting and mud logging started on 12/25/2014. Services provided
included; morning reports, noon reports, evening reports, sample examination, sample
recording via pictures, production of vertical and horizontal mudlogs, geosteering,
sample collection and bagging, sample mailing and a final end of well report.
2.1.1 Geosteering
Within the Middle Bakken, the primary objective was to drill below the packstone marker
at the top of our set window. The bottom of the cool gamma (the packstone marker) was
found to be 15' below the top of the Middle Bakken. Gamma patterns were compared
with the offset log and a TVD log was created while landing the curve to in order to land
in the targeted zone. Steering in the lateral was accomplished by calculating dip from
relevant gamma markers, as well as by using lithology, total gas and ROP to determine
our position within the formation.
2.1.2 Gamma and Surveys
Gamma and survey MWD services were provided by Leam. Proposed Azimuth of the
well was 175.97°. Surveys were taken every 30' through the curve and turn from 9690'
MD through 11072' MD. Surveys were taken every 90' through the lateral from 11164'
MD to TD at 20614' MD. The tie in used was at 2099' MD, 2098.94' TVD. Gamma
within the target window ranged from mid-140s to mid-80s api values. Marking the top
of the target window was a hot (high 140s) gamma marker, and marking the bottom of
the target window was a cool (low 80's) gamma marker. These markers were
encountered multiple times within the lateral to help confirm dip.
2.2 Mud Logging
2.2.1 Sample Examination
Samples were collected every 30 ft in the straight hole and build section, and every 30 ft
while drilling the lateral. Descriptions included; mineralogy, color, firmness, argillaceous
content, structure, texture, allochems, porosity, oil stain, and hydrocarbon fluorescence.
Carbonate identification was determined with 10% dilute HCl-, alizarin red and
calcimeter. Hydrocarbon fluorescence was determined using a fluoroscope with a UV
lamp.
2.2.2 Gas Detection
Gas was logged using a Bloodhound total gas/chromatograph system. The gas detection
system uses an infra-red detector to measure total gas and the chromatograph separates
and measures gases C1, C2, C3, iC4 and nC4. Gas was recorded in units where 1 unit
equals 100 ppm. The gas detection system measured gases: C1, C2, C3, IC4, NC4, H2S,
O2 and CO2.
3
The Bloodhound Gas Detection and Chromatograph system use digital signal processing
techniques and non-dispersive infrared and chemical sensors for gas detection. The
system uses a proprietary chromatograph, which has the capability to detect from 0 to
10,000 gas units. This translates as 0 to 100% typical naturally-occurring hydrocarbon
gas mixtures. Calibration is performed using National Institute of Standards and
Technology (NIST) traceable calibration gases. Lab calibration points include 0%, 2.5%,
and 100% pure methane. Complete immunity to saturation or damage in the presence of
high concentrations of both light and heavy hydrocarbon gases precludes the necessity of
constant re-calibration or zero referencing. This allows the Bloodhound to react to
hydrocarbon based gases from zero to 100% in concentration without dilution.
Lag time was approximated from a calculation of annular velocity based on: pump
output, open-hole diameter, cased hole diameter, collar diameter, drill pipe diameter and
bottom whole assembly. Connection gases were monitored to confirm lag time
calculations and thereby adjust lag time when needed.

3.4 Gas and Oil Shows
The vertical-build section was drilled with oil-based mud (OBM) and the lateral was
drilled with brine/production water. The OBM contributed a background gas of 30-50
units and saturated cuttings with oil, masking formation oil shows. Gas shows were
consistently between 1000-2000 units in the lateral within the target zone.
3.4.1 Oil Shows
OBM was used in the vertical, masking any oil shows. In the lateral part of the well, the
oil shows in the dolomitic siltstone of the Middle Bakken ranged from: NFSOC (no
fluorescence, stain, odor or cut) to v tr yel flor, stmg bri bl cut, sl tr sp brn stn. At times
the sample was far too contaminated with lube to record an oil show indicative of the
area. These sample oil shows were recorded as NFSOC or left blank.

7.0 SUMMARY AND CONCLUSION
The Nelson157-100-25A-36-2H is a south lateral Middle Bakken well with a SHL
located in SEC 25, T157N, R100W in Williams County, North Dakota. The primary pay
zone was 16'-26' under the top of the Middle Bakken. This pay zone was picked for its
production potential and quality of reservoir rock. The objective was to steer the well
within the defined pay zone and within the legal requirements of the state. The majority
of the well was drilled within the siltstone within our target zone. In-zone percentage and
footage are 71% and 29% out of zone. The gamma within the target window ranged from
miod-80s to mid-140s api values. Marking the top of the target window was a hot (140s)
gamma marker, and marking the bottom of the target window was a cool (80's) gamma
marker.
The targeted pay zone for this well was 16' to 26' below the top of the Middle Bakken.
The wellbore started in the middle of the window and moved down through section
below the target window. The first half of the well was drilled near the bottom of the
window near the cool 80s marker. Eventually, the well was up and encountering the hot
(165 api) gamma marker @ 15700' MD at the top of the window; this is also where the
four foot fault occurred. After steering the wellbore back into the target window, almost
the entire rest of the well was spent drifting between the hard-streak at the top and the
markers at the center of the window. At 19400' MD is where the wellbore worked its
way to the bottom of the target window and then finished back up above the packstone.
The faults were picked due to one or a combination of the following: gamma trends not
matching up before and after certain measured depths, gamma "spikes," and gas spikes.
The formation had an overall dip of 89.49° (including faults). The well was mostly
drilled in our set window, venturing out the target top two times and the target base three
times. The objective while drilling the lateral was to keep the wellbore in the warm
gamma marker, between 16' and 26' below the top of the Middle Bakken.
Currently the well is awaiting completion.
