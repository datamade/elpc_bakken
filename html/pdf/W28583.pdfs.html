INTRODUCTION
The Oxy USA, Inc. Schneider 3-28-33H-143-96 [SWSE Sec 21, T143N, R96W] is located approximately
10 miles southwest of Manning, North Dakota. The subject well was drilled in Dunn County, ND by the
Nabors X-18 rig in the Fayette Field of the Bakken pool. The subject well was planned with one
approximately 10,060' [Intermediate casing to projected total depth (TD)] long southerly lateral bore with
intent to intersect regional hydraulic fracture trends that might enhance reservoir quality within the Middle
Bakken. OXY USA intends to develop oil production in the late Devonian Middle Bakken target porosity
zone with a stratigraphy consisting of siltstone, silty sandstone and limestone with depositional lithofacies
ranging from onshore terrestrial to offshore marine deposits.
ENGINEERING
Surface, Vertical & Curve Operations:
A 9 " surface hole was drilled on July 28, 2014 by Nabors X-18 and surface casing was set at
2,372'. The vertical hole operations commenced on July 30, 2014 with Baker Hughes providing
equipment and personnel for MWD and directional drilling services.
The first vertical Bottom Hole Assembly (BHA), consisting of MWD tools, 1.50° bend motor and
Tercel MR616MCB PDC bit, drilled to a depth of 7,897'. At this depth, a trip was made due to a low rate
of penetration (ROP). Upon pulling this assembly, a new PDC bit (Security MM65D) and 1.50° bend
motor made up the second vertical BHA. This BHA drilled to 9,477' and was removed due to low ROP.
The third BHA consisted of the curve assembly (Smith MDi PDC bit and 2.0° bend motor) and drilled to a
kick off point (KOP) of 9,923' MD.
This assembly remained in the hole to kick off the curve at 9,923' MD. This BHA drilled to a curve
TD of 11,415' MD. This placed the well bore approximately 6' into a 22' thick (shale to shale) Middle
Bakken zone and 2-3' into the 4' thick packstone which marks the top of the interval. This 7º/100' curve
was drilled in a total of 86 hours.
No E-logs were run due to the close proximity of the Reuben Schneider 1-27-34H-143-96 well.
Directional tools were then pulled out of the hole and a string of 7" intermediate casing was run, set, and
cemented at a depth of 11,405' MD, after which the rig skidded over to the Schneider 2 surface hole.
Diesel invert drilling fluid was used throughout the vertical hole and curve build sections
maintaining a 10.0-10.2 ppg mud weight for stable downhole conditions.
Lateral Operations:
Baker Hughes again provided equipment and personnel for MWD and directional drilling services.
The directional drillers and Payzone geologists worked closely together throughout the project to make
steering decisions in order to maximize the amount of borehole exposure to the pay zone.
A six bladed 6" bit (Security MM64), 1.40º BHI mud motor, two stabilizers, and one MWD tool
comprised the lateral BHA (5th BHA overall) and was run in the hole on 4" drill pipe starting at 11,415' MD.
This BHA drilled to a depth of 11,986' MD and was pulled due to a MWD failure. The Security bit drilled
571' in 7.7 hours of total drilling time and was cored out when investigated. The sixth BHA consisted of
the same BHI motor with a new set of MWD tools, two stabilizers, one cross-over and a new Smith
(MDSi613) bit. Once on bottom, the assembly drilled 66' and it was observed that the MWD tools failed
and the BHA was pulled to replace them. The same BHA with a new string of MWD tools were re-run
back into the hole. This BHA encountered a Lower Bakken Shale strike at 14,268'. The decision was to
pull back to a MD of 13,647' and perform an open-hole sidetrack. Once drilling resumed, this BHA drilled
to 21,261' where TD was reached. The lateral was drilled in 10 days in total with two trips for MWD
failure and one short trip for the sidetrack. Moreover, spud to TD was a total of 20 days.
The lateral resulted in a TD of 21,261' MD (10,718.48' TVD) at a final azimuth of 179.46º,
resulting in a closure vertical section of 10,895.78'. The BHL is located 10,895.30' South & 101.97' East of
surface location or approx. 270.70' FSL & 2,008.03' FEL, SW SW Section 33, T143N, R96W (Figure 2).
Salt water drilling fluid was used from the intermediate casing to TD with mud weights throughout
the lateral varying from 9.6-9.9 ppg.

GEOLOGIC EVALUATION
Methods:
Geologic supervision of the well was provided by Payzone Consulting, Inc. with three well site geologists.
Shane Nicklaus, Bob Troutman and Laura Godowski drilled the vertical and curve, and Shane Nicklaus
and Bob Troutman drilled the horizontal sections. A wireless Totco Gas WatchTM gas chromatograph was
interfaced with a Rigwatch electronic data recorder system and provided total gas, C1, and C3
information for viewing around location. Lagged samples were caught in 30' intervals from 8,000' to
11,415' and 100' intervals from 11,415'. Cuttings were examined under a binocular microscope and
complete lithologic descriptions are provided in the lithology document within this evaluation. Two sets of
dry cuttings were collected, with one set sent to the North Dakota Geological Survey Core Library and the
second set sent to Ed Schirm, the project geologist overseeing operations.

LATERAL GEOLOGY
Gamma Signature:
Within the Middle Bakken silty sandstone/siltstone/limestone target interval, gamma counts varied
between 29 and 120 API. The packstone (20-36 API) was present when landing the curve. However, the
packstone pinched out at or before the wellbore approached 16,400' MD.
A hotter, dirtier silty
sandstone/siltstone (95-120 API) replaced the limestone in this section of the lateral. The center of target
zone depicted high 30's- low 60's counts with gamma trending towards the 70's-80's counts at the top
and bottom of the 8' target window. Three hard streaks were encountered in this targeted interval and
located near the top, middle and bottom of target zone. These hard streaks depicted gamma counts
ranging from the mid-40's to the mid-50's API and permitting excellent tracking for the well bore when in
this vicinity.
As the lateral commenced after casing operations, there was excellent control for geo-steering based on
the gamma signature.
The dirty siltstone, which replaced the packstone, allowed geologists to infer
where the well bore was in target zone and once encountered, directional was advised to slide down. The
packstone was approximately 4' thick and thinned/pinched out at or before 16,400' MD.
Below the
packstone, a silty sandstone consisted of 60-70's count gamma which trended into the mid 50's gamma.
This was where the hard streak was encountered multiple times, permitting the well bore to deflect up.
Once the well bore was below this hard streak, gamma counts varied from 45-60 API (center of target
zone). Below the center of target, another hard streak was encountered and depicted counts between
68-88 API.
Lastly, located at the bottom of target zone was another 80-90's gamma marker which
permitted geologists to consult the directional driller to slide up. While rotating, the BHA had a tendency to
drop.
Around 13,900' MD, while drilling at the bottom of the target zone, the BHA began to drop. Directional
was not able to recover before we hit the Lower Bakken shale at 14,150' MD. Drilling continued to a MD
of 14,268' before drilling was stopped and the decision was made to short trip back to 13,647' MD and
perform an open-hole sidetrack. Troughing and time drilling were successful in starting the sidetrack.
After the sidetrack, the entire well bore was drilled in between the 22' projected shale to shale zone with
excellent gamma markers and trends that represented the stratigraphy within the lateral. These markers
and trends aided geo-steering tremendously. The wellbore was in Middle Bakken throughout the entire
lateral and 89% within the 8' targeted interval and 100% within the shale to shale zone.
These gamma markers were used for determining formation dip and depth within the target interval and
permitted efficient geosteering. They are plotted on the Wellsite horizontal log (Figure 6) and dip profile
(Figure 7).

Geo-steering:
The target zone within the Middle Bakken was established based on the best drill rates, gamma markers
and gas concentrations. An 8' zone of >6% porosity was expected to be encountered in the targeted
interval. Samples from the target zone varied in porosity, oil staining, color, cementation, and pyrite
content. Stratigraphic locations in the target zone were based on drill rates, gas shows, gamma ray
values and sample observations.
The Middle Bakken was penetrated at 11,291' MD; 10,757' TVD and consisted of siltstone, limestone and
silty sandstone when landing the curve. After gyro was run after casing operations, there was an 11'
discrepancy in TVD. After adjusting the MWD surveys accordingly with the gyro data, the Middle Bakken
was adjusted to a TVD of 10,768'. Initially, a 10' target window in an approximately 25' thick Middle
Bakken was selected. However, after the Lower Bakken shale strike, it was determined that the Middle
Bakken was 22' thick. Geologists thinned the target zone to 8' to correlate with a projected 22' thick zone
(shale to shale at TD).
The wellbore stayed within the 8' targeted interval for approximately 8,803' of 9,856' or 89% (rat hole out
of intermediate casing to TD). The well was drilled in between the 22' shale to shale target zone for 100%
of the lateral after the sidetrack.
Using the provided offset log information and the structural data
provided from Oxy USA Inc., Payzone geologists were able to anticipate and interpret the dip rate for the
well.
The TVD of the formation decreased 61.37' over 9,970' (first Middle Bakken contact to TD
projection at bit) resulting in an overall up dip of 0.35º or 90.35°.
A total depth of 21,261' was reached on October 1, 2014 at 02:30 MST. Total depth resulted in 100%
overall exposure within the Middle Bakken Member of the Bakken Formation after the sidetrack and 90%
within the defined 8' targeted interval. (Figure 7).

Summary

The Nabors X-18 drilling rig successfully drilled a two-section horizontal wellbore within the
Middle Bakken Member of the Bakken Formation after the sidetrack at the OXY USA, Inc.
Schneider 3-28-33H-143-96.

A mud program consisting of diesel invert (9.5-10.1+ ppg) was used from spud to curve landing
with salt water drilling fluid used from the intermediate casing to 21,261' MD (TD). Salt water
mud (9.7-10.1 ppg) was efficient for maintaining stable down hole conditions and permitted
adequate analysis of gas concentrations in the lateral. Background gas concentrations ranged
from 70-475+ units with connections as high as 475 units. These stable gas levels were
continuous throughout the lateral and the highest concentrations were recorded during the first
2,000' of the lateral.

A successful sidetrack was performed at 13,647' MD due to a Lower Bakken shale strike at
14,150'. The initial wellbore was drilled to a depth of 14,268' MD.

Steering was accomplished by sample evaluation and by following correlative drilling rate and
MWD gamma ray markers; making dip estimations based on intersections of these markers with
the well bore. Projections of dip were successfully used to maintain the well bore within the
Middle Bakken. A net of 8,803' (89%) of gross 9,856' of the lateral was drilled in the ideal 8' thick
Middle Bakken target with 100% exposure within the 22' shale to shale Middle Bakken Member of
the Bakken Formation after the sidetrack.

Samples from the target consisted of white to off-white, light brown, trace light gray, and light gray
to dark gray siltstone/silty sandstone. Traces of intergranular porosity were seen throughout the
lateral. Samples from the ideal zone contained a spotty to even brown oil stain.

The transitional packstone was present when landing the curve and pinched out during the first
half of the lateral. Two hard streaks were present: one in the middle of the target zone and the
other approximately 1' above the bottom of the targeted interval. These hard streaks permitted
the well bore to track nicely throughout much of the lateral.

The Schneider 3-28-33H-143-96 was a successful drilling effort as measured by gross exposure
to the target. The well will be fitted with a 4 ½" production liner and swell packers in preparation
for a fracture stimulation that will determine the true commercial value of the well.

Schneider 3-28-33H-143-96 Lithology
Rig crews caught samples in 30' intervals from 8,000' to 11,415' and 100' intervals from
11,415' to 21,261' (TD). Formation tops and lithologic markers have been inserted into
the sample descriptions below for reference. Sample descriptions began in the Amsden
Formation. Samples were examined wet and dry under a binocular microscope and were
checked for hydrocarbon cut fluorescence during the lateral. Hydrocarbon cut was
masked by invert mud through 11,405' (intermediate casing) where the invert mud was
exchanged to a saltwater mud for the duration of the lateral. Quantifiers in order of
increasing abundance are trace, rare, occasional, common and abundant. Subsea is
delineated as the negative numbers within the parentheses located next to the measured
depth or true vertical depth. Sample quality was excellent to good. Overall, the samples
within the Middle Bakken target consisted of silty sandstone with grains that varied from
off-white, white, light to medium brown with traces of tan to light cream, and light grey
in color.
Siltstone and limestone were present above the top of the target window.
Variances occurred throughout the lateral and are noted below.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
