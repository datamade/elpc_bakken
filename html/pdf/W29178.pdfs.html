Introduction
XTO Energy Inc. Moses 21X-9A NE NW Section 9, T151N, R98W] is located approximately 8 miles
north of Watford City, North Dakota. The Moses 21X-9A is a single-section horizontal Middle Bakken
within the Garden Field of McKenzie County, North Dakota. The well will serve to develop Section 9
with a southerly transecting lateral. The curve will be kicked off in the Lodgepole Formation and landed
within the Middle Bakken drilling target. Directional drilling and geo-steering techniques will then be
used in tandem to maintain maximum exposure to a 20' target window.

Geology
Lithology
Lithologic descriptions and sample analysis began in the Charles Formation in order to log the Base of
Last Charles Salt. Formations with notable oil staining and gas shows include the Mission Canyon, Upper
Bakken Shale, and Middle Bakken.
The Mission Canyon was drilled at a MD of 9,634' and a TVD of 9,632', which was 6' high to
prognosis and 10' low to Lundeen 31X-9C. The Mission Canyon consisted of primarily limestone,
grading to argillaceous limestone with occasional dolomitic limestone and anhydrite in its upper sections.
The Mission Canyon had significant background gas and multiple gas shows. Background gas over 80
units was common with large gas shows reaching 336 units. Trace to rare spotty brown dead oil staining
was seen throughout the Mission Canyon.
The Upper Bakken Shale (UBS) was drilled at a MD of 11,125' and a TVD of 11,003', which was 8'
high to both prognosis and Lundeen 31X-9C. The UBS was composed of black, carbonaceous and
petroliferous shale, characterized by very high gamma ray values (>400API). Background gas within the
UBS was commonly over 1,500 units with shows reaching 2,800 units.
The Middle Bakken was logged at a MD of 11,179' and a TVD of 11,023', which was 7' high to
prognosis and 9' high to Lundeen 31X-9C. The Middle Bakken consisted of light to medium gray-brown
with varying amounts of white to cream and light to medium gray, very fine grained, well to very well
sorted, and moderately, calcite-cemented silty sandstone. The 20' target zone of the Middle Bakken
exhibited trace to rare oil staining and trace intergranular porosity.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
