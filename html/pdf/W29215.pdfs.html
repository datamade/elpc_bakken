1.0 INTRODUCTION
RUUD 5493 41-23 5B is a North lateral Middle Bakken well with a surface location of 235' FSL- 488'
FWL T154N R93W Sec 23, Mountrail County, North Dakota. The primary pay zone extended from
10.5' to 30' below the top of the Middle Bakken. This pay zone was picked due its drilling properties,
its reliable gamma markers, for its production potential, and quality of reservoir rock. The objective
was to steer the well within the defined pay zone and within the legal requirements of the state.
Directional and MWD services were performed by Pathfinder/Schlumberger. Chris Haynes, Ben
Huffman, and Austin Wiitanen were the well site geologists providing geo-steering and mud logging
services. In the vertical portion of the well Chris Haynes, and Austin Wiitanen provided logging
services, and steering through the curve, all from Columbine Logging, Inc.

2.1.1 Geosteering
Columbine Logging arrived on location and rigged up for vertical drilling operations to provide mud
logging and geosteering throughout the curve and lateral. Our primary offset GR TVD logs were from
3
the RUUD 5493 42-23H well, located in 154N 93W 23 SE SW Mountrail County ND. From this
offset log a target zone of 19.5' was determined to be 10.5' to 30' below the top of the Middle Bakken
Unit. Within the lateral, the primary objective was to drill within the "TVD drilling window" defined
by the 19.5 foot section identified above. Gamma markers were well defined throughout most of the
well. An expected gamma cross section was provided by Nathan Gabelman from Oasis Petroleum and
can be seen as appendix B.
The primary marker targeted was the D marker. This target was identified in gamma as a
localized low of 50api to 60api. This area was bracketed above by a high of approximately 85-95api,
identified as being the C marker. Below the target is also bracketed by an extended area of moderately
high gamma ranging from 65-75api. Below the D marker, below the area of moderately high
indiscriminate gamma is a low identified as marker E. This low could range from 55-65api and is
generally considered the bottom of the drilling window. The bracketing of low values above and
below the moderately low target area, made the target window easily identifiable. Throughout the
lateral there was very little difference in samples. The major difference in lithology seen was a shift
from the arenaceous, moderate to poorly cemented siltstone, seen through most of the drilling window,
i.e. C marker to E marker, to the arenaceous packstone. This packstone is often referred to as the
shoal at the top of the drilling window, identified as marker B. The grains throughout the lateral
occasionally displayed qualities common with dolomitic recrystalization, although most of the grains
appeared to be siliciclastic, with calcium rich, possibly micritic cementation. These slight differences
in lithology were used to help correlate the location within the target window but were not distinct
enough to specify exact location alone. Steering in the lateral was accomplished by calculating dip
from relevant gamma markers, as well as by using lithology, and ROP to determine our position
within the formation. The well reached its total depth (TD) approximately 3 feet above the center of
the "TVD drilling window".
2.1.2 Gamma and Surveys
Gamma and survey MWD services were provided by Pathfinder. The gamma ray peaked at the upper
extent of the A gamma marker. The A marker is approximately 3 feet above the top of the TVD
drilling window. This gamma was approximately 90-100api. The highest consistent gamma recoded
throughout the lateral, and within our drilling window was approximately 90api. These values were
associated with the localized peak identified as being the C marker. The C marker is approximately 4
feet above the center of the TVD drilling window. The lowest consistent gamma was located just
below the top of the drilling window. This was associated with the B marker and had an average of
40api. This low gamma is often referred to as the limestone shoal. While drilling in the "TVD
drilling window" gamma fluctuated primarily between 50 and 90 API.

Samples were collected by Columbine Logging every 30 feet from 7900' TVD in the vertical to 7"

color and longevity of the cut. Acetone was used as a cutting agent.
2.2.2 Gas Detection
Gas was logged using a Bloodhound total gas chromatograph system. The gas detection system uses
an infra-red detector to measure total gas and the chromatograph separates and measures gases C1
(methane), C2 (ethane), C3 (propane), iC4 (iso-butane) and nC4 (normal butane). Gas was recorded in
units where 1 unit equals 100 ppm. The gas detection system measured gases: C1, C2, C3, IC4, NC4,
H2S, O2 and CO2, but only C1 through a total value of C4 (iC4+nC4) were logged.
The Bloodhound Gas Detection and Chromatograph system uses digital signal processing techniques
and non-dispersive infrared and chemical sensors for gas detection. The system uses a proprietary
chromatograph. This chromatograph has the capability to detect from 0 to 10,000 gas units. This
translates as 0 to 100% typical naturally-occurring hydrocarbon gas mixtures. Calibration is
performed using National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) traceable calibration gases.
Lab calibration points include 0%, 2.5%, and 100% pure methane. Complete immunity to saturation
or damage in the presence of high concentrations of both light and heavy hydrocarbon gases precludes
the necessity of constant re-calibration or zero referencing. This allows the Bloodhound to react to
hydrocarbon based gases from zero to 100% in concentration without dilution.
Lag time was approximated from a calculation of annular velocity based on: pump output, open hole
diameter, cased hole diameter, collar diameter, drill pipe diameter and bottom hole assembly.
Connection gases were monitored to confirm lag time calculations and to adjust lag time when needed.

speed ranged from moderately fast, to instant, and color was very dull milky blue-white to dark
yellowish brown. The overall ratings observed in the lateral ranged from moderate to fast very light
blue-ish white cut all the way to instantaneous brownish yellow cut. Oil was consistently visible in the
possum belly and on the shakers. Acetone was used as a cutting solvent.

6.3 SUMMARY AND CONCLUSION
The RUUD 5493 41-23 5B is lateral well in the middle member of the Bakken Formation. Spud date
was November 20, 2014 from a surface location of 235' FSL- 488' FWL T154N R93W Sec 23,
Mountrail County, North Dakota. The lateral was drilled to a total depth of 20,317'MD,
10,477.73'TVD, 10033.63 VS, (projected) on January 8, 2014 with a projected bottom hole location of
NWNW 285.32' FNL, 547.78' FWL SEC 14-T154N-R93W, Mountrail County, ND, USA.
The primary pay zone extended from 10.5' to 30' below the top of the Middle Bakken. Per the Geo
prog provided by Oasis Petroleum the anticipated dip of the formation was 90.30 degrees, based on a
subsurface structure map provided by Oasis Petroleum. Our primary offset was RUUD 5493 42-23H
well, located in 154N 93W 23 SE SW Mountrail County ND.
The formation had a fairly consistent dip rate that varied from 89.28 degrees at its lowest, (granted this
was for a very short period and calculation could have been affected by local thinning of the
formation) to a 90.65 at its steepest. Average experienced dip of the formation was 90.28 degrees
which is slightly less than the expected dip of 90.30 degrees. The total average ROP throughout the
entire lateral was approximately 106'/hr while drilling. The ROP varied significantly from the
beginning of the lateral to the end of the lateral. The average ROP in the first half of the lateral was
126 ft/hr. Were as the average ROP in the second half of the lateral was 91 ft/hr. This difference can
be accounted for through a number of factors. In the second half of the lateral hole drag was
significantly higher and the bits tendency to fall was slightly more extreme. This tendency to lose
inclination forced the directional team to spend more time sliding taking corrective action. This well
was completed with 3 BHA's. The well reached TD at 20,317' MD, 10,477.73' TVD, 10,033.63 VS
(projected) on January 8, 2015.
Currently the well is awaiting completion.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
Thank you for your cooperation.
