INTRODUCTION
The Hess Bakken Investments II, LLC. GO-Perdue-157-97-0112H-2 (Lot 1 Section 1, T157N,
R97W), is located roughly 11 miles north of Ray, North Dakota in Williams County. The GO-
Perdue-157-97-0112H-2 is a double-section horizontal Middle Bakken well located on a four
well pad; it consists of one 10,076'-long lateral drilled towards the south (188.05°). Drilling of
the vertical and curve portions of the well were completed by H&P #309. H&P #293 was moved
onto the pad to complete the lateral portions of the wells. Directional drilling and geo-steering
techniques were employed to maintain maximum possible exposure to the target reservoir
throughout the lateral.
ENGINEERING
Lateral Operations
Lateral segment drilling of the GO-Perdue-157-97-0112H-2 commenced on January 24, 2015.
Drilling began at a measured depth of 10,575' and was drilled in a single run, totaling 82 drilling
hours. Salt water drilling fluid fluctuated in density between 9.8-10.0 ppg (pounds per gallon)
throughout the lateral. The lateral was completed at a total depth (TD) of 20,369' MD on January
29, 2015 with a closure azimuth of approximately 188.05° and a closure distance of
approximately 10,075.58'. The bottom-hole location (BHL) is estimated to be 9,976.32' south
and 1,410.77' west of the surface location or approximately 313.09' FSL and 1,819.77' FEL, SW
SE Sec. 12, T157N, R97W in Williams County, North Dakota.
GEOLOGY
Prospective Pay Zone
The Middle Bakken Member was logged at 10,164' TVD (-7,826' MSL). The top of the ideal
Middle Bakken drilling target was encountered roughly 12' TVD below the base of the Upper
Shale Member. A soft limestone layer deposited 11' TVD below the base of the Upper Shale
Member with a thickness varying from 3'-5' TVD comprised the upper 2'-3' of the targeted pay
zone. Sample returns from the lateral portion of the well consist of very fine-grained, slightly
pyritic silty sandstone with dolomite and calcite cement with rare to common amounts of oil
staining (Figure 1). Sample cuts were examined throughout the lateral and displayed milky white
to pale yellow streaming and diffuse fluorescence with speeds varying from very slow to
moderate.

Geosteering
Baker Hughes provided equipment and personnel for MWD and directional services for the
lateral part of the well. Directional drillers, MWD field engineers, Sunburst Consulting, Inc.
well-site geologists and the Hess operations geologist collaborated throughout the project to
make decisions regarding steering and exploration of formations for production of hydrocarbons.
The 12' thick target zone within the Middle Bakken was established by the Hess operations
geologist based on core analysis, electric logs from offsetting wells and seismic data of the area.
The upper 2' of the targeted geologic window was characterized by cooler gamma signatures
(40-70 API) that are associated with the presence of a limestone layer. The center of the targeted
geologic window was characterized by a "hot" gamma marker (100-110 API). The bottom half
of the targeted geologic window was characterized by warmer, more ambiguous gamma
signatures (70-100 API). Overall gross apparent dip is calculated at 0.29° down, as seen in
Figure 3. The GO-Perdue-157-97-0112H-2 reached TD (20,369' MD) while staying within the
ideal geologic window for roughly 93% of the well.

SUMMARY
1)
The Hess GO-Perdue-157-97-0112H-2 lateral was successfully drilled as a Middle
Bakken lateral at Ray field in 6 drilling days, reaching a TD of 20,369' on January 29,
2015.
2)
Samples from this Middle Bakken lateral consisted mostly of very fine-grained silty
sandstone, varying in color from off white to light-medium gray to light brown, with
dolomite and calcite cement and traces of disseminated pyrite and rare to common oil
staining.
3)
The highest gas shows were observed at the bottom and at the top of the ideal geologic
window. Background gas values varied throughout the lateral ranging from 20-400 units.
No flare was observed while drilling GO-Perdue-157-97-0112H-2.
4)
Overall apparent dip of GO-Perdue-157-97-0112H-2 is calculated at approximately 0.29°
down. The steering team successfully opened ~10,076' of wellbore to the potentially
productive Middle Bakken reservoir rock while staying within the ideal geologic window
for 93% of the lateral.

RE:
Filter Socks and Other Filter Media

Oil and Gas Wells
Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
Thank you for your cooperation.

Flaring Alternatives:
Mobile Gas Treatment Units:
GTUIT is a mobile flare gas capture technology for remote natural gas liquids (NGLs) extraction and gas
conditioning processes. The systems strips out NGLs from the gas stream and refrigerates them for
storage reducing volatile organic compounds (VOCs) thereby mitigating environmental impacts by
significantly decreasing emissions from flare stacks. The Units can be combined and scaled up to handle
high volumes of gas.

Hess has contracted with GTUIT to provide mobile gas treatment units (MTUs). As of 6/23/2014, Hess has
five GTUIT units being utilized in the field and expects delivery of three additional units by 8/1/2014. The
units have capacities ranging from 500 MSCFD to 1,000 MSCFD and can be relocated from one site to
another. The units also can be linked onsite to provide a larger daily capacity intake.

Hess is developing methodologies that will result in an order-of-merit list of candidate sites at which to
deploy the MTUs, based on environmental, regulatory, economic, and operational factors. There are
commercial constraints present which pertain to Hess's commercial agreement with GTUIT; wells must
produce at least half of the MSCFD unit capacity and cover the initial connection fees in order for use of
the GTUIT unit to be economic.
Natural Gas Generators:

Natural Gas Generators are an alternative to diesel generators to provide electricity to well pads located
in remote areas. The units are tied directly into the natural gas production onsite and supplement liquid
propane for use during hourly production fluctuation. Natural gas generators require liquid propane to be
used when gas is not being produced from the well. As production of gas increases and decreases hourly,
the units may switch from propane to natural gas and then back to propane.

Hess performed two pilot programs to evaluate performance characteristics of natural gas generators
from two manufacturers. Both manufacturers require further development to increase their reliability and
performance of units.

Economics favor diesel generators over natural gas generators at this time due to cost and reliability. Pilot
program was not able to produce consistent results to move forward with field implementation at this
time. Natural gas generators will continue to be evaluated as an alternative in the future.
Bi-Fuel (Blended) Diesel Generators:

Bi-Fuel diesel generators are diesel generators with natural gas "makeup" kit (blend kit) allowing units to
use produced gas onsite to power the facility.

Hess is currently in the conceptual phase of evaluation of Bi-Fuel Diesel Generators.
