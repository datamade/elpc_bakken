Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.

Flaring Alternatives:
Mobile Gas Treatment Units:
GTUIT is a mobile flare gas capture technology for remote natural gas liquids (NGLs) extraction and gas
conditioning processes. The systems strips out NGLs from the gas stream and refrigerates them for storage
reducing volatile organic compounds (VOCs) thereby mitigating environmental impacts by significantly
decreasing emissions from flare stacks. The Units can be combined and scaled up to handle high volumes
of gas.

Hess has contracted with GTUIT to provide mobile gas treatment units (MTUs). As of 6/23/2014, Hess has
five GTUIT units being utilized in the field and expects delivery of three additional units by 8/1/2014. The
units have capacities ranging from 500 MSCFD to 1,000 MSCFD and can be relocated from one site to
another. The units also can be linked onsite to provide a larger daily capacity intake.

Hess is developing methodologies that will result in an order-of-merit list of candidate sites at which to
deploy the MTUs, based on environmental, regulatory, economic, and operational factors. There are
commercial constraints present which pertain to Hess's commercial agreement with GTUIT; wells must
produce at least half of the MSCFD unit capacity and cover the initial connection fees in order for use of
the GTUIT unit to be economic.
Gas Lift Technology:

Gas lift technology pushes high pressured gas down a well in order to lift oil that was not obtainable by
other means. A portion of the natural gas produced from the well is cycled through a compressor to
"pressurize" the gas which is then sent down hole. The remaining produced natural gas from the well is
put into sales or flared. The "pressurized" gas returns to the surface with the produced oil and repeats the
cycle. Some gas lift technologies burn part of the produced gas in a reciprocating engine as part of the
compression process. Other gas lift technologies are a closed system which would not reduce any flaring
onsite.

Hess is not currently utilizing this system because compressed gas is piped from the TGP for gas lift wells.
The company continues to evaluate this technology on remote well locations.
Bi-Fuel Rig Conversion:

Bi-Fuel Conversion is a process where diesel engines are converted to operate on a combination of diesel
fuel and natural gas.

Hess has partnered with Nabors Drilling to equip six "PACE B" rigs operating in the Bakken with Bi-Fuel
Kits. GTI Altronics is a retrofit technology allowing diesel engines to operate on a mixture of natural gas
and diesel fuel. Boiler sheds will be converted to run on 100% natural gas during winter operations. The
units have a gas requirement of 50%. The Bi-Fuel System required approximately 75-100 MSCFD and the
boilers require approx. 100-150 MSCFD.

Hess is unable to utilize this technology on depleted wells lacking gas production. Well surging creating
intermittent gas supply is another issue related to Bi-Fuel usage. Safety is also a concern as the system
requires aboveground gas lines.
Natural Gas Generators:

Natural Gas Generators are an alternative to diesel generators to provide electricity to well pads located
in remote areas. The units are tied directly into the natural gas production onsite and supplement liquid
propane for use during hourly production fluctuation. Natural gas generators require liquid propane to be
used when gas is not being produced from the well. As production of gas increases and decreases hourly,
the units may switch from propane to natural gas and then back to propane.

Hess performed two pilot programs to evaluate performance characteristics of natural gas generators
from two manufacturers. Both manufacturers require further development to increase their reliability and
performance of units.

Economics favor diesel generators over natural gas generators at this time due to cost and reliability. Pilot
program was not able to produce consistent results to move forward with field implementation at this
time. Natural gas generators will continue to be evaluated as an alternative in the future.
Bi-Fuel (Blended) Diesel Generators:

Bi-Fuel diesel generators are diesel generators with natural gas "makeup" kit (blend kit) allowing units to
use produced gas onsite to power the facility.

Hess is currently in the conceptual phase of evaluation of Bi-Fuel Diesel Generators.
Fleet Vehicles Bi-Fuel Conversions:

Similar to the bi-fuel conversion on rigs, individual vehicles can be converted to operate on either
compressed natural gas or gasoline/diesel. The vehicles would run on the compressed natural gas and
then switch to a reserve tank of gasoline or diesel when the natural gas is low.

Hess is currently in the conceptual phase of evaluation of fleet vehicle bi-fuel conversions. The company is
looking at the possibility of using compressed natural gas from TGP to fuel vehicles. Currently, one
refueling location has been scoped.

Because there is only one refueling location for compressed natural gas, it would be difficult to make this
project economically viable. Cost savings come from using compressed gas, rather than diesel fuel or
gasoline.

Individual production facilities are not being considered for use as compressed natural gas stations at this
time.

Hess will continue to evaluate this technology.
