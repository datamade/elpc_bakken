Lateral Leg:
Penetration of the lateral section started on December 29, 2014, at 16:21 hrs., at a depth
of 10,711 MD . We started drilling with a 6 Baker Hughes T406 6X18, Geo Force XL
motor 1.50 deg. Bend.
The well plan was to drill out of casing and verify landing inclination from the vertical
build then build at a dip of 90.52 deg. in the Three Forks D reservoir. Our first point of
reference the TF A marker gave us a repeat of 90.4 deg. When we drilled into Section
24, we continued to 12,000 MD until seeing the cooler gamma counts indicating the
wellbore was near the TF A . For the majority of the well we used the control points
and Dip Rates based off the previous Bakken Well the Tandem. We continued to drill
ahead in section following below the target line at a dip of approximately 90.4 to 90.6
based on the data gathered from the TANDEM well borehole eventually ending the

lateral at 19,600 MD at a dip of 89.88 deg... Based on the structure map and the control
wells, we expected the formation to be as expected at the beginning of the hole and then
take a downward turn the last half mile of the second section. The downward turn
eventually presented itself almost right at the expected position of 16,500 MD in the
borehole. The hotter and cooler gamma were seen very unevenly across the whole length
of the lateral however the cooler gamma noted by the TF A marker were always present
needed to verify dip rates respectively. We drilled the entire Three Forks wellbore
between the desired target zone, and the TF A marker, the entire cross section of this
lateral relies on good gamma information acquired. There were no trips during the course
of the lateral. The lateral ended at 19,600 MD, 10,371.91 TVD, on January 3, 2015 at
18:15 hrs.
The entire well was drilled with invert/diesel drilling fluid. The starting mud weight in
the beginning of the lateral was at 10.5 lbs. The mud was built up during the course of the
lateral from 10.6 to 10.8. Gas averaged at the beginning of the lateral at approximately
300-500 units, connection gases were on the average between 200 units up to 600 units
from the beginning and built 1500-2000 units mid hole dropping off to 800-900 units by
the end of the well.
There was a presence of 10% up to 50% light brown to brown/black oil staining observed
throughout the lateral and fluorescence of a slight to spotty dull, even dull to bright
yellowgold to yellowgreen to bright yellowgold, with milky white to blue white, slow to
fast streaming, diffusing to blooming cuts observed in the cuttings throughout the lateral
section. The cuttings were washed very well with Citrasolv solution, water, and/or a good
detergent to remove as much of the diesel invert as possible. Mineral fluorescence was
taken into consideration when describing the sample lithology. Both wet and dry cuts
were observed and photos were taken every 200 of lateral and also upon achieving
formation tops in the vertical portion of the well.
There were inconsistent gamma counts from the high 50 s to 120-130 s observed in the

Filter Socks and Other Filter Media

Oil and Gas Wells
Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
Thank you for your cooperation.
