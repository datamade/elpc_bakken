Introduction
Continental Resources, Inc. successfully drilled the Anderson 2-4H1 horizontal
well at an average Azimuth of 178.40º and an average Inclination of 89.81°, targeting
the first bench of the Three Forks Formation from a surface hole location Section 4,
T152N, R100W, (235' FNL & 1012' FWL) in Williams County, North Dakota, drilling
south to the southwest quarter of the southwest quarter of Section 9, T152N, R100W,
with a spacing unit consisting of 1,280 acres. The well is located in the Willow Creek
Field in the West Central region of the Williston Basin, North Dakota. The main well
used for correlation was the Anderson 1-17H in Section 4, T152N, R100W, in Williams
County, North Dakota. The other offset well used was the Stokke 22-22 1 in Section 22,
T152N, R100W, in Williams County, North Dakota.
Operations Summary
Vertical Section
The Anderson 2-4H1 was spud on June 20, 2014 and the surface casing
was drilled out on July 23, 2014. Brara Geologic Consulting arrived on site July
15, 2014 and first began analysis on the Anderson 3-4H. After drilling the vertical
and curve section of the Anderson 3-4H the rig was skid over to drill the vertical,
build and horizontal sections of the Anderson 2-4H1. Brara Geologic Consulting
started logging the Anderson 2-4H1 on July 25, 2014 at 19:05 and at a measured
depth (MD) of 9100'. A Mudlogging Systems, Inc. Redbox gas detection system
was used for analyzing and recording total gas and chromatograph data. The
Anderson 2-4H1 had preset 9 5/8" surface casing at 1961' MD. An invert mud
system was used to drill out from surface throughout the landing of the curve
section. The first BHA used included (Bit #1) an 8 ¾'' Security MM65D, and a

July 26, 2014 at 10276' MD, 10275' TVD, (-8372' SS).

29-30, 2014 and cemented.

Base of Last Charles Salt Lithology Descriptions
Samples were caught from the Base of the Last Salt Formation at
9116' MD to 9346' MD (Mission Canyon top), every 30'.
Samples high in the section contained abundant amounts of halite that
was off-white to cream, semi-translucent to translucent and frosty, with coarse
grain.
Dolomite was light gray to gray, tan, light brown, sub-blocky to blocky,
firm, and brittle. Texturally, most of the dolomite was microcrystalline and
mudstone. There was generally no to very poor inter-crystalline porosity
throughout the formation. Sample fluorescence was dull yellow/orange when
observed under a fluoroscope, though fluorescence is most likely due to residual
invert mud in the cuttings. Faint milky cuts that were observed were also
attributed to the presence of invert residue in the samples.
The limestone in the samples was gray to dark gray, and tan. Samples
were mostly microcrystalline and mudstone. Cuttings were predominately sub-
blocky, firm, with no to poor visible inter-granular and inter-crystalline porosity.
Traces of anhydrite were observed in some samples. Limestone samples
fluoresced dull yellow/orange when observed under a fluoroscope, though
fluorescence is most likely due to residual invert mud in the cuttings. Faint milky
cuts that were observed were also attributed to the presence of invert residue in
the samples.

Three Forks Lithology Descriptions
Composite samples were caught in 100' intervals through the Three Forks
Formation from 10877' MD to total depth at 21279' MD/TD.
The Three Forks dolomite samples were light gray to gray, occasionally
tan, light gray brown, and mottled gray and light brown. Cuttings were blocky to
sub-platy, soft to firm, and microcrystalline to fine crystalline with a chalky matrix.
Samples were frequently argillaceous to silty, occasionally grading to sandy.
Cuttings were tight, exhibiting poor visible porosity. Even dull yellow/orange
fluorescence was observed in nearly all samples when examined under a
fluoroscope. Moderately slow to fast milky cuts were noted throughout the Three
Forks. Throughout the lateral there was trace to abundant amounts of blue-green
marine shale. This shale was blocky, firm and waxy. There was more of the
marine shale above and below the cool gamma located within the target zone.
There was also a slight trace to an abundant amount of disseminated pyrite
found throughout the target zone of this Three Forks lateral. Some cuts and
fluorescence from samples taken were contaminated by the petroleum-based
lube sweeps used during slides.
Closing Summary
The Anderson 2-4H1 was drilled from North to South in the first bench of Three
Forks Member with the target consisting of fifteen vertical feet from seven feet below the
Lower Bakken Shale to ten feet above the Internal 1 Shale. There was one sidetrack
due to a possible pronghorn strike at 17173' MD. There was a trip made at 17173' MD
for the BHA and after not being able to get back to bottom on the trip back in the hole
the decision was made to pull back to 16275' MD and start a sidetrack. This decision
was made at 17:50 on August 5, 2014. Time drilling for the sidetrack started at 21:45 at
16275' MD and was successfully completed at 13:00 on August 6, 2014 when drilling
resumed. The target zone was lowered, for the remainder of the lateral, to fifteen feet
below the Lower Bakken Shale to five feet above the Internal 1 Shale. At 21279' MD we
were unable to get back to bottom again due to another possible pronghorn strike. The

target zone, leading to a Pronghorn strike at 17173' MD and a sidetrack at 16275' MD.

consistent through the remainder of the lateral. The expected updip remained absent

second sidetrack was begun at 20285' MD. Total depth of the lateral was called at

Conclusions
1.) The Continental Resources, Inc. Anderson 2-4H1 was a successfully drilled
three-run lateral well in the Three Forks Formation from a surface location in
Section 4, T152N, R100W (235' FNL & 1012' FWL) to a total depth (TD) of
21279' MD, 10791' TVD, (-8888' SS), with a bottom hole location in Section 9,
T152N, R100W (519' FSL & 1338' FWL) in Williams County, North Dakota.
The well was drilled from the intermediate casing shoe to TD with three trips.
The first trip was made for an MWD tool at 11169' MD, on July 31,
2014.There was a second trip for MWD tools at 12763' MD on August 2,
2014.The third trip was made for BHA at 17173' on August 4, 2014. A total of
10260' of open hole was drilled in the lateral from the casing shoe to TD.
Total depth was called at 21:35 on August 10, 2014. The Anderson 2-4H1
required nineteen total days of drilling and setting intermediate casing.
2.) Ultraviolet sample shows in this lateral would be rated as fair to good with
immediate milky cuts with residual rings. Total gas shows and overall porosity
would be also rated as fair to good. Wellbore placement was within the target
zone for approximately 81% of the total 10260' MD of lateral section, which
should allow for excellent production opportunities.

Continental Resources standard CCL, CBL, 4-1/2" liner running and testing procedures
Continental Resources' standard practice for running the cement bond log and casing
caliper log is to run both logs immediately after coming out of the hole after TD, prior to running
the 4-1/2" liner, to the deepest depth obtainable; however, if there are well control concerns that
require us to run the liner sooner, only the CBL will be run and the CCL will be run after setting
the liner.
Based on the CCL results, we determine the actual API minimum burst allowance for the 7"
casing. If the downgraded API burst pressure is below our minimum required frac pressures, we
will run a 4-1/2" frac string; if severe wear or holes are found in the casing, we will run a 5"
cemented, to surface, tie back string.
The CBL log is run in order to determine the top of cement, as required by the NDIC.
Our current 4-1/2" liner program for a 1280 unit is 30, evenly spaced, stages with 29 swellable
packers. The liner shoe is set approximately 180 feet off bottom. The shoe stage below the last
packer has 2 joints, a double valved float, one joint, and a ported guide shoe ­ appx 130 ft. The
liner is run using a running tool on the end of 4" DP. The 7" packer/hanger is set about 40 ft
above KOP between two casing collars but conditions occasionally occur that require setting
higher, either through unexpected failure or in order to isolate casing wear close to KOP.
Recently we have tried 40 stage liners and the trend to explore the optimum stage count will
continue.. Once the liner is at depth, a ball is dropped through the DP, the ball is pressured up
against the setting tool to approximately 2500 psi, and the 7" packer/hanger is set.
A push pull test is done to confirm the hanger has set. Then, a 4500 psi pressure test is
completed on the back side of the 4" DP to confirm the packer has set. The setting tool is then
backed off and the 4" DP/running tool is laid down.
Immediately after the rotary rig has been moved off the well location, the 7" csg and liner
packer/ hanger are tested to the frac pressure. The testers will rig up and test the tubing head to
5000 psi. Next a test plug will be run and set, using wire line, in the top of the 7" packer/hanger.
Testers will pressure up to our frac pressure, typically 8500 psi, to confirm the 7" is ready for
completion.
