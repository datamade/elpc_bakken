Introduction
Emerald Oil, Inc. successfully drilled the Dudley Dawson 7-2-11H horizontal well
at an average Azimuth of 181.02º and an average Inclination of 89.86°, targeting the
Middle Bakken Member beginning in Section 32, T 149 N, R 102 W, (370' FSL & 1200'
FEL) in McKenzie County, North Dakota, drilling south into the southeast quarter of the
southeast quarter of Section 11, T 148 N, R 102 W, with a spacing unit consisting of
1280 acres. The well is located in the Sheep Butte Field in the southwest portion of the
Williston Basin, North Dakota. The primary offset well used for correlation was the Joel
Goodsen 7-32-29H in Sections 32, T 149 N, R 102 W.
Operations Summary
Vertical Section
The Dudley Dawson 7-2-11H was spud on September 25, 2014. Brara Geologic
Consulting arrived on site September 29, 2014 and began analysis on the Dudley
Dawson 7-2-11H. A Mudlogging Systems, Inc. Redbox gas detection system was used
for analyzing and recording total gas and chromatograph data. Surface casing was set
at 2158' measured depth (MD) in the pre-drilled surface hole. Fresh water was used
while drilling surface, and replaced with a diesel-based invert mud system to drill the
vertical and curve.
The first bottom-hole assembly (BHA) used in the vertical included (Bit #2) an 8
¾'' Security PDC MM650, a Hunting 1.5° mud motor, and an HDS-1 MWD tool, used to
drill out of surface casing at 2158' MD. This BHA drilled 8312' in 79.5 circulating hours,
to the kick-off point (KOP) at 10470' MD, 10466' TVD, (-8049' SS). The KOP was
reached at 10:35 on October 2, 2014, and a trip was made to pick up the curve
assembly.
Curve Section
Drilling operations resumed on October 3, 2014 at 00:22 CST with the curve
assembly, which included (Bit #3) an 8.75'' Security MMD55M paired with a Pathfinder
2.5° bend mud motor and HDS-1 MWD/gamma tool. This curve assembly drilled 269' in
5.2 hours before being pulled due to motor failure at 10739' MD. The next BHA
consisted of a re-run of (Bit #3), a Hunting 2.12° mud motor, and a HDS-1 MWD/gamma
tool. This curve assembly drilled 561' in 19.55 hours to the completion of the curve at
11300' MD, 10949.52' TVD, (-8532.52' SS). The curve was landed at 16:15 on October
4, 2014 at an Inclination of 89.43° and an Azimuth of 189.67°, with a landing point
location in Section 2, T 149 N, R 102 W, (294.3' FNL & 631.3' FEL).

sub), non-mag pin by pin X/O, HDS-1L (directional and back up gamma). This assembly

Base of Last Charles Salt
The Base of Last Charles Salt was picked from samples and ROP at 9440' MD,
9437' TVD, (-7020' SS), five feet high to prognosis depth.
Base of Last Charles Salt Lithology Descriptions
Samples were caught through the Base of the Last Charles Salt from
9440' MD to 9613' MD every 30' by manually catching rock cuttings samples at
the shakers.
3
4
Samples in the base of section contained abundant amounts of halite that
was off-white, semi-translucent to translucent, and occasionally frosty, with
coarse grains.
The limestone/dolomite in samples was mudstone, light to medium gray,
very fine crystalline, slightly soft to occasionally firm, and rare silt. Anhydrite in
the formation remained predominantly white to off white, with microcrystalline
texture, very soft but occasionally firm, amorphic and massive.
Mission Canyon Formation
The Mission Canyon formation top was picked from samples at 9613' MD, 9610'
TVD, (-7193' SS), four feet higher in TVD than estimated by prognosis data. Formation
background gas through the Mission Canyon varied from 30 to 60 units. Slow blue/white
diffuse cuts with blue residual ring were observed, but were attributed to the presence of
invert residue in the samples. No DST was recommended or conducted.
Mission Canyon Formation Lithology Descriptions
Samples were caught in 30' intervals from 9613' MD to 10157' MD
(Lodgepole Formation Top) through the Mission Canyon formation by manually
catching rock cuttings coming over the shakers.
Limestone in samples was a light to dark gray, gray/brown, cream, sub-
blocky, soft firm, argillaceous mudstone, with trace microcrystalline limestone.
Visible inter-granular porosity was absent to very poor. No oil staining was
observed.
Moderate light green mineral fluorescence was occasionally observed
under a fluoroscope, though fluorescence was most likely due to residual invert
mud in the cuttings. Slow blue/white diffuse cuts with blue residual ring that were
observed were also attributed to the presence of invert residue in the samples.
Lodgepole Formation
The Lodgepole top was picked from samples at 10157' MD, 10154' TVD, (-7737'
SS), two feet higher than prognosis. The KOP of 10470' MD was reached on October 2,
2014, resulting in a trip gas of 2247 units observed in the middle of the formation.
Background gas was between 30 to 40 units until 10900' MD when gas increased to
1100-4000 units with a maximum gas show of 5516 units.
Lodgepole Formation Lithology Descriptions
Composite samples were caught in 30' intervals from 10157' MD to 11083'
MD (False Bakken Top) in the Lodgepole formation 30' by manually catching
rock cuttings in samples.
4
5
The Lodgepole was predominately comprised of argillaceous limestone
that varied between a mudstone and wackestone, ranging from light to dark gray,
with a very fine crystalline texture. Firm hardness was observed with occasional
laminations, very earthy textures, and no visible porosity with differing amounts of
silt through the formation. Slow white/blue cuts with blue residual rings were
observed but most likely due to residual invert drilling fluid.
False Bakken Shale
The False Bakken top came in two feet lower than prognosis at 11083' MD,
10918' TVD, (-8501' SS), confirmed by samples, gamma ray, gas shows, and
penetration rate.
False Bakken Shale Lithology Descriptions
Samples were caught from 11083' MD to 11117' MD (Upper Bakken Top)
in 10' intervals. Samples were comprised of shale and limestone.
Shale in the False Bakken was mostly very dark brown, fissile in part, hard
to brittle, blocky to sub blocky, earthy textured with occasionally waxy surfaces.
Limestone returns were mudstone to wackestone with various amounts of
argillaceous tendencies, ranging from light to dark gray, with occasional
laminations. Predominantly massive, platy to sub platy, very earthy textured with
no visible porosity and occasional trace amounts of pyrite were observed
throughout. Dull, even yellow/orange fluorescence was observed in the samples
when viewed under a fluoroscope. Cuts in the False Bakken were moderately
fast milky blue/white but were heavily contaminated by invert drilling fluid.
Upper Bakken Shale
Upper Bakken Shale top was picked from samples, gamma ray, and penetration
rate at 11117' MD, 10929' TVD, (-8512' SS). The Upper Bakken top came in two feet
lower than prognosis.
Upper Bakken Shale Lithology Descriptions
Samples were caught from 11117' MD to 11161' MD (Middle Bakken Top)
in 10' intervals through the Upper Bakken by manually catching samples.
Shale in the Upper Bakken was black, mainly fissile but occasionally hard
to brittle, blocky to sub-blocky, earthy, petroliferous, and carbonaceous. Even
yellow mineral fluorescence and moderate bright blue/white cuts were observed
when examined under a fluoroscope, partially attributed to intense cleaning due
to invert drilling fluid in the cuttings.

Middle Bakken Dolomite
The Middle Bakken top was picked from sample lithology, gamma and
penetration rate at 11161' MD, 10940' TVD, (-8523' SS).
Middle Bakken Lithology Descriptions
Samples were caught in 30' intervals through the Middle Bakken from
11161' MD to 20736' (MD/TD) by manually catching cuttings from the shakers.
The Middle Bakken Member was dominantly light grey to tan with some
light to medium brown grading to a limey, argillacious dolostone. White,
translucent mottling was observed in rare calcite inclusions. White and light
brown micro laminations were present. The shape of the cuttings was sub blocky
to sub platy. The texture was dominantly a grainstone varying from
microcrystalline to very fine grain crystalline. Visible porosity was generally poor.
Trace to small amounts of disseminated pyrite with occasional nodules was
observed throughout the Middle Bakken. Common cement varied between
dolomite and calcite.
Wellbore position in zone did not correlate consistently to composition of
color, accessory minerals, or availability of visual porosity. Texture ranged from
being a mudstone to a wackestone, with a high position in zone near the
packstone lithology showing more wackestone consistency. Blue-white
fluorescent shows during cuts were fairly continuous throughout the lateral.
Closing Summary
The Dudley Dawson 7-2-11H was drilled to the south in the Middle Bakken
Member with the target beginning at the bottom of the packstone to two feet above the
Lower Bakken Shale, or 13' overall. The well bore is estimated to have been in target
zone 100% of the lateral. The overall dip was 90.1°.
Formation climbed to 90.3º in the beginning of the lateral between the
intermediate casing shoe to 14000' MD. This section had an average of zero to nine
percent C3 and C4 with background gas averaging from 300 to 450 units. At 14000' MD
formation dip began to level out to 90.0°
From 14000' MD to 15600' MD a localized dip of 90.18° was observed. Gas
through this section of the well averaged between 300 and 600 units with zero to 12%
C3 and C4. The middle of the target showed the highest gas readings with higher C3
and C4.
At 15600' MD dip began to come down towards 89.5° for the next 1300'. From
16900' MD to 18400' MD formation dip was 90°. Formation gases were averaged

between 200 and 350 units with 15 to 20% C3 and C4. Formation dip came up to 90.4°
between 18400' MD to 18800' MD.
At 18800' MD the formation dip came down to 89.9° and remained for the next
1936' to total depth (TD). Formation gas between 18800' MD and 20736' (MD/TD)
averaged 200 units, with between 10 and 20% C3 and C4.
Most of the samples exhibited a trace to moderate amount of light yellow to
green mineral fluorescence when examined under a fluoroscope. Higher in zone small
amounts of light yellow oil fluorescence was observed. Oil cuts were commonly slow to
moderately fast, diffuse milky blue to yellow in color, with a few trace slow yellow
streaming cuts, leaving spotty yellow to blue residual rings. Some samples were
contaminated with petroleum-based lubricant, which was used to achieve adequate bit
weight to drill and to slide. This contamination masked some fluorescence and oil cuts
in some of the samples.
After total depth of 20736' (MD/TD) was reached on October 13, 2014, Brara
Geologic Consulting was released from the Dudley Dawson 7-2-11H location while the
rig was preparing to run production casing on October 14, 2014.
Conclusions
1.) The Emerald Oil, Inc. Dudley Dawson 7-2-11H was a successfully drilled
three-run lateral well in the Middle Bakken Formation from a surface location in
Section 32, T 149 N, R 102 W (370' FSL & 1200' FEL) to a total depth (TD) at
20736' MD, 10960.27' TVD, (-8543.27' SS), with a bottom hole location in
Section 11, T 148 N, R 102 W (251.53' FSL & 559.09' FEL) in McKenzie County,
North Dakota. The well was drilled from the intermediate casing shoe to TD with
in three runs. Total depth was reached at 18:17 on October 13, 2014. The
Dudley Dawson 7-2-11H required 20 total days of drilling and setting
intermediate casing.
2.) Ultraviolet sample shows in this lateral would be rated as poor to fair with slow
to moderately fast diffuse cuts to slow streaming cuts with spotty to yellow/blue
residual rings. Total gas shows would also be rated as poor to fair. Overall
porosity was poor.

Filter Socks and Other Filter Media

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
