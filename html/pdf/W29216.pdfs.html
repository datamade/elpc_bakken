1.0 INTRODUCTION
RUUD 5493 41-23 4T3 is a North lateral Three Forks 3rd Bench well with a surface
location of 235' FSL- 455' FWL T154N R93W Sec 23, Mountrail County, North Dakota.
The primary pay zone extended from 9.5' to 32.5' below the top of the Three Forks 3rd
Bench. This pay zone was picked due its drilling properties, its reliable gamma markers,
and for its production potential and quality of reservoir rock. The objective was to steer
the well within the defined pay zone and within the legal requirements of the state.
Directional and MWD services were performed by Pathfinder/Schlumberger. Chris
Haynes and Austin Wiitanen were the well site geologists providing geo-steering and
mud logging services. In the vertical portion of the well Chris Haynes, and Austin
Wiitanen provided logging services, and steering through the curve, all from Columbine
Logging, Inc.

2.1 Well site Geology (Columbine Logging, Inc.)
Geological consulting and mud logging, for the vertical, started on 12/10/2014 and ran
until 12/30/2014. Services provided included; Morning reports, Afternoon reports,
Evening reports, Midnight reports, Sample examination, Production of lithographic logs,
Geo steering, Sample collection, sample mailing, and a final end of well report.
2.1.1 Geosteering
Columbine Logging arrived on location and rigged up for vertical drilling operations to
provide mud logging and geosteering throughout the curve and lateral. Our primary
offset GR TVD logs were from the RUUD 5493 42-23H well, located in 154N 93W 23
SE SW Mountrail County ND. From this offset log a target zone of 23' was determined
to be 9.5' to 32.5' below the top of the Three Forks 3rd Bench. Within the lateral, the
primary objective was to drill within the "TVD drilling window" defined by the 23 foot
section identified above. Gamma markers were well defined throughout most of the well.
An expected gamma cross section was provided by Nathan Gabelman from Oasis
Petroleum and can be seen as appendix B.
The primary markers targeted were bracketed by the B and C markers. This
target was identified in gamma as a localized high of 95api to 105 api. This area was
bracketed above by a low of approximately 65-70api, identified as being the lower extent
of the B marker. Below the target is also bracketed by a localized low identified as being
the center of the target window and the upper extent of marker C. The values of this
localized low ranged from 65-75api. Below the C marker was a low associated identified
as marker D. This low could range from 50-60api. The bracketing of low values above
and below the moderately high target area, made the target window fairly easily
distinguishable. Throughout the lateral there was very little difference in samples. The
major differences in lithology seen were: a shift from an argillaceous red silt stone to an
arenaceous white to buff siltstone. This difference was often associated with slight
differences in percentage of the light green shale seen throughout the lateral, and the
occasional appearance of trace amounts of pyrite. The upper portion of the window had a
much higher level of cementation experience between the silt grains. This area was
identified as being white to grey with occasional brown grains cemented in a possibly
dolomitic to calcareous matrix. These slight differences in lithology were used to help
correlate the location within the target window but were not distinct enough to specify
exact location alone. Steering in the lateral was accomplished by calculating dip from
relevant gamma markers, as well as by using lithology, and ROP to determine our
position within the formation. The well reached its total depth (TD) approximately 2 feet
above the center of the "TVD drilling window".

2.2 Mud Logging (Columbine Logging, Inc.)
2.2.1 Sample Examination
Samples were collected by Columbine Logging every 30 feet from 7900' TVD in the
vertical to 7" casing point at 11,167'MD and 10,745 TVD. While drilling the lateral,
Columbine Logging collected samples every 30 feet, when possible, and 60 ft when ROP
was above 150 feet per hour. Sample descriptions included; Lithology, color, firmness,
grain size, structure, texture, oil stain, and hydrocarbon fluorescence. Hydrocarbon
fluorescence was determined using a fluoroscope with a UV lamp. The ratings are based
on 7 descriptors: none, slight trace, trace, fair, moderate, good, and Excellent. The
descriptor used is based on the loggers' observations and best judgment of brilliance,
color and longevity of the cut. Acetone was used as a cutting agent.
2.2.2 Gas Detection
Gas was logged using a Bloodhound total gas chromatograph system. The gas detection
system uses an infra-red detector to measure total gas and the chromatograph separates
and measures gases C1 (methane), C2 (ethane), C3 (propane), iC4 (iso-butane) and nC4
(normal butane). Gas was recorded in units where 1 unit equals 100 ppm. The gas
detection system measured gases: C1, C2, C3, IC4, NC4, H2S, O2 and CO2, but only C1
through a total value of C4 (iC4+nC4) were logged.
The Bloodhound Gas Detection and Chromatograph system uses digital signal processing
techniques and non-dispersive infrared and chemical sensors for gas detection. The
5
system uses a proprietary chromatograph. This chromatograph has the capability to
detect from 0 to 10,000 gas units. This translates as 0 to 100% typical naturally-
occurring hydrocarbon gas mixtures. Calibration is performed using National Institute of
Standards and Technology (NIST) traceable calibration gases. Lab calibration points
include 0%, 2.5%, and 100% pure methane. Complete immunity to saturation or damage
in the presence of high concentrations of both light and heavy hydrocarbon gases
precludes the necessity of constant re-calibration or zero referencing. This allows the
Bloodhound to react to hydrocarbon based gases from zero to 100% in concentration
without dilution.
Lag time was approximated from a calculation of annular velocity based on: pump
output, open hole diameter, cased hole diameter, collar diameter, drill pipe diameter and
bottom hole assembly. Connection gases were monitored to confirm lag time calculations
and adjust lag time when needed.

4.1 Oil Shows
Invert was used as a drilling fluid during the vertical and curve sections of the well. The
use of invert makes oil show calculations impossible during these sections. Oil was
minimally present throughout most of the lateral. Fluorescence in the cuttings was
described as: Dull yellow to occasionally bright yellow hydrocarbon fluorescence and
ranged from scattered to consistent. The overall oil show was rated on 7 descriptors:
none, slight trace, trace, fair, moderate, good, and Excellent. The descriptor used, are
based on the loggers' observations and best judgment of brilliance, color and longevity of
the cut. The descriptors used to describe the speed of the cut range from very slow to
fast. Color descriptors are just that, the color observed by the logger. During the lateral
the speed ranged from no cut, to very slow, and color was very dull milky blue-white.
The overall ratings observed in the lateral ranged from no cut to fast very light blue-ish
white cut. Oil was never visible in the possum belly and on the shakers. Acetone was
used as a cutting solvent. There were a few points were the directional team required
enough lube in the mud that discerning fluorescence and cut from oil verses lube became
impossible to accomplish accurately. So intermittently throughout the lateral there are
samples were no oil shows were recorded.

6.0 WELLBORE
The surface location of RUUD 5493 41-23 4T3 is 235' FSL- 455' FWL T154N
R93W Sec 23, Mountrail County, North Dakota. The ground elevation is 2278' and KB
elevation was 2303', referenced to the Kelly bushing of Ensign 158. The curve was
landed roughly 2.5 feet below the center of the target window, at 11,167'MD; 10,745'
TVD, within the Three Forks 3rd Bench Formation.
From the 7" shoe to TD the gamma markers throughout the formation had a fairly
consistent pattern. The primary marker targeted was the low gamma (approximately 60-
70api) found between the B and C markers. This target was identified in gamma as a
localized low in values, bracketed above by a moderate high of approximately 90-100api,
known as marker B. Below the target is also bracketed by a moderately high values
ranging from 95-105api. Directly below this area of high gamma is another low ranging
from 60-70api and is considered the center of the drilling window. This low is also
considered to be the upper edge of the C marker. Below the C marker is a low associated
with the D marker. This low was the lowest values experienced within the lateral and
could range 50-60api. The bracketing characteristics made the target window fairly
easily distinguishable.
The bit tended to climb throughout most of the well bore, with only a few
exceptions. The bit only did fully exit the target window at 15,673'MD, and stayed out
of the window till 16,066' MD.
The total length of the lateral from shoe to TD was 9418 feet in length. We spent
approximately 393 feet outside the window. The 393 feet outside the window accounted
for 4.2 percent of the lateral being out of the window, and 95.8 percent within the
window. A TD of 20,585' MD, 10,683.06 TVD, and location NWNW 280' FNL, 536.17'
FEL SEC 14-T154N-R93W, Mountrail County, ND, USA, was reached at 14:12 CST on
12/30/2014.

6.3 SUMMARY AND CONCLUSION
The RUUD 5493 41-23 4T3 is a single lateral Three Forks 3rd Bench well, spud on November 20,
2014 from a surface location of 235' FSL- 455' FWL T154N R93W Sec 23, Mountrail County, North
Dakota. The lateral was drilled to a total depth of 20,585' MD, 10,683.06' TVD,
10044.89 VS, (projected) on December 30, 2014 with a projected bottom hole location of NWNW
280' FNL & 536.17' FEL Sec.14 T154N R93W, Mountrail County, ND, USA.
The primary pay zone extended 23' was determined to be 9.5' to 32.5' below the top of the Three
Forks 3rd Bench. Per the Geo prog provided by Oasis Petroleum the anticipated dip of the formation
was 90.25 degrees, based on a subsurface structure map provided by Oasis Petroleum. Our primary
offset was RUUD 5493 42-23H well, located in 154N 93W 23 SE SW Mountrail County ND.
The formation had a fairly consistent dip rate that varied from 88.47 degrees at its lowest, (granted this
was for a very short period and calculation could have been affected by local thinning of the
formation) to a 91.15 at its steepest. Average experienced dip of the formation was 90.35 degrees
which is greater than the expected dip of 90.25 degrees. The total average ROP throughout the entire
lateral was approximately 111'/hr while drilling. This well was completed with 6 BHA's. The well
reached TD at 20,585' MD, 10,683.06' TVD, 10,044.89 VS (projected) on December 30, 2014.
Currently the well is awaiting completion.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
