Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.

Purpose of the Plan
The purpose of the emergency plan is to prepare Oasis Operations and Regulatory
Agencies in the event that an emergency will occur that would cause an interruption in
operations or a potential release of hydrocarbons from the location due to an unforeseen
emergency. Flooding is a unique type of potential emergency, although the contact
information and general response procedure will not vary from this plan.
The National Weather Service in Bismarck will issue warnings due to spring snow melt
ice jams, flash floods, and main river flooding; they normally will indicate which areas
have the flood warnings and the expected rise in height of water. The National Weather
Service will normally issue first a Flood Watch next a Flood Warning.
A Flood Watch is issued when the risk of a hazardous hydrologic event has increased
significantly, but its occurrence, location, and/or timing is still uncertain. It is intended to
Page 3 of 7
provide enough lead time so those who need to set their plans in motion can do so.
Watches evolve into warnings, advisories, or they are canceled.
A Flood Warning is issued when the risk of a hazardous hydrologic event is occurring,
is imminent, or has a very high probability of occurrence. A warning is used for
conditions posing a threat to life or property. The NWS is the single "official" voice
when issuing warnings for life-threatening situations.
A Flood Advisory is for less serious conditions that cause significant inconvenience and,
if caution is not exercised, could lead to situations that may threaten life and/or property.
Up to date tracking information can be obtained by monitor the NOAA website at
http://www.noaawatch.gov/floods.php or NWS Advanced Hydrologic Prediction
Service . Another useful site that gives links to NOAA, US Army Corp of Engineers, and
NWS is the North Dakota Department of Emergency Services located:
http://www.nd.gov/des/info/flood.html . This website also has a listing of local county
emergency managers (click on "Local Resources") on the same site; for the area of
concern which is both McKenzie and Williams County.
This plan provides for Operational response should an emergency jeopardize the well site
location. To be effective, the plan requires the cooperation and effort of each person and
agency participating in the operation of the well. Each person must know his/her
responsibilities and duties should the location face an emergency scenario. Each person
and agency should familiarize themselves with the plan and make any suggestions that
will improve the plan.
It is the intention of Oasis Petroleum to make every effort to provide adequate safeguards
against harm to persons or the environment in the event of an emergency or flood that
may jeopardize this location. However, the initiative rests with the suggestions of the
individuals involved in the operations of the well, who are welcomed to act as a
fundamental tool for providing the safest working conditions possible in the event of an
emergency by following the plan and providing adequate communication to those
involved should the plan need modification. During potential flooding season the well
site should be prepared to implement the plan under short notice.
Operating Procedures
The intent is to produce the well in a way that will not damage the reservoir nor result in
the uncontrolled release of hydrocarbon on the surface. Any spills of hydrocarbons
should be reported to the Production Superintendent and the appropriate governmental
agencies. Operations clean up procedures should be implemented to minimize the impact
to the environment and approved by the appropriate governmental agency.
The complex job of producing oil and gas is a 24 hour around the clock job. Even with
much of the work performed during daylight, oil and gas are being produced at all hours
of the day and night. It is the job of the production operations people to keep the well

these operations will be the Lease Operator, Roustabouts, Production Foreman,

Emergency Procedures
Drilling Operations:
Drilling operations will be conducted during the winter period when flood or ice jams
severity potential is at a minimum. The drilling location should maintain a 24 inch berm
around the location to contain fluids on location. The drilling will be completed using a
closed loop mud system with no open earth pits. Once drilling and completion operations
are finished the pad size should be shrunk and the disturbed area reclaimed. The
Production pad should have a minimum of two foot diversion berm placed around the
production location. The production facilities will be located on the neighboring Kestrel
pad.
Should the threat of potential flooding occur during drilling operations, then all fuel,
lubricants, chemicals, mud, vehicles, and excess equipment should be moved out of the
flood zone and temporarily stored on the nearest available location at an elevation outside
of the potential flood level. A packer should be set in the deepest casing string and the
drill pipe set on the slips and excess joints laid down. The operations should be shut
down until the Flood Warning or Watch is canceled.

Procedure to Return well to Production post Flooding Event:
Production Superintendent (Conrad Ellis) will confer with the NDIC (John Axtman),
Production Manager (Andy Davison), and Operations General Manager (Eric Bayes) to
make an initial inspection of the location. Impacted Government Agencies should
communicate any additional information or concerns to the Production Superintendent.
Based on this site inspection, a plan will be developed to safely return the well to
production. An approved plan by the NDIC will be implemented and should consider the
following:
1. The Production Superintendent will continue to communicate with impacted
Governmental Agencies and modify the implementation of the plan as required
and modify the plan with any concerns or issues raised by the impacted parties.
2. The access road should be inspected to ensure it stable enough to handle truck and
equipment loads. Repairs should be completed as necessary.
3. The production site should be cleared of excess water within the containment
berm. The water should be tested to see if it can be used in Oasis Operations, or if
safe diverted outside of the berm (this will require a permit from the
NDIC).Otherwise dispose of the water in the proper facilities. After the excess
water has been removed, the site should be allowed to dry. If the 2' containment
berm has been damaged this should be repaired.
4. Any water trapped within the production containment berm should be tested for
use in operations or hauled to disposal as the volumes dictate. The site should be
allowed to dry.
5. Any excess soil or sediments deposited by the flooding event should be removed
or added to the containment berm, the location should be leveled and have
additional gravel base applied to allow safe operations. Correct drainage
diversion, culverts, cattle guards should be cleaned or repaired as necessary.
6. The production equipment should be evaluated to ensure that it is still stable and
has not moved due to the flooding event. All footings and cement slabs should be
inspect and ensure that they are still level.
7. Electrical motors and transfer pumps should be reinstalled. A certified electrician
should inspect wiring and repair damaged panels. The site should then have
electricity restored.
8. The pumping unit should be inspected for flood damage; repairs should be made
to level the unit. Lubricants should be restored.
9. All lines and valves should be inspected and retested as required. The Lease
Operator, Field Foreman, and Production Superintendent should re-commission
the well for production.
10.Seek final approval from the NDIC that the well has tested and ready for
production. Return well to Production.

DESCRIPTION OF HYDROGEN SULFIDE GAS
H2S is a colorless gas, which smells similar to rotten eggs in low concentrations.
In large concentrations or over long periods of exposure, the sense of smell may be
paralyzed. H2S is an extremely toxic gas that must be treated with extreme care to
prevent injury to people. H2S is heavier than air (specific gravity =1.19) and on still
days tends to accumulate in low places. This accumulation could build up and lead to
dangerous concentrations. However, if the H2S gas is warmer than air, it will tend to
rise until cooled off and could affect workers above the escaping source.
TOXICITY
Hydrogen sulfide is extremely toxic (poisonous). It is almost as toxic as
hydrogen cyanide. It produces irritation to the eyes, throat and respiratory tract. The
sense of smell can be lost in 2-15 minutes in low concentrations due to paralysis to the
olfactory nerves. The sense of smell can be lost in 60 seconds or less in higher
concentrations. Susceptibility to H2S poisoning varies to the number of exposures.
The second exposure being more dangerous than the first.
The results of inhalation of H2S may be strangulation in a few seconds of
exposure to high H2S concentrations. This produces symptoms such as panting, pallor,
cramps, paralysis of the pupil, and loss of speech. This is generally followed by
immediate loss of consciousness. Death may occur quickly from respiratory and
cardiac paralysis. One quick sniff of high concentration can cause death. Coughing,
eye burning and pain, throat irritation, and sleepiness come from exposure to low
concentrations.

HYDROGEN SULFIDE CONTINGENCY PLAN
OASIS PETROLEUM NORTH AMERICA, LLC
This plan provides for personal safety programs, precautionary measures, safety
equipment and emergency procedures, and sets forth responsibilities and duties
pertaining to drilling in a sour gas area.
To be effective, the plan requires the cooperation and effort of each person
participating in the drilling of an H2S well. Each person must know his/hers
responsibilities and duties in regard to normal drilling operations as well as emergency
and safety procedures. He or She should thoroughly understand and be able to use
with accuracy all safety equipment while performing their normal duties, if the
circumstances should arise. He or She should therefore, familiarize themselves with
the location of all safety equipment and check to see that it is properly stored, easily
accessible at all times, and routinely maintained.
It is the intention of OASIS PETROLEUM and the drilling contractor to make every effort
to provide adequate safeguards against harm to persons on the rig and in the
immediate vicinity from the effects of hydrogen sulfide, which may be released into the
atmosphere under emergency conditions. However, the initiative rest with the
suggestions of the individuals involved in the drilling of these wells are highly welcomed
and act as a fundamental tool for providing the safest working conditions possible.
The drilling foreman is required to enforce these procedures. They are set up for
your safety and the safety of all others.
PURPOSE
It is OASIS PETROLEUM intent to provide a safe work place, not only for its
employees, but also for those other firms who are aiding in the drilling of this well.
There is a possibility of encountering toxic hydrogen sulfide gas. Safety
procedures must be adhered to in order to protect all personnel connected with the
operation, as well as people living within the area.
OASIS PETROLEUM foreman must enforce what may seem to be stringent
requirements. This job will become easier. Carefully study the following pages
and use COMMON SENSE.

IGNITING THE WELL
RESPONSIBILITY
1. The decision to ignite the well is the responsibility of OASIS PETROLEUM
foreman. In his/her absence or incapacity, the contractor's Tool Pusher
assumes his/her responsibilities. In their absence or incapacity, the Contract
Driller will be in charge.
2. The decision to ignite the well is to be made as a last resort when it is clear
that;
A. There is a definite threat to human life and property.
B. There is no hope of containing the well under prevailing conditions.
C. Time and circumstances permitting, an attempt should be made to notify
the area office. If human life is threatened, the decision must not be
delayed.
INSTRUCTIONS FOR IGNITING THE WELL
1. Two people are required for the actual igniting operation. Both people will
wear self-contained breathing units and will have 200 foot retrieval ropes tied
around their waist. One person is responsible for checking the atmosphere
for explosive gases with an explosion meter. The other is assigned special
duties within the "Safe Briefing Area". Those in the "Safe Briefing Area" will
be on alert to the needs of the two people assigned to ignite the well. Should
fumes overcome either of these people, they will immediately pull him to
safety by the retrieval ropes.
2. The primary method for igniting the well is a 25mm meteor type flare gun. It
has a range of approximately 500 feet. If this method fails or well conditions
are such that a safer or better method is apparent, then the alternate method
should be used.
3. If the well is ignited, the burning hydrogen sulfide will be converted to sulfur
dioxide which is also poisonous. Therefore, DO NOT ASSUME THAT THE
AREA IS SAFE AFTER THE GAS IS IGNITED. CONTINUE TO OBSERVE
EMERGENCY PROCEDURES AND FOLLOW THE INSTRUCTIONS OF
YOUR SUPERVISORS.
