1.0) Well Introduction and Location
1.1.1) Well Overview
Zavanna, LLC Simmental 2-11 2TFH [SW SE Section 35, T154N, R99W] is located
approximately 16 miles southeast of Williston in Williams County, ND. The Simmental 2-11
2TFH is a horizontal Three Forks well within the Williston Basin consisting of one lateral drilled
towards the south. Wellsite geologists will begin logging during vertical hole drilling, just above
the Base Last Salt marker in order to gather data to ensure a curve landing within the desired
target. The curve will be kicked off in the Lodgepole and landed in the Three Forks. Seven-inch
intermediate casing will then be set to isolate the target reservoir, after which directional drilling
and geo-steering techniques will be used to maintain maximum exposure to the target zone.
1.2.1) Vertical Offset Wells & Curve Landing Target

· Zavanna, LLC Gust 2-11 1H [Lot 4 Section 2, T153N, R99W] located 0.6 mile west of the

· Zavanna, LLC Nelson 3-10 2TFH [NW NE Section 3, T153N, R99W] located 1.0 mile west of

The TVD of the landing target, which represents the top of the target zone, was calculated by
assuming comparable formation thicknesses (isopach) between the subject well and offset wells
from the Base of Last Salt Marker to the top of the target (Figure 4). As drilling progressed
through the various formation tops and markers, this target was continually monitored and
updated as necessary. A landing target of 11,228' TVD was calculated based off of the Gust 2-11
1H well and its corresponding Base of Last Salt Marker. The landing target was adjusted to
11,226' TVD after the Upper Bakken Shale marker was logged. The curve was landed just above
this at a depth of 11,219' TVD and 11,535' MD (Figure 2).

2.0) Wellsite Geology observations and Geo-steering
2.1.1) Lithology
Lithologic descriptions and sample analysis began in the Charles Formation in order to log the
Base Last Salt for target landing purposes.
The Three Forks Formation is overlain by the Bakken Formation, which is recognized as the start
of the primary oil-producing zones. The Three Forks Formation is a complex petroleum system
within the Williston Basin that is sourced by the Lower Bakken Member. The formation
represents a series of transgressive-regressive events that were deposited during the Devonian
time period, and subsequently has undergone diagenesis creating its current lithologic make-up.
There are varying facies within the Three Forks which are commonly referred to as "benches".
The first bench of the Three Forks is the zone of interest for oil production. It is comprised of off
white to cream-colored dolomite, classified as mudstone-packstone in Dunham's classification,
with varying degrees (trace to common) of pale yellow to medium brown oil staining,
interbedded with blocky, earthy-textured shale with color varying from off white to mint green,
and light to medium blue gray. Traces of pale yellow oil staining was also observed
intermittently on shale samples. Throughout the lateral trace to common disseminated and
nodular pyrite were observed.
2.2.1) Gas Monitoring
The overall gas trend of this well suggests the gas was the highest in the areas proximal to or
within the ideal target zone. The first half of the lateral yielded higher gas averages due to the
area of the Three Forks being targeted. Background gas averaged 1,400 units, with gas shows
ranging from approximately 700-4,600 units while drilling near the ideal target zone compared to
an average background gas of 1,020 units, with gas shows ranging from approximately 530-
2,350 units in the area below the ideal target zone. The largest gas reading was observed halfway
through drilling the lateral, just above target zone, and measured 4,604 units at a measured depth
of 16,194'. It is important to note, the final significant gas shows for the Simmental 2-11 2TFH
well were observed within the last 180'MD of the lateral, and ranged from 3,331-3,734 units.
Similarly, the adjacent Simmental 2-11 3H well had significant gas shows within the last
308'MD its lateral.

2.2.2) Gas and Oil Shows Within the Vertical and Curve Drilling
The first significant gas show was observed in the vertical at a measured depth of 9,780'. It was a
gas show of 97 units and correlated with a drilling break in ROP as the top of the Mission
Canyon was drilled. The sample was described as medium brown, occasional cream-medium
gray limestone with earthy to mottled mudstone texture, and friable to firm hardness. There was
no visible porosity and traces of dead oil staining. Trace amounts of sparry calcite were also
observed.
While building the curve near the base of the Lodgepole member a series of fracture gas shows
were observed. These fracture gas shows were located just above the False Bakken and ranged
from 95-110 units. There was also a fracture gas show of 791 units near the base of the Upper
Bakken Shale. The final interval of significant fracture gas shows were observed while drilling
within the Lower Bakken Shale. These fracture gas shows ranged form 946-1,881 units.
2.3.1) Geo-steering by Gamma Signature
The first bench of the Three Forks of Simmental 2-11 2TFH is estimated to be 25' in thickness,
with alternating high gamma peaks and low gamma signatures. The target zone was defined by
Sunburst well-site geologists and the Zavanna prospect geologist as a 6'-thick, and is noted in the
below figure. The target interval was based primarily on a slightly higher porosity zone and the
ease of geo-steering.
Figure 4. Offset gamma signatures from the Angus 34-27 4TFH for the Simmental 2-11 2TFH
(Jenn Torres, Sunburst wellsite geologist)
As drilling progressed it became apparent the first bench of the Three Forks had several localized
variations regarding the gamma character. This interpretation is supported by the comparison of
the local type logs with the cross-sectional interpretation of the Simmental 2-11 2TFH borehole
(Figure 5). These observed anomalous gamma signatures presented a significant challenge for
geo-steering. Beginning at ~16,550'MD the wellbore entered a gamma signature similar to the
top of the ideal target zone. A spot sample was caught to help determine the wellbore's
stratigraphic location within the Three Forks. The sample yielded lithology characteristics known
to be associated with proximity to the ideal target zone. This coupled with the gamma suggested
it was possible to be near the top of the ideal target zone. It was requested to slide down to
confirm location within the formation; however, a shale strike occurred at ~17,089'MD. It was
subsequently decided to adjust the drilling target to the interval below the ideal target zone,
including the cool gamma marker above the second bench for the remainder of the well.

Offset wells indicated that the Three Forks structure should drop 20' from curve landing to TD.
However, gamma signatures and sample analysis suggest that the formation had an initial
structural drop of 1.01' (TVD) over the first 444' (MD), yielding a down dip of 0.13°. This is the
first lateral of the pad to yield an initial structural drop. The next interval observed yielded a
structural gain of approximately 24' (TVD) over the next 4,162' (MD) of the lateral. This
yielded an apparent up dip 0.33° for this interval of the well. Over the next 4,440'(MD) of the

approximately 37' (TVD), yielding an apparent down dip of 0.48° for the second half of the well.

apparent down dip of 0.04°. Geo-steering techniques exposed 58% of the borehole exposure
being within the ideal target zone. The well reached a TD of 21,530' on November 26, 2014.
2.3.2) Geo-steering by Sample Observation
In addition to the general lithology described earlier in this report, it has been observed that the
first bench of the Three Forks has differing lithologic characteristics depending stratigraphic
location within the formation. The most helpful characteristic is apparent color and structural
changes of the shale, and can be an invaluable tool for geo-steering. The samples taken above the
ideal target zone had varying characteristics, and consisted of off white to tan dolomite, with
microsucrosic to sucrosic texture and trace to occasional yellow to medium brown spotty oil
staining. It important to note that the cool gamma marker above the ideal target zone contained
trace to occasional loose transparent to frosted, subangular to rounded quartz grains (Pictures 2-

3.0) Summary
The Zavanna, LLC Simmental 2-11 2TFH was spud on July 24, 2014, and reached TD in 25 total
drilling days on November 26, 2014. Samples from the Three Forks target indicated a mudstone
to packstone dolomite of variable oil staining with interbedded shale of varying color depending
on stratigraphic location within the first bench. The oil staining was described as trace to
common pale yellow to medium brown, and in some cases contained visible intercrystalline
porosity. The vertical and curve drilling revealed two significant gas shows within the Mission
Canyon and Lodgepole Formations, as well as the interval drilled from the False Bakken through
the Lower Bakken interval. There was a sidetrack drilled beginning at a measured depth of
16,360' after a shale strike at ~17,089'MD. MWD gamma ray markers, sample analysis, and
ROP were successfully utilized to expose 58% of the lateral borehole to ideal target zone. The
Simmental 2-11 2TFH awaits completion operations to maximize its production potential.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
