Vertical and Curve

terminating at MD 21720', TVD 11234', 237' FSL & 1307' FEL S36-T155N-R98W Williams

TARGET ZONE IDENTIFICATION IN THE LATERAL
The initial Primary Target Zone was a 10' thick segment of the Three Forks Formation
beginning 15' beneath the base of the Lower Bakken Shale to one foot above the Internal One.
API gamma counts from 45 to 100 reflected the thinly bedded nature of the horizons in the
Target Zone. Sharp, high gamma (120-155 API) readings signified entering the Internal One
from the low gamma Target Zone with gamma of 130 API denoting the top of the Target Zone,
greatly enhanced zone identification. Lithology in the target was observed to be arenaceous to
siltaceous dolomite with a definite lithological change to siltaceous dolomite with common gray
green waxy micro-pyritic shaley dolomite at the upper boundary of the zone. Lithology below
zone consisted of dolomite with cuttings being slightly more calcareous than cuttings from the
target zone and the presence of light gray green waxy micro-pyritic dolomitic shale. Gamma
interpretation exhibited narrowing of the Target Zone to eight feet at approximately MD 15000'
and seven feet at MD 17000' to TD.
LATERAL DRILLING OBSERVATIONS
Diesel invert mud used while drilling the vertical and curve portions of the hole was replaced
9.8# to 10.05 # sodium chloride brine for the remainder of drilling operations. Two 6" PDC bits,
two 1.5 degree steerable motors, and three MWD tools were used to drill the lateral section.
The first survey upon exiting the casing shoe revealed an inclination of 90.70 degrees and,
with the wellbore having landed above zone, directional slides were required for inclination as
well as azimuth that had been planned. These slides were successful and the Target Zone
was entered at MD 11766'. The initial formation dip of 89.59° was calculated and a dip
reversal of 90.47° was encountered at MD 13000'. The ensuing structural interpretation
showed three calculated dips ranging from 89.29° to 89.99° to approximately MD 15600'
where flattening to 90.09° was observed to MD 16500'. From this point formation dips ranged
89.44° to 90.00° to MD 21000' where a second reversal to 90.23° was interpreted before a
resumption of the down dip at approximately MD 21200' to TD at MD 21720'. A formation dip
of 89.77° was calculated for the entire lateral. The 6" bore exposed 10206 linear feet of hole
through the Three Forks Formation. Rotational drilling of 9234' (90.0%) and directional slides
of 1022' (10.0%) comprised drilling of the lateral. Average rotating ROP was 149'/Hr. and
sliding ROP was 30'/Hr. The wellbore remained in the Target Zone approximately 5249'
(51.4%), below the target zone 3181' (31.2%) and above the target zone 1776' (17.4%).

SUMMARY
The Boston 3-25H1 was drilled in the Three Forks Formation and oil shows observed were fair
with relatively strong gas shows. Oil shows in samples were consistent displaying rare to
some medium brown stain and faint, dull, spotty to even, moderately bright yellow
fluorescence. Subsurface structural control utilizing data from Boston 2-25H, cuttings
evaluation and MWD gamma, was sufficient to allow drilling the hole with low potential for
stratigraphic positional misinterpretation. Steering to correct for inclination or azimuth was
responsive until MD 14400' to 17500' where possible dulling of the bit may have hampered
directional drilling control. Boston 3-25H1 will be completed with multi-stage fracture
stimulation after which its production potential will be revealed. Samples and surveys have
been delivered to the North Dakota Industrial Commission.

RE:
Filter Socks and Other Filter Media
Leakproof Container Required
Oil and Gas Wells
Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
