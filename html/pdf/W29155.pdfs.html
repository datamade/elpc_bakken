Well Evaluation
Hanson 11X-12A [NW NW Section 12, T144N-R96W] is located approximately 6.0 miles south
west of Killdeer in Dunn County, North Dakota. The Hanson 11X-12E is a double-section
horizontal Three Forks well within the Williston Basin consisting of one lateral drilled to the south.
It expands the existing Murphy Creek Field. The curve would be kicked off in the Lodgepole and
landed within the Three Forks Formation. Directional drilling and geo-steering techniques would
then be used to maintain maximum exposure to the target zone.
GEOLOGY
Lithology
Lithologic descriptions and sample analysis began in the Charles Formation in order to log the
Base Last Salt for curve landing purposes.
The Three Forks Formation was logged at 10,815' TVD (-8,481' MSL). ). It consists primarily of
dolomite with finely laminated intervals of pale green shale containing trace to rare amounts of
spotty to even brown oil staining. The Three Forks was the target reservoir for the lateral portion
of the well.
Gas Monitoring
Background gas fluctuated between 20u-75u. The highest gas value recorded was a show at
11,072' MD of 310u. The entire ideal target zone was observed to contain relatively low gas
concentrations and consistent oil staining. The highest gas concentrations were seen between
13,000' MD and 14,500' MD correlating with the top half of our ideal target zone (Figure 1).
Figure 1. Profile of the Hanson 11X-12E well with background gas in the range of 20-75 units. (Miles Anson, Anson Geophysical well site
geologist)
Curve Landing
Data including rate of penetration (ROP), torque, and lagged samples were used to determine
marker and formation tops during the vertical hole. Measurement-while-drilling (MWD) gamma
data was utilized in addition during the curve build. During both the vertical and curve, tops were
compared to our primary offset wells, in order to ensure a successful landing in the target zone.
The true vertical depth (TVD) of our initial landing target (10,837' TVD) was calculated by
assuming comparable formation thicknesses from the Base Last Salt to the landing target gamma
signature. Directional successfully landed the curve at 10,836' TVD.
Geosteering
The ideal target zone was defined as a 20' interval, the top of which was initially set at 10,828'
TVD, 28' below the base of the Lower Bakken Shale (Figure 2). Offset wells indicated that the
Three Forks would be up-dipping approximately 0.23° from entry of the formation to TD. The
final gross overall change in TVD from entry of the Three Forks Formation to the end of the lateral
was estimated at -23' over 9,535' (0.14° down, Figure 2). The bottom-hole location is estimated
to be 9,997.96' south & 365.01' west of surface location or approximately 251.62' FSL & 930.99'
FWL, SW SW Section 13, T144N, R96W. Geo-steering techniques exposed 100% of uncased
borehole to the 20' Three Forks ideal target. The well reached a TD of 20,571' on December 25,
2014.
Figure 2. Cross-sectional interpretation of the Hanson 11X-12E well high gamma associated with the shale intervals
and low gamma within the dolomite intervals. (Miles Anson, Anson Geophysical well site geologist)
SUMMARY
1)
The XTO Energy, Inc. Hanson 11X-12E was spud on October 12, 2014 and reached TD in
21 days on December 25, 2014.
2)
Samples from the Three Forks target consisted of interbedded and interlaminated shale and
dolomite with fair intercrystalline porosity. Oil shows in samples were visible as spotted
to even rare brown oil stain yielding encouraging fluorescent cuts.
3)
Poor gas shows were present throughout the well; most significant was a gas show of 310u
at 11,072' MD while landing the curve within the Three Forks.
4)
The Hanson 11X-12E awaits completion operations to maximize its production potential.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
