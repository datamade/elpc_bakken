INTRODUCTION
XTO Energy, Inc. Ranger 21X-18B [NE NW Section 18, T151N, R99W] is located approximately 6
miles north-northeast of Arnegard, in Tobacco Garden Field of McKenzie County, North Dakota (Figure
1). The well is designed as a Middle Bakken horizontal well to develop Sections 18 and 19. Directional
drilling and geo-steering will be employed to land in the Middle Bakken reservoir and maintain maximum
exposure to a 20' target zone.
GEOLOGY
Methods
Geologic analysis for the Ranger 21X-18B was provided by a Sunburst Consulting, Inc. geology team.
Sunburst well site geologists caught lagged samples at 30' intervals just before the Base Last Charles Salt.
Rock samples, rate of penetration and gamma ray data were used in geologic analysis to aid in geo-
steering and dip calculations.
Prospective Lithology
The Ratcliffe was drilled at 9,626' MD / 9,625' TVD, 15' low to prognosis and 2' high to Ranger 21X-
18G. This interval was mainly mudstone to wackestone limestone, which was cream to medium brown in
color, friable and with an earthy texture. It also consisted of anhydrite and dolomite. A 316 unit gas show
was witnessed towards the base, against a 9.6 ppg mud weight.
The Mission Canyon Formation was drilled at 9,768' MD / 9,767' TVD, 2' high to both prognosis and
Ranger 21X-18G. The Mission Canyon consists primarily of clean, argillaceous and dolomitic limestone
interbedded with dolomite. The upper Mission Canyon is primarily friable to firm, light to medium gray
argillaceous limestone. While the lower lithology consisted primarily of off white to medium gray
microcrystalline limestone mudstone. Hydrocarbon shows ranged from 251 to 447 units in 9.65 to 9.75
ppg drilling mud.
The argillaceous limestone signifying the top of the Lodgepole Formation was penetrated at 10,297'
MD / 10,296' TVD which is 1' high to prognosis and 3' high to Ranger 21X-18G. This slope marine
deposit consisted of mostly gray to brown argillaceous mudstone that was firm to hard, microcrystalline
and contained rare disseminated pyrite. Also present were relatively thin beds of clean limestone that
were light gray to brown, microcrystalline and had an earthy texture. Three large fracture gases within the
Lodgepole read 4,017 units, 4,223 units and 5,055 units against 10.1 ppg drilling mud.
The Scallion limestone was drilled at 11,208' MD / 11,089' TVD which was 5' high to Ranger 21X-18G.
The Scallion consists of clean, cream to light brown microcrystalline wackestone, which contains trace
amounts of disseminated pyrite. Fracture gases here ranged from 4,001 to 4,891 units against 10.2 ppg
mud.
The Bakken Formation has four members, two of which are organic-rich, pyritic shale, separated by a
siltstone and silty sandstone middle member and a medium gray siltstone, known as the Pronghorn
Member underneath. The carbonaceous Upper Bakken Shale was penetrated at 11,224' MD / 11,096'
TVD, which was 3' high to prognosis and to 7' high to Ranger 21X-18G. Gas shows through the shale
read upwards of 2,997 units against 10.25 ppg mud. The Middle Bakken was penetrated at 11,275' MD /
11,112' TVD, 5' high to prognosis and 7' high to Ranger 21X-18G. The targeted Middle Member
lithology consisted of light gray to medium gray-brown, friable to firm, sub-blocky, very fine grained
silty sandstone which was moderately calcite-cemented (Figure 2). Trace light brown oil staining was
typical along with disseminated pyrite throughout most of the target zone as well as trace intergranular
porosity.
Figure 2: Wet cuttings of the upper target Middle Bakken silty sandstone (left) & lower target silty sandstone (right).
Lateral Gas and Oil Shows
The background gas for the Ranger 21X-18B ranged from 445 to 4,004 units during the course of the
lateral. The highest gas show was 4,078 units at 18,124' MD against 9.9 ppg mud. Higher gas shows were
generally witnessed in the lower to middle part of the target zone. The gas buster was turned on at a
measured depth of 12,300' and a 2' to 15' foot flare was witnessed throughout the lateral (Figure 3).
Figure 3: 2' to 15' intermittent flare witnessed throughout the majority of the lateral.
Geo-steering
A 20' target zone was chosen for the Ranger 21X-18B by XTO Energy geologists that began 10' below
the Upper Bakken Shale and ended 13' from the top of the Lower Bakken Shale. The thickness was
estimated using control well logs. A well drilled previously on the pad, the Ranger 21X-18G, provided
guidance for expected gamma ray markers while drilling the lateral (Figure 4). The top of the target zone
would have gamma readings relatively high at about 88 API. Below this, gamma would reach its lowest
point, reading into the high 20s. Gamma would then level off and stay around 72 API before a "ratty
gamma section at the middle of target zone that would read between 43 and 73 API. Gamma below this
would level off again at about 70 to 72 API. The lower target zone was only penetrated with the second
gamma tool which tended to read 20 API higher. At this point a low streak of gamma would get down to
53 API.
Figure 4. Gamma type log of the Middle Bakken from the Ranger 21X-18G well; green
is Upper and Lower Shale, yellow is above target zone, blue is target zone, and purple is
below target zone.
The prognosis projected the curve landing at 11,137' TVD. The Lodgepole 3 marker came in 6' high to
the Ranger 21X-18G, so the decision was made to move the target up to 11,133'. The Middle Bakken
Member came in at a TVD of 11,112' which locked the landing target in at 11,132' TVD. The curve was
landed successfully at 11,455' MD with a projected TVD of 11,126.72'--5.28' high to the final landing
target and 4.72' into the target zone.
Suggested prognosis dip provided by XTO Energy, Inc., stated that dip would be close to 0.53° down
until around 14,000' MD, then dip would be flat until 18,000' MD. After the flat section it was suggested
an up dip of around 0.30° would be witnessed for the remainder of the lateral. Structurally, the formation
dropped an estimated 25.00' over 9,465' from landing to the end of the lateral resulting in an overall
apparent dip of 0.15° down (Figure 5).

Bottom-Hole Location
Total depth resulted in a closure azimuth of 178.31° and a closure distance of 9,877.44'. The bottom-hole
location is projected to be 9,873.15' south and 291.29' east of the surface location or approximately
250.25' FSL & 1,691.29' FWL, SE SW Section 19, T151N, R99W. The hole was prepared for liner and
multi-stage fracture stimulation directly after drilling operations concluded.
SUMMARY
The XTO Energy, Inc. Ranger 21X-18B was spud on September 1, 2014 by H&P 456. The well
efficiently reached TD in 18 total drilling days on September 19, 2014. Samples from the Middle Bakken
target contained oil staining that was described as trace and spotty with visible intergranular porosity.
MWD gamma ray markers, sample analysis, and ROP were successfully utilized to expose 100% of the
lateral borehole to the Middle Bakken target zone. The Ranger 21X-18B awaits completion operations to
maximize its production potential.

Drilling pit
NDAC 43-02-03-19.4 states that "a pit may be utilized to bury drill cuttings and solids generated during well
drilling and completion operations, providing the pit can be constructed, used and reclaimed in a manner that will
prevent pollution of the land surface and freshwaters. Reserve and circulation of mud system through earthen pits
are prohibited. All pits shall be inspected by an authorized representative of the director prior to lining and use.
Drill cuttings and solids must be stabilized in a manner approved by the director prior to placement in a cuttings
pit."

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
