Synopsis
The Hanover Federal 5300 44-11 4B [SE SE Sec. 11 T153N R100W] is located in the Willow
Creek Field in Williams County approximately eight miles South East of Williston, North
Dakota. The well plan proposed a single lateral leg trending 180.00° south to be drilled from the
SE SE corner of Section 11 to the SW SE corner of Section 23. The lateral portion of the
Hanover Federal 5300 44-11 4B targeted a zone in the porous silty sandstone of the Middle
Bakken formation, approximately 10 to 30 feet below the Upper Bakken Shale formation.
Control Wells
There were many completed wells used as control offsets for the project. The Slette­Strangeland
2 [NW NE Sec. 14, T153N, R100W] was used as the main control offset for the Hanover
Federal 5300 44-11 4B. Throughout the curve progression, gamma-ray data from the Slette­
Strangeland 2 was utilized to help determine proper landing depth for the Hanover Federal 5300
44-11 4B.
The Hanover Federal 5300 13-14 7T [NW NE Sec. 14, T153N R100W] was also considered
closely when picking the landing target for the curve.
A graph was constructed to determine which markers and formation tops could be accurately
used in landing the curve. The most consistent isopach for uphole formations was observed to be
the Base of the Last Salt and the Mission Canyon. The Lodgepole formation does not appear
to be a reliable marker for isopach thickness in the immediate area.

Geologic Assessment
Methods
Geologic supervision of Hanover Federal 5300 44-11 4B was provided by two experienced
RPM well site geologists. Gas and chromatograph levels were measured using NOV Wellsite
Gas Watch real time gas detector and chromatograph system. The NOV Wellsite Gas Watch
system uses non-dispersive infrared and chemical sensor gas detection to evaluate gases liberated
from the formation by the drill bit and carried to the surface by the drilling fluid
The NOV Wellsite Gas Watch was interfaced with a Pason electronic data recorder system Pason
provided rate of penetration, on-off bottom and pump strokes to the NOV Wellsite Gas Watch
and received total gas information from the NOV Wellsite Gas Watch for viewing on location
and remotely.
Under the direction of RPM well site geologist, rig crews were instructed to catch lagged drill
cutting samples at 30' intervals from 8,500 to the kick off point (KOP) at 10,561'. Samples were
continuously logged at 50' intervals during the curve and throughout the lateral section.
Sampled drill cuttings were examined wet and dry under a binocular microscope using plain
(broad spectrum) and transmitted light. Cuttings were evaluated for hydrocarbon "cut" by
immersion in carbo-sol (trichloroethylene) and inspection under a UV fluoroscope. 10%
hydrochloric acid and alizarin red were used to determine the calcareous and dolomitic content
of formation and cementing.
Vertical Operations
Overview
The Hanover Federal 5300 44-11 4B was spud on 23 January 2015 using Patterson 330 drilling
rig. Prior to rig up of RPM mud logging services, a 13 1/2" hole was drilled with fresh water to
a depth of 2,397' and isolated with 9 5/8" 36# J-55 casing cemented to surface. Bit #2 (8 ¾"
NOV DS616M) was used to drill from 2,397' to 8,712'. It drilled 6,315' in 37.5 hours, averaging
168.4 ft/hr. Bit#3 (8 ¾" NOV DS616M) was used to drill from 8,712' to 9,460'. It drilled 748'
in 9 hours, averaging 83.1 ft/hr, and was replaced after a motor failure. The final bit in the
vertical, Bit #4 (8 3/4" Smith MDSi616) was used to drill from 9,460' to 10,561' (KOP). It
drilled 1,101' in 16.5 hours, averaging 66.7 ft/hr.
RPM well site geologists arrived at site on the 27th of January, 2015 and began logging the
vertical section at 8,500'.
Diesel invert drilling fluid with a mud weight of ranging from 9.85-10.2 ppg was used during
remainder of the vertical hole and in the curve builds sections.

Directional Operations
RPM provided equipment and personnel for directional services. Ryan Energy Technologies
(Ryan) provided equipment and personnel for MWD services. RPM geologists worked closely
with RPM directional and Ryan MWD teams to formulate steering decisions to maximize
exposure of borehole in the pay zone. RPM well site geologists closely observed MWD gamma-
ray information and penetration rates to aid in steering decisions and dip degree estimations.
Curve Build
Overview
Bit #4, 5 & 6 were used in front of a 2.35° fixed mud motor to drill the curve from measured
depth 10,561' to 11,663'and was used to finish the curve in 28.5 hours generating an average
ROP of 38.6 ft/hr.
During the curve progression, isopach data of gamma-ray signatures from the Hanover Federal
5300 44-11 4B was compared to Hanover Federal 13-14 7T MWD tool gamma-ray readings
from the Base Charles Salt to the Middle Bakken landing target (Table 1). Prospective markers
were entered into Table 2 to determine the target landing point. Based on the marker pick for the
Base Charles Salt, the target TVD for the curve left the same at 11,038'. The curve with the
addition of a tangent, to cross the hard line to the south, and a rat hole were completed at a
measured depth of 11,663'and a TVD of 11,048'. The target was seen at 11,178' MD and
11,035' TVD.

Lateral
Overview
Drilling fluid consisting of a closed system diesel invert (9.6 ­ 10.0 ppg) was used while drilling
the lateral section. Two 6" PDC bits were used to drill the lateral.
Bit #7 & 8 (6" Baker), PDC bit in front of a 1.5° mud motor was used to drill the lateral from
11,663' to 20,445'. The average ROP in the lateral was 120.9 ft/hr and was drilled in 77.5 hours.
Drilling operations were concluded at 17:15 hours on April 1, 2015, at a bottom hole location of
10727.01' S & 117.83' W of surface location or approximately 304.11' FSL & 909.83' FEL, SE
SE Section 23, T153N, R100W.
Lithology
To aid in communications while drilling the lateral, a type log was created using the gamma
signature from the Hanover 3T (Diagram 1). The target zone was an area marked roughly
between the "A" and "B" markers on chart 1. The attempt was made to keep the well bore in
this region for as much of the drilling operations as possible.
The Middle Member of the Bakken Formation seen in drilling samples consisted of 2 primary
facies. The upper most was the off white, gray siltstone seen while landing the curve and
described above. The second was described as sandstone interbedded with a light brown silty
sandstone. This silty sandstone consisted of a siltstone that was sub angular and grey in color
interbedded with a light brown sandstone (Figure 6). There was a substantial amount of oil stain
present in the sandstone. Samples from the top of the Middle Bakken member were described as:
SILTY SANDSTONE: light gray to gray, off white to white, very fine grain, friable to soft, sub angular,
moderately sorted, vitreous, moderate disseminated and trace nodular pyrite, moderately calcareous,
trace oil stain, trace intergranular porosity, possible fracture porosity
Figure 6: Photograph of silty sandstone with disseminated pyrite seen in the upper part of the target zone
As the bit approached "D" in the middle member, silty sandstone was seen in sample (Figure 7).
This silty sandstone was light to medium grey in color, containing a trace amounted of sandstone
and held together with a weaker calcite cementing. This silty sandstone comprised the primary
target zone that we were attempting to keep the well bore in (Diagram 1).

Gas and Hydrocarbon Shows
Total gas levels were recorded with an average background of ~100-200 units while drilling the
build section through the Lodgepole formation. Gas levels elevated at the Upper Bakken Shale
with a peak of 274 units and an average background gas of ~ 100 units. Gas levels stayed
consistent after the penetration of the middle member of the Bakken formation.
Total gas levels for the entire vertical section were recorded with an average background gas of ~
700-1200 units with few notable peaks. The NOV gas chromatograph measured propane and
methane all through the entire lateral.
Hydrocarbon cuts using trichloroethylene were weak to moderate though the entire lateral. The
target zone and above was characterized by weak white to yellow diffuse cuts and common weak
steaming cuts. No correlation was found between the strength of the hydrocarbon cuts and the
position of the bit in the Middle Bakken formation.

Summary
1) The Hanover Federal 5300 44-11 4B was spud the January 23th 2015 east of the town
Williston in Williams County, North Dakota. The vertical hole was drilled to a total depth of
10,561'.
2) A mud program consisting of diesel invert (9.6 ­ 10.0 ppg) after surface casing and
throughout the curve build sections. The drilling mud was successful in maintaining stable
downhole conditions and minimizing washout through the salt intervals.
3) Three 8 3/4" PDC bits were used to drill the vertical hole from surface casing to a vertical
depth of 10,561' in a total of 63 hours. Three 8 3/4" bit was used to drill through the curve,
landing at a measured depth of 11,663' in 28.5 hours. The entire 10,727.01' lateral was drilled in
104.5 hours with two 6" PDC bits.
4) The lateral was targeting a 14' thick section of the middle member of the Bakken
formation situated fourteen feet below the upper shale.
5) Gas levels of between 700 and 1200 units were maintained through most of the drilling
operations.
6) The Middle Bakken consisted of 2 gross types of lithology. Directly below the upper Bakken
Shale, RPM geologists observed a dark gray/brown siltstone. Below this siltstone, a light grey
siltstone interbedded with light brown to white oil stained sandstone. The target zone consisted
of off white to white, light gray/ brown porous silty sandstone.
7) Hydrocarbon cuts were few to moderate through the lateral. Common slow diffuse cuts and
few streaming cuts were present in the top of the target zone.
8) Drilling operations were concluded at 18:00 hours on April 1, 2015 at a bottom hole location
of 10,727.01' S & 117.83' W of surface location or approximately 304.11' FSL & 909.83' FEL,
SE SE Section 23, T153N, R100W
9) Oasis Petroleum Hanover Federal 5300 44-11 4B awaits completion operations to determine
ultimate production potential.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
