Introduction
Emerald Oil, Inc. successfully drilled the Eric Stratton Federal 5-36-25H
horizontal well at an average Azimuth of 360.0º and an average Inclination of 89.9°,
targeting the Middle Bakken Formation beginning in Section 36, T 147 N, R 104 W,
(225' FSL & 2640' FWL) in McKenzie County, North Dakota, drilling north to the
northwest quarter of the northeast quarter of Section 25, T 147 N, R 104 W, with a
spacing unit consisting of 1,280 acres. The well is located in the Mondak Field in the
southwest portion of the Williston Basin, North Dakota. The main wells used for
correlation were the vertical and curve sections of the USA 43-6-117; in Section 6 T 146
N, R 103 W, and BN 13-5; in Section 5, T 146 N, R 103 W.
Operations Summary
Vertical Section
The Eric Stratton Federal was spud on November 12, 2014. Brara
Geologic Consulting arrived on site and began well site operations on November
14, 2014. A Mudlogging Systems, Inc. Redbox gas detection system was used
for analyzing and recording total gas and chromatograph data. Surface drilling
was completed on November 13, 2014 and 9 5/8" surface casing was set to a
depth of 2080' (MD). An invert mud system was used to drill out from surface
throughout the landing of the curve section.
The first bottom-hole assembly (BHA) used in the vertical consisted of
(Bit #1) an 8 ¾'' Halliburton MM65D bit, a Hunting 1.5° mud motor, and a HDS-1
MWD tool which drilled out of surface casing at 2080' (MD). This BHA drilled
7995' in 82 hours to the kick-off point (KOP) at 10075' MD, 10070' TVD, (-7755'
SS). The KOP was reached at 20:30 on November 16, 2014, and a trip was
made to pick up the curve assembly.
Curve Section
Drilling operations resumed on November 17, 2014 at 11:00 with the curve
assembly. This BHA included, (Bit #2) an 8 ¾'' Halliburton MMD55M bit paired
with a Hunting 7/8 lobe configuration 7.0 stage, 2.38° and 5.35' bit to bend mud
motor and HDS-1 MWD/gamma tool. This assembly drilled 800' in 17 hours to
the landing of the curve at 10875' MD, 10590.5' TVD, (-8275.5' SS). The curve
was landed at 05:00 on November 18, 2014 at an inclination of 82.48° and an
azimuth of 33.54°, with a landing point location in Section 36, T 147 N, R 104 W,
(652' FSL & 2263' FEL). Seven inch (7") intermediate casing was set at 10850'
(MD), and cemented.

configuration, 1.5º and 5.00 bit to bend motor, an AWR LWD (Resistivity/Gamma
tool), ABS (Battery sub), non-mag pin by pin X/O, HDS-1L (Directional and back

Base of Last Charles Salt
The Base of Last Charles Salt top was picked from samples, Gamma Ray and
ROP at 9133' MD, 9126' TVD, (-6811' SS). This top came in 28' higher than prognosis.
Base of Last Charles Salt Lithology Descriptions
Composite samples were caught through the Charles Salt in 30' intervals
from 9000' (MD) to 9133' (MD) (Base of Last Charles Salt), continuing to 9302'
(MD) (Mission Canyon Top) by manually catching rock cuttings and collecting
them in sample bags.
Samples in the base of section contained abundant amounts of halite that
was off-white, semi-translucent to translucent and frosty, with coarse grains.
The Limestone/Dolomite in the samples were mudstone, light to medium
gray, very fine crystalline, slightly soft to occasionally firm, and occasionally
silted. Anhydrite in the formation remained predominantly white to off white with
occasional graying, microcrystalline, very soft but occasionally firm, amorphic
and massive.

Mission Canyon Formation
The Mission Canyon formation top was picked from samples and Gamma ray at
9302' MD, 9299' TVD, (-6984' SS), 11 feet higher in TVD than in prognosis. Formation
background gas through the Mission Canyon varied from 10 to 20 units. Faint
yellow/white cuts were observed, but were attributed to the presence of invert residue in
the samples. No DST was recommended or conducted.
Mission Canyon Formation Lithology Descriptions
Composite samples were caught in 30' intervals from 9302' (MD) to
9835' (MD) (Lodgepole Formation Top) through the Mission Canyon formation by
manually catching rock cuttings and collecting them in sample bags.
Limestone in the samples was light to dark gray, gray/brown, sub-blocky,
soft firm, argillaceous mudstone to wackestone, with trace microcrystalline
limestone. Visible inter-granular porosity was absent to very poor. No oil staining
was observed.
The samples had fluorescence is dull yellow when observed under a
fluoroscope, though fluorescence is most likely due to residual invert mud in the
cuttings. Faint yellow-white cuts that were observed were also attributed to the
presence of invert residue in the samples.
Lodgepole Formation
The Lodgepole top was picked from samples and Gamma Ray at 9835' MD,
9831' TVD, (-7516' SS). The kick-off depth of 10070' (MD) was reached on November
16, 2014. Very slow to slow diffuse white cuts were observed in the Lodgepole due to
residual invert fluid after cleaning. Background gas was between 30 to 40 units.
Lodgepole Formation Lithology Descriptions
Composite samples were caught in 30' intervals from 9835' (MD) to
10705' (MD) (False Bakken Top) by manually catching rock cuttings and
collecting them in sample bags.
The Lodgepole is predominately comprised of argillaceous limestone that
ranged from being a mudstone to a wackestone, shades of gray, mainly very fine
crystalline, firm-hard, occasional laminations, very earthy textures, and no visible
porosity with differing amounts of silt through the formation. Slow blue white cuts
were observed but were possibly due to residual invert drilling fluid.

False Bakken Shale
The False Bakken top was picked from samples, Gamma Ray, gas shows and
penetration rate at 10705' MD, 10560' TVD, (-8245' SS). Dull, even yellow/orange
fluorescence was observed in the samples when viewed under a fluoroscope. Cuts in
the False Bakken were moderate to slow blue/white but were heavily contaminated by
invert drilling fluid.
False Bakken Shale Lithology Descriptions
Samples were caught from 10705' (MD) to 10773' (MD) (Upper Bakken
Top) in 10' intervals through the False Bakken formation by manually catching
rock cuttings and collecting them in sample bags. Samples were comprised of
shale and limestone.
Shale in the False Bakken was mostly black, fissile in part, hard to brittle,
blocky-sub blocky, earthy textured with occasionally waxy surfaces. Limestone
returns were mudstone-wackestone with varying amounts of argillaceous,
shades of gray ranging from light to dark, occasional laminations but
predominantly massive, platy-to sub platy, very earthy textured with no visible
porosity and occasional trace amounts of pyrite.
Upper Bakken Shale
Upper Bakken Shale top picked from samples, Gamma Ray, and penetration rate
at 10773' MD, 10578' TVD, (-8263' SS). Dull, even yellow/orange fluorescence was
frequently observed in the samples when viewed under a fluoroscope, cuts in the Upper
Bakken were moderately fast blue/white but were heavily contaminated by oil based
drilling fluid.
Upper Bakken Shale Lithology Descriptions
Composite samples were caught from 10773' (MD) to 10828' (MD)
(Middle Bakken Top) in 10' intervals through the Upper Bakken by manually
catching samples.
Shale in the Upper Bakken was black, mainly fissile but occasionally hard
to brittle, blocky to sub-blocky, earthy and occasionally waxy. Even yellow/orange
fluorescence and slow, faint bright blue/white cuts were observed when
examined under a fluoroscope but were heavily contaminated by oil based
drilling fluid.

Middle Bakken Dolomite
The Middle Bakken top was picked from sample lithology, Gamma and
penetration rate at 10828' MD, 10586' TVD, (-8271' SS).
Middle Bakken Lithology Descriptions
Composite samples were caught in 30' intervals through the Middle
Bakken from 10828' (MD) to 20599' (MD/TD) by manually catching samples from
the shakers. Middle Bakken dolomite samples were light gray to gray,
occasionally tan, light gray brown, and mottled gray and light brown. Cuttings
were blocky to sub-platy, soft to firm, and microcrystalline to fine crystalline with a
chalky matrix. Samples were frequently argillaceous to silty, occasionally grading
to sandy. Cuttings were tight, exhibiting poor visible porosity. Even dull
yellow/orange fluorescence was observed in nearly all samples when examined
under a fluoroscope. Moderately slow to fast milky cuts were noted throughout
the Middle Bakken. Some cuts and fluorescence from samples taken while
sliding were contaminated by the petroleum-based lube sweeps used during
slides.
Well Summary
The Eric Stratton Federal 5-36-25H was drilled from South to North in the Middle
Bakken Formation. The target consisted of nine to 10 vertical feet, from one foot below
the Middle Bakken top to one foot above the Lower Bakken shale. The well bore is
estimated to have been in target zone 94% of the lateral. The overall dip was 89.9°. 30'
surveys were taken throughout the turn in the lateral to 360º and 90' surveys until total
depth (TD).
Formation dip averaged 89.85° over the first half of the lateral between the
intermediate casing shoe and 16500' (MD). This section had an average of 10 to 15%
C3 and C4 with background gas averaging from 200 to 800 units. At 16500' (MD) dip
began to level out and then increase to 90.25° until 19500' (MD) where dip decreased to
89.8° and stayed consistent until the end of the well at 20600' (MD). This section had an
increase in average of C3 and C4 to 50 to 80% with background gas averaging 800 to
1500 units. The well bore was primarily located in the middle of target in this section.
Nearly all samples exhibited spotty to even yellow/orange fluorescence when
examined under a fluoroscope. Oil cuts were commonly slow to moderately fast, diffuse
to slow streaming and light blue in color, leaving occasional residual rings. Some
samples were contaminated with petroleum-based lubricant, which was used to achieve
adequate bit weight to drill and slide. This contamination masked some fluorescence
and oil cuts in some of the samples.

After total depth (TD) was reached on November 28, 2014, Brara Geologic
Consulting remained onsite to monitor gas while short-tripping back to the shoe and

Conclusions
1) The Emerald Oil, Inc. Eric Stratton Federal 5-36-25H was a successfully drilled 4-
run Horizontal well in the Middle Bakken Formation from a surface location in
Section 36, T 147 N, R 104 W (225' FSL & 2640' FWL) to a total depth (TD) of
20599' MD, 10609.47' TVD, (-8294.47' SS), with a bottom hole location in
Section 25, T 147 N, R 104 W (248.9' FNL & 1884' FEL) in McKenzie County,
North Dakota. The well was drilled from the intermediate casing shoe to TD with
one trip. The trip was made due to tool failure at 11016' (MD). Total depth was
reached at 12:40 on November 28, 2014. The Eric Stratton Federal 5-36-25H
required 16 total days of drilling and setting intermediate casing.
2) Ultraviolet sample shows in this lateral were slow-moderately fast light blue cuts
with residual rings. Total gas shows were fair ranging from a 200 to 800 unit
average to an 800 to 1500 unit average. Overall porosity was poor to fair, which
is common for the Middle Bakken Formation. Wellbore placement was within the
target zone for approximately 94% of the total 9726 feet of lateral section, which
should allow for excellent production opportunities.

Filter Socks and Other Filter Media

Oil and Gas Wells
Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
