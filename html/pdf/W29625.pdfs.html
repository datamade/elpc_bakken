INTRODUCTION
The Oasis Petroleum North America, LLC Kukuk 6192 43-21B [SW SE, Section 21,
T161N, R92W] is located approximately 13 miles north of Powers Lake in Burke County,
ND. It shares a pad with the Osen 6192 43-21T well. The Kukuk 6192 43-21B is a horizontal
Middle Bakken well within the Williston Basin consisting of one 9,625' lateral drilled toward
the north. Geologically, the Williston Basin is an intracratonic, structural, and sedimentary
basin located at the western edge of the Canadian Shield. Within the basin, the Middle
Bakken consists of four members: the basal pronghorn member, the organic rich lower shale
member, the coastal-carbonate middle member which consists of calcareous silty sandstone
and the upper shale. The shales act both as traps and sources rocks and the Middle Bakken
Member acts as a reservoir. Advances in drilling and well stimulation have made the Bakken
Formation an extremely viable oil and gas resource.
OFFSET WELLS
Offset well data used for depth correlation during curve operations are found `Structure'
section appended to this report. Offset well control is essential in curve building operations,
in order to successfully land within a targeted zone of the Bakken Formation. Formation
thicknesses expressed by gamma ray signatures in these wells were compared to gamma data
of offset wells in order to successfully land the curve. Due to the long string design, the
desired inclination for landing the curve was 85º. The offset wells used for the Kukuk 6192
43-21B were the Osen 6192 43-21T, Gustafson 29-32-161-92H, and the Woll 6093 12-1T.
ENGINEERING
The Kukuk 6192 43-21B was spud on November 29, 2014 by Xtreme 21. Drilling fluid was
fresh water for the surface and diesel invert mud for the vertical, curve and lateral. The curve
was landed at a TVD of 8,567' at 83.7°, 8,805' MD. Surface casing was set to 2,157'.
Intermediate casing was not run at the end of the curve. To start the lateral, a 6 Reed PDC
bit, MWD tool, and a 1.5° fixed mud motor were used. This bit was rerun twice then replaced
by another Reed PDC bit at 15,952' MD after a trip for a wash in the drill pipe. The BHA
accomplishments are tabulated in an appendix (BHA Record) to this report. The well reached
total depth (TD) at 18,430' MD at 1705 hrs CDT on December 29, 2014, generating an
exposure of 9,625 linear feet of 6 hole through the Midddle Bakken target interval. The
bottom hole location (BHL) lies 10,026.9' North and 503.46' East of surface of the surface
location or approximately 237.37' FNL and 1,324.24' FEL, NW NE Section 16, Township
161N, Range 92W.

Gas and Oil Shows
Hydrocarbon constituents (C1 through C4) are expressed in parts-per-million (ppm)
concentrations. Diesel-invert mud was used to drill the vertical, curve, and lateral. During the
vertical, gas shows ranged from 9-547 units above a background of ~10 units against an
average mud weight of 9.8 lb/gal diesel-invert. Methane and Ethane (C1-C2) were observed
throughout the vertical. Gases heavier than C2 were not observed. There were promising
shows just above the top of the Mission Canyon with highest show being 547 units, and in
the Mission Canyon there were promising shows of 108 to 153 units. After kicking off the
curve, shows were similar with respect to the vertical. Shows remained low from kick-off
until entry of the Upper Bakken Shale, where background gas went up to over 300 units, and
there was a 1,031 unit gas show. Gas shows became low again upon entry of the Middle
Bakken.
The lateral began with low gas shows of 30 to 100 units. Shows continued to be low for most
of the lateral. Shows would increase to over 200 units background against an average mud
weight of 9.9 lb/gal diesel-invert when near the top of the ideal target zone. Shows would
typically drop to 50 units background when near the bottom and out of zone against an
average mud weight of 9.9 lb/gal diesel-invert. Methane and Ethane (C1-C2) were seen
throughout the lateral. Propane (C3) was seen only in the ideal target zone.
GEOSTEERING
The ideal target zone of the Kukuk 6192 43-21B was identified by evaluating gamma ray
data from the nearby Hazel 6192 43-23T (Figure 3). For geosteering purposes the original
ideal target is set from the inflection of the 2 marker down 12'. At measured depth 11,252',
the target was changed to 12' below the inflection of the 6 marker, due to inconsistencies in
the 3, 4, and 5 markers.

highlighted in green (Curtis Johnson, Oasis Geologist).
The original prognosis had the curve landing at 8,668' TVD at 90.0°. The Base of Last Salt
top did not provide cause to shift the target. At the Lodgepole B marker it became apparent
that dip was steeper than originally expected and the target needed to be moved up. The
decision was made to move the target up 3' so that we could still land at 85º and safely not
land in the Upper Bakken Shale. The curve was landed at a TVD of 8,567' at 83.7°, about 31'
TVD below the top of the Middle Bakken, 12' below the ideal target zone.
The original ideal target zone for the Kukuk was a 12' target starting 8' below the base of the
Upper Bakken Shale on the inflection point of a high gamma marker. The 12' zone was
picked for its useful gamma markers to aid in geosteering. However these markers proved to
be inconsistent so a new geosteering strategy was adopted. Under the new strategy the target
remained 12' thick but started 20' into the Middle Bakken instead of 8'. The hot gamma
marker at top of the target zone provided the best steering marker during the lateral. The well
successfully reached a total depth of 18,430' on December 29, 2014. The well remained in
the target zone for 84% of the lateral.

SUMMARY
The Oasis Petroleum North America, LLC Kukuk 6192 43-21B was drilled by Xtreme 21
from spud to TD in 20 days. The Kukuk reached a total depth of 18,430' MD on December
29, 2014. Geologic data, hydrocarbon gas measurements, and sample examination obtained

RE: Filter Socks and Other Filter Media
Leakproof Container Required
Oil and Gas Wells
Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
