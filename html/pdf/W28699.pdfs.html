Introduction
Emerald Oil, Inc. successfully drilled the Joel Goodsen 5-32-29H horizontal well
at an average azimuth of 359.0º and an average inclination of 89.75°, targeting the
Middle Bakken Formation beginning in Section 32, T149N, R102W, (370' FSL & 1250'
FEL) in McKenzie County, North Dakota, drilling north into the northwest quarter of the
northwest quarter of Section 29, T149N, R102W, with a spacing unit consisting of 1280
acres. The well is located in the Moline Field in the southwest portion of the Williston
Basin, North Dakota. The main wells used for correlation were the vertical and curve
sections of the Davis-Choctaw Cross 24-32 in Section 32 T149N, R102W, the Joel
Goodsen 6-32-29H, and the Joel Goodsen 7-32-29H; both drilled on the same pad
location.
Operations Summary
Vertical Section
The Joel Goodsen 5-32-29H was spud on October 18, 2014. Brara
Geologic Consulting arrived on site October 22, 2014 and began analysis on the
Joel Goodsen 5-32-29H. A Mudlogging Systems, Inc. Redbox gas detection
system was used for analyzing and recording total gas and chromatograph data.
Surface casing was pre-drilled and set at 2167' MD, with fresh water used for
drilling surface. A diesel invert mud system was used in the vertical and curve.
The first bottom-hole assembly (BHA) used in the vertical was comprised
of (Bit #2) an 8 ¾'' Security MM65D, a Hunting 1.5° mud motor, and an HDS-1
MWD tool. This assembly drilled 8302' in 100.5 hours to the kick-off point (KOP)
at 10469' MD, 10468' TVD, (-8048' SS). 0.5 hours. KOP was reached on October
24, 2014 at 18:15, and a trip was made to pick up the curve assembly.
Curve Section
Drilling resumed on October 25, 2014 at 09:30 with the curve assembly,
which included (Bit #3), an 8.75'' Smith MDi516 paired with a Pathfinder 2.5°
bend mud motor and HDS-1 MWD gamma tool. This assembly drilled 806' in
19.25 hours to the landing of the curve at 11275' MD, 10952' TVD (-8532' SS).
The curve was landed at 0605 on October 26, 2014 at an inclination of 88.5° and
an azimuth of 324.24°, with a landing point location in Section 32, T149N,
R102W, (759.03' FNL & 1519.75' FEL).

non-mag pin by pin X/O, and an HDS-1L (directional and back up gamma). This
BHA drilled 2917' in 55.5 hours to 14151' MD, when a trip was made due to mud

Base of Last Charles Salt
The Base of Last Charles Salt was picked from samples, and ROP at 9441' MD,
9440' TVD, (-7020' SS), four feet high to prognosis.
Base of Last Charles Salt Description
Samples were caught through the Charles Salt from 9200' MD to 9441'
MD (Base of The Last Charles Salt), continuing to 9613' MD (Mission Canyon
Top) every 30' by manually catching rock cuttings coming over the shakers.
Samples in the base of section contained abundant amounts of halite that
were off-white, semi-translucent and frosty, with coarse grain.
The limestone and dolomite in the samples was mudstone, light to
medium gray, very fine crystalline, slightly soft to occasionally firm, and
occasionally silty. Anhydrite in the formation remained predominantly white to off
white with occasional gray, microcrystalline in texture, very soft and occasionally
firm, amorphic and massive.
Mission Canyon Formation
The Mission Canyon Formation top was picked from samples at 9613' MD, 9611'
TVD, (-7191' SS). Formation background gas through the Mission Canyon varied from
30 to 60 units.
Mission Canyon Formation Lithology Descriptions
Samples were caught in 30' intervals from 9613' MD to 10167' MD
(Lodgepole Formation Top) through the Mission Canyon Formation by manually
catching rock cuttings in sample bags.
Limestone in sample was a light to dark gray, gray/brown, cream, sub-
blocky, soft to firm, argillaceous mudstone to wackestone, with trace
microcrystalline limestone. Visible inter-granular porosity was absent to very
poor. No oil staining was observed.

Lodgepole Formation
The Lodgepole top was picked from samples at 10167' MD, 10165' TVD, (-7745'
SS), four feet high to prognosis. Background gasses ranged between 20 to 40 units with
a trip gas of 100 units observed after picking up the curve assembly at KOP.
Lodgepole Formation Lithology Descriptions
Samples were caught in 30' intervals from 10167' MD to 11036' MD (False
Bakken Top) in the Lodgepole Formation by manually catching rock cuttings
coming over the shakers.
The Lodgepole was predominately comprised of argillaceous limestone
that ranged from being a mudstone to a wackestone. Color variations from light
to dark gray, with a very fine crystalline texture, firm hardness, occasional
lamination, very earthy texture, and occasional silt. No visible porosity was
observed throughout the formation. Slow white/blue cuts with a blue residual ring
were observed but were ascribed to residual invert drilling fluid in sample after
cleaning.
False Bakken Shale
The False Bakken top came in nine feet high to prognosis. The False Bakken top
was picked from samples, gamma ray, gas shows, and penetration rate at 11036' MD,
10910' TVD, (-8490' SS). Gas shows peaked at 3960 units in the False Bakken.
False Bakken Shale Lithology Descriptions
Samples were caught from 11036' MD to 11071' MD (Upper Bakken Top)
in 10' intervals through the False Bakken. Samples were comprised of shale and
limestone.
Shale in the False Bakken was mostly black, fissile in part, hard to brittle,
blocky-sub blocky, with an earthy texture and occasionally waxy surfaces.
Limestone in sample was mudstone to wackestone with rare silt. Coloration
ranged from light to dark gray, with occasional laminations, a very earthy texture
with no visible porosity and occasional trace amounts of pyrite.
Dull, even, yellow/orange fluorescence was observed in the samples when
viewed under a fluoroscope. Cuts in the False Bakken were moderately fast and
milky blue/white, but were heavily contaminated by invert drilling fluid.

Upper Bakken Shale
The Upper Bakken Shale top was picked from samples, gamma ray, and
penetration rate at 11071' MD, 10923' TVD, (-8503' SS), seven feet lower than
prognosis data. Total gas shows in the Upper Bakken reached a high of 5318 units.
Upper Bakken Shale Lithology Descriptions
Samples were caught from 11071' MD to 11109' MD (Middle Bakken Top)
in 10' intervals through the Upper Bakken by manually catching samples at the
shakers.
Shale in the Upper Bakken was black, mainly fissile but occasionally hard
to brittle, blocky to sub-blocky, earthy, petroliferous and carbonaceous. Even,
yellow, mineral fluorescence and moderate, bright, blue/white cuts were
observed when examined under a fluoroscope, possibly a result of intense
cleaning due to invert drilling fluid in the cuttings.
Middle Bakken Dolomite
The Middle Bakken top was picked from sample lithology, gamma and
penetration rate at 11109' MD, 10934' TVD, (-8514' SS).
Middle Bakken Lithology Descriptions
Samples were caught in 30' intervals through the Middle Bakken from
11109' MD to 11275' MD, and 100' intervals from 11275' MD to 20888' MD, by
manually catching cuttings from the shakers.
The Middle Bakken Member was dominantly light gray to tan with some
light to med brown argillaceous dolostone. Some micro calcite inclusions with
varying opacity and white coloration was observed. White and light brown micro
laminations were occasionally present. Cuttings ranged in shape from sub-blocky
to sub-platy with a dominantly grainstone texture varying from microcrystalline to
very fine grain crystalline. Visible porosity was generally poor. Trace to small
amounts of disseminated pyrite with occasional nodules was observed
throughout the Middle Bakken. Cementing was dolomite and calcite.
Overall, vertical wellbore position in formation showed no consistent
correlation to composition of color, accessory minerals, or availability of visual
porosity in samples.

Closing Summary
The Joel Goodsen 5-32-29H was drilled from south to north
in the Middle
Bakken Member with the target beginning 13 vertical feet from the bottom of the
packstone to two feet above the Lower Bakken Shale. The well bore was estimated to
have been in target zone for 98% of the lateral. The average calculated dip was 90.21°.
Local formation dip was calculated at 90.2º in the beginning of the lateral
between the intermediate casing shoe to 12000' MD. This section had an average C3
percentage of 0% to 3% with total background gas averaging from 200 to 350 units. At
12400' MD local dip began to level out to 90.0°
From 14000' to 16400' a localized dip of 89.8° was observed. Gas through this
section of the lateral averaged between 400 and 550 units with 0% to 3% C3 and C4
heavy gasses. The middle of the target showed the highest gas readings with higher C3
and C4 percentages. From 16771' MD to 16961' MD the wellbore briefly entered the
Lower Bakken Shale for a total footage of 190' due to a combination of LWD tool failure,
deflection of the wellbore after a downslide, and a reversal of local dip by approximately
1 degree.
At 16800' MD formation dip began to come up to a 90.8° for the next 1500' to
18300' MD. Formation gases averaged between 200 and 300 units with 0% to 3% C3
and C4 heavy gasses. Formation dip reversed again to 89.7° between 18400' to 18900'
MD.
At 19000' MD the formation dip came down to 89.5° and remained at a decline
to 20888' TD. Formation gas between 19000' and 20888' MD/TD averaged 275 units
with 0% to 8% C3 and C4.
Most samples in the lateral exhibited a trace to moderate amount of light yellow
to white mineral fluorescence when examined under a fluoroscope. Wellbore position
high in zone showed small amounts of light yellow oil fluorescence. Oil cuts were
commonly slow to moderately fast, diffuse, milky blue to yellow in color, with spotty
yellow to blue residual rings. Some samples were contaminated with petroleum-based
lubricant, which was used to achieve adequate bit weight to reduce torque and drag
during drilling and sliding. Lubricant contamination masked some fluorescence and oil
cuts in some of the samples.
After of 20888' MD/TD was reached on November 8, 2014, Brara Geologic
Consulting was released from the Joel Goodsen 5-32-29H location while the rig was
preparing to run production casing on November 9, 2014.

Conclusions
1. The Emerald Oil, Inc. Joel Goodsen 5-32-29H was a successfully drilled eight-
run lateral well in the Middle Bakken Formation from a surface location in
Section 32, T149N, R102W (370' FSL & 1250' FEL) to TD of 20888' MD, 10967'
TVD, (-8547' SS), and bottom hole location in Section 29, T149N, R102W
(251.82' FNL & 1922' FEL) in McKenzie County, North Dakota. The lateral was
drilled from the intermediate casing shoe to TD in four runs. Total depth was
reached at 05:15 on November 8, 2014. The Joel Goodsen 5-32-29H required
21 total days of drilling and setting intermediate casing.
2. Ultraviolet sample shows in the Joel Goodsen 5-32-29H lateral would be rated
as poor to fair with slow to moderately fast, diffuse and streaming cuts, and
spotty, yellow/blue residual rings. Total gas shows would also be rated as poor
to fair, with overall visible porosity rated as poor.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
