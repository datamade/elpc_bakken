GEOLOGIST'S SUMMARY
INTRODUCTION
XTO Energy, Inc.'s Eide 31X-29C (250' FNL, 2479' FEL, NWNE Sec 29, T158N-R95W, Williams Co.,
ND) is located approximately 6 miles northwest of Tioga, in Williams Co. ND. The well is east of the
Nesson anticline and part of a 4 well pad program designed to develop oil production in a unit
composed of parts of Sections 29 and 32. The Eide 31X-29C well was drilled to 1,914' and 9-
5/8"surface casing set to a depth of 1,909' MD, then drilled to 9950' and 7" intermediate casing was set
to a depth of 9,936'. The lateral was then drilled to 19304' and a production liner run. The well is to be a
Middle Bakken lateral. The intent was to remain in the 18' thick Target Zone of the Middle Bakken,
stratigraphically situated from 22' to 40' beneath the base of the Upper Bakken Shale. Through
geosteering and directional drilling techniques the wellbore was drilled to maximize its extent in the
target zone to the Total Depth of 19304'.
EQUIPMENT AND SAMPLING
Gas detection and wellsite geological supervision commenced at 8,000', above the base of the last salt.
Geologic analysis for Eide 31X-29C was provided by a Decollement Consulting geology team.
Information was networked through a Pason electronic data recorder system. This equipment provided
depth, ROPs, pump strokes, total gas units, and other drilling parameters to monitors across the drilling
location and internet.
Sample collection began above the base of the last salt and continued with 30' interval samples until
total depth was reached. Rock cuttings were analyzed in wet and dry conditions under a binocular
microscope and also with a fluoroscope to analyze fluorescence and solvent cuts. Detailed lithologic
descriptions are included in the lithology portion to this report. Sample quality was good for most of the
well. Cuttings will be sent to the North Dakota Geologic Survey and to XTO Energy, Inc.
OFFSET DATA
The primary offset wells used for structural correlation during curve operations were the XTO Energy,
Inc. Eide 31X-29G, and the Petro-Hunt Pederson AI 1 wells. These wells were useful in the vertical
hole and during curve drilling operations while landing the well. The TVD log of the Middle Bakken from
the Eide 31-29G well was used as the main reference during the horizontal geosteering of the well.
DRILLING THE VERTICAL AND CURVE HOLE SECTIONS
The Eide 31X-29C spudded October 20, 2014 and reached a surface casing depth of 1914' and was
cased and cemented to 1909'. The intermediate and curve hole section was then drilled to 9950' and 7"
casing was run to 9936' and cemented in preparation for the drilling of the lateral in the Middle Bakken.
Gas detection and wellsite geological supervision commenced at 8,000', several hundred feet above
the base of the last salt. Hydrocarbon constituents (C1 through C4) are expressed in part-per-million
concentrations. Sampled gas was pulled from Pason's agitator in the possum belly and analyzed by
Pason's Gas Analyzer to deliver the compositional data.
A successful Middle Bakken landing was accomplished by comparing gamma signatures from the
offsets to the MWD TVD gamma ray log produced during the drilling of the Eide 31X-29G. The landing
target true vertical depth (TVD) was periodically updated to ensure accurate landing of the curve within
the Middle Bakken as near the target window as possible.

The Base of the Last Salt of the Charles Formation was encountered at a depth of 8,134' MD (TVD
8,130'). At MD 8,175' (TVD 8,171') the Ratcliffe was entered, and was a light to medium grey algal
limestone (mudstone) was drilled. Some stringers of anhydrite were also noted. The Mission Canyon
formation (a gray dense to finely crystalline mudstone) was then drilled into at MD 8,335' (TVD 8,332').
The Lodgepole formation came in at 8,950' MD (8,947 TVD). Samples showed a medium to dark grey
mudstone. Drilling then continued through the Lodgepole limestone to the KOP at 9,155' MD. The BHA
was changed out to a curve building assembly, the BHA was then tripped in and the curve was drilled
to 9,950' MD. Background gas varied from 45 units to 116 units during the curve drilling of the
Lodgepole. Sliding continued through the Lodgepole formation to the top of the Upper Bakken Shale,
which gamma showed coming in at MD 9,755' (TVD 9,590'), a 14' zone of black carbonaceous shale
with some pyrite. The highest gas reading recorded was 685 units. The Middle Bakken Dolomite was
encountered at MD 9,755' (TVD 9,590'), with gamma and samples confirming the light gray, very silty
dolomite. Some dull yellow fluorescence was observed that had a good flush cut when exposed to
solvent. Total gas units ranged from 90 to 510 units with proportionate C1 through C4's.
GEOLOGIC INTERPRETATION OF THE MIDDLE BAKKEN TARGET SECTION
For geosteering and evaluation purposes a TVD log of the well's target zone was created from the
MWD data recorded during the drilling of the Eide 31X-29G and then used as a reference during the
drilling of the lateral by the wellsite team. See figure 1.
Figure 1. TVD gamma log of the Eide 31X-29G well.
During the lateral there were some continuing similarities in the samples and gamma readings as the
wellbore traversed the stratigraphic section of the target zone. The drilled cuttings observed were
mostly composed of calcareous, very silty and sandy dolomites ranging from mudstone to wackestone
in the Dunham classification. The colors were mostly light grey and very light brown. The cuttings
ranged from soft to firm throughout the target section and were clean to slightly argillaceous. Minor
constituents were mostly of anhydrite and pyrite seen mainly as the well path went through the upper
portion of the target zone in the first half of the well and below the target zone in the second half of the
well where the anhydrite and pyrite lessened considerably in the target zone. The most distinctive
changes when drilling through the section were noticed in the color of the uppermost portion of the
target zone when drilling the low gamma, clean dolomitic limestone known locally as the "packstone". It
was off white to a very light brown and contained significant amounts of pyrite in some samples. This
6
clean rock also damaged the bit so that a trip for a new bit was required after drilling around 500 feet of
it.
The hydrocarbon shows seen during the lateral consisted of a sometimes barely perceptible, very dull
to dull yellow fluorescence that varied from less than 10 up to 70 percent of the cuttings, with the
solvent cuts ranging from poor to good flush cuts.
DRILLING AND GEOSTEERING OF THE MIDDLE BAKKEN LATERAL
The geosteering of the lateral was done by the XTO Operations Geologist with input from the wellsite
geologic team, with a goal of maximizing the wellbore contact with the best reservoir rock available
while minimizing dogleg severity and drilling as fast as conditions permitted.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
