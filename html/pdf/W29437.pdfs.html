INTRODUCTION
XTO Energy, Inc. Sorenson 14X-33A [SW SW Section 33, T150N, R98W] is located approximately 2.5
miles south-southeast of Watford, in Siverston Field of McKenzie County, North Dakota (Figure 1). The
well was designed as a Middle Bakken horizontal well to develop Sections 33 and 28. Directional drilling
and geosteering would be employed to land in the Middle Bakken reservoir and maintain maximum
exposure to a 20' target zone.
GEOLOGY
Methods
Geologic analysis for the Sorenson 14X-33A was provided by a Sunburst Consulting, Inc. geology team.
Sunburst well site geologists caught lagged samples at 50' intervals starting just above the Tyler
Formation and increased to 30' samples just above the Base Last Charles Salt. Rock samples, rate of
penetration and gamma ray data were used in geologic analysis to aid in geosteering and dip calculations.
Prospective Lithology
The Tyler Formation was logged at 8,387' MD / 8,385' TVD, 19' low to GV 44-32NEH. The lithology
here was mostly black to gray shale which was bulky with earthy texture and showed trace disseminated
pyrite. There was two notable gas shows within this formation that reached 175 units and 168 units
against 9.65 ppg mud weight.
The Charles Formation was logged at 9,071' MD / 9,069' TVD, 1' high to GV 44-32NEH. This
formation consists of relatively thick salt layers interbedded with clean and argillaceous limestone and
dolomite. A layer of dolomite around the middle of the Charles produced a gas show of 171 units against
a 9.75 ppg mud weight.
The Ratcliffe was drilled at 9,799' MD / 9,797' TVD, 4' low to prognosis and 5' high to GV 44-32NEH.
This interval was mainly mudstone limestone, which was cream to medium gray brown in color, friable to
firm and with an earthy texture. It also consisted of anhydrite and dolomite. Gas shows ranged from 88
units to 126 units against a 9.8 ppg mud weight.
The Mission Canyon Formation was drilled at 9,949' MD / 9,947' TVD, 4' high to prognosis and 15'
low to GV 44-32NEH. The Mission Canyon consists primarily of clean, argillaceous and dolomitic
limestone. The upper Mission Canyon is primarily friable to firm, light to medium gray argillaceous
limestone. While the lower lithology consisted primarily of medium to dark gray, microcrystalline
limestone mudstone. Hydrocarbon shows ranged from 104 to 298 units in 9.8 ppg drilling mud.
The argillaceous limestone signifying the top of the Lodgepole Formation was penetrated at 10,500'
MD / 10,498' TVD which is 20' high to prognosis and 9' low to GV 44-32NEH. This slope marine
deposit consisted of mostly gray to brown argillaceous mudstone that was firm to hard, microcrystalline
and contained rare disseminated pyrite. A fracture gas at the base of this formation read 503 units against
a 10.1 ppg drilling mud.
The False Bakken shale was drilled at 11,418' MD / 11,315' TVD which was 18' low to prognosis and
7' low to GV 44-32NEH. This relatively thin lithology consists of medium to dark gray brown shale. A
single gas show of 889 units was witnessed against a 10.1 ppg mud weight.
The Scallion limestone was drilled at 11,429' MD / 11,320' TVD which was 19' low to prognosis and 8'
low to GV 44-32NEH. The Scallion consists of clean, cream to light brown microcrystalline wackestone,
which contains trace amounts of disseminated pyrite. A fracture gases here read 681 units against 10.1
ppg mud.
The Bakken Formation has four members, two of which are organic-rich, pyritic shale, separated by a
siltstone and silty sandstone middle member and a medium gray siltstone, known as the Pronghorn
Member underneath. The carbonaceous Upper Bakken Shale was penetrated at 11,441' MD / 11,325'
TVD, which was 8' low to prognosis and 7' low to GV 44-32NEH.
Gas shows through the shale
measured upwards of 3,122 units against 10.20 ppg mud. The Middle Bakken was penetrated at 11,501'
MD / 11,346' TVD, 10' low to prognosis and 10' low to GV 44-32NEH. The targeted Middle Member
lithology consisted of light to medium brown gray, friable to firm, sub-blocky, very fine grained silty
sandstone which was moderately calcite-cemented (Figure 2). Trace light brown oil staining was typical
along with disseminated pyrite throughout most of the target zone as well as trace intergranular porosity.
Figure 2: Wet cuttings of the upper target Middle Bakken silty sandstone (left) & lower silty sandstone (right).
Lateral Gas and Oil Shows
The background gas for the Sorenson 14X-33A ranged from 124 to 1,676 units during the course of the
lateral. The highest gas show was 1,695 units at 19,503' MD against 10.15 ppg mud. Higher gas shows
were generally witnessed in the upper and middle part of the target zone. The gas buster was venting gas
throughout the lateral and mud was fully circulated through the gas buster starting at 19,590' MD. A 2' to
12' intermittent flare was witnessed after mud was circulated through the gas buster.
Geosteering
A 20' target zone was chosen for the Sorenson 14X-33A by XTO Energy geologists that began 10' below
the Upper Bakken Shale and ended 13' from the top of the Lower Bakken Shale. The thickness was
estimated using control well logs. A well drilled previously, 0.3 miles away, the GV 44-32NH, provided
guidance for expected gamma ray markers while drilling the lateral (Figure 3). The top of the target zone
had thin beds of high gamma that were interbedded with low gamma beds. The higher gamma would read
above 100 API, getting as high as 125 API, while the cooler gamma markers would read as low as 30
API. Below this gamma would normalize to around 85 API before getting into a zone of very "ratty"
gamma. In this zone, gamma would move up and down from 50 API to 100 API very quickly. Below this
"ratty" gamma was a zone of moderate gamma reading around 90API. At the bottom of zone was a low
gamma marker reading 55 API and at the very edge of the lower target area gamma would get into the
low 100s API.
Figure 3. Gamma type log of the Middle Bakken from the GV 44-32NH well. Shaded green is the Upper and
Lower Bakken Shale, yellow is the area above target, blue is targeted zone and brown is below target.
The prognosis projected the curve landing at 11,356' TVD. While drilling the curve, Lodgepole markers
started coming in lower than expected and it was decided to lower the target to 11,362' TVD. The False
Bakken came in 8' low to the Sorenson 14X-33F so it was decided to move the target down another 2' to
11,364'. The Middle Bakken came in at a TVD of 11,346' which moved the landing target to 11,366'.
The curve was landed successfully at 11,643' MD with a projected TVD of 11,364.86'--1.14' high to the
final landing target and 8.86' into the target zone.
Suggested prognosis dip provided by XTO Energy, Inc., stated that dip would be close to flat until
13,000' MD where it would change to 0.29° down. At 17,000' MD prognosis dip would change to 0.29°
up. Dip would then go flat at 19,000' MD. Structurally, the formation dropped an estimated 8.20' over
9,313' from entering the Middle Bakken to the end of the lateral resulting in an overall apparent dip of
0.05° down (Figure 4).

Bottom-Hole Location
Total depth resulted in a closure azimuth of 358.58° and a closure distance of 9,811.11'. The bottom-hole
location is projected to be 9,807.21' north and 276.83' west of the surface location or approximately
253.23' FNL & 818.17' FWL, NW NW Section 28, T150N, R98W. The hole was prepared for liner and
multi-stage fracture stimulation directly after drilling operations concluded.
SUMMARY
The XTO Energy, Inc. Sorenson 14X-33A was spud on November 2, 2014 by Advanced Energy Rig #36
and re-entered by H&P 456 on January 22, 2015. The well efficiently reached TD in 18 total drilling days
on February 4, 2015. Samples from the Middle Bakken target contained oil staining that was described as
trace and spotty with visible intergranular porosity. MWD gamma ray markers, sample analysis, and ROP
were successfully utilized to expose 100% of the lateral borehole to the Middle Bakken target zone. The
Sorenson 14X-33A awaits completion operations to maximize its production potential.

RE:
Filter Socks and Other Filter Media
Leakproof Container Required
Oil and Gas Wells
Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
Thank you for your cooperation.
