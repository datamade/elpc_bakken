GEOLOGICAL SUMMARY & CONCLUSIONS
LOCATION:
Continental Resources, Inc. drilled the Debrecen 3-3H1 as a two-section horizontal well
targeting the Late Devonian, Three Forks Formation in Stark County, North Dakota. The surface
hole coordinates are 330' FNL, 750' FWL of Sec. 3, T140N, R99W. The well traversed South at
an approximate 180° azimuth and 90.28° inclination to a final bottom hole location at 230' FSL,
679' FWL of Sec. 10, T140N, R99W.
OPERATIONS:
Continental Resources, Inc. drilled the Debrecen 3-3H1 using Helmerich & Payne
Drilling Company Rig #257. NorAm Wellsite Services provided the mud logging and geological
supervision. Lagged total gas, which was calibrated to a one percent methane in air standard, and
chromatography data were monitored and recorded using NorAm Wellsite Services Advanced
Gas Detector Chromatographs (AGD) 01 and 07. Geosteering and borehole guidance were
accomplished with the aid of total gas data, lagged geological samples, NOV EDR workstation,
gamma ray logging, and directional drilling survey data. MS Energy Services provided the
Directional Drilling (DD) and Measurement While Drilling (MWD) services.
9 5/8" diameter surface casing was drilled out at 2352' MD, 2352' TVD, 394' SS at 19:24
CST on August 21st, 2014. NorAm Wellsite Services began geological supervision at 8910' MD
at 20:57 on August 24th, 2014. The curve began at Kick Off Point (KOP) at 10349' MD, 10348'
TVD, -7602' SS within the Lodgepole Formation at 02:55 on August 28th, 2014. Drilling the
curve, the objective was to land the bit at 90.0° inclination at approximately 10794' TVD, 12'
TVD below where the Three Forks top was expected. The curve was landed at 11057' MD,
10788' TVD, -8042' SS, at an inclination of 90.2°, 14' TVD into the Three Forks at 15:06 on
August 28th, 2014. The 7" diameter intermediate casing was installed and cemented in place at
11034' MD, 10788' TVD, -8042' SS. The horizontal portion of the well began after drilling out of
the cement, float, and shoe at 11057' MD at 05:11 on August 31st, 2014. TD was reached at
21594' MD, 10742' TVD, -7996' SS at 15:45 on September 7th, 2014.
GEOLOGIC CONCLUSIONS:
Formation tops, projected targets, and geologic steering were determined by correlating
distinct gamma signatures, wellbore surveys, rock cuttings, gas and oil shows, rates of
penetration, and differential pressures.
Target zone was 5-25' TVD into the Three Forks. The top of target zone, 5' TVD into the
Three Forks, was defined by a decrease in gamma radiation signatures from 110 cpm and higher
down to 60 cpm and lower. Marker 1, 17' TVD into the Three Forks, was defined by a high
gamma radiation signature of 90 cpm and higher. The bottom of target zone, 25' TVD into the
Three Forks, was defined by a low gamma radiation signature of 70 cpm and lower. Marker 2,
32' TVD into the Three Forks, was defined by a high gamma radiation signature of 105 cpm and
higher.

GEOLOGICAL SUMMARY & CONCLUSIONS (CONT.)
The best drilling occurred between Marker 1 and the bottom of target zone. Hard streaks
occurred at Marker 1 and the bottom of target zone, which often deflected the bit.
Rock cuttings above target zone exhibited dark gray to brown dolomite with gray, blue,
and green shales. Trace disseminated pyrite occurred here. Within the top and middle of target
zone, rock cuttings exhibited lighter colored dolomite, a general lack of green shale, and more
abundant anhedral and euhedral pyrite. In the bottom of target zone and below, the rock cuttings
exhibited similar features as within upper target zone, but with less euhedral pyrite, and a return
of green shales.
Fluorescence, cuts, and gas shows generally decreased as depth into the Three Forks
increased. Above target zone and in the top of target zone, background gasses were up to 800
units. In the bottom of target zone and below target zone, background gasses were significantly
lower, ranging from 300-650 units. Above target zone, rock cuttings exhibited yellow green
fluorescence with moderately fast yellow, green, and white blooming and diffuse cuts. Rock
cuttings within target zone exhibited dull yellow and green fluorescence or no fluorescence at all
with slow diffuse yellow green diffuse cuts. Rock samples below target zone exhibited very little
yellow and green fluorescence or no fluorescence at all with limited to no diffuse yellow green
cuts.

Dear Operator,
North Dakota Administrative Code Section 43-02-03-19.2 states in part that all waste material associated with
exploration or production of oil and gas must be properly disposed of in an authorized facility in accord with all
applicable local, state, and federal laws and regulations.
Filtration systems are commonly used during oil and gas operations in North Dakota. The Commission is very
concerned about the proper disposal of used filters (including filter socks) used by the oil and gas industry.
Effective June 1, 2014, a container must be maintained on each well drilled in North Dakota beginning when the
well is spud and must remain on-site during clean-out, completion, and flow-back whenever filtration operations
are conducted. The on-site container must be used to store filters until they can be properly disposed of in an
authorized facility. Such containers must be:
leakproof to prevent any fluids from escaping the container
covered to prevent precipitation from entering the container
placard to indicate only filters are to be placed in the container
If the operator will not utilize a filtration system, a waiver to the container requirement will be considered, but
only upon the operator submitting a Sundry Notice (Form 4) justifying their request.
As previously stated in our March 13, 2014 letter, North Dakota Administrative Code Section 33-20-02.1-01
states in part that every person who transports solid waste (which includes oil and gas exploration and production
wastes) is required to have a valid permit issued by the North Dakota Department of Health, Division of Waste
Management. Please contact the Division of Waste Management at (701) 328-5166 with any questions on the
solid waste program. Note oil and gas exploration and production wastes include produced water, drilling mud,
invert mud, tank bottom sediment, pipe scale, filters, and fly ash.
